# Executive Summary

Q1 2026 opened under conditions of **controlled instability**. It closed under conditions of **structural rupture**. Economic growth remained positive through the quarter, but markets were operating under tighter financing, uneven disinflation, and heightened geopolitical constraint when a decisive escalation changed the calculus entirely.

On April 2, 2026 — designated "Liberation Day" by the US administration — sweeping reciprocal tariffs were announced across virtually all trading partners. The move was not incremental. It was architectural: a deliberate restructuring of the terms on which global trade operates. Within days, the S&P 500 had lost approximately 10–12% from recent highs, the US dollar weakened contrary to conventional risk-off logic, oil fell sharply on demand destruction fears, and the US 10-year Treasury yield spiked toward 4.5% before partial retreat. A 90-day pause on some tariffs was announced April 9 — but US-China tariffs remained in force at 145% (US) and 125% (China). The pause reduced noise; it did not reduce the underlying risk.

**This report has been updated to version 1.3.0 to integrate these developments.** The scenario set, major economy readings, sector map, and board recommendations have all been revised accordingly.

## Quarter-Defining Realities

1. **Disinflation did not restore simplicity** — sticky core inflation constrained central bank flexibility throughout Q1
2. **Geopolitics turned allocational** — capital was already discriminating by jurisdiction before April 2
3. **The market premium shifted to governed durability** — balance-sheet quality and policy stability commanded a growing premium
4. **Liberation Day restructured the trade architecture** — what had been geopolitical risk became operational fact for global supply chains, pricing models, and board-level capital decisions

> **Bottom line for v1.3.0:** The quarter rewarded disciplined positioning under friction. The close of the quarter demanded boards reprice assumptions entirely. Capital is no longer pricing growth under globalisation. It is pricing survivability under fragmentation.

**Capital is no longer just pricing growth; it is explicitly pricing survivability, strategic optionality, and jurisdictional discipline. After April 2, it is also pricing the durability of supply chains, the stability of trade relationships, and the credibility of corporate revenue models built on cross-border flow assumptions.**

## Practical Implications — Updated

- Valuations increasingly reflect balance-sheet integrity, tariff exposure, and policy risk
- Geographic diversification is now filtered through sanction, regulatory, and tariff exposure simultaneously
- Supply chains built on US-China interdependence face structural repricing, not cyclical disruption
- Boards must read markets as stress transmitters and trade-policy execution environments — not applause meters
- The 90-day pause is a tactical signal, not a strategic resolution; boards should not plan into the pause as though it were stability

# Global Macro Snapshot — Updated April 2026

## Growth and Inflation

- IMF global growth projection revised downward from ~3.3% to approximately **2.8%** for 2026, with further downside risk contingent on tariff duration
- UNCTAD more conservative, clustering around **2.3–2.5%** under escalation scenarios
- Private houses have moved estimates toward **2.5–2.7%**, with US recession probability now cited by several at 40–60% over a 12-month horizon
- Core inflation in the US remains sticky; the Liberation Day tariffs introduce a **supply-side inflation shock** that puts the Federal Reserve in a paralysed position — raising rates risks recession, cutting rates risks re-igniting inflation

## The Federal Reserve: Frozen Architecture

The Federal Reserve entered April 2026 on hold. The tariff shock has compounded this position into a structural freeze. The Fed cannot cut without appearing to validate inflationary supply disruption. It cannot raise without amplifying the economic slowdown. Expect extended hold, with the first cut now pushed well into H2 2026 at the earliest — and only if growth deterioration dominates the inflation signal.

## Interpretation

Q1 2026 should be read not as recovery, but as **managed strain entering unmanaged rupture**. The controlled instability of January–March gave way to a structural inflection point in April. Boards that were still stress-testing against the January environment are already operating on stale assumptions.

# Major Economies — Updated Readings

## United States

### Quarter Observations — Updated

- Consumption remained the primary growth driver through Q1 but faces forward pressure from tariff-driven price increases
- Core inflation remains sticky; Liberation Day tariffs will add an estimated 1–2 percentage points to consumer price pressure over the next two to four quarters
- Equity leadership had already narrowed; post-April 2 selloff has broadened the correction
- Political risk is now fully allocational — the administration's willingness to absorb short-term market pain changes the risk calculus for all dollar-denominated assets
- The dollar weakened despite the risk-off environment — an unusual signal suggesting loss of confidence in US policy predictability as a safe-haven anchor

### Strategic Reading — Updated

The US remains the most liquid and institutionally credible jurisdiction, but the credibility of that framing is under active stress. The higher-for-longer cost-of-capital regime, combined with tariff-driven supply-side inflation and a Fed unable to act, creates a narrow path for corporate models dependent on cheap capital and predictable input costs.

**Board instruction:** Do not anchor to pre-Liberation Day growth assumptions. Stress-test revenue models against 12–18 months of elevated tariff costs. Model Fed inaction through at least Q3 2026.

## China

### Major Themes — Updated

- China's retaliatory tariffs at **125%** on US goods represent a near-total severance of normal trade economics between the two largest economies
- Property drag continues; industrial policy remains the primary stabilisation mechanism
- The yuan has been managed carefully; any significant depreciation would represent a further escalation signal
- Foreign capital remains selective and is now actively re-routing supply chains away from China-US interdependence
- Chinese exports are being redirected toward ASEAN, Middle East, and African markets with speed — the trade network is actively restructuring

### Strategic Reading — Updated

China is managing a controlled transition away from US-market dependence while protecting internal stability. Boards with China-based manufacturing serving US markets face the most acute exposure. The 145%/125% tariff structure makes direct China-US supply chains economically inoperable at most margin levels.

**Board instruction:** Any supply chain with China manufacturing serving US end-markets requires immediate structural review. The question is no longer whether to restructure — it is how fast and at what cost.

## Eurozone

### Pressure Points — Updated

- Uneven industrial competitiveness was the pre-existing condition; US tariffs have added external shock
- The EU is preparing countermeasures; the timeline and scope remain subject to internal negotiation
- European exporters with US exposure face an asymmetric position: caught between US tariffs and domestic fiscal constraints
- The ECB has more room to move than the Fed and may cut earlier — which creates a relative monetary policy divergence that bears watching for EUR/USD positioning

### Strategic Reading — Updated

The Eurozone is a zone of defensive sophistication under additional external pressure. The EU's countermeasure preparation signals institutional resolve, but execution will be slow. Boards with significant European manufacturing or US-export exposure through Europe need to model the countermeasure timeline as an operating variable.

## United Kingdom

### Quarter Themes — Updated

- Services remain stronger than goods
- The UK's relative position is ambiguous: outside the EU, the UK faces US tariffs without the bloc-level negotiating leverage of the EU
- City of London capital flows have shown resilience but are not immune to dollar uncertainty
- Sterling has performed better than might be expected — partly reflecting the UK's lower direct goods-trade exposure to the US relative to Germany or China

### Strategic Reading — Updated

The UK remains structurally relevant in law, finance, and capital plumbing. The tariff shock is less acute for the UK than for manufacturing-heavy economies, but the secondary effects — dollar weakness, global demand slowdown, EU economic fragility — will transmit through financial channels.

# Cross-Cutting Markets — Updated

## Commodities and Energy

**Updated Reading**
Oil fell sharply on Liberation Day demand destruction fears — WTI and Brent dropped materially as markets priced a global slowdown. Commodity exposure is geopolitical exposure in disguise, now amplified by tariff-driven demand uncertainty. Energy transition commodities face a complex dynamic: higher demand structural thesis, lower near-term demand signal.

---

## FX and Rates

**Updated Reading**
The dollar's weakness in a risk-off environment is the most significant and anomalous signal of the Liberation Day aftermath. Typically, dollar strengthens in risk-off. The reversal suggests markets are beginning to price US policy unpredictability as a risk to the reserve currency premium. This is not a conclusion — it is a signal that warrants monitoring. US 10-year yields spiked toward 4.5% before partial retreat — Treasury market stress is a leading indicator of financial condition tightening regardless of Fed action.

---

## Equity and Credit Conditions

**Updated Reading**
Public markets have repriced sharply. The S&P 500 correction of 10–12% from highs reflects genuine uncertainty, not mere sentiment. The 90-day pause produced a partial recovery, but the underlying tariff architecture remains. Credit conditions tighten as corporate revenue models face supply-side cost shock. Quality and cash-flow resilience remain the only reliable equity selection framework.

# Scenario Set — Updated for Q2 2026

## Scenario A — Negotiated De-escalation (Reduced Probability)

The 90-day pause produces bilateral agreements that reduce tariff levels materially. Markets recover. Growth forecasts stabilise. Probability: **15–20%**. Requires both US and China to accept face-saving off-ramps within a compressed timeline.

## Scenario B — Managed Fragmentation (Base Case)

Tariffs remain elevated but stabilise. Supply chains restructure over 12–24 months. Global growth slows to 2.5–2.8%. Inflation remains sticky. Fed holds. Equity markets remain range-bound with high volatility. Probability: **40–45%**.

## Scenario C — Escalation Spiral

Countermeasures proliferate. EU implements retaliatory tariffs. Dollar weakness accelerates. US Treasury market stress increases. Fed is forced to choose between inflation and recession. Probability: **25–30%**.

## Scenario D — Confidence Fracture

A sovereign funding event, corporate credit crisis, or geopolitical incident breaks the controlled-strain narrative entirely. Systemic repricing. Probability: **10–15%**, but consequence is non-linear.

## Base Case

**Managed Fragmentation** — extended elevated tariffs, slow supply chain restructuring, range-bound markets, Fed on hold through Q3 2026, global growth 2.5–2.8%. Boards should plan into this scenario while stress-testing for Escalation Spiral.

# Board-Level Recommendations — Updated

1. **Re-price all growth assumptions** — pre-Liberation Day forecasts are operationally stale. Every revenue model with cross-border dependency requires revision.

2. **Audit tariff exposure by supply chain node** — map every input source, every manufacturing location, and every end-market against the current tariff schedule. This is not a finance exercise; it is a board-level strategic audit.

3. **Strengthen cash discipline** — the cost of capital has not fallen and will not fall quickly. Companies that entered 2026 with weak balance sheets face compounding pressure.

4. **Do not plan into the 90-day pause as stability** — the pause reduced noise; it did not resolve the underlying architecture. Boards that restructure their assumptions around the pause risk a second shock when it expires or escalates.

5. **Model the Fed freeze explicitly** — do not assume monetary relief. The Fed is structurally constrained. Financial planning through H1 2026 should assume no rate cuts.

6. **Build supply chain optionality deliberately** — nearshoring, friend-shoring, and dual-sourcing strategies that were medium-term considerations are now urgent capital allocation decisions.

7. **Favour jurisdictional resilience over yield** — capital allocation should weight policy stability, rule of law, and tariff insulation alongside return metrics.

8. **Elevate trade policy monitoring to board level** — this is no longer a management-level operational consideration. The speed and scope of April 2 demonstrated that trade policy can restructure operating assumptions within days.

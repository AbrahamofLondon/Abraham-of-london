Global growth continues — institutional consensus remains in the low-3 percent area — but it is doing so under materially higher policy friction, more selective capital allocation, and less confidence in frictionless global trade. Organisations that are still pricing the environment as a temporary disruption to pre-2025 integration assumptions are taking structural risk disguised as continuity.

### Current Decision Window

Q3 2026.

### Five Non-Optional Operating Decisions

1. Convert scenario planning into structural planning.
2. Build dual or multi-node supply chains regardless of near-term efficiency cost.
3. Treat geopolitical risk as a financial reporting input.
4. Maintain defensive liquidity buffers as core strategy.
5. Identify and exit structurally impaired exposures before the market forces repricing.

1. **USD behaviour under stress conditions** — continued dollar instability during risk-off events signals reserve currency stress beyond normal volatility.

Managed Fragmentation — structural hypothesis: system stabilisation under elevated friction, consistent with continued slow growth in the IMF-consistent low-3 percent range. Operative probability: 45–50%. This is an analytical framework, not a demonstrated endpoint.

- Tariff rollback below structural thresholds on a sustained bilateral basis.
- Sustained USD strength during stress (rather than instability).
- Coordinated central bank easing that materially changes financial conditions.
- Re-synchronisation of global trade flows beyond current rerouting patterns.

## 3. Executive Summary

Q2 2026 is not a recession call. It is a regime-change call.

Global markets are still growing — institutional consensus, including the IMF, kept 2026 growth expectations in the low-3 percent range even under trade-policy pressure. But they are doing so under materially higher policy friction, more selective capital allocation, and substantially less confidence in frictionless global trade. That combination is the regime change.

What began as repricing in Q1 has transitioned into the absorption phase: markets are not reacting to policy signals. They are reorganising around them.

Three conditions now define the operating environment:

- Tariffs are functioning as persistent policy architecture, not temporary instruments.
- Capital allocation is becoming more jurisdiction-selective, though global fungibility persists in many channels.
- Liquidity management is shifting toward defensiveness, particularly among institutions exposed to tariff-affected sectors.

April's escalation was the trigger. Q2 is the absorption phase.

**Bottom line (Q2):** The structural question is not whether growth continues — it is whether organisations are pricing the friction correctly and building the optionality the new regime requires.

## 8. Major Economy Readings

### United States

**Reality shift:** Fiscal strength remains, but policy unpredictability is now priced in markets. Corporate margins facing compression cycle pressure in tariff-exposed sectors. Dollar behaviour under stress is a key monitoring variable — episodic weakness in risk-off conditions has been observed, but this is not yet confirmed as a broad regime shift in reserve dynamics. *(MONITORING — source evidence for Q2 USD behaviour pending.)*

**Strategic position:** The US remains the system anchor — but now as an anchor with elevated volatility. The credibility of that anchor role depends on policy trajectory; it is not structurally guaranteed.

**Board instruction:** Assume prolonged elevated cost structures in tariff-exposed inputs. Stress-test USD volatility scenarios in both directions without asserting a direction. Treat policy risk as a recurring pricing input, not a one-time externality.

### China

**Reality shift:** Trade decoupling now operational, not theoretical. Domestic stabilisation measures increasing. External demand re-routing accelerating.

**Strategic position:** China is transitioning into a parallel economic sphere under pressure.

**Board instruction:** Classify China exposure into domestic, regional, and US-linked. Treat US-linked flows as structurally impaired assets.

### Europe

**Reality shift:** Industrial base under sustained external pressure. Policy coordination improving but slow. Energy and defence spending rising.

**Strategic position:** Europe is becoming a defensive industrial bloc with institutional depth.

**Board instruction:** Model regulatory timelines as operational constraints. Position for policy-led sector support in defence, energy, and infrastructure.

### United Kingdom

**Reality shift:** Strength consolidating in services, legal systems, and capital routing. Relative insulation from direct tariff shocks.

**Strategic position:** The UK is emerging as a coordination hub within fragmentation — not a production centre, but a system connector.

**Board instruction:** Leverage UK positioning for structuring, financing, and dispute frameworks. Expand role in cross-border capital orchestration.

### India

**Monitored development:** India is directionally positioned as a beneficiary of supply chain realignment — trade-rebalancing commentary and manufacturing expansion signals support this reading. However, the pace of capital inflow absorption, execution capacity, and actual Q2 investment-flow data require source confirmation before this is presented as a hard Q2 conclusion. *(OPERATOR_JUDGEMENT supported by directional signals; Q2 capital flow evidence pending — INSTITUTIONAL_SOURCE required.)*

**Strategic position:** India is an emerging frontline beneficiary — the question under monitoring is whether execution capacity is keeping pace with realignment opportunity.

**Board instruction:** Treat India as a structural candidate for supply chain diversification; require due diligence on execution risk before committing capital.

### Southeast Asia

**Monitored development:** Industrial inflows and manufacturing realignment are directionally supported by trade-rebalancing dynamics. Origin-compliance enforcement is increasing and represents a real filter on routing arbitrage. The extent and speed of real-capacity build in specific markets require Q2 source confirmation. *(OPERATOR_JUDGEMENT — directional signals supported; Q2 specifics pending.)*

**Strategic position:** A high-opportunity, high-verification region. Real capacity building will be differentiated from routing arbitrage as enforcement tightens.

**Board instruction:** Require verification of genuine capacity, not transit positioning, before treating Southeast Asia as a confirmed supply chain alternative.

### Middle East

**Reality shift:** Oil price volatility affecting fiscal flows. Sovereign wealth deployment becoming more strategic and selective.

**Strategic position:** Emerging as a capital-allocator and logistics-intermediary bloc within fragmentation architecture.

### Africa

**Monitored development:** The thesis that Africa is transitioning toward strategic leverage — via critical minerals, infrastructure investment, and geopolitical positioning — is plausible and directionally supported by existing trade-rebalancing commentary. It is not yet supported by confirmed Q2-specific capital flow or investment data. *(LOW confidence — CALL-008 review window is Q3 2026. Present as monitored development, not hard Q2 conclusion.)*

**Strategic position:** Africa is a strategic monitoring priority within global fragmentation — the evidence base for a stronger claim will develop over Q3.

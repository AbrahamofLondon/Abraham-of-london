## 3. BOARD SUMMARY – Q2 2026

### Core Thesis

**Evidence posture: Directional / high confidence.**

Q2 2026 is a **regime‑change call**, not a recession call. Global growth continues — but the institutional outlook has *dispersed*, not converged: 2026 real‑GDP forecasts now span roughly 2.5%–3.0% (World Bank 2.5%, OECD 2.8%, Goldman Sachs 2.8%, IMF July 3.0% — revised down from 3.1% in April), reflecting materially different assumptions about tariff duration, conflict-driven energy disruption, and financial spillovers. That dispersion — under higher policy friction, more selective capital allocation, and less confidence in frictionless global trade — is itself the signal, and the direction of the July IMF revision (down, on conflict and fragmentation risk) reinforces rather than weakens it. Organisations still pricing the environment as a temporary disruption to pre‑2025 integration assumptions are taking structural risk disguised as continuity.

**Two regimes now operate at once — this is the Q2 frame.** A *structural* regime — the fragmentation of trade and capital into aligned blocs, which is slow-moving and durable — is running simultaneously with a *cyclical* regime — a stagflation shock combining sticky inflation (US headline CPI 4.2%, core 2.9% in May) with dispersed, constrained growth. Most operators are equipped to manage one at a time. The Q2 discipline is governing both together: the structural regime dictates *where* to position (bloc alignment, supply-chain optionality), while the cyclical regime dictates *how hard* to defend (liquidity buffers, margin protection, refinancing risk). Treating either in isolation misprices the other.

## 4. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Q2 2026 is not a recession call. It is a regime‑change call.

Global markets are still growing – but the institutions no longer agree on how strongly. 2026 growth forecasts have *spread apart* rather than converged (World Bank 2.5%, OECD 2.8%, Goldman Sachs 2.8%, IMF July 3.0% — cut from 3.1% on lock day, citing conflict-driven energy disruption), and they are being made under materially higher policy friction, more selective capital allocation, and substantially less confidence in frictionless global trade. The dispersion — not any single point estimate — is the regime change: it prices rising sensitivity to how long the tariff architecture persists.

**What began as repricing in Q1 has transitioned into the absorption phase:** markets are not reacting to policy signals. They are reorganising around them.

Three conditions now define the operating environment:

1. **Tariffs are functioning as persistent policy architecture**, not temporary instruments. (Confirmed – HIGH)
2. **Capital allocation is becoming more jurisdiction‑selective**, though global fungibility persists in many channels. (Directional – MEDIUM; Q2 hard data pending)
3. **Liquidity management is shifting toward defensiveness**, particularly among institutions exposed to tariff‑affected sectors. (Confirmed – HIGH)

**Bottom line (Q2):** The structural question is not whether growth continues – it is whether organisations are pricing the friction correctly and building the optionality the new regime requires.

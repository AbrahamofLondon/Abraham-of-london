## 4. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Q2 2026 is not a recession call. It is a regime‑change call.

Global markets are still growing – institutional consensus, including the IMF, kept 2026 growth expectations in the low‑3 percent area even under trade‑policy pressure. But they are doing so under materially higher policy friction, more selective capital allocation, and substantially less confidence in frictionless global trade. That combination is the regime change.

**What began as repricing in Q1 has transitioned into the absorption phase:** markets are not reacting to policy signals. They are reorganising around them.

Three conditions now define the operating environment:

1. **Tariffs are functioning as persistent policy architecture**, not temporary instruments. (Confirmed – HIGH)
2. **Capital allocation is becoming more jurisdiction‑selective**, though global fungibility persists in many channels. (Directional – MEDIUM; Q2 hard data pending)
3. **Liquidity management is shifting toward defensiveness**, particularly among institutions exposed to tariff‑affected sectors. (Confirmed – HIGH)

**Bottom line (Q2):** The structural question is not whether growth continues – it is whether organisations are pricing the friction correctly and building the optionality the new regime requires.

## 14. WHAT WOULD PROVE THIS THESIS WRONG

The central thesis weakens if:

1. Tariff escalation reverses durably (effective rate <50% for two consecutive quarters) rather than pauses tactically.
2. Global trade flows re‑synchronise without material bloc formation (WTO data showing re‑integration).
3. Capital allocation returns to broad global fungibility rather than jurisdictional selectivity (EM flows broad‑based).
4. The dollar consistently behaves as a conventional safe‑haven asset under stress (DXY strengthens >5% during risk‑off).
5. Credit conditions stabilise despite persistent trade friction (HY spreads <350bps for three months).
6. Regional reallocation into India, Southeast Asia, and Africa fails to appear in Q3–Q4 2026 investment data.

**What would force repricing:**
- Tariff rollback below structural thresholds (~50% effective rate) – de‑escalation becomes operative.
- Sustained USD strength during stress – dollar credibility stress thesis would require revision.
- Coordinated central bank easing – Q3 monetary environment repriced.
- Re‑synchronisation of global trade flows – fragmentation‑persistence thesis would require revision.

Absent these triggers, fragmentation persists as the operative structural condition.

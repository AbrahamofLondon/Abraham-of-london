Managed Fragmentation — structural hypothesis: system stabilisation under elevated friction, consistent with continued slow growth in the IMF-consistent low-3 percent range. Operative probability: 45–50%. This is an analytical framework, not a demonstrated endpoint.

### What Would Change This View

- Tariff rollback below structural thresholds on a sustained bilateral basis.
- Sustained USD strength during stress (rather than instability).
- Coordinated central bank easing that materially changes financial conditions.
- Re-synchronisation of global trade flows beyond current rerouting patterns.

## 3. How to Read This Report

This report separates evidence into four operating categories:

**Confirmed:** Observed developments supported by source evidence or market data.

**Directional:** Developments supported by evidence but not yet strong enough to treat as settled.

**Monitored:** Important signals that may become material but require further evidence before stronger conclusions are drawn.

**Scenario-Based:** Structured assumptions used for planning, not predictions or settled outcomes.

The purpose of this report is not to force certainty. It is to help operators distinguish confirmed conditions, directional signals, monitored developments, and scenario assumptions before making consequential decisions.

## 4. Executive Summary

Q2 2026 is not a recession call. It is a regime-change call.

Global markets are still growing — institutional consensus, including the IMF, kept 2026 growth expectations in the low-3 percent area even under trade-policy pressure. But they are doing so under materially higher policy friction, more selective capital allocation, and substantially less confidence in frictionless global trade. That combination is the regime change.

**Evidence posture: Directional, with sourced forecast basis pending Q2 close.**

Global growth remains constrained and uneven. Major institutional forecasts still point around the low-3% area for 2026, while downside risks remain elevated under persistent trade-policy friction, tariff uncertainty, and uneven capital allocation.

The report does not treat Q2 2026 as a recession call. It treats Q2 as a regime-change call: growth may continue, but under higher policy friction and reduced confidence in frictionless global integration.

What began as repricing in Q1 has transitioned into the absorption phase: markets are not reacting to policy signals. They are reorganising around them.

Three conditions now define the operating environment:

- Tariffs are functioning as persistent policy architecture, not temporary instruments.
- Capital allocation is becoming more jurisdiction-selective, though global fungibility persists in many channels.
- Liquidity management is shifting toward defensiveness, particularly among institutions exposed to tariff-affected sectors.

April's escalation was the trigger. Q2 is the absorption phase.

**Bottom line (Q2):** The structural question is not whether growth continues — it is whether organisations are pricing the friction correctly and building the optionality the new regime requires.

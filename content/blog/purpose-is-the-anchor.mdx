Life without purpose is hardly one worth living — and not because life becomes *hard*, but because it becomes *loose*.

> “Where there is no vision, the people perish…” — **Proverbs 29:18 (KJV)**
> When people do not accept divine guidance, they run wild.
> But whoever obeys the law is joyful.
> — Proverbs 29:18 (NLT)
>
> See also: Exodus 32:22, 25.

Purpose is the anchor.
Take it away, and you don’t just drift… you *meander*. You wake up busy but not advancing. You win small battles and still feel strangely defeated. You gather experiences, relationships, even applause — and yet your soul stays underfed.

Without a defined outcome, hopelessness isn’t a question of *if*.
It becomes a matter of *when*.

## The uncomfortable truth: we thrive through challenge

Here’s the paradox many people don’t want to admit:
we thrive when we face and overcome challenges.

If you disagree, don’t argue with me — argue with the outcomes.

Look at what happens to many people raised in extreme privilege, insulated from difficulty, protected from consequences, spared the weight of resistance. Too often, the story ends the same way: confusion, boredom, emptiness, addiction, despair. A life with every comfort… and very little meaning.

That’s not merely philosophy. That’s psychology. Neurology. Sociology. It shows up wherever you turn the light on and actually look.

Because the human being wasn’t designed to be constantly entertained.
He was designed to be *formed*.

## Life is not meant to be happy — it is meant to be meaningful

I’ll say it plainly:

Life is not meant to be lived happily.
It is meant to be lived meaningfully.

Happiness is a mood — a visitor, not a landlord.
Meaning is a foundation — it holds when everything else is shaking.

And often — not always, but often — meaning makes its home through suffering. Not because suffering is holy in itself, but because suffering **reveals** what matters. It forces clarity. It burns off illusions. It humbles pride. It tests whether your values are real or decorative.

Meaning is what gives you fulfilment — not power.
Not fame.
Not “more.”
Not hedonistic pleasure.
Not even pleasure itself.

Meaning outlives moods. Meaning outlasts applause. Meaning is still there when the lights go off.

## If you’re struggling, you’re not necessarily lost

Now listen: I’m not romanticising pain. I’m not selling struggle like it’s a product. I’m saying something more surgical:

If you’re struggling — whether *in spite* of success, or *because* you haven’t yet reached it — your pain may be a signal, not a sentence.

If you are successful and still empty, it may be because you achieved a goal that wasn’t connected to your purpose.

If you’re not yet successful and you feel hopeless, it may be because you are chasing outcomes without an anchor — trying to build a life from results instead of from meaning.

Either way, the prescription is the same:

## Find what brings you alive

Audit your inner world.

What touches something in you like nothing else does?
What questions won’t leave you alone?
What topics pull you into clarity, conviction, energy?

That — my friend — is not random.

That is a strong pointer toward your purpose.

And if you look long enough, you’ll notice another layer of confirmation:

Your strengths are often aligned with the same direction.

Not your vanity strengths — the ones you *perform*.
Your real strengths — the ones that show up even when you’re tired, even when no one’s clapping.

That alignment is a bridge.
It’s often the bridge that takes you home.

## Pleasure can imitate joy, but it cannot produce it

Let’s draw a hard line, because confusion here ruins lives.

Pleasure can make you happy.
But happiness is temporary.

Joy is different.

Joy is not a temporary state of being — that’s happiness.
Joy is a posture of the soul — a steadiness that doesn’t collapse every time life gets loud.

Here’s the analogy:

Happiness is your representative.
Joy is your true character.

Pleasure can feel powerful. It can feel convincing. But it is often a short-term agreement with your ego — not a long-term investment in your destiny.

Pleasure feeds your ego.
Purpose fulfils your soul.

Viktor Frankl’s point (paraphrased) was simple: when people lack meaning, they often reach for distraction — and pleasure becomes a substitute currency.

And your soul always knows when it is being bribed.

## The audit: what are you really chasing?

So here is my call to you today:

Audit yourself with brutal honesty.

- Are you chasing temporary pleasures in your activities?
- In your vocation?
- In your relationships?
- Even in your religion — not truth, but comfort; not God, but therapy?

Or are you on a journey that produces something steadier — a dependable, reliable state of fulfilment?

Because purpose doesn’t remove suffering.
It gives suffering a frame.

And a framed life is a life that can endure.
A framed life is a life that can say, “This hurts — but it makes sense.”
A framed life is never without hope.

## Calibrate — and watch your life shift

Whatever you discover from your audit, don’t wallow in guilt. Don’t dramatise it. Don’t spiral.

Calibrate.

Small corrections compound.
A new direction changes everything.

And when you return to purpose, something strange happens:

You don’t need to wait for the destination to be fulfilled.
You become fulfilled *on the road*.

That is what meaning does.
It makes the journey livable.

It makes the journey noble.

It makes the journey yours.

## Final word

Let’s love courageously, without fear.

1. Explore the **Shorts** — they’re built for quick clarity and daily recalibration.
2. Share this with one person who needs an anchor right now.

Because you don’t need a perfect life.
You need a purposeful one.

And purpose, once found, is a home you can return to — again and again.

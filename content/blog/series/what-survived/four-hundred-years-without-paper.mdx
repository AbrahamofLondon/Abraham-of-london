The Iliad and the Odyssey describe events set in the Mycenaean period, around 1200 BCE. The poems were composed in their known form around the eighth century BCE — roughly four hundred years after the events they describe. They were oral compositions, transmitted by performance, for most of their existence. A stable written text was not established until the sixth century BCE in Athens, another two hundred years after composition. For approximately six hundred years, the story of Achilles and the fall of Troy existed in human memory and human performance — not in any document.

The formulaic structure of Homeric verse — "rosy-fingered Dawn," "swift-footed Achilles," "the wine-dark sea" — was not poetic decoration. It was mnemonic technology: the system by which a performer could reconstruct a poem of fifteen thousand lines from internal cues without having memorised it word for word. The performer who forgot what came next had a system of conventional phrases that bought time, filled space, maintained the rhythm while the right line came. The poem was not memorised. It was reconstructed, every time, from a set of internalised patterns so deeply embedded that a trained performer could produce any scene from the poem on demand.

This kept a war alive for four hundred years before a scribe set it down.

The oral tradition that maintained the Iliad was not a single chain of transmission. It was a community of performers, competing and collaborating, each carrying partial versions, the best versions surviving through use. The redundancy was the architecture. One performer dying did not end the poem. The tradition was distributed across enough people that the loss of any single carrier was survivable. The system was designed for resilience in a way that the griot's single-chain transmission was not.

*We have the Iliad because the performance tradition survived long enough to be written down. We do not have the epics that were lost when their transmission chains broke before writing arrived. There is no inventory of the poems that were carried for centuries and then simply stopped.*

The comparison between oral transmission and digital storage is not sentimental. It is technical. The Homeric tradition preserved a fifteen-thousand-line poem across four centuries with no external storage medium, using only the trained minds of performers and the social infrastructure that maintained them. Digital culture has lost entire early internet archives, web-native art movements, and decades of online community in thirty years — not through deliberate destruction but through format obsolescence, server decommissioning, and platform closure. The oral tradition that kept Troy alive for four hundred years is more durable, by the measure of actual survival, than the digital culture that has existed for forty and is already losing significant parts of itself. The technology that seemed more fragile was not. The technology that seemed permanent is not.

Performance kept what could not be written. Writing kept what performance could not carry across enough time. And then someone discovered that what writing had preserved could also be removed — quietly, precisely, without leaving a gap where the thing had been.

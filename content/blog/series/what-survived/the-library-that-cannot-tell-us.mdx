The Library of Alexandria was damaged or destroyed across multiple events attributed to multiple actors across multiple centuries: Julius Caesar in 48 BCE, Aurelian in the 270s CE, Theophilus in 391 CE, the Arab conquest in 642 CE. The precise sequence and extent of destruction is itself contested — the historical record of the Library's destruction is incomplete. What is documented: a significant portion of the ancient world's accumulated written knowledge no longer exists. Estimates of what was lost range from forty thousand to seven hundred thousand scrolls. No inventory of the Library's holdings survived to allow precise accounting.

The Library cannot tell us what it lost because the Library's own catalogue is gone. The inventory that would allow us to measure the loss was part of the loss. We are left with the shadow of the shadow: references in surviving texts to works that did not survive, citations of authors whose names we know and whose words we do not, arguments responding to positions we cannot read because the positions themselves are gone. We know Aristotle wrote a second book of the Poetics, the one on comedy, because other writers refer to it. The book does not exist. We know it existed. We cannot read it. We cannot know what it contained except that it contained something about comedy, and that comedy was the only major subject Aristotle addressed that he did not leave us with a surviving text on.

This is the condition of catastrophic archival loss: the thing you most want to know — what was in the inventory — is precisely what the catastrophe took. The gap cannot be measured from inside the gap.

*The Library of Alexandria cannot tell us what it held, because what told it what it held is also gone. The inventory of losses must be compiled from the accidents of reference — from the texts that survived elsewhere and mentioned what did not. It is an inventory of shadows, not of substances. We do not know what we do not know we lost.*

The Internet Archive estimates it has preserved less than a quarter of what has been created digitally. The rest — early websites, digital art movements, born-digital journalism from the 1990s and early 2000s, entire online communities and their accumulated knowledge — is already gone. Unlike Alexandria, this loss is not dramatic. There is no fire, no conqueror, no single moment of destruction. There is link rot, server decommissioning, format obsolescence, platform closure, and the quiet expiry of hosting contracts. The Library of Alexandria was mourned because it was visible — a building, a collection, a catastrophe that could be witnessed. The digital Library of Alexandria is dissolving silently, incrementally, without witnesses, and the inventory of what is being lost is already incomplete because the things being lost are the things that would have told us what to count.

The Library was lost by accident, by politics, by fire and time. But not everything that is gone was lost accidentally. Some things survived precisely because the people who held power wanted them to survive — and did not survive despite being valuable, but because being preserved was a form of power.

For a thousand years, the most important historical records in West Africa were not written anywhere. They were kept alive by people whose entire purpose was keeping them alive.

The griot — the hereditary custodian of oral history, genealogy, and political memory — was not someone who happened to remember things. He was someone whose family had been designated as rememberers across generations, trained from childhood in the structures that made the transmission reliable, maintained by a community that understood the knowledge would not exist without the person who carried it. The role was inherited. It was formally trained. It was socially enforced. A griot who forgot a lineage or altered a genealogy was not merely inaccurate. He was breaking a contract that had been in place longer than any living person could remember.

The knowledge was not casual. It was structured, versified, maintained through repeated performance that served as both transmission and rehearsal. When a dispute arose about a land boundary or a marriage alliance or the terms of a treaty made three generations ago, the griot was the record. His memory was the archive. The community had organised itself around the maintenance of his memory the way a literate culture organises itself around the maintenance of its documents.

When European colonisers arrived and described African societies as "without history" because they lacked written records, they were making a category error that had catastrophic consequences. They dismantled the political structures that gave the griot his function. They disrupted the conditions that made the transmission of knowledge worth maintaining. The storage system was intact. The environment that made the storage meaningful was what was destroyed.

*What was lost when the griot died without a successor is unknown. The record of what he carried existed only in the carrying. There is no inventory of the transmission that ended.*

The griot system is described as fragile — oral tradition vulnerable to disruption in a way that written records are not. The comparison is wrong in both directions. Written records burn. They rot. They require institutions to maintain them and political conditions to preserve those institutions. A trained human memory, maintained by a community of practice with strong social incentives for accuracy, is extraordinarily resilient — until the community that maintains it is disrupted. The griot system did not fail because of its medium. It came under pressure when the societies that gave it meaning were dismantled from outside. The weakness was not in the storage. It was in the assumption that a storage system can survive the destruction of the culture it belongs to.

The griot kept the record by keeping it alive — by transmitting it across generations through the only medium that could carry it. The question is whether the most ambitious oral archive in Western history did the same thing, and how long it lasted.

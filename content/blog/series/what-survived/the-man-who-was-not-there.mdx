There is a photograph of Stalin walking beside the Moscow Canal in 1937. Three men walk together: Stalin, Molotov, Yezhov.

After Yezhov was purged and executed in 1940, the photograph was retouched. Yezhov disappeared from the image. The canal bank where he stood became empty water. The retoucher was so skilled that if you do not know to look, you do not look. You see two men and a canal. The before and after photographs exist. The before shows three men. The after shows two men and a smooth canal bank.

The retoucher did not lie. They removed. The canal bank is accurate — there is a canal bank there. The image shows what was there after the removing. This is the distinction that matters: fabrication adds something false, but removal takes something true and leaves no trace of the taking. A fabrication can be detected by what it added. A removal can be detected only if the original survives somewhere, available for comparison. In Yezhov's case, the original did survive — which is why we know. In cases where the original did not survive, we do not know what was removed. We know only the smooth canal bank. We do not know there was ever a man standing on it.

The removal of Yezhov from the Soviet historical record was not performed once, in one darkroom, by one retoucher. Subscribers to the Great Soviet Encyclopaedia received inserts in the post with instructions to cut out pages and paste in replacements. The erasure was distributed to the people who owned the books, performed by thousands of hands, with scissors and paste, in thousands of homes. The archive was not edited at the centre. It was edited everywhere, simultaneously, by the people who owned it. Their compliance was the mechanism.

*We know Yezhov was removed because the before-photograph survived. What we cannot know is how many removals left no before. The inventory of the absent is compiled only from the accidents of survival, and the accidents of survival are not systematic.*

The Yezhov retouching required a skilled technician, darkroom chemistry, time, and state resources. Those friction costs were the only barrier between the operation and anyone who wanted to perform it. The friction is gone. What took the Soviet state months of careful work and the cooperation of thousands of book-owners can now be done in seconds by anyone with access to an image editor, applied to any photograph that has ever been digitised, without leaving a trace the original did not survive to contradict. The operation has not changed. The cost of performing it has collapsed to zero. The inventory of removals we cannot detect has grown accordingly, and we have no way of knowing by how much, because the removals we cannot detect are, by definition, the ones that left no before.

Yezhov was removed from one photograph and we know it because the original survived. The Library of Alexandria was lost all at once and we know what it contained only approximately, because some of what it held was duplicated elsewhere. The question is what happens when an entire archive goes — not one photograph but everything — and the catalogue of what it contained burns with everything else.

The Bayeux Tapestry is a seventy-metre embroidered cloth produced in the 1070s, approximately ten years after the Norman Conquest of England. It depicts the events leading to the Battle of Hastings and the conquest itself. It was almost certainly commissioned by Odo, Bishop of Bayeux, half-brother of William the Conqueror. Odo appears in it multiple times, in prominent roles. Harold Godwinson is depicted as an oath-breaker — which is the Norman legal and moral justification for the invasion.

The tapestry is magnificent. It is also a legal brief. Its entire narrative structure serves one argument: William's claim to England was legitimate because Harold swore a sacred oath to support it and broke that oath. Every scene — the oath, the comet that portended Harold's doom, the battle where Harold falls — serves this argument. The tapestry is not a record of what happened in 1066. It is the Norman account of what happened in 1066, produced a decade after the fact, commissioned by the Conqueror's brother, displayed in a cathedral.

It survived the French Revolution, the Franco-Prussian War, and the Second World War, during which it was briefly held by the SS, who were interested in it as evidence of Germanic warrior culture. It is now in Bayeux, France.

The Saxon account of 1066 — the account that might have presented Harold's perspective differently — did not survive in equivalent form. Not because it was less true or less valuable. Because the Saxons did not control the preservation infrastructure of England after 1066. The Normans did.

*The Bayeux Tapestry survived because the people who made it won. What the losers made, if they made anything, did not survive in equivalent form. The inventory of the Saxon record of 1066 is compiled from the silence where the Norman record is loud.*

Survival is not neutral. The Bayeux Tapestry did not survive because it was the most accurate record of 1066, or the most beautiful, or the most complete. It survived because the people who made it controlled the preservation infrastructure of England for the following century. The inverse is equally true: what did not survive did not perish because it was less valuable or less true. It perished because the people who made it lost. The historical record is not a representative sample of what was created. It is a sample biased toward what the powerful found worth preserving — which is, reliably, the record of their own legitimacy. Every archive is also an argument. The argument is: this is what deserves to survive. And the people who made that argument were the people who won.

The Normans preserved the tapestry because they controlled the cathedrals. We preserve what we control. The question is what happens when what we create is not in our control at all — when it exists on servers we do not own, in formats we did not choose, on platforms that can close without notice, and the thing we made is simply gone.

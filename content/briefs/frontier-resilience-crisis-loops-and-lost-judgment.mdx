**Frontier Rule**: An institution that lives in emergency tempo eventually loses the ability to tell the difference between a true crisis and the cost of its own neglect.
</BriefAlert>

## I. The Pressure Pattern

Some organisations do not merely face crises. They become structurally organised around them. Attention, energy, and legitimacy all begin to cluster around whatever is most urgent in the moment.

## II. What This Pattern Actually Does

That pattern creates a loop. Weak sequencing creates preventable emergencies. Emergencies justify abandoning cadence. Abandoned cadence creates more preventable emergencies. Over time, the institution forgets what governed normality even feels like.

## III. Diagnostic Lens

The key diagnostic question is whether most high-urgency work is genuinely unforeseeable or simply the delayed consequence of earlier avoidance, weak ownership, and poor review rhythm. If it is the latter, the crisis loop is self-authored.

## IV. Strategic Implication

Resilience requires leaders to re-establish distinctions: between urgent and important, crisis and noise, escalation and interruption, response and governance. Without those distinctions, adrenaline becomes an operating philosophy.

## V. Closing Judgment

Institutions do not become resilient by proving they can survive repeated emergency. They become resilient when they stop wasting judgment on crises they had the power to prevent.

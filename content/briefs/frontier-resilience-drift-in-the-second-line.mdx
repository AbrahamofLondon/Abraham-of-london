**Frontier Rule**: Institutions do not become resilient when only the top layer can think clearly. They become resilient when the second line can carry judgment without constant rescue.
</BriefAlert>

## I. The Pressure Pattern

The second line is where resilience is either distributed or denied. When managers and operators below the top layer cannot interpret priorities, carry standards, or resolve ordinary complexity, the institution remains dangerously top-heavy.

## II. What This Pattern Actually Does

This drift is often missed because the second line can still look competent administratively. Calendars are managed, updates are sent, meetings are held. But the harder question is whether this layer can actually govern reality when pressure rises.

## III. Diagnostic Lens

A useful test is to identify which issues routinely travel upward not because they are truly strategic, but because the second line lacks confidence, permission, or judgment to handle them locally.

## IV. Strategic Implication

Resilient institutions deliberately train the second line in interpretation, not just execution. They build leaders who can hold standards, make bounded decisions, and protect cadence without constant founder intervention.

## V. Closing Judgment

Where the second line is thin, the institution remains one emergency away from top-level overload. Frontier resilience requires leadership depth, not just leadership visibility.

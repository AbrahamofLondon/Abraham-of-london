In the six weeks following a contested election, the most widely shared piece of political content on one platform was a story that was entirely fabricated. Not exaggerated, not misleading, not selectively edited — fabricated, with named sources who did not exist and events that did not occur. It was shared 8.7 million times. The true story it was designed to displace was shared 736,000 times.

The ratio: twelve to one.

Do not read past that. Sit with the ratio. Twelve to one.

The fabricated story did not spread because twelve times as many people chose to believe it. It spread because the platform's engagement algorithm identified, correctly, that it produced a stronger emotional response than the true story. Stronger emotional response meant more shares. More shares meant more reach. More reach meant the fabricated story appeared in more feeds, which produced more emotional responses, which produced more shares. The system was not amplifying lies. It was amplifying emotional intensity. The lie and the emotional intensity happened to coincide. They happen to coincide with remarkable consistency.

The feed — whether on social media, news platforms, or search engines — is not a neutral window onto information. It is a ranking system optimised for engagement. The content that produces the strongest emotional response is the content that gets surfaced first. The content that is accurate, nuanced, and calm is systematically deprioritised. This is not a bug. It is the design.

The feed shapes what the mind believes is true not by suppressing falsehood but by amplifying what the mind is most likely to engage with — which is, reliably, what confirms what it already believes. The result is a belief architecture that is structurally biased toward outrage, tribalism, and the thrill of being right.

Here is the turn you did not see coming. The study also found that the people who shared the fabricated story were not, in the main, people who had chosen to believe it. Many of them knew it was probably not accurate. They shared it because it was interesting. Because it was striking. Because their share came with a caption that said: "Can't believe this if true." The sharing mechanism had been decoupled from the believing mechanism. You could signal interest without claiming truth. The share button did not ask: do you believe this? It asked: do you want others to see this? Those are not the same question. The system was designed around the second question. The consequences fall on the first.

*The hand that shapes the feed shapes what the mind believes is true. The feed is shaped by engagement, not by truth. Those are not the same optimisation target.*

Consider what this means for the architecture of public belief. A system that amplifies emotional intensity over accuracy does not merely spread falsehoods. It reshapes what feels true. The mind does not encounter information in a neutral order and then decide what to believe. It encounters information in the order determined by the engagement algorithm, and that order itself carries a persuasive force. The story that appears first, that is shared most, that everyone is talking about — that story feels important, and what feels important is more likely to be accepted as true.

This is not a new phenomenon. Propaganda has existed as long as organised power has needed to shape what people believe. What is new is the precision and scale of the shaping. The feed does not need a central authority to decide what is true. It needs only an optimisation target — engagement — and the rest follows automatically. The result is a belief environment that is more fragmented, more volatile, and more vulnerable to manipulation than any that has existed before, not because the manipulators are more skilled, but because the system is structurally predisposed to amplify whatever produces the strongest response.

The contemporary implication is uncomfortable. You cannot simply blame the algorithm and return to scrolling. The algorithm is responding to you. It is learning what you engage with and giving you more of it. The feed is a mirror, but it is a mirror that magnifies what it reflects. The more you engage with outrage, the more outrage the feed serves you. The more you engage with tribalism, the more tribal the feed becomes. The system is not a passive conduit. It is an active participant in shaping what you believe, and it is using your own behaviour as its training data.

The attention landscape determines what the mind encounters. The feed determines what the mind believes is true. The interface determines what the mind expects from knowledge itself — what it thinks knowing looks like, feels like, requires. That is a deeper layer, and it is being reshaped right now.

# Drift

Drift is governance decay that announces nothing. It is the slow widening of the gap between what an institution says it does and what it actually does. No single decision causes drift. It accumulates through small compromises, deferred reviews, and exceptions that quietly become norms. Decision infrastructure treats drift as the default condition — not something that might happen, but something that is always happening unless actively detected and corrected.

## In decision infrastructure

Drift is the primary adversary of governed decision-making. The infrastructure must include mechanisms that measure the distance between declared standards and operational reality at regular intervals. This is not auditing in the traditional sense. Auditing looks backward at compliance events. Drift detection looks at the present trajectory and asks whether current practice is converging with or diverging from the governance baseline.

The discipline requires governance owners to reaffirm standards periodically — not because the standards have changed, but because the practice around them has. Decision infrastructure encodes these reaffirmation cycles and flags when they are missed, treating a skipped review as a drift indicator in its own right.

## Failure pattern

When drift is undetected, institutions lose the ability to distinguish between their governance as designed and their governance as practiced. Leaders reference policies that no longer reflect operations. New members inherit drifted norms as if they were the original standard. The organisation's stated values and its actual behaviour diverge until an external event — a crisis, a regulatory action, a leadership transition — forces the gap into visibility.

## Practical test

When did you last compare your organisation's current decision-making practice against its documented governance standards — and what did the comparison reveal?

# Narrative

Narrative is the interpretive frame an institution uses to explain itself to itself. It determines which facts are treated as relevant, which outcomes are considered acceptable, and which questions are permitted. Decision infrastructure treats narrative not as communication but as a governance force — one that shapes decisions before they reach the decision table by defining what the institution believes is possible, necessary, and true.

## In decision infrastructure

Narrative operates as the pre-filter of governed decision-making. Before evidence reaches a decision authority, the institutional narrative determines how that evidence is interpreted. A narrative that says "we are a growth organisation" will interpret cost reduction as retreat. A narrative that says "we are under threat" will interpret investment as recklessness.

Decision infrastructure must make the governing narrative visible and subject to examination. This means surfacing the assumptions embedded in the story the institution tells about itself and testing those assumptions against operational evidence. The infrastructure must also detect narrative capture — the condition where the story has become so dominant that contradictory evidence is reinterpreted rather than confronted.

## Failure pattern

When narrative is ungoverned, it becomes doctrine. The institution can no longer distinguish between its story and its reality. Evidence that contradicts the narrative is dismissed, reframed, or attributed to external forces. Decision-making becomes circular: the narrative defines what counts as evidence, and the curated evidence confirms the narrative. The institution loses its capacity for strategic surprise because it has structurally eliminated the possibility of being wrong.

## Practical test

What is the one story your organisation tells most often about itself — and when was the last time someone in a position of authority tested whether that story is still true?

# Optionality

Optionality is the preserved ability to choose before conditions force a path. It is the institutional discipline of maintaining viable alternatives rather than committing prematurely to a single course. Optionality is not indecision; it is the governed refusal to close doors until the evidence justifies closure. An institution with options negotiates from strength. An institution without them accepts whatever terms remain.

Optionality is purchased with discipline and destroyed by impatience.

## In decision infrastructure

Optionality functions as the strategic reserve of the decision system. Decision infrastructure preserves optionality by requiring commitment gates -- defined thresholds that must be met before irreversible decisions are authorised. Governed institutions track their option inventory: what choices remain open, what would close them, and what is the cost of keeping them open. Optionality has a carrying cost -- resources allocated to maintaining alternatives -- and decision infrastructure must weigh that cost against the value of preserved flexibility. The infrastructure must also identify and flag optionality-destroying decisions before they are executed, not after.

## Failure pattern

When optionality is ignored, institutions lock into positions prematurely, often driven by enthusiasm or sunk-cost commitment. When conditions change, they have no governed alternatives and must improvise under pressure. When optionality is hoarded indefinitely, it becomes paralysis -- the institution preserves choices but never makes them, mistaking open options for strategic positioning while competitors act. The fatal pattern is unconscious optionality destruction: decisions that eliminate future choices without anyone recognising the foreclosure until it is irreversible.

## Practical test

Name the most valuable option your institution currently holds open, the cost of maintaining it, and the trigger condition that would justify exercising or releasing it.

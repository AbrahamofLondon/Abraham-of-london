# Time-Horizon

A time-horizon is the planning boundary that shapes decision consequence. It defines how far forward the institution looks when evaluating choices. Short time-horizons optimise for survival. Long time-horizons optimise for legacy. Neither is inherently correct; what matters is that the time-horizon is chosen deliberately, not inherited by default from quarterly reporting cycles or crisis-driven urgency.

The time-horizon an institution selects reveals what it actually values, regardless of what it claims.

## In decision infrastructure

Time-horizon functions as the calibration dial of the decision system. Decision infrastructure requires every significant decision to declare its time-horizon explicitly: is this a 90-day operational choice or a 10-year structural commitment? Different time-horizons demand different evidence standards, different authority levels, and different accountability structures. Governed institutions maintain multiple concurrent time-horizons -- operational, strategic, and generational -- and ensure that short-term decisions do not foreclose long-term options. The infrastructure must prevent time-horizon collapse, where urgent pressures compress all planning into the immediate.

## Failure pattern

When time-horizons are undeclared, institutions default to the shortest one. Every decision becomes reactive, optimised for immediate relief rather than structural positioning. When long time-horizons are declared but not resourced, they become aspirational fiction. The critical failure is time-horizon mismatch -- where an institution makes generational commitments with quarterly governance, ensuring that the commitment outlives the accountability structure that authorised it.

## Practical test

For your institution's most important current initiative, what is the declared time-horizon -- and does your governance structure extend to the full length of that horizon?

There is a handprint on a cave wall in France that is thirty-six thousand years old. The person who pressed it is long dead. Their name is unknown. Their language is lost. Their songs, their stories, their gods — all gone. But the handprint survived.

I have been thinking about what it means to leave a mark that outlasts you. Not a monument. Not a fortune. Not a name on a building. Just the evidence that you were here, that you existed, that you wanted something to remain.

The handprint did not need to be believed. It needed only to be present. That is the difference between a mark made by a witness and a mark made by power. And that difference is the subject of a new series I am writing — nine parts, from the cave wall to the synthetic image, about who gets to decide what the record says.

The first part is published today.

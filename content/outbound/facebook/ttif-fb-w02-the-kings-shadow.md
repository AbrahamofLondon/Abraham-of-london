The oldest surviving record of a battle is the Stele of the Vultures, carved by the Sumerian city-state of Lagash around 2450 BCE. It shows the king victorious, the god holding the enemy in a net, the victorious dead given proper burial. The enemy dead are shown as carrion for birds.

The losers left no stele.

That is not incidental. That is how the relationship between writing and power has worked since writing was invented. The king pressed his handprint into history and called it stone. The conquered pressed nothing. That is why we know one name and not the other.

I wrote about this in the second part of my series on documentation, truth, and power. About what happens when the record is controlled by the people whose version of events it preserves. About what gets left out — and why the leaving out is not an accident but a feature.

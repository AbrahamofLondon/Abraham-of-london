In 1804, Napoleon walked into Jacques-Louis David's studio, looked at the nearly finished coronation canvas, and said: add my mother. She was not at the coronation. She was in Rome, in self-imposed exile. David painted her in anyway.

The painting hangs in the Louvre. Millions of people have seen it. Most of them have no idea that Napoleon's mother was absent from the event the painting claims to document. The painting became the memory. The painting replaced the event.

Napoleon's handprint is on every canvas David painted. His mother's is on none of them — even the ones she appears in.

I wrote about Napoleon and David, about Goya and the other side of the same war, about what happens when realism becomes a weapon. It is the third part of a series I am writing about who controls the record.

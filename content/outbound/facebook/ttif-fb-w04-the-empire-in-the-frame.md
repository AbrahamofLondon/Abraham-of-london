In 1858, Franz Xaver Winterhalter painted the portrait of a fourteen-year-old boy in silk and jewels. Queen Victoria hung the painting in her private rooms as proof of a devoted son.

The boy was not her son. He was Maharaja Duleep Singh, the last ruler of the Sikh Empire. He had been removed from his throne at ten, his kingdom annexed, his mother imprisoned, his diamond — the Koh-i-Noor — taken and presented to Victoria as a gift from a child who had no choice about giving it.

Forty years later, he wrote to the British government asking for the diamond back. The empire did not answer.

One hangs in a palace. The other sits in an archive, unanswered. History decided which one counted. It has not finished deciding.

In 1994, three people pushed through a gap in a rock face in southern France and found a room that had been sealed for twenty thousand years.

The animals on the walls moved in the torchlight. The painters had carved curves into the stone beneath the pigment so the horses appeared to run when a flame passed across them. Someone, thirty-six thousand years ago, understood perspective. Understood animation. Spent days in the dark making something beautiful.

We found the paintings. We did not find the songs. We did not find the stories. We did not find the names.

The paintings survived because the cave sealed and the conditions were exactly right. The meaning did not survive because meaning travels by voice and by performance and by the transmission of one person handing it to another — and that chain broke somewhere in the ice age.

What we found in 1994 was not what was made thirty-six thousand years ago. What we found was the part that stone could carry.

The series I am writing begins here — with the first thing that survived and the first thing that did not.

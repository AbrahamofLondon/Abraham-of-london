For centuries, the most important historical records in West Africa were not written anywhere. They were kept alive.

The griot — the hereditary custodian of oral history, genealogy, and political memory — was not someone who happened to remember things. They were someone whose entire family had been designated as rememberers, trained across generations, maintained by a community that understood the knowledge would not exist without the person who carried it.

When European colonisers described African societies as "without history" because they had no written records, they were making a category error that had catastrophic consequences. The storage system was intact. What was destroyed was the environment that made the storage meaningful — the political structures, the social roles, the incentives that made the transmission of knowledge worth maintaining.

The griot system did not fail because oral transmission is fragile. It came under pressure when the societies that gave it meaning were dismantled from outside.

The weakness was not in the storage. It was in the assumption that a storage system can survive the destruction of the culture it belongs to.

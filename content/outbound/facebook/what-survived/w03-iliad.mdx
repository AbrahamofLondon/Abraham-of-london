The Iliad kept a war alive in human memory for four hundred years before anyone wrote it down.

Not by accident. By design. The formulaic structure of Homeric verse — the repeated epithets, the stock phrases, the conventional scene-types — was not poetic decoration. It was mnemonic technology: the system by which a performer could reconstruct a fifteen-thousand-line poem without memorising it word for word. The poem was not remembered. It was reconstructed, every performance, from a set of patterns so deeply embedded they could be recovered on demand.

The average lifespan of a URL is two years.

I am not being sentimental about oral tradition. I am being precise about durability. The Homeric tradition preserved the equivalent of a 15,000-line poem across four centuries with no external storage medium, using only trained minds and the social infrastructure that maintained them. Digital culture has lost significant parts of itself in forty years — not through destruction but through format obsolescence, platform closure, and the quiet expiry of hosting contracts.

The technology that seemed more fragile was not.

The Library of Alexandria cannot tell us what it held because its own catalogue burned with everything else.

The gap cannot be measured from inside the gap. We know what was there only from references in texts that survived elsewhere — from citations of authors whose names we have and whose words we do not, from arguments responding to positions that are gone. We know Aristotle wrote a second book of the Poetics, about comedy, because other writers mention it. The book does not exist. We know it existed. We cannot read it.

This is the condition of catastrophic archival loss: the thing you most want to know — what was in the inventory — is precisely what the catastrophe took.

The Internet Archive estimates it has preserved less than a quarter of what has been created digitally. The rest is dissolving — not through fire, not through political purge, but through link rot, format obsolescence, and platform closure. Without witnesses. Without mourning.

The Library of Alexandria burned visibly. The digital Library of Alexandria is dissolving silently.

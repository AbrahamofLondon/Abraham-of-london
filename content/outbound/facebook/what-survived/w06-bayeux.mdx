The Bayeux Tapestry is seventy metres of embroidered cloth produced ten years after the Norman Conquest of England. It depicts the events leading to the Battle of Hastings and the conquest itself. It was commissioned by Odo, Bishop of Bayeux — half-brother of William the Conqueror.

It survived the French Revolution, the Franco-Prussian War, and the Second World War. It is now in Bayeux, France, and it is magnificent.

It is also a legal brief. Its entire narrative structure serves one argument: William's claim to England was legitimate because Harold swore a sacred oath and broke it. Harold is depicted as an oath-breaker. The Normans are the rightful inheritors. The Battle of Hastings is a correction, not a conquest.

The Saxon account of 1066 — the account that might have shown us Harold's perspective — did not survive in equivalent form. Not because it was less true or less valuable. Because the Normans controlled the preservation infrastructure of England for the following century.

Survival is not neutral. The historical record is a sample biased toward what the powerful found worth preserving.

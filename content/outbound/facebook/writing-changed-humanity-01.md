Writing did not change humanity by storing more facts. It changed humanity by changing the mind that used it.

Every communication technology is also a cognition technology. Oral culture trained memory. Writing trained analysis and permanence. Print trained mass literacy and comparison. Digital media trained speed and fragmentation. Each tool did not merely carry thought across time — it reshaped the thinking that produced thought in the first place.

The question for anyone navigating the current moment is not whether AI is useful. The question is: what kind of mind does AI reward? If the answer is delegation and retrieval, that is fine for some tasks. But durable wisdom has always required something more effortful — the slow friction of writing out an argument, the discipline of returning to a text, the embodied practice of being made to think before you could outsource the thinking.

Organisations, leaders, and serious readers would do well to ask which capacities they are protecting — and which they are quietly allowing to atrophy.

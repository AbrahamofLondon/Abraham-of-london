Before writing, knowledge did not disappear when a person died. It was preserved — through ritual, genealogy, proverb, rhythm, and communal recitation. Oral cultures were not primitive; they were highly engineered. The community itself was the archive.

Writing changed the terms of that bargain. Knowledge became detachable from any particular person, durable beyond any particular moment. It could accumulate, be revised, be audited, be compared across centuries. Institutions became possible at a scale that living memory alone could never have supported.

But oral culture gave something that written culture tends to trade away: the deep cognitive discipline of having to hold ideas in the body, to make them rhythmic enough to survive without being written down. That discipline built a particular kind of mind — attentive, relational, present.

The question for institutions now is whether they are losing the oral habits that writing was always meant to supplement, not replace. Meetings that think, discussions that revise, conversations that remember — these are not inefficiencies. They are the infrastructure.

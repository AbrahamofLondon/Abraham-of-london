When writing was new, those who could read and write did not merely possess a useful skill. They controlled interpretation, records, and transmission. That made literacy a gatekeeping technology — shaping class, governance, and social mobility for centuries.

Print reduced some of those barriers. Digital tools reduced more. But platform control and algorithmic visibility have reintroduced new ones.

This pattern has implications for any organisation that assumes broad access to information means broad capacity for insight. Access and comprehension are not the same thing. Information abundance and wisdom are not the same thing.

The editorial explores this arc — from clay to code — with particular attention to what it means for people who are responsible for how institutions think, decide, and transmit knowledge across generations.

Escalation avoidance has a recognisable pattern. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

Stage 1: The issue is reframed as complexity. "It is more nuanced than it appears."
Stage 2: The timeline gets extended. "We need another cycle."
Stage 3: More stakeholders appear. "Let us bring in the wider team."
Stage 4: The decision is described as "still being worked." Nobody can say what changed since last month.
Stage 5: Someone new inherits the problem. The cycle restarts.

Each stage feels reasonable in isolation. Together, they are a system for converting urgency into delay without anyone being accountable for the delay.

The cost is not the time lost. The cost is the options that disappeared while the pattern ran.

If a decision in your organisation has completed stages 1 through 3, it will complete stages 4 and 5 unless someone interrupts the sequence. That interruption is not a meeting. It is a forced decision with a named owner and a deadline.

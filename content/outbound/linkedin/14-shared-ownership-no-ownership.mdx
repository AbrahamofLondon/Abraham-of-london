Shared ownership is the most expensive lie in corporate governance.

It sounds mature. Collaborative. Distributed. It looks good in the RACI chart.

In live decisions, it usually means nobody is carrying consequence.

When three people "co-own" a decision, each one waits for the others to move first. Nobody escalates because escalation implies the others failed. Nobody commits because commitment implies the others agreed.

The result: permission to delay without visible refusal. The work continues while the decision does not. Meetings get scheduled. Updates get shared. Progress gets reported. But the thing that actually needs to happen — one person saying "this is what we are doing, and I accept the downside" — never occurs.

I have never seen a shared-ownership decision move faster than a single-owner one. Not once.

If you have a decision with shared ownership right now, ask this: which of the co-owners would take the call alone if the other two were unavailable?

That person is the real owner. Name them.

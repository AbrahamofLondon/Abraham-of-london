Revenue was bleeding. The room had the answer for three weeks. Nobody would say it.

Here is what happened.

A commercial team identified a revenue issue that required a strategic pivot. Clear data. Clear options. Clear urgency.

They scheduled a meeting. Then another. Then four more.

After six meetings over three weeks, the stated blocker was "cross-functional alignment." The commercial team needed buy-in from product, finance, and operations before they could move.

Except they did not. I asked: "If the CEO walked in right now and said 'decide by Friday,' who would make the call?"

Everyone looked at the same person. That person had not spoken in any of the six meetings.

The real blocker was never alignment. It was that nobody wanted to own the downside of the call. The pivot would upset a partnership. Someone would have to deliver that message. And nobody wanted to be the person who pulled the trigger.

Once that owner was named — explicitly, with the consequence attached — the decision moved in a single day. Not because new information appeared. Because the avoidance structure was broken.

Three weeks. Six meetings. Thousands in opportunity cost. All because the question "who owns the downside?" was never asked out loud.

This is the pattern. It repeats in every organisation I have examined.

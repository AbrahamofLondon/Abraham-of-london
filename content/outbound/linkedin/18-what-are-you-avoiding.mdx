Strip away the project language for a minute. The updates, the dependencies, the timelines.

What are you actually avoiding?

A hard tradeoff that closes options. A visible escalation that changes a relationship. A named owner who becomes accountable. A decision that, once made, cannot be softened in the next meeting.

Most delay becomes completely understandable the moment you answer that question honestly. The delay is not irrational. It is protective. It is the organisation preserving optionality, avoiding confrontation, and deferring consequence.

The problem is that avoidance has a cost. And unlike the decision itself, the cost of avoidance is not optional. It accumulates whether you acknowledge it or not.

I built a system that forces the plain answer. It takes your stated blocker, your forced action under time pressure, and shows you the gap between them.

If the gap is real, the blocker is real. If the forced action bypasses the blocker, the blocker was never preventing the decision. It was justifying the avoidance.

The first half of 2026 has made one thing clear: the global economy is no longer behaving as if trade, capital, and policy can be treated as separate systems. Businesses are now operating in a world where tariffs, supply chains, FX moves, credit conditions, and political decisions are all affecting each other at the same time.

That is the context for our new Q2 2026 market intelligence report.

The report is not a forecast for traders. It is a decision-support briefing for leaders who need to plan under uncertainty. Its central message is simple: the old assumption of smooth global integration no longer works. Instead, organisations need to think in terms of resilience, optionality, and jurisdictional risk.

In Q2, the key question is no longer, "Where is growth strongest?" It is, "Where is the system most durable?" That means paying closer attention to supply chain exposure, policy risk, capital access, and the quality of balance sheets. It also means being more selective about where expansion, investment, and dependency are allowed to grow.

A few themes stand out:

- Trade policy is now a structural force, not a temporary headline.
- Capital is becoming more selective by jurisdiction.
- Liquidity is increasingly a defence mechanism, not just an efficiency tool.
- Businesses with strong cash flow and flexible supply chains are in a better position than those relying on growth narratives alone.

For many organisations, the hardest shift is mental. Planning for a fragmented world requires different assumptions from planning for a globalised one. Efficiency still matters, but it is no longer the only priority. In some cases, resilience is now worth more than scale.

That is why this report focuses on operating conditions rather than market noise. It is designed to help boards, investors, and strategic operators think more clearly about what comes next.

The main takeaway is straightforward: waiting for a return to normal is not a strategy. The new task is to build structures that can perform in a more contested, less predictable environment.

Our Q2 report explores these shifts in detail, including the implications for growth, inflation, capital flows, supply chains, and strategic sector positioning.

The first half of 2026 is making one thing increasingly clear: the global economy can no longer be understood as separate systems of trade, capital, policy, supply chains, FX, credit, and political risk.

They are now interacting in real time.

Tariffs affect supply chains. Supply chains affect margins. Margins affect capital allocation. Capital allocation affects currency behaviour, credit appetite, and strategic positioning.

That is the context behind our Q2 2026 Global Market Intelligence work.

This is not a trading forecast.

It is a strategic decision-support briefing for leaders who need to plan under uncertainty, pressure, and policy friction.

The central question is no longer simply:

> Where is growth strongest?

The better question is:

> Where is the system most durable?

That shift matters.

A world built around smooth global integration rewarded scale, efficiency, and expansion. A more fragmented world rewards resilience, optionality, jurisdictional awareness, and balance-sheet discipline.

Several signals are now under active review:

- Trade policy is becoming a structural operating variable, not just a headline risk.
- Capital is becoming more selective by jurisdiction.
- Liquidity is increasingly defensive, not merely efficient.
- Supply-chain exposure now belongs in strategic and financial modelling, not only procurement.
- Businesses with durable cash flow, flexible supplier nodes, and clearer policy-risk visibility are better positioned than those relying on growth narratives alone.

The difficult adjustment is mental.

Many organisations are still waiting for a return to "normal." But waiting for clarity can become its own risk. In a contested environment, leadership requires building structures that can perform before certainty arrives.

That is why the Q2 intelligence process focuses on operating conditions rather than market noise.

It tracks tariff persistence, Treasury-yield stress, institutional growth forecasts, AI-productivity offsets, capital-flow selectivity, and regional positioning signals.

The aim is not to predict markets with false certainty.

The aim is to distinguish what is confirmed, what is directionally supported, what remains under monitoring, and what must not yet be overstated.

That is also why every quarterly report in this intelligence line reviews the material calls from the previous quarter before issuing the next one.

The point is simple:

> Intelligence should compound through verification, not just publication.

The Q2 report remains in preparation. It will not be released until the prior-quarter calls have been reviewed, the evidence base has been tested, and the release gate is satisfied.

The main takeaway for operators is already clear:

Waiting for a return to normal is not a strategy.

The new task is to build decision structures that can survive fragmentation, policy volatility, and capital selectivity.

Most organisations have conflated two different activities and labelled both of them "documentation."

The first is creation: the act of capturing something — a decision, a process, a client commitment — and writing it down. The second is stewardship: the ongoing act of maintaining, reviewing, updating, and eventually retiring what has been written. The first is an event. The second is a discipline. Most documentation cultures are very good at the event and almost entirely absent on the discipline.

The accountant in Uruk was not filing. His clay tablets were operationally live. The record of twelve black goats from one household and seven brown from another was not an archive — it was a working instrument. He updated it as the animals moved. He consulted it when obligations came due. The tablet was part of how he thought, not a by-product of how he worked.

Somewhere between 3200 BCE and now, organisations separated the tool from the use. Documentation became something you produce, not something you steward. The knowledge base fills up. The policy library grows. The shared drive accumulates. Nobody goes back.

The consequence is a particular kind of institutional opacity: an organisation that has written everything down and can therefore find almost nothing useful. The records exist. They are just inert.

Three diagnostic questions that are worth sitting with:

What percentage of documents in your organisation's central knowledge repository have been actively reviewed — not just accessed, but deliberately reviewed for accuracy and continued relevance — in the past twelve months? If you cannot answer this question, that itself is the answer.

Who owns the substance of your records, as distinct from the platform that stores them? Platform ownership is an IT question. Substance ownership is a governance question. The two are routinely collapsed, which means that when a system migrates, records migrate — but the person responsible for their accuracy does not transfer with them.

When did your organisation last formally retire a document? Not delete it because storage was full, not move it to an "archive" folder that nobody ever opens, but formally declare it obsolete, record what superseded it, and remove it from active circulation? For most organisations, the honest answer is never.

The accountant in Uruk would have found this baffling. A record that nobody maintains is not a record. It is a rumour in writing.

When you write something down, the cognitive burden does not disappear. It changes address.

This seems obvious when stated plainly, and yet it contains something that most knowledge management thinking quietly sidesteps. We speak of "offloading" memory to systems, as though the goal is to carry less. But the weight does not go away. It becomes distributed — held partly in your mind, partly in the medium, and partly in the relationship between the two that you now have to maintain.

The accountant in Uruk did not free himself from the burden of remembering twelve black goats and seven brown ones. He moved that burden from his working memory to a clay tablet, and in doing so, took on a new burden: keeping the tablet, consulting it, trusting it, updating it when animals moved. The medium held the specific facts. He held everything else — the interpretive context, the operational judgement, the knowledge of when to look and what to look for.

There is something worth noticing in the phrase "the burden changes hands." Not "the burden is lifted." Not "the burden is solved." Changes hands. Custody transfers. The weight is still there; someone else — or something else — is now holding part of it.

This matters because it shapes what we should expect from knowledge systems. We build them hoping to be lighter. We find instead that we are differently loaded. The specific recall is easier. The interpretive work is not. Knowing where to find a thing and knowing what to do with it once you have found it are different skills, and systems tend to develop the first at the expense of the second.

The Sumerians knew something we sometimes forget: the record is not the knowledge. It is the custody arrangement for knowledge. The knowledge itself lives in the relationship between the record and the person who understands what the record is for.

That relationship is the thing worth tending.

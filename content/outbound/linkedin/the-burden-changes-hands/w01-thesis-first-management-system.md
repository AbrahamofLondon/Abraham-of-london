The first management system was probably exhaustion.

Not vision. Not strategy. Not a commitment to institutional knowledge management. Exhaustion — the point at which too many obligations were moving too fast to fit reliably inside a single skull.

The accountant in Uruk around 3200 BCE was not trying to build a knowledge base. He was trying to get through the week. Twelve black goats from one family. Seven brown from another. Four white promised elsewhere and not yet delivered. The animals moved through the warehouse faster than memory could hold them. He picked up a reed, pressed it into wet clay, and accidentally founded the architecture of every institution that has existed since.

This matters for a reason we are still missing.

We tend to discuss knowledge management as though it were an intellectual ambition — a question of vision, digital transformation, curation. We invest in platforms and architectures and information strategies. We commission reviews. We appoint Chief Knowledge Officers.

But the founding act was nothing like that. It was reactive, urgent, and entirely operational. The first institutional memory was built because someone was overwhelmed, not because someone was visionary.

That is still largely how it happens. The knowledge base gets built when something breaks. The process gets documented when the person who understood it leaves. The archive gets organised when the audit reveals that nothing can be found.

Institutional knowledge management is, in most organisations, a response to cognitive saturation — a system built under pressure, then extended incrementally, then retroactively described as a strategy.

There is nothing wrong with this. The accountant in Uruk was not wrong to respond to the emergency in front of him. But there is something worth examining in the gap between the ad hoc origin and the strategic framing that tends to replace it twenty years later.

The question worth asking is not whether your organisation has a knowledge management strategy. It is whether the strategy you have reflects what you actually built — or whether it describes something more coherent than the pressured, crisis-driven decisions that actually produced the system you are using.

The reed is still wet. The clay is still warm. Most organisations just no longer remember who first picked up the stylus, or why.

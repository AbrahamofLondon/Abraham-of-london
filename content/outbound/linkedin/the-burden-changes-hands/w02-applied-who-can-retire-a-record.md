One of the most revealing questions you can ask about an organisation's knowledge management is this: who has the authority to retire a record?

Not delete it — deletion is a technical action, often reserved for IT. Not archive it — moving something to an archive folder is housekeeping, not governance. But formally retire it: declare it obsolete, record what superseded it, and remove it from active circulation.

In most organisations, nobody has this authority in any clear sense. The creation of records is easy — there are workflows for it, sometimes even policies. The retirement of records exists in a procedural void. Records accumulate not because anyone decided to keep them but because nobody was tasked with deciding to remove them. The default position is permanence.

This is not a storage problem. Most organisations have ample storage. It is a governance problem: the absence of a defined process means that retirement requires someone to take an action that nothing in the organisational structure specifically encourages or rewards.

Three things tend to be true in organisations where this has been addressed well.

First, records have named owners — not platform owners (the team that manages the intranet) but substance owners (the person or function accountable for the accuracy of the content). When a record has no named substance owner, it effectively has no owner, and unowned records are never retired.

Second, records have review dates baked in at creation. Not as a field that can be left blank, but as a genuine constraint: if no review date is set, the record cannot be published. The discipline of declaring when something will be revisited is a forcing function that most content creation workflows lack.

Third, the retirement event is treated as an institutional act, not a silent deletion. When a record is retired, something records that it was retired, why, and what it was superseded by. The history of a record's lifecycle is itself institutional knowledge.

The accumulation of stale records is not a content problem. It is an accountability problem. Solving it requires not better software but clearer answers to the question that most organisations have quietly avoided: who is responsible when a record is wrong, and who is empowered to say when a record is done?

Learning from the past and being governed by it are not the same thing. The difference is operational, not philosophical. An organisation that learns from the past treats its records as a source of evidence — material to be weighed, contextualised, and applied selectively. An organisation governed by the past treats its records as instructions — things to be followed because they have been written down, by people who presumably knew what they were doing.

The trouble is that organisations rarely know which mode they are in. The records look the same either way. The policy document from eight years ago does not announce whether it was written under conditions that still obtain. The strategic framework produced before a significant market shift does not flag that the market has shifted. The record waits, and organisations move between learning from it and being governed by it without always noticing the transition.

The phrase that seems right to me is: the past should advise, not govern. This is not a call for institutions to ignore their history — an institution without memory is not free, it is simply amnesiac. It means that the appropriate relationship between an institution and its accumulated records is one of active interpretation, not passive compliance. The record offers what it knows about what was decided and why. The institution offers what it knows about whether those conditions still apply.

When this relationship breaks down — when records are treated as instructions rather than evidence — organisations become strangely deferential to their own past selves, giving the decisions made under previous conditions more authority than the people currently responsible for the outcome. There is a kind of institutional modesty in this, but it is modesty in the wrong direction.

The past is a body of evidence. It is not a management team.

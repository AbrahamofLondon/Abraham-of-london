The archive has no sense of its own expiry.

This is not a problem with bad archives. It is a structural feature of all archives. Records persist until someone actively removes them. They do not know they have become obsolete. They sit, patiently authoritative, holding information that was accurate once and may not be accurate now, with no visible indication of which condition currently applies.

Consider what this means in practice. An institutional record — a policy, a procedure, an analysis, a client commitment — comes into existence at a moment of known accuracy. It is correct when written. It is filed. Time passes. Circumstances change, decisions are revisited, the underlying reality shifts. The record does not change with it. The record waits.

The problem is not that records wait. Records are supposed to wait; waiting is what they are for. The problem is that the record gives no indication of how long it has been waiting, or what has happened in the interval, or whether any of it matters for the question currently being asked.

We sometimes speak of "stale" information as though staleness were an exceptional condition — a failure of housekeeping, an oversight to be corrected. But in most organisations, staleness is the default state of the majority of records. The fresh record is the anomaly. Documents are created at peak accuracy and then left to depreciate without disclosure.

The discipline this demands is not more documentation but more deliberate obsolescence. Every record that is created is also a liability — a commitment to either maintain it or retire it. Organisations that recognise only the asset side of this ledger accumulate knowledge bases that grow in size while declining in usefulness.

The archive, left to itself, becomes a map that no longer corresponds to the territory. It looks comprehensive. It feels authoritative. It is neither.

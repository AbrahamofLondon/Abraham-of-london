"We have the data" is one of the most consequential phrases in modern institutional life. It is often true. It is rarely sufficient. And it is deployed, regularly, to close discussions that the data alone cannot actually close.

Having data about something is not the same as understanding it. Understanding requires the data, yes, but also knowledge of how the data was collected, what it was designed to measure, what it might have missed, and whether the conditions under which it was gathered still apply to the question now being asked. These are not supplementary concerns. They are the interpretive layer without which the data is just numbers or text sitting in a system.

The Sumerian tablet could record that twelve goats had come from one household and seven from another. It could not record whether the first family had disputed the count, whether the goats were healthy, whether the delivery had been delayed in a way that affected the agreement. The record held the declared transaction. The judgement about whether the transaction had actually been completed satisfactorily lived elsewhere — in memory, in relationship, in context.

In practice, this plays out in a recognisable pattern. A strategic decision gets made. An analysis is commissioned. The analysis produces findings. The findings get written up and filed. Two years later, someone cites the analysis in a new context. The analysis says what it says. What it does not say — the assumptions it rested on, the limitations the original authors acknowledged, the ways in which the underlying situation has since changed — is not in the document. It may not be anywhere.

The discipline this requires is not more data. It is better provenance: a commitment to recording not just findings but assumptions, not just conclusions but the conditions under which those conclusions hold.

Data has a context of production. When you use it without that context, you are not being analytical. You are being credulous.

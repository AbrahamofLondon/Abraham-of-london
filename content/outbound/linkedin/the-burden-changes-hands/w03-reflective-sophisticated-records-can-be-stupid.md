An organisation can have extraordinarily sophisticated records of entirely the wrong things.

This is worth sitting with, because it is genuinely paradoxical. We associate sophisticated record-keeping with institutional intelligence — as though the presence of good systems were evidence of good thinking. It is not. The system records what it was designed to record. Whether those things are the right things to record is a prior question that the sophistication of the system cannot answer.

I think of organisations that can tell you, with great precision, how many times a knowledge base article has been viewed, how long users spent on each page, at which point they navigated away. The analytics are impeccable. What they cannot tell you is whether the people who read those articles understood them, whether they acted on them, whether the articles were accurate, or whether the questions driving the searches were being answered or merely addressed. The record of engagement is detailed. The record of comprehension does not exist.

This is not a criticism of any particular system. It is a structural observation about what records can and cannot hold. Records can hold measurable things. Comprehension is not easily measurable. Accuracy is not self-declaring. The interpretive quality of a knowledge system — whether it actually helps people think — tends to fall outside the scope of what the system itself can report.

The result is that organisations optimise for what they can measure and call the result "knowledge management." The records are meticulous. The metadata is comprehensive. The tagging is consistent. And the question of whether any of it is making the institution more capable of thinking well remains, largely, unanswered.

Sophistication in record-keeping is not the same as wisdom about what to record. The tablet is very good at being a tablet. What it holds is a different question entirely.

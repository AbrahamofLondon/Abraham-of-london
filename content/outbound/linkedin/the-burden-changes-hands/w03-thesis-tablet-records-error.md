The clay tablet records error with exactly the same patience it records truth.

This is one of the more unsettling properties of written records: the medium is entirely indifferent to the quality of what it holds. A confident misapprehension pressed into wet clay will survive millennia with no indication that it was ever wrong. It will sit alongside accurate records, equally preserved, equally legible, equally durable. The passage of time does nothing to distinguish them.

We tend to trust records partly because they are old — as though survival were evidence of accuracy. But records survive by being preserved, not by being correct. The mechanisms of preservation — a dry climate, a careful archivist, a governance decision to maintain the collection — do not filter for quality. They filter for material condition. A perfectly preserved wrong record is not more trustworthy than a recently written wrong record. It is just harder to identify as wrong, because challenging it requires reasoning against the authority that age confers.

The Sumerians were meticulous. Their clay tablets from the third millennium BCE are extraordinary records of transactions, obligations, inventories, administrative decisions. And some of them are wrong — records of transactions that were contested, obligations that were disputed, inventories that did not match the goods. The tablet recorded what was declared, not what was true. It could not evaluate itself.

Neither can your organisation's records evaluate themselves. A database of client information holds both the accurate and the inaccurate without distinction. A policy library contains the considered and the outdated, arranged alphabetically. A strategic analysis sits in a shared folder, ageing without announcement.

The question this raises is not "do we have good records?" It is "do we have a way of knowing which of our records are good?" These are different questions, and most organisations can answer the first far more confidently than the second.

The medium is patient. The error is patient too.

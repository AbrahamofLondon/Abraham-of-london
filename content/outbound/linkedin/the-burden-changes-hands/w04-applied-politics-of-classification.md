The person who names the category decides what appears when you search.

This is the politics of classification, and it is almost never discussed in knowledge management conversations, which tend to focus on search quality, storage architecture, and user experience. These are real concerns. But upstream of all of them is a question that shapes every answer: who decided how things would be labelled, and what assumptions were embedded in those decisions?

Classification systems are not neutral. Every taxonomy reflects a set of choices about what matters, what belongs together, what should be distinguished. A knowledge base that organises content by business unit assumes that the most useful way to retrieve knowledge is by the team that produced it. An archive organised by date assumes that time is the primary retrieval variable. A library catalogued by subject assumes that the subject of a document is a fixed and knowable property. Each of these assumptions will be correct for some retrieval needs and systematically unhelpful for others.

The practical consequence is that the categories available for classification determine what can be found when someone asks a question. If a piece of institutional knowledge has been classified under the producing team rather than the topic it addresses, it will not surface when someone from a different team searches for that topic. The knowledge exists. The person who needs it is looking. The system cannot make the connection, because the category did not anticipate the retrieval need.

This matters for leadership because classification decisions are often treated as technical choices delegated to whoever is implementing the system. They are, in fact, organisational choices with lasting consequences. The taxonomy you build reflects a model of how your organisation thinks — what it considers related, what it considers distinct, what it considers worth naming. Getting this right requires the people who understand the institution's actual intellectual work, not only those who understand the software.

Who holds the stylus holds the search results.

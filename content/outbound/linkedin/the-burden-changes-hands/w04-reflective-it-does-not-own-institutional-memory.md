IT builds the filing cabinet. The institution fills it. These are different jobs — and most organisations have quietly collapsed them into one, with consequences that are now difficult to reverse.

IT builds and maintains the infrastructure — the servers, the search index, the interface, the permissions model. This is genuinely difficult and genuinely important work. But the infrastructure is not the knowledge. The knowledge is what gets put into the infrastructure, how it is organised, whether it is accurate, whether it is maintained, and whether the people who need it can find and use it. These are not IT questions. They are institutional questions.

The scribal class in ancient Mesopotamia makes this distinction vivid. The scribe provided a technical service — the ability to write and to read — but the substance of what was written was the responsibility of the institution that commissioned it. A merchant dictating terms of trade was not delegating the terms to the scribe, only the recording of them. The scribe's authority was over the form. The merchant's authority was over the content.

What has happened in many organisations is a collapse of these two distinct responsibilities. Because the platform is managed by IT, substantive decisions about knowledge organisation — what to keep, what to classify, what to retire, what counts as the authoritative version of something — have drifted into technical ownership by default. Nobody decided this. It happened because the decisions looked technical and because the people with substantive expertise were not asked to participate in making them.

The result is systems that are well-maintained in a technical sense and poorly curated in an institutional sense. The infrastructure works. The knowledge doesn't.

IT does not own institutional memory. It houses it. The institution owns it — and should act accordingly.

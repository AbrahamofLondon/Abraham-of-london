Within three centuries of the first clay tablets, there were scribal schools.

This is a remarkable timeline. Writing was invented as a response to operational necessity — a way of tracking obligations that outgrew memory. Within roughly a hundred and fifty years, it had spawned an educational institution. By the time of the third dynasty of Ur, the scribal class was a defined professional group with its own training, its own standards, and its own relationship to institutional power.

The reason is not difficult to understand. Once writing exists, the ability to write becomes a form of authority. The scribe who drafted contracts was not merely a secretary; he determined the language in which obligations were recorded, the categories in which transactions were classified, the level of specificity with which arrangements were committed to clay. These choices are not neutral. The form of the record shapes what the record can say. The person who holds the stylus shapes the record.

This dynamic did not disappear when typewriters replaced styluses, or word processors replaced typewriters, or digital platforms replaced paper. It migrated. In contemporary organisations, the people who design the information architecture — who decide what fields exist in a system, what categories are available for classification, what metadata is captured and what is not — exercise a form of authority that is rarely recognised as authority. They determine what can be found when someone searches. They determine what questions the system can answer, and by implication, which questions it cannot.

Custody is power. Not glamorous power, not the kind that appears in organisation charts, but the quiet and durable power of determining what the institution knows about itself.

The interesting question for any organisation is not whether this authority exists — it always does — but whether it is being exercised deliberately or accidentally.

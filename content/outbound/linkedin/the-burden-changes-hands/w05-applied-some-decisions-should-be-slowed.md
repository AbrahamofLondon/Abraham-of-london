Not all decisions benefit from being made fast. Some — a specific and identifiable category — actually deteriorate with speed, because their quality depends on how much analogous experience from the past has been brought to bear. Speed cuts you off from that context. Slowness does not.

The Babylonians understood this instinctively about astronomical prediction. No single observation, however precisely made, was sufficient to predict the behaviour of celestial bodies. The value was in the pattern — the recognition, across centuries of records, of cycles that repeated with sufficient regularity to be anticipated. One observation in isolation was data. Eight hundred years of observations was intelligence.

In organisational terms, the analogous question is: which of our recurring decisions actually improve when they are informed by systematic historical comparison, and which do not?

Some decisions are genuinely novel — first-of-kind situations where historical analogy is limited. For these, speed is probably not the enemy of quality. But many organisational decisions are not novel. They are instances of a recurring type: how to price a service, how to respond to a particular kind of client situation, how to structure a particular kind of partnership. For these decisions, the organisation has usually seen versions of this before. The question is whether it has maintained the records in a form that allows that historical experience to be brought to bear.

The practical work is identifying which decisions belong in this category, and then building the retrieval infrastructure — not just fast search but organised historical comparison — that allows past experience to inform present judgement.

Some decisions should be slowed. Not because deliberation is a virtue in itself, but because the information that makes them better is behind you, not in front of you.

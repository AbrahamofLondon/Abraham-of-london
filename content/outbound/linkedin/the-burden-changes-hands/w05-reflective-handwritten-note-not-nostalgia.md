I still take notes by hand in some meetings.

This is not nostalgia. I do not believe that analogue processes are inherently superior to digital ones, or that the physical act of writing produces some mystical clarity unavailable to the typed word. The reason I write by hand in certain contexts is simpler and more operational: it slows me down in a way that turns out to be useful.

When I type, I can record approximately as fast as things are said. This seems like an advantage. It is sometimes an advantage. But it also means that I am, in effect, transcribing rather than processing. The note is detailed and accurate and I have not necessarily thought about any of it. The hand, being slower, forces a selection. I cannot write everything, so I must decide what matters. That decision — that compression — is a form of thinking.

The Babylonian astronomer who pressed stylus to clay at the end of each observation night was not taking a full transcript of the sky. He was recording what he had decided was worth recording. The record and the judgement were inseparable.

I am not suggesting that organisations replace their digital tools with clay tablets, or that handwriting is a knowledge management strategy. The point is structural, not material. Slowing down a process of recording can produce better thinking if the slowdown forces the recorder to exercise judgement rather than transcription — to ask "what is actually important here?" rather than capturing everything and deferring that question indefinitely.

The institutions that built the slow intelligence did not do it by accident. They built structures that required patience, that demanded selection, that rewarded the accumulated judgement of people who had to decide what was worth keeping. Speed is not the enemy of intelligence. But it can be the enemy of this particular kind, if we are not careful.

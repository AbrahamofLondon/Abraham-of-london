The Babylonian astronomers kept records for eight hundred years.

Not eight years. Not eighty. Eight hundred. The records they maintained — careful, systematic observations of celestial phenomena across centuries — were not immediately useful to the individuals making them. No single observer would live long enough to benefit from the pattern that eight centuries of data would eventually reveal. The intelligence was slow by design. It could only emerge across generations of custodians who were willing to maintain records whose value they would never themselves see.

This is a different model of institutional intelligence from the one that dominates contemporary management thinking. The prevailing model optimises for retrieval speed: the ability to surface the right information quickly, to access the specific record you need at the moment you need it. This is genuinely useful. It is also, on its own, insufficient.

Retrieval speed and comparative intelligence are not the same thing. Retrieval speed is the ability to find a specific record. Comparative intelligence is the ability to hold many records in relation to one another over time, to notice patterns across them, to identify what changes and what remains constant across different contexts and conditions. The Babylonians were not optimising for retrieval. They were building towards comparison.

Most organisations do not have eight hundred years. But the question of timescale is still live, in a compressed form. An organisation that optimises purely for fast retrieval — ensuring that any record can be found immediately — may simultaneously fail to develop the comparative intelligence that comes from holding records in relation to one another. Fast access to individual records is not the same as wisdom about what those records collectively mean.

The slow intelligence cannot be rushed. It requires accumulation, patience, and an institutional commitment to maintaining records that will not be useful until they are old.

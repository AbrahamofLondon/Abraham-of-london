When a Mesopotamian water-rights code was inscribed in stone, the author left the room.

Not immediately. Initially the author was present — available to interpret, to clarify, to explain what had been meant when a particular provision proved ambiguous in application. But the text outlasted its author. It outlasted everyone who had been present for the drafting. Centuries later, administrators applying the code were working with words whose original context was entirely unavailable to them. The text had become the only thing that remained. And the text could not be asked what it had intended.

Authorship and governance separated at the moment of inscription. What had been one activity — deciding what the rules were — became two: the historical act of writing and the ongoing act of interpretation. The institution that applied the code was not its author. But it was responsible for deciding what the code meant in circumstances the author had never anticipated.

This dynamic is being dramatically accelerated by large language models. When an AI system generates text — a policy draft, a client communication, a knowledge base article, an internal report — the text enters circulation without a human author in any traditional sense. There is no person who decided that this sentence should come before that one, who weighed those particular word choices, who takes responsibility for the framing. There is a prompt, and there is output.

The question this raises is not whether AI can write well. It can write fluently. The question is who is accountable for what the text says. Authorship has traditionally bundled together the activities of composition and responsibility. AI separates them. Someone composes a prompt; the system produces the text; responsibility becomes diffuse.

The most important AI governance question is therefore not about capability. It is about accountability. If an AI-generated document contains an error, misleads a client, or embeds an assumption that causes a downstream problem — who held the stylus?

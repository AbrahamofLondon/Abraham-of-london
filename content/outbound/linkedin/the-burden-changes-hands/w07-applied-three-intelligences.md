There are three distinct capacities that an organisation can develop in relation to its knowledge, and most organisations have only developed the first.

The first is storage intelligence: the ability to accumulate and retrieve. Can you find a specific record when you need it? Is the search functional? Are documents accessible to the people who need them? Most organisations have invested in this, and many have got reasonably good at it. Modern knowledge management platforms, properly implemented, handle storage intelligence well.

The second is retrieval intelligence: the ability to surface relevant knowledge in response to a question, even when the question does not match the exact language of the record. This requires better tagging, better search, better architecture — and increasingly, the kind of semantic search that AI-assisted tools are beginning to provide. Some organisations have made progress here. Many have not.

The third is interpretive intelligence: the ability to take knowledge that has been retrieved and understand what it means in the current context — what assumptions it rested on, what has changed since it was produced, what parts of it apply to the current situation and what parts do not. This is not a technology problem. It is a human capacity problem. It requires people who know enough about the domain, and about the history of the organisation's thinking about the domain, to read old knowledge critically rather than deferentially.

Most knowledge management investment goes into the first capacity, some into the second, very little into the third. This allocation is understandable: storage and retrieval can be built systematically, tested, measured, and demonstrated. Interpretive intelligence is harder to commission and harder to measure. It lives in people, not platforms.

The enduring archive — the Ashurbanipal model, the institution that holds not just records but meaning — requires all three. An organisation that has excellent storage and retrieval but poor interpretive capacity can find everything and understand very little of it. The bottleneck is not the search. It is the reader.

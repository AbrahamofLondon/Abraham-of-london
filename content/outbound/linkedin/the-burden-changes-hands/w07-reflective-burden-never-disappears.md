The cognitive burden does not disappear. It changes hands.

This is where we started, seven weeks and five thousand years ago. The accountant in Uruk picking up a reed and pressing it into wet clay, not to build an institution but to get through the week. The Babylonian astronomers filling tablet after tablet with observations they would never live to see synthesised. Ashurbanipal sending agents across the ancient world to copy what was at risk of being lost. The scribal schools of Ur, training a new generation to hold what one generation could not.

Each of these was a different attempt to answer the same structural problem: how does an institution carry what its members cannot hold alone?

Every answer has changed the nature of the burden rather than removed it. Writing moved the burden from individual memory to the record — but created the burden of stewardship. The archive moved the burden from scattered records to organised collection — but created the burden of curation. The knowledge base moved the burden from curators to search — but created the burden of retrieval quality and interpretive capacity. The AI system moves certain burdens from human authors to generative models — but creates the burden of governance and accountability.

None of these transitions is a story of progressive lightening. They are stories of progressive redistribution. The total cognitive weight of knowing what an institution knows, maintaining its relevance, and using it wisely has not declined across the arc of recorded history. It has been reorganised.

What has changed — what keeps changing — is where the burden sits, and who is responsible for carrying which part of it.

This seems to me the correct framing for anyone thinking about knowledge management, AI adoption, or institutional intelligence: not "how do we reduce the burden" but "how do we distribute it well, and ensure that the parts that require human judgement stay in human hands."

The clay is long dry. The question is still wet.

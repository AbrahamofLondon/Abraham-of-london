While David was painting Napoleon's mother into a scene she had never witnessed, another painter was watching the same empire from the other side.

Francisco Goya was in Spain when French troops occupied Madrid in 1808. He watched as soldiers rounded up civilians and shot them. He went home and made a painting.

The Third of May 1808 shows a man in a white shirt, his arms raised, his face a mixture of terror and defiance. He is about to die. The soldiers are faceless, identical, mechanical. The lantern at their feet casts a harsh light on the scene.

Goya's painting is as realistic as David's. The anatomy is precise. The composition is deliberate. But Goya used realism for the opposite purpose. He did not glorify power. He documented its brutality.

David's painting was commissioned by power. Goya's was painted in spite of power. David's painting was displayed in the palace. Goya's was hidden in his studio, not published until after his death.

Two painters. Same war. Two completely different versions of what a painter is for.

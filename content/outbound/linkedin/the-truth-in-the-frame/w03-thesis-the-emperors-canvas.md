In 1804, Napoleon walked into Jacques-Louis David's studio and said: add my mother. She was not at the coronation. She was in Rome. David painted her in anyway.

The painting hangs in the Louvre. It is nine metres wide, six metres tall. Millions of people have stood before it. Most of them have no idea that Napoleon's mother was absent from the event the painting claims to document. The painting became the memory. The painting replaced the event.

That is the power of realism. And that is the moment modern propaganda was born.

Napoleon understood something that earlier rulers had only glimpsed: that a painting could be more powerful than a battle, because a painting could be remembered. And a painting that was remembered could be believed — even when it was not true.

The style can be perfect and the relationship between image and reality can be broken. Realism is not the same as truth.

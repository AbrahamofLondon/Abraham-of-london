In 1858, Franz Xaver Winterhalter painted the portrait of a fourteen-year-old boy in silk and jewels. Queen Victoria hung the painting in her private rooms as proof of a devoted son.

The boy was not her son. He was Maharaja Duleep Singh, the last ruler of the Sikh Empire. He had been removed from his throne at ten. His kingdom had been annexed. His mother had been imprisoned. The Koh-i-Noor diamond had been taken from him and presented to Victoria as a gift from a child who had no choice about giving it.

The empire called him a son because it was easier than calling him what he was. A son is family. A captive is a problem. A son can be painted in silk and hung in the palace as proof of benevolent rule. A captive would have to be accounted for.

Winterhalter painted the son. The empire hung the painting. The boy who sat for it was not consulted about any of it.

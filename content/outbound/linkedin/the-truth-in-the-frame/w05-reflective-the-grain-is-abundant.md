In 1932, the Soviet state printed a poster titled The Grain Is Abundant. It shows a collective farm worker standing in a field of wheat that reaches her shoulders. She is smiling. The sky is clear. The caption reads: "Fulfil the Bolshevik harvest plan — the country needs grain."

The poster was displayed in village reading rooms, collective farm offices, and schools across the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic — the same region where the Holodomor was killing an estimated four million people between 1932 and 1933.

The poster does not show the grain requisition squads who took the harvest from the farmers who grew it. It does not show the children whose deaths were recorded as "weakness from birth" because the state did not permit famine to be listed as a cause of death. The poster shows a woman in a field. The wheat is abundant. The country got the grain. The people who grew it did not.

The poster is still in the archive. You can view it in the Library of Congress. It is still beautiful.

The peasant left no archive.

In 1934, the Soviet Writers' Congress issued a mandate that would define official art for half a century. Socialist realism was not to depict life as it was. It was to depict life as it was becoming — the Communist future, already visible in the present, rendered with such conviction that the viewer would believe the transition had already occurred.

The practical consequence was that a painter could not paint what they saw. They could only paint what the doctrine required them to see. If the harvest failed, the harvest was not painted. If the factory was dangerous, the danger was not painted. If the leader was wrong, the leader was not painted as wrong.

Boris Ioganson's At an Old Ural Factory (1937) shows workers in heroic light — muscles tensed, faces determined, the machinery gleaming. The Gulag was operating forty miles from the factories Ioganson painted. The prisoners who built the railway that carried the factory's output were not included in the composition. They could not be. The doctrine did not permit it.

The painter was not a documentarian. The painter was a prophet whose prophecies were required to come true.

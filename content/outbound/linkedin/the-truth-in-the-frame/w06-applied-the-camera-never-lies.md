Three photographs. Three different relationships between image and power.

Iwo Jima (1945): A real event, photographed in real time, that became something larger than itself through distribution and use. The photograph is not fabricated. The icon is constructed. The bond campaign raised $26.3 billion. The moment and the meaning are different objects.

Napalm Girl (1972): A photograph that told a truth the official record was suppressing, taken by a photographer who was not working for the state. Nick Ut drove the children to hospital before filing the photograph. He broke protocol. The photograph that changed public opinion about the Vietnam War was taken by a man who almost did not take it because he was too busy being a person.

Tank Man (1989): A photograph taken by multiple photographers from a hotel window. The man's identity has never been confirmed. His fate is unknown. The photograph circulates freely everywhere except China, where it is systematically removed from every digital platform. The photograph exists. The people it belongs to have not seen it.

The photograph of the flag is true. The photograph of the girl is true. The photograph of the man is true. What you do with a true image, who sees it, what meaning gets pressed into it — that is not photography. That is everything that surrounds photography.

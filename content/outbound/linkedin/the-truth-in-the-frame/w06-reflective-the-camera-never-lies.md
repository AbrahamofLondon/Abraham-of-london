We do not know who Tank Man was. We do not know what happened to him. The photograph is complete. The person is not.

That incompleteness — held without resolution — is the most honest thing photography can give us. The camera records the surface. What is behind the surface is not always available. Not to the photographer, not to the historian, not to the reader.

Sometimes the handprint is all you get, and you will never know whose hand it was.

The photograph of the flag is true. The photograph of the girl is true. The photograph of the man in front of the tanks is true. What you do with a true image, who sees it, what meaning gets pressed into it, what wall it gets hung on and whose hands did the hanging — that is not photography. That is everything that surrounds photography.

The handprint is not the mark. The handprint is whoever got to decide the mark would survive.

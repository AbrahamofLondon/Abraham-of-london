You are watching a video of a person you know. They are saying something they did not say. You cannot tell. Not from the video. Only from what you know about them — from the trust you have built, the context you carry, the years of witness that no image can replace.

This is the condition we now live in. The question used to be: does this photograph show what it claims to show? Now the question is: do I trust the person who sent this to me? Do I trust the institution that published it? Do I trust the chain of custody from event to image to my screen?

That is not a new question. It is the oldest question. It is the question the cave community asked of the person who pressed the handprint. It is the question the conquered people asked of the king who carved the stele, and the answer was no, and they were right, and their knowledge did not survive because they had no stone.

Personal trust — the verification of source, the weight of prior relationship, the accumulated credibility of witness over time — is the technology that preceded photography by thirty thousand years. The synthetic image does not defeat it. The synthetic image reveals that it was always what we were actually relying on, even when we thought we were relying on the image.

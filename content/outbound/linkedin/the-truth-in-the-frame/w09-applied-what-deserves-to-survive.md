Look for the handprint. Ask whose hand it was. Ask what they needed the mark to say, and what they needed it to hide. Ask who was in the room and who was not. Ask what is happening just outside the frame.

This is not a metaphor. It is a practice.

When you see a photograph, ask who took it and why. When you read a statistic, ask who collected it and what they were trying to prove. When you watch a video, ask who published it and what they wanted you to feel. When you hear a story about a person you do not know, ask who benefits from you believing it. When you are told that something is the official record, ask whose official record it is and what alternative records were not preserved.

This takes longer than believing what you see. It takes longer than sharing what confirms what you already think. It takes longer than scrolling past something that feels wrong because you do not have the energy to investigate it.

It takes longer, and it is harder, and it will make you less certain about more things. That uncertainty is not a weakness. It is the only adequate response to a world where the tools for manufacturing records are more powerful and more accessible than the tools for verifying them.

Certainty in such a world is not confidence. It is negligence.

Your hand has been on the wall this entire time.

Every message you send, every account of events you relay to another person, every choice you make about what to include in a story and what to leave out — these are handprints. Small ones. But the same operation, in miniature, as everything this series has documented.

You crop the photograph when you choose which detail to mention. You adjust the canvas when you soften what happened to protect someone's feelings. You optimise for engagement when you tell the version of the story that will get the strongest reaction. You generate a synthetic face when you present yourself as something you are not.

The tools are smaller. The operation is the same. You are not standing outside the frame. You are inside it. You have always been inside it.

The handprint on the cave wall has survived for 36,000 years. The person who pressed it has not. We do not know their name. We do not know what they intended. We know only that they were here, and that they wanted something to remain.

That is enough to make the mark. It is not enough to make the mark honest. Honesty requires someone, in every generation, who is willing to ask what the mark is hiding.

The truth does not defend itself. That is your job.

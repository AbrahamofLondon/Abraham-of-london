This is the record. The hand on the wall. The king's inscription. The emperor's canvas. The portrait of a captive prince. The poster of abundant grain. The cropped photograph. The algorithm's feed. The synthetic face.

You have read them all now. You know what they cost.

The connecting thread is not power, though power runs through all of it. It is not technology, though technology enabled each version. The connecting thread is the human need for the record to be more than it is — to be not just documentation but legitimation, not just memory but authority, not just what happened but what it means that it happened.

Every king who carved a stele, every court painter who corrected the canvas, every algorithm that optimised for engagement was responding to the same human hunger: to have the record confirm the story, not just the event.

That hunger is not shameful. It is human. It is also dangerous, because it makes records vulnerable to anyone who understands that serving the hunger matters more than serving the truth.

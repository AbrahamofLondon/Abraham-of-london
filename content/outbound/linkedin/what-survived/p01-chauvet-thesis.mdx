What survives is not what was made — it is what stone could carry.

In 1994, three people found a cave in southern France that had been sealed for twenty thousand years. The animals on the walls moved in the torchlight. Someone thirty-six thousand years ago had understood animation.

We found the paintings. We did not find the songs. We did not find the stories. We did not find the names.

The paintings survived because the cave sealed and the mineral conditions were exactly right. The meaning did not survive because meaning travels by voice and by performance — and that chain broke somewhere in the ice age.

What we found was not what was made. What we found was the part that stone could carry.

Your organisation's institutional knowledge lives in people, not documents. When the person leaves, the knowledge leaves. The question is whether you know what you are carrying — and whether you have made sure it can be carried by someone else.

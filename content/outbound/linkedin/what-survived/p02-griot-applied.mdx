When the person who holds the knowledge leaves without passing it on, the knowledge does not move to a document. It disappears.

The griot system worked because the transmission was structured, rehearsed, and socially enforced. The knowledge was not casually transferred — it was the legal and political foundation of the society. The stakes were high enough that the transmission was taken seriously.

Most organisations treat knowledge transfer as an exit interview and a handover document. That is not transmission. That is a gesture.

Real transmission requires:
- The receiver to be identified before the carrier leaves
- The knowledge to be structured enough to be transferred
- Time for the transfer to happen under real conditions
- A mechanism to verify that the transfer succeeded

If any of these is missing, the knowledge will leave when the person does.

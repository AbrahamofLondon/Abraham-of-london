The most durable storage system in human history was a trained human memory, maintained by a community with everything at stake.

For a thousand years, the most important historical records in West Africa were not written anywhere. They were kept alive by the griot — a hereditary custodian whose entire family had been designated as rememberers across generations. The role was inherited. It was formally trained. It was socially enforced.

When a dispute arose about a land boundary or the terms of a treaty made three generations ago, the griot was the record. His memory was the archive.

When European colonisers described these societies as "without history" because they lacked written records, they were making a category error with catastrophic consequences. They dismantled the political structures that gave the griot his function. The storage system was intact. The environment that made the storage meaningful was what was destroyed.

Who is your organisation's griot — and is the succession plan real?

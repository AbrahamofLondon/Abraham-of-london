The removal of Yezhov from Soviet history was not performed by one person in one darkroom. It was performed by thousands of hands, in thousands of homes, with scissors and paste.

Subscribers to the Great Soviet Encyclopaedia received inserts in the post with instructions to cut out pages and paste in replacements. The erasure was distributed to the people who owned the books. The archive was not edited at the centre. It was edited everywhere, simultaneously, by the people who owned it.

Distributed erasure is not a historical curiosity. It is a design option.

What records exist of the things your organisation decided not to do — the options declined, the roads not taken, the strategies considered and rejected? If those records are gone, the learning is gone. And if they were never kept, the erasure happened before anyone noticed.

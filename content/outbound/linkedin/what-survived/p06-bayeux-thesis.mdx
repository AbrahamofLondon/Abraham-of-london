The Bayeux Tapestry survived because the people who made it won. Survival is not neutral.

The tapestry is a seventy-metre embroidered cloth produced ten years after the Norman Conquest. It depicts the events leading to the Battle of Hastings. It was commissioned by the Conqueror's half-brother. It is magnificent.

It is also a legal brief. Its entire narrative structure serves one argument: William's claim to England was legitimate because Harold broke a sacred oath. The Saxon account of 1066 — the account that might have shown us Harold's perspective — did not survive in equivalent form. Not because it was less true. Because the Normans controlled the preservation infrastructure.

The historical record is not a representative sample of what existed. It is a sample biased toward what the powerful found worth preserving.

Every organisation has a Bayeux Tapestry — the official account of what happened, made by the people who controlled the outcome. Ask what is not in the tapestry.

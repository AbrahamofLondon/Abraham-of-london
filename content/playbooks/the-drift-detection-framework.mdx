# The Drift Detection Framework

## Identify Silent Organisational Decay Before It Becomes Structural Failure

---

## The Problem

Drift is invisible until it becomes expensive.

Unlike a crisis, which declares itself with symptoms and headlines, **organisational drift** accumulates quietly—through slower decisions, higher rework, and subtle shifts in leadership behaviour.

If left unchecked, this slow erosion:

- Undermines execution discipline.
- Degrades trust between levels.
- Increases the cost of change.

The Drift Detection Framework turns these subtle signals into a structured, measurable early‑warning system before the organisation reaches full‑blown dysfunction.

---

## Detection Signals

Use these four core signals as the starting point for each Drift Detection session:

### Decision latency increase

The time between decision‑request and final decision is growing without a clear explanation.

### Rework frequency

The same work is being redone repeatedly, often with minor or cosmetic changes.

### Leadership inconsistency

Leadership statements, tone‑of‑voice, and risk appetite shift frequently, creating confusion and second‑guessing.

### Cultural friction

Increased tension in meetings, more "heated discussions," and informal complaints about "how things are really done" versus "how they're supposed to be done."

These signals are not isolated events; they are **early markers of systemic decay** that the framework is designed to surface and quantify.

---

## Framework

The Drift Detection Framework is structured around three clear phases, designed to move from raw observation to structured intervention.

---

### Phase 1 — Signal Capture

Track behavioural indicators that reveal underlying drift.

#### Objective

Convert vague discomfort ("something feels off") into concrete, observable data points.

#### Key actions

- Review historical data across:
  - Decision logs.
  - Project timelines and rework notes.
  - Meeting summaries and escalation records.
- Run a short, structured input session with leadership and core teams to capture:
  - How long it *feels* like decisions take now versus 12 months ago.
  - How often the same topic reappears on the agenda.
  - Where people feel expectations are inconsistent or shifting.
- Document recurring themes without interpretation (yet).

#### Output

**Signal inventory** of drift‑related observations, ready for pattern‑recognition.

---

### Phase 2 — Pattern Recognition

Identify recurring misalignment and the system conditions driving it.

#### Objective

Move from "we see these symptoms" to "we see these patterns."

#### Key actions

- Cluster signals into categories such as:
  - Decision‑making dysfunction (escalation without closure, lack of clear owners).
  - Coordination overhead (too many review layers, rework due to unclear handovers).
  - Leadership‑tone shifts (changing priorities, mixed messages on risk and trade‑offs).
- Map each pattern back to the organisation's structure, incentives, and communication habits.
- Ask:
  - "Is this pattern getting stronger over time?"
  - "Where does it show up most often—by function, by level, by project type?"

#### Output

- **Pattern map** of drift‑driving behaviours.
- Input for the escalation‑threshold definition.

---

### Phase 3 — Escalation Threshold

Define precisely when drift shifts from "manageable noise" to "corrective‑intervention required."

#### Objective

Turn diagnosis into a decision‑rule, so leadership does not wait until the organisation is visibly broken.

#### Key actions

- For each major drift pattern, define:
  - A **baseline** (e.g., average decision latency in days, percentage of work requiring rework).
  - A **warning band** (e.g., "if decision latency increases by 20%, trigger a review").
  - A **hard threshold** (e.g., "if decision latency doubles, mandate a structured intervention").
- Assign ownership and timelines for each threshold level.
- Agree in advance on what "intervention" looks like at each level (e.g., facilitated workshop, governance review, role‑clarification, or external support).

#### Output

**Intervention trigger points** for the organisation's operating rhythm.

---

## Output

The Drift Detection Framework yields a compact but powerful diagnostic package:

### Drift index (0–100, or 0–1.0)

A composite metric summarising the level of drift across the organisation, based on decision‑latency, rework, leadership‑inconsistency, and cultural‑friction signals.

Designed to be recalculated periodically (e.g., quarterly) as a "health pulse."

### Risk classification

A simple classification (e.g., Low / Medium / High / Critical) for each major drift pattern or domain (decision‑making, coordination, leadership tone, culture, etc.).

Helps leaders prioritise where to intervene first.

### Intervention trigger points

Time‑based or value‑based rules (e.g., "if decision‑latency exceeds X days for Y consecutive projects, trigger a review") tied directly to the Drift index and individual signals.

### Early warning indicators

A short list of leading‑behaviour flags that practitioners can watch for in daily operations, such as:
- Re‑opened decisions.
- Repeated agenda‑items.
- Escalation‑without‑closure patterns.

---

## Strategic Principle

Drift ignored becomes dysfunction.

The Drift Detection Framework is not a one‑off "health check"; it is a **continuous discipline**—a way to institutionalise early detection into the organisation's operating system.

By deliberately measuring and monitoring the small signs of decay, leaders can correct course before drift hardens into structural failure.

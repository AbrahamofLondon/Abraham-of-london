There is a specific kind of organisational failure that looks, from the outside, like success.

The archive exists. It is large. The filing conventions are consistent. The records go back years. New content is added regularly. The system is searchable. The permissions are configured. Everything is, in a technical sense, in order.

And nobody can navigate it. Not with any confidence that they are finding the right things, understanding them correctly, or deriving insight from their relationship to each other. The archive is present. The knowledge it was supposed to preserve has drifted away from the people who need it, or left with the people who created it, or become inaccessible in the subtler sense of being technically retrievable but practically uninterpretable without a context that no longer exists.

This is memory outliving meaning. The record survives. The understanding that would make it useful does not.

The organisations that avoid this tend to have three things that most organisations lack — not in their systems, but in their governance.

The first is curatorial commitment. Someone is responsible, on an ongoing basis, for deciding what goes into the archive and in what form, what gets updated as circumstances change, and how the whole is structured so that different parts of it can be placed in conversation. This is not an IT function. It is a knowledge function. And it requires people who understand the substance of what the organisation does, not just the mechanics of the system that holds it.

The organisations that treat knowledge management as purely an infrastructure problem — selecting the platform, configuring the permissions, establishing naming conventions, then considering the work done — consistently find that their archives become unusable within a few years. Not because the platform failed. Because no one was tending the content.

The second is interpretive capacity. People who can navigate the archive, cross-reference records, and derive non-obvious understanding from what the combination reveals. This is not a universal skill. It tends to be present in individuals who happen to be drawn to it, not in the functions formally responsible for it. Building it deliberately means protecting the time of people doing slow, comparative work — which the operational pressure of most organisations consistently absorbs.

The third is archival authority: the institutional standing of the archive within decision-making. An archive that is consulted when convenient and easily overridden when inconvenient is not intelligence. It is decoration. Genuine archival intelligence requires that historical record is treated as evidence — that the question of whether the archive has something relevant to say is asked explicitly, and that the answer has actual weight.

These three conditions are not expensive to develop. They are expensive to protect, because the institutional pressures that erode them are constant and require active resistance.

The alternative is an archive that grows and grows, and means less and less, and one day becomes a very detailed record of an organisation that is no longer sure what it learned or when.

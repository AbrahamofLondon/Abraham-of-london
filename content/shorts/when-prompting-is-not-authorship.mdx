The scribe in Uruk was not a passive instrument.

This is easy to miss. The official decided what was traded. The merchant decided what was agreed. But the scribe decided — in the practical moment of setting reed to clay — how those decisions were represented. What words were used. Which context was included. Whether the verbal understanding that preceded the written agreement appeared in the official record.

These were not transcription choices. They were authorial ones. The scribe was not writing down what happened. They were producing a representation of what happened — shaped by the conventions of the system, the pressures of the moment, and their own exercise of professional judgement about what the record required.

Authorship, properly understood, is not the act of producing text. It is the act of being accountable for the claims the text makes.

This distinction matters now, in a specific practical context.

When a practitioner uses an AI writing tool to produce a report, a policy draft, a client communication, or an internal analysis, the question of authorship does not disappear. It migrates. The text was generated by a system that has no accountability. The person who prompted the system, reviewed the output, and introduced the document into institutional circulation is the author — responsible for the accuracy of the claims, the appropriateness of the framing, and the consequences of acting on what it says.

Prompting is not authorship. Accepting the output is.

This has practical implications for how practitioners should use AI writing tools in institutional contexts. The prompt should be specific enough that the output can be evaluated. The output should be read critically against domain knowledge, not accepted because it is fluent and well-formatted. The claims should be verified before the document is circulated, with the same rigour that would be applied to a document the practitioner had written themselves — because the practitioner has, in effect, written it.

The institutional failure mode is the opposite of this. The document is generated, glanced at, forwarded, and filed. The fluency of the output substitutes for the verification of its contents. The archive receives it with the same fidelity it receives everything, and the document begins to speak with the authority of inscription, carrying claims that nobody has taken responsibility for.

The author-function still exists. Someone still has to perform it.

That someone is the person who accepted the output and put it into the world.

There is a Mesopotamian legal code governing water rights that was inscribed during a period of relative rainfall.

Two centuries later, the inscriptions were still in place. Officials still pointed to them. The code still governed. And the river was contracting.

The code did not know the river was contracting. It could not know. It was stone. It described its world with perfect confidence and perfect blindness, speaking in present tense as if the conditions that gave it meaning had not shifted while it was standing still.

This is the paradox at the centre of documented authority. Writing gives knowledge the power to outlast its author. That power is real — it is the mechanism of institutional continuity, the means by which organisations and laws and disciplines can persist beyond the lifetimes of the people who formed them. But the same quality that makes this persistence possible — the fixity of inscription — is also what makes it dangerous. The author leaves the room. The text keeps speaking. The world keeps moving.

There is a moral responsibility in this that most writers of institutional documents never consider, because they are focused on what the document needs to say right now, not on what it will say in ten years in conditions they cannot foresee.

That responsibility is this: if you are writing something that will carry authority beyond your presence, you are making decisions about the future. You are choosing what the institution will believe, how it will reason, what it will treat as settled, long after you have moved on or retired or left. Those decisions deserve the same care you would give to any other decision with long-term consequences.

The practical form of this discipline is specific. It means writing documents that contain the conditions of their own review — specifying not just what they say but when they should be revisited and by whom. It means being explicit about the conditions under which the document is valid, so that future readers can ask whether those conditions still obtain. It means accepting that retiring a document is an act of institutional maturity rather than defeat.

And it means recognising that the document will eventually govern people who never met you, who have no access to the conversation that produced it, who will treat the words themselves as the authority because the words are all that remains.

Write with that reader in mind.

The author who wrote carefully, for their moment, and also for the reader who would arrive later in different conditions — that author discharged a fuller duty than the one who simply captured the present as accurately as possible and assumed someone else would sort out the future.

The room rearranges itself around the text. The text should be worth the rearrangement.

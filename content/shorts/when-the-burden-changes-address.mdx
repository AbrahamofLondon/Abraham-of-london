The burden does not disappear when it leaves the body.

It changes address.

When memory leaves the skull and enters a medium — clay, document, database — the cognitive load is relieved. The accountant sleeps a little better. The project manager stops carrying the context alone. The departing executive writes up what they know, hands it over, and feels that they have discharged a duty.

They have discharged part of it.

Because the moment the knowledge enters the medium, custody begins. And custody is not a technical function. It is a governance one.

Someone must learn to read the record. Someone must teach them. Someone must verify that what was written is accurate, that what was accurate is still current, that what is current is accessible to the people who need it. Someone must protect the medium from the neglect that accumulates quietly in every organisation where records are maintained in principle and tended in practice only when something goes wrong.

None of this is automatic. None of it is free.

More importantly: none of it is neutral. The person who holds the record stands differently from the person whose obligations appear within it. The official who controls the archive stands differently from the one who depends on it. Access to records and control over records are both forms of institutional power — not incidental side effects, but structural features, built into the system at the moment the first mark was pressed.

This is why "knowledge management" as a concept routinely fails in organisations that treat it as infrastructure rather than governance. They solve the storage problem and ignore the custody problem. They build the archive and hire no one to navigate it. They implement the system and then watch it drift from useful to merely present.

The burden was never the weight of holding knowledge in a skull.

The burden is the ongoing work of keeping the knowledge alive: accurate, current, accessible, and under the care of someone who understands both what it says and why it was written.

When that work is done well, the organisation compounds. When it is not, the archive silts up.

The clay survives. The meaning does not.

# ABRAHAM OF LONDON — UNIVERSAL WRITING STANDARDS
## Permanent Standards for All Content Production

These standards apply to every piece of content produced from this platform — every series, every editorial, every short, every social post, every agent instruction, every source kit update. They are not suggestions. They are the standard.

---

## THE EIGHT UNIVERSAL STANDARDS

### 1. Truthmode

Write as though the reader will fact-check every claim and already knows the subject better than you do.

**What it means:**
- Every claim must be defensible from a primary or well-sourced secondary source.
- No exaggeration for effect. If the number is disputed, say it is disputed. If the evidence is partial, say it is partial.
- The reader who knows the subject should find nothing to correct. The reader who does not know the subject should be given nothing they will later have to unlearn.
- Precision is not optional. "Approximately four million" is better than "millions." "Between three and five million" is better than "approximately four million" when the sources disagree.
- When the evidence is uncertain, the uncertainty is stated — not hidden, not hedged, but stated with the same precision as the certainty.

**The test:** Could this sentence survive a hostile cross-examination by someone who knows more than the writer? If not, rewrite it until it could.

**The failure:** "Napoleon understood something that earlier rulers had only glimpsed" — this is a claim about what Napoleon understood and what earlier rulers understood. It cannot be verified. It is a rhetorical assertion dressed as a fact. Truthmode deletes it.

**The standard:** *"The state called it socialist realism. The peasant called it hunger."* — every word is defensible. The state did call it that. The peasant did experience it as hunger. No claim is made beyond what the evidence supports.

---

### 2. Redteam

Assume the writer is wrong. Assume the argument has a blind spot. Find it before the reader does.

**What it means:**
- Every piece must be tested against its strongest counter-argument before it is published.
- The counter-argument is not dismissed. It is engaged — either refuted with evidence or acknowledged as a legitimate limitation.
- The piece that acknowledges its limitations is stronger than the piece that pretends it has none.
- Redteam is not performed as a separate exercise. It is built into the writing. The writer anticipates the objection and answers it before the reader can raise it.

**The test:** What is the most damaging question someone could ask about this argument? Is it answered in the piece? If not, the piece has not been redteamed.

**The failure:** Part 5 of The Truth in the Frame argues that socialist realist posters were propaganda that erased famine. A reader could object: "But the posters were not the cause of the famine. The famine was caused by policy. The posters were a symptom, not a weapon." The piece answers this by showing that the posters were part of a system that controlled what could be documented — the frame was policy, not decoration. That is redteam built into the writing.

**The standard:** The piece that has been redteamed does not feel defensive. It feels complete. The reader who disagrees must disagree with the evidence, not with an unaddressed gap in the argument.

---

### 3. Hormozi

Write for the reader who is scanning, distracted, and has fifty other things they could be reading. Every sentence must earn its place by being either interesting or necessary.

**What it means:**
- The first sentence is the most important sentence. It must stop the reader.
- Every sentence after the first must justify the reader having read the one before it.
- No sentence exists to "set up" a later sentence. If a sentence is not doing work, it is doing damage.
- Compression is a form of respect. The reader's attention is finite. Do not waste it.
- Short sentences are not always better. But every sentence should be as short as it can be without losing precision.
- The piece should be as long as it needs to be and no longer. When in doubt, cut.

**The test:** Read each paragraph. Ask: if this paragraph were deleted, would the piece lose something essential? If no, delete it. Then test each sentence within each surviving paragraph with the same question.

**The failure:** "In this piece, I will argue that the photograph appears to have no commissioner, and that this appearance is what makes it different from everything that came before it" — the reader has already decided whether to read on. This sentence tells them what they are about to read instead of making them want to read it.

**The standard:** *"The photograph appears to have no commissioner."* — eight words. The reader is inside the argument before they know they are reading one.

---

### 4. /Human

Every piece must be legible to a general reader who does not have specialised knowledge. The subject may be complex. The writing must not be.

**What it means:**
- No jargon without immediate definition or context.
- No assumption that the reader knows the background. If a name, event, or concept is essential, it is introduced in the action — not in a footnote, not in a parenthetical, not in a prior sentence that the reader may have skipped.
- The reader should never have to stop reading to look something up. If they stop, they may not come back.
- Complexity of thought is not the same as complexity of language. The most sophisticated arguments can be stated in the plainest words.
- /Human does not mean dumbing down. It means the writer has done the work of translation so the reader does not have to.

**The test:** Could someone who has never heard of the subject understand the first paragraph? If the first paragraph requires prior knowledge, the piece has failed /Human before it has begun.

**The failure:** "The Stele of the Vultures, carved by the Sumerian city-state of Lagash around 2450 BCE, demonstrates the epistemological asymmetry inherent in monumental recordkeeping" — the reader who does not know what the Stele of the Vultures is has already been lost. The name and date arrive before the reader has a reason to care.

**The standard:** *"The oldest surviving record of a battle is a stone carved by the winner. The losers left no stone. That is not incidental."* — no specialised knowledge required. The reader knows what a battle is, what a stone is, and what it means to leave nothing.

---

### 5. Futureyou

Write for the reader you want the reader to become, not the reader they are now.

**What it means:**
- The piece should leave the reader slightly changed — more discerning, more sceptical of easy answers, more alive to a question they had not considered.
- The closing is not a summary. It is a charge. The reader should carry something forward.
- The piece does not do the reader's thinking for them. It gives them the tools to think further.
- Futureyou is why the closing question is never rhetorical. It is a genuine question the reader can take into their own life.

**The test:** What will the reader do differently after reading this piece? If the answer is "nothing," the piece has failed Futureyou.

**The failure:** A piece that ends with "in conclusion, power manipulates records" — the reader already knew this. They have not been changed.

**The standard:** *"The truth does not defend itself. That is your job."* — the reader who finishes the series cannot unread this. They have been given a responsibility.

---

### 6. Unlearn

The piece must identify and challenge at least one assumption the reader holds before they begin.

**What it means:**
- Every piece should contain at least one moment where the reader thinks: "I did not know that" or "I had not thought of it that way."
- The assumption being challenged should be one the reader did not know they held.
- Unlearn is not about being contrarian. It is about being precise enough to reveal that the common understanding is incomplete.
- The challenge should come through evidence, not through assertion. The reader should feel they have discovered the correction themselves.

**The test:** What does the reader believe about this subject that is wrong or incomplete? Does the piece address it? If the piece only confirms what the reader already knows, it has failed Unlearn.

**The failure:** A piece about propaganda that tells the reader "propaganda is bad" — the reader already knows this. They have not unlearned anything.

**The standard:** *"The photograph appears to have no commissioner. That is what makes it different from everything that came before it."* — the reader who thought photographs were objective records has just had that assumption challenged before they knew they held it.

---

### 7. 80/20

Twenty percent of the effort produces eighty percent of the impact. Identify that twenty percent and put all the weight there.

**What it means:**
- Not every section of a piece is equally important. Identify the one or two moments that carry the argument — the scene, the turn, the closing — and spend disproportionate craft on them.
- The remaining eighty percent should be functional, not ornamental. It should move the reader from one high-impact moment to the next without calling attention to itself.
- 80/20 applies to research as well. Twenty percent of the research material will yield eighty percent of the argument. Find that twenty percent. Do not include the rest just because it was researched.
- 80/20 applies to revision. Twenty percent of the sentences will contain eighty percent of the problems. Find them. Fix them. Do not polish the sentences that are already working.

**The test:** If you could only keep one paragraph from the piece, which one would it be? Is that paragraph the best-written paragraph in the piece? If not, the weight is in the wrong place.

**The failure:** A piece where the opening is adequate, the middle is thorough, and the closing is weak — the weight is in the wrong place. The closing is the highest-impact moment. It should receive the most craft.

**The standard:** *"She will not remember making it."* — six words. The entire weight of Part 9's opening rests on them. The sentence is the twenty percent that does eighty percent of the work.

---

### 8. Socrates

The piece should proceed by question, not by assertion. The reader should feel they are discovering the argument rather than being told it.

**What it means:**
- The structure of the piece should follow the logic of inquiry: here is a question, here is evidence that bears on it, here is what the evidence suggests, here is a deeper question that opens.
- The writer's voice is not the voice of someone who has all the answers. It is the voice of someone who has thought carefully and is now thinking aloud.
- The closing question is not a device. It is the logical endpoint of the inquiry — the question that the evidence raises but cannot resolve.
- Socrates does not mean writing in questions. It means writing in the spirit of inquiry — letting the evidence lead, rather than leading the evidence.

**The test:** Does the piece feel like it is discovering its argument, or does it feel like it is delivering a pre-packaged conclusion? If the reader can predict the conclusion from the first paragraph, the piece has failed Socrates.

**The failure:** A piece that opens with the thesis statement and then spends the rest proving it — the reader has nothing to discover. They are watching a conclusion being justified, not an argument being made.

**The standard:** *"The photograph appears to have no commissioner. That is what makes it different from everything that came before it."* — the reader does not yet know where this is going. They are in the inquiry, not in the conclusion.

## THE STANDARD IN PRACTICE

These eight standards are not applied sequentially. They are applied simultaneously. Every sentence must pass all eight tests.

A sentence that is true (Truthmode) but unreadable (Hormozi) fails.
A sentence that is readable (Hormozi) but assumes prior knowledge (/Human) fails.
A sentence that challenges an assumption (Unlearn) but cannot be defended (Truthmode) fails.
A sentence that is well-crafted (80/20) but leaves the reader unchanged (Futureyou) fails.

The standard is not eight separate standards. It is one standard with eight dimensions.

```
Redteam      — tested against counter-arguments
Hormozi      — every sentence earns its place
Socrates     — proceeds by inquiry, not assertion
Lindymode    — built to last beyond the moment
```

## LINDYMODE — THE NINTH STANDARD

Lindymode is the standard that governs the others. It is the meta-standard.

**What it means:**
- The piece should be worth reading in ten years.
- This does not mean "timeless" in the sense of avoiding contemporary references. It means the argument should not depend on the reader sharing the writer's temporal context. A piece about Facebook's 2020 algorithm research is about Facebook in 2020. But the argument — that engagement optimisation produces the same structural effects as state propaganda — should be legible and relevant in 2030, 2040, and beyond.
- Lindymode rejects the assumption that newer is truer. An idea from 1934 (socialist realism as doctrine) may be more relevant to understanding 2026 than an idea from 2025.
- Lindymode writes for the reader who will encounter the piece out of its original context — five years from now, in a different country, in a different political climate. Will it still make sense? Will it still matter?

**The test:** If this piece were discovered in a decade, would it still be worth reading? If the answer depends on the reader remembering what was happening in the news when it was published, the piece has failed Lindymode.

**The standard:** *"The handprint on the cave wall is thirty thousand years old. Every other record from that time — the songs, the stories, the names — is gone."* — this sentence will be true and powerful in any decade.

# FATHERING WITHOUT FEAR

## A Memoir of Faith, Fatherhood, and Inheritance

### Abraham of London

## Core Proposition

Three people died telling Abraham of London to live.

His twin sister Jumoke appeared to him on the day of her death and made him promise to fulfil a destiny he was too young to understand. His brother David, the companion who stood beside him through childhood, school, university, and every private crossing of his life, appeared to him in a trance the night before his death. His sister Funke died two days before divorce proceedings began, leaving behind a grief he still carries as debt.

Each death came with a different wound. Each wound carried the same command:

**Do not disappear.**

*Fathering Without Fear* is the story of a man born under ancestral expectation, preserved by faith, marked by death, and forced by fatherhood to confront the oldest question of his life: whether he would vanish under pressure or stand.

This is not a misery memoir.
It is not a legal complaint.
It is not a parenting book.

It is a literary memoir about inheritance under siege — and one father's refusal to let fear become the final author of his life.

## The Book

Abraham is born in Lagos as one of triplets, into a family shaped by Yoruba spiritual memory, ancestral authority, Christian encounter, political sacrifice, and the belief that certain children arrive carrying unfinished business. Rejected at birth by a grandmother who saw in him a troubling spiritual resemblance, he grows into a boy who speaks too early, questions too deeply, survives too strangely, and is repeatedly forced to interpret life through events that ordinary language cannot fully hold.

At eleven, after the death of his twin sister Jumoke, he experiences a near-death vision and bargains with God for his life. He wakes into a literal fire, saves his sisters, and carries forward a vow: if God gives him seventy-five years, he will serve Him.

Years later, in London, that vow is tested not in a pulpit, but in court corridors, immigration limbo, fatherhood under suspicion, marriage collapse, social isolation, and the slow bureaucratic violence of being reduced from a man to a file.

The son he loves becomes the future he must defend.

The court record says one thing.
Family memory says another.
Faith says something deeper.

This book exists where those records collide.

## Governing Thesis

The central thesis of *Fathering Without Fear* is simple:

**Fatherhood is the battlefield, but inheritance is the war.**

The story is not merely about one father fighting to remain present. It is about what happens when a man spends his whole life being told that he carries a purpose, and every major event in his life attempts to prove the opposite.

The book asks:

- What does a man inherit before he understands himself?
- What do the dead demand from the living?
- What does faith mean when God preserves your life but not your comfort?
- What does a father owe his child when disappearance would be easier?
- What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying the people still inside the story?

## Positioning

*Fathering Without Fear* occupies a rare literary space:

**A West African spiritual inheritance narrative collides with the British family court system.**

The book combines:

- Yoruba ancestral memory;
- Christian conversion and biblical faith;
- near-death experience;
- political inheritance;
- immigrant manhood;
- institutional failure;
- family court struggle;
- fatherhood under erasure;
- grief as command;
- survival as vocation.

Its closest commercial relatives are *Educated*, *The Glass Castle*, *When Breath Becomes Air*, and *The Return* — but none occupy this precise shelf.

This is its white space:

**A man raised inside African spiritual memory and Christian destiny fights, in modern Britain, to remain father to the child who gives that destiny its final meaning.**

## Structural Architecture

The book is built in four movements, using interwoven timelines.

The ancestral timeline explains the man.
The present timeline tests him.
The theological thread interprets both.

The structure deliberately avoids a flat "I was born, then this happened" memoir. The reader enters through pressure, mystery, and consequence before being taken backward into the forces that made Abraham the kind of man who could not disappear.

## PART ONE — THE CHILD WHO WAS NAMED BEFORE HE UNDERSTOOD

This section opens in the present pressure of fatherhood and then descends into the ancestral world that shaped Abraham before he could name himself.

### 1. Something Always Happens

A present-day courtroom or contact-centre scene opens the book mid-action. The reader meets Abraham not as a child, not as a victim, but as a father under pressure. The sentence that governs the memoir emerges immediately: something always happens.

### 2. Isua to Agege

Iyalode and her husband walk from Osun to Lagos. The family line begins not with comfort, but with migration, labour, risk, and spiritual authority. The husband is a tailor, farmer, builder of men, and the first major figure in the family's pattern of sacrifice.

### 3. The Boy Who Was Already Old

Abraham is born as one of triplets. One child is lost early. He is rejected by Iyalode because of what she believes he represents spiritually. Yet the rejected child becomes the one who asks questions no child should yet have language for.

### 4. A Hearing Date

The present-day legal machinery enters. The court language is cool, procedural, and bloodless. A father's love is translated into filings, waiting rooms, orders, reports, delays, and managed access.

### 5. Jumoke

Jumoke is established first as a living child, not a symbol. Only after her personhood is felt does the book reveal her death, her appearance in the kitchen, and the sentence that changes Abraham's life: she died so he could live to fulfil his destiny.

## PART TWO — THE BARGAIN WITH GOD

This section establishes the spiritual contract of the memoir: Abraham's life is not merely extended; it is obligated.

### 6. The Fire and the Deal

In a dream, Abraham dies and understands himself to be in hell. He bargains with God for seventy-five years of life in service. He wakes into an actual fire, saves his sisters, and carries the vow forward as the central covenant of his life.

### 7. The Name That Had to Be Powerful

Iyalode is the first person to speak the name of Jesus to him with unmistakable weight. The same woman who rejected him becomes the first witness to the power that will later govern him.

### 8. The Spiritual Covering

Iyalode is rendered in full contradiction: beautiful, commanding, spiritually feared, self-directed, absent, powerful, and eventually Christian. She is not simplified into saint or villain. She is inheritance embodied.

### 9. The Boy Who Became a Father at Fifteen

Abraham's father, David senior, is abandoned into responsibility at fifteen and raises his younger siblings. His own dead father appears to guide him, promising provision that arrives when needed. This chapter establishes the paternal pattern Abraham will later inherit.

### 10. Leaving Lagos

August 2017. Abraham leaves Nigeria carrying grief, ambition, uncertainty, and the illusion that relocation will give him a cleaner future. Instead, the old story follows him across borders.

## PART THREE — LOVE, DEATH, AND COLLATERAL DAMAGE

This section shows how romance, spiritual warning, misplaced confidence, bereavement, marriage collapse, and institutional conflict converge.

### 11. A Post on Facebook

A small post becomes the doorway to a relationship that appears providential at first. Later, Abraham recognises mirroring, speed, emotional exhaustion, and the danger of being seen at the exact moment one is most vulnerable.

### 12. Married by December

Four months after arriving in London, Abraham is married. The chapter is not written as stupidity or romance alone, but as speed: the frightening velocity with which an entire future can be rearranged before a man realises what he has surrendered.

### 13. Stephanie

The national service romance introduces spiritual danger, desire, prophecy, and youthful misreading. Stephanie's journal, the spirit-husband disclosure, and the warnings that follow become part of Abraham's education in forces he once underestimated.

### 14. David Was Missing From the Wedding

David is not treated as a cousin or footnote, but as brother. His absence from a wedding in a trance becomes the warning. His death becomes the confirmation. The chapter is about brotherhood before it is about loss.

### 15. Funke

Funke's death two days before divorce proceedings begins becomes one of the book's deepest wounds. Abraham could not pay the medical bill. The chapter must carry guilt without melodrama and grief without performance.

### 16. Pregnant and Discarded

The collapse of the marriage is handled through consequence, not accusation. The chapter shows what changed, what it cost, what was documented, what Abraham understood then, and what he understands now. It does not speculate where the record cannot safely go.

## PART FOUR — THE FATHER WHO REFUSES TO DISAPPEAR

This final movement brings inheritance, faith, law, grief, and fatherhood into one confrontation.

### 17. What the System Sees

The family court system is presented not as cartoon villainy, but as machinery. It sees risk, files, history, allegations, procedure, reports, and delay. It struggles to see love.

### 18. Seven Years

The legal battle is not summarised. It is accumulated through specific hearings, contact sessions, small cruelties, missed opportunities, bureaucratic contradictions, and moments where a father's continued presence becomes a radical act.

### 19. Damisi

This is the quiet heart of the book. Damisi is not a legal object or symbolic child. He is rendered as a living boy: his words, gestures, humour, questions, tenderness, and the ordinary details that make the fight morally unavoidable.

### 20. Love Does Not Fear

The theological synthesis. Abraham returns to 1 John 4:18 and finally distinguishes fearlessness from recklessness. The verse becomes not decoration, but interpretation: love does not remove danger; it refuses to be governed by punishment.

### 21. Devotion

David senior's death becomes an emotional summit, beginning in Norwich with the strange waking feeling and the call that confirmed it, before arriving at the scene itself. Born Monday, 12 September 1932; died Monday, 15 August 2017. A man shaped by politics, sacrifice, corruption battles, loss, faith, and heartbreak dies during family devotion, calling on Iyalode and Jesus in the same breath. The whole inheritance gathers here.

(Structural note: this is the only chapter in the book where the present and ancestral timelines are not interwoven but identical — the call in Norwich and the death itself are one continuous event, not two threads.)

### 22. The Father Who Refuses to Disappear

The book refuses a false resolution. It does not end because the battle is neatly won. It ends because the promise has been kept. Abraham remains. The father is still there. The story has not disappeared.

## Voice

The voice must be controlled fire.

Not rage.
Not self-pity.
Not legal pleading.
Not spiritual exhibitionism.
Not revenge.

The voice is that of a man testifying under oath before God, history, his son, and himself.

Every sentence must earn belief through precision.

The supernatural material is narrated plainly, without apology and without inflation. The legal material is narrated through scene and consequence, not procedural overload. The family material is written with tenderness and restraint. The theological material is embodied before it is explained.

## Craft Rules

### 1. Consequence, not accusation

Where legal or relational risk exists, the manuscript shows consequence rather than assigning hidden motives.

### 2. The dead must live before they die

Jumoke, David, Funke, Iyalode, and David senior must each be rendered with ordinary human detail before death or symbolism enters.

### 3. No abstract openings

Every chapter must open in a scene, object, sentence, place, date, gesture, or remembered physical fact.

### 4. Faith must be carried by story

The book may be unapologetically Christian, but theology must emerge through lived cost, not sermonising.

### 5. The son must remain protected

Damisi is written with love, dignity, and privacy. He is not used as evidence. He is honoured as a child.

### 6. The narrator must remain self-accountable

Abraham's blind spots, naivety, misplaced confidence, and delayed understanding are part of the book's authority. The memoir becomes trustworthy because the narrator does not exempt himself from judgment.

## Final Positioning Statement

*Fathering Without Fear* is a literary memoir about a man born under ancestral expectation, preserved by faith, marked by death, and forced by fatherhood to confront the oldest command of his life: do not disappear.

Across Lagos, Norwich, London, family memory, spiritual warning, courtroom language, immigration pressure, and the fragile tenderness of father and son, Abraham of London tells the story not as a victim pleading to be believed, but as a witness reclaiming the record.

This is the story beneath the story.

The one the court could not hold.
The one grief refused to bury.
The one a son will one day need to know.

## Final Editorial Standard

This book reaches its full power when it stops asking the reader to care about what happened and compels the reader to confront what it means.

Not:

**A father fought the system.**

But:

**A man spent his whole life being told not to disappear — and fatherhood became the place where that command was finally tested.**

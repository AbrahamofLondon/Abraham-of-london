SOURCE MATERIAL — WORKING DRAFT — NOVEL ARCHITECTURE

This file is the world and structure beneath the published teaser. It is
not the teaser itself, and it is not the memoir. It governs an allegorical
novel built from the Stephanie material and the marriage material,
deliberately merged into one figure, rendered in a liminal register where
the real and the symbolic are never cleanly separable.

================================================================
GOVERNING PREMISE
================================================================

A novel cannot tell you a true thing happened. It can only show you a
true thing's shape, and trust the reader to recognise the shape when they
meet it in their own life. This is the book's entire justification, and
its only real protection — not secrecy, not deniability, but the honest,
ancient contract of allegory: I am not telling you what happened. I am
telling you what it was like to be inside it.

================================================================
THE WORLD
================================================================

Liminal register, deliberate and structural, not decorative.

— Place-names are real-adjacent but never exact. A coastal town that
  could be Lagos, could be somewhere that does not exist. A grey northern
  city that could be Norwich, could be any grey city a man goes to in
  order to disappear. The reader should feel the almost-recognition of a
  real geography without ever being able to confirm it.

— Time behaves the way it behaves in grief and dream, not the way it
  behaves in records. A scene's length is governed by its spiritual
  weight, not its clock-time duration. Trance sequences and "real"
  sequences should, by the novel's second half, become genuinely harder
  for the reader to separate — this is not confusion for its own sake,
  it is the book's central formal argument: he can no longer cleanly
  tell which register he is in, and neither can the reader.

— The supernatural is never explained, footnoted, or rationalised. It
  has its own internal logic, stated plainly. No character ever asks
  "was that real?" — the question is structurally forbidden. Asking it
  would collapse the liminal register into a lesser, more conventional
  book.

================================================================
THE CENTRAL FEMALE FIGURE
================================================================

Working name: ADAEZE (placeholder, open to revision — Igbo, "princess /
daughter of the king," chosen to carry royal and prophetic weight without
drawing from any real name in the source material).

She is a deliberate merge: Stephanie's spiritual material (the journal
written before they ever met, the spirit-husband disclosure, a dedication
that predates the relationship entirely) fused with the documented
pattern of the later marriage (the speed of commitment, the isolation
that followed, the reversal once a child was conceived).

Craft rule, non-negotiable: she is never written as a villain in any
conventional sense. Real spiritual danger rarely arrives as villainy — it
arrives partly true, partly broken, partly weaponised by something the
person carrying it may not have fully chosen and may not fully
understand. The reader should finish the book unable to hate her
cleanly. Every scene of harm she causes must sit near — earlier or later,
not necessarily adjacent — a scene of genuine vulnerability or genuine
gift. The journal-before-they-met material is written as a gift in this
novel, not merely a warning sign visible only in retrospect. This duality
must be sustained for the entire book, including in the ending.

================================================================
THE THREE FORCES
================================================================

Distinct manifestations, in tension with each other — not one antagonist
wearing three masks. None can be defeated in any conventional sense.

— THE SPIRIT HUSBAND
  A presence, not a person. Older than the relationship, claiming prior
  right. Jealous in the Old Testament sense, not the romantic sense.
  Function: CLAIM. The force that insists she was never fully available
  to be loved by a living man, because something else arrived first.
  Patient, territorial, rarely speaks. Usually felt as absence — cold
  rooms, a particular silence, her sudden unreachability at specific
  hours.

— THE VOICE
  Speaks once, decisively, with explicit threat. Function: INTIMIDATION.
  Direct, immediate, transactional — names a cost and dares the
  protagonist to test it. Should appear rarely across the whole novel —
  once, perhaps twice — so its rarity preserves its weight. Overuse turns
  a genuine threat into a device.

— THE TRANCE
  Not an antagonist. A messenger-force, morally neutral or even
  protective, delivering true information through means the waking world
  cannot verify. Function: WARNING ARRIVING TOO LATE TO PREVENT, IN TIME
  TO CONFIRM. This is the force that shows an absence before a death is
  known. It never threatens. It only shows. Its horror is entirely in
  its accuracy, never in malice.

The tension between the three forces is the novel's actual engine: a man
caught between a force that claims her, a force that threatens him, and a
force that only ever tells him true things after it is too late to act.
None can be fought. The novel's resolution is not about defeating them —
it is about what it means to keep walking inside a world governed by
forces that cannot be fought, and can only, eventually, stop being
bargained with.

================================================================
THE PROTAGONIST — DELIBERATELY UNNAMED
================================================================

He is not Abraham with the names changed — that would collapse the
allegory into roman à clef and forfeit everything the liminal register
exists to protect. He is also not invented from nothing, severed from
the emotional truth beneath him. His naming, when it happens, should
follow the same instinct that gave Iyalode her title rather than her
name in the memoir — something that carries function and weight rather
than biography.

Open question, to be resolved once more of the novel exists: which of
his qualities does the book need to foreground? Candidates include the
fearlessness born of early near-death certainty, an inherited
people-pleasing pattern, a promise made to a dead sibling, or a quality
the novel invents fresh that he does not share with his real-world
counterpart at all. Do not resolve this prematurely — let the opening
chapters reveal which quality the world actually needs from him.

================================================================
STRUCTURAL POSTURE — CONFIRMED
================================================================

OPENING: In medias res. The novel opens with the relationship already
underway and the Spirit Husband's presence already established as fact
in the world — not introduced, not explained, simply present, the way
the protagonist himself experienced it: no innocent beginning to look
back on, only a current he is already inside when the reader arrives.

ENDING: Earned, not reported — and deliberately NOT a resolution. The
ending complicates rather than resolves, sustaining the liminal register
to the final page rather than collapsing it into conventional closure.

Confirmed ending shape:

1. THE THREE FORCES SWAP ROLES, THEY DO NOT RESOLVE.
   By the novel's end, the Voice has gone silent — its threat was never
   defeated, it simply stops needing to speak. The Trance, once a
   warning about her, becomes active again at the very end — except now
   it is warning him about something or someone new, already beginning,
   deliberately unspecified. The Spirit Husband's claim is never broken
   or honoured; it simply stops being the loudest thing in the room — the
   way grief does not end, it stops being the loudest sound in a life
   that has grown louder around it.

2. ADAEZE'S UNFINISHED BUSINESS RHYMES WITH HIS — IT DOES NOT EXPLAIN
   HIM, AND IT IS NEVER EXPLAINED ITSELF.
   The novel must never supply a tidy backstory for what she carries —
   doing so would cheapen both her mystery and his arc. Instead: one
   image, near the end, suggesting she is also walking toward something
   unresolved, entirely separate from him, that the novel will never
   show the reader. The reader should leave the book wondering about her
   life exactly the way a person in this position often keeps wondering,
   permanently, about someone they once loved.

3. THE FINAL SCENE IS HIS ALONE.
   Not a goodbye scene with Adaeze — that would still structurally
   centre her as the thing being resolved. Instead: the protagonist
   alone, doing something small and ordinary, with all three forces
   present in the room with him, unresolved, no longer organising his
   whole life around them. Not victory. Something closer to what real
   spiritual weight becomes over enough time: not defeated, just no
   longer the only thing in the room.

================================================================
WHAT REMAINS OPEN
================================================================

— The protagonist's name and his foregrounded quality (see above).
— Full chapter architecture — not yet built. Should not be attempted
  until the protagonist question is resolved, since chapter structure
  will likely follow his arc more than Adaeze's.
— Whether the three forces each get a structural "home" in the book
  (e.g., the Spirit Husband governs the domestic scenes, the Voice
  governs a single mid-book turning point, the Trance governs the
  novel's relationship to its own ending) — worth deciding once chapter
  architecture begins.
— Title of the novel itself within the fiction (currently using the
  working publishing title "The Fiction Adaptation," which may end up
  functioning more as a frame/meta-title than the in-world title of the
  story itself — worth revisiting once the world is fully built).

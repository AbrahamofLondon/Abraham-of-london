# Ch.3 Source Recall — The Boy Who Was Already Old

## What the chapter currently has

The chapter now opens on Abraham's behavioral quality as a child — spoke too early, questions that
silenced adults, sitting where others moved, watching as adults watch. It then carries Iyalode's
doubt (wrong child for the name), David Senior's recognition (his father returned), the triplet
context, the name in daily use, and the closing sentence.

The governing question the chapter holds: **What happens to a child when people begin reading age,
burden, and destiny into him before he can understand himself?**

## What is still missing

One specific physical memory-anchor. The behavioral rendering (sitting still, waiting, watching,
questions that made adults careful) is confirmed and in the chapter. But it floats without a scene.

The chapter needs one moment that carries the quality in a specific place.

---

## Recall questions — answer any that have a real memory behind them

**The quality itself:**

1. What is the earliest memory you have of an adult treating you as older than you were? Not the interpretation — the specific moment. What were you doing? Where were you? What did the adult do or say?

2. Was there ever a moment as a child when you asked something and an adult went quiet — not because you were rude, but because the question was unexpected? What was the question? What did the adult do afterward?

3. Did you ever feel the weight of David Senior's expectation on you as a child — not just that he loved you, but that he was watching you for something specific? Was there a moment, an exchange, a look that you remember?

**Iyalode's doubt made visible:**

4. Did you ever witness Iyalode and David Senior in the same room in a way that made their different readings of you felt to you as a child — even without knowing what the readings were? A meal, a gathering, a visit, a moment? What were you doing? What did they do?

5. Did anyone ever say anything to you directly about the name — about being named after the founding ancestor, about the weight of it, about what was expected? What was the setting? What exactly was said?

**The household and the name:**

6. What did the house feel like where you grew up in Lagos? Not description for its own sake — one specific detail: the floor, a smell, a sound, the light at a particular time of day, something you saw from a particular window or door.

7. When David Senior called you "Abraham" — was there something particular in how he said it compared to how others said it? Not abstract feeling — a specific instance, a specific tone, a room.

**Yemisi:**

8. What do you remember of Yemisi from those early years? Not the full portrait — one specific ordinary image. Where was she? What was she doing? What does the image carry?

**What you misunderstood:**

9. Was there something about David Senior's attention on you that confused you as a child — something you could feel but couldn't account for, something you interpreted wrongly until later? What did you think it was at the time?

10. When did you first understand that you had been named into an expectation — not just given a name, but given a mandate? Where were you? How old? What made you understand it?

---

## What this recall will unlock

If any of the above yields a specific physical memory — a room, a person's face, a sound, a sentence,
an object, a moment of being watched — it becomes the chapter's opening image.

The treatment requires: *a moment of the child already in excess of what is expected — a specific
word used too early, a question that silences adults, something small and particular that marks him
as different not through tragedy but through a gravity that precedes understanding.*

That specific moment is what the chapter still needs. Everything else is in place.

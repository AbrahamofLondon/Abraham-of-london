# Chapter 6 Candidate — A Year Without Home Source Session

**Date captured:** 2026-06-24
**Purpose:** Source capture for a proposed missing early-formation chapter after Ch.5, "Jumoke," and before the current Ch.6, "The Fire and the Deal."
**Status:** ACCEPTED FOR STRUCTURAL TESTING — SOURCE CAPTURE ONLY

---

## Structural Verdict

`ACCEPT — MISSING EARLY-FORMATION CHAPTER`

This source supplies an early-formation movement that the current manuscript undercarries. The current Ch.19 contains the later wound in summary form:

> A few months after Jumoke died, he was sent to Aquinas. He did not return home for a year.

That sentence is structurally too important to remain only explanatory. The source explains how bereavement became separation, how separation became silent resistance, and how early disappearance and disappointed hope shaped later attachment, fatherhood, and refusal-to-disappear themes.

This chapter must not replace the current final chapter. Ch.23, "Final Room," remains the final chapter.

---

## Recommended Placement

Insert between:

- Ch.5 — `Jumoke`
- Ch.6 — `The Fire and the Deal`

If integrated, this chapter becomes the bridge between bereavement and spiritual/fire formation. Current Ch.6 onward would be renumbered later.

**Placement classification:** STRUCTURALLY STRONG

**Reason:** Ch.5 ends with the loss of Jumoke and the grief-protection echo. The new chapter shows the consequence: Abraham does not only lose his sister; he loses home. Ch.6 can then follow as the fire/covenant chapter with the reader already understanding that the child entering the fire has already been separated, silent, and internally displaced.

## Chapter Question

What happens to a child when grief is followed not by return, but by removal from home?

Secondary question:

What kind of father listens when a child's protest has already become behaviour?

---

## Governing Emotional Movement

After Jumoke, Abraham was sent away.

Away taught him a form of silence.

He resisted without collapsing.

His father listened and gave him choice.

He chose hope.

The people he hoped to find were gone.

## Confirmed Source Facts

### F1 — Age and Timing

Abraham was around 12 or 13 in 1990.

After Jumoke's death, he left home.

He spent a whole year without seeing his family.

He then spent additional years in Akure before returning to Lagos.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE

**Use note:** The emotional fact is the year without family. Do not make the chronology more precise than the source supports unless dates are later confirmed.

---

### F2 — First Akure School

The first Akure school was St Thomas Aquinas College, Akure.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE

**Use note:** School may be named if author confirms public-use comfort. Otherwise "Aquinas in Akure" is already present in the manuscript and appears safe.

---

### F3 — Mary's House

Abraham initially stayed with his older cousin Mary.

Mary had lived with Abraham's parents in her younger years, as one of the people who passed through their home.

Mary had no malice toward Abraham.

Abraham was not domesticated.

Mary tried to toughen him by making him do household chores and farm work.

She treated him the same way she treated her only son, Matthew.

Matthew later died a few years after Abraham left them.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE WITH RESTRAINT

**Privacy note:** Mary and Matthew are private family individuals unless public-name use is confirmed. Default prose should use "his older cousin" and "her son" unless author approves names.

**Craft note:** Render Mary fairly. This is displacement, not cruelty. Do not make Mary a villain. Do not turn Matthew's later death into a subplot unless the author explicitly requests it.

---

### F4 — Faith Turning Point

In Akure, Abraham devoted his life to Jesus as a teenager in April 1990.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE WITH RESTRAINT

**Use note:** Important but not the chapter's centre. Treat as a remembered turning point inside loneliness, not as triumphant conversion testimony. Do not make this a sermon.

---

### F5 — Refusal to Attend Class

Abraham refused to attend class for almost a whole year.

Physically, he went out with other absconding students and roamed the streets during school hours.

Despite this, he still passed normal school tests and examinations.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE

**Craft note:** Do not make the refusal comic or delinquent. This is a child saying no with his feet because he cannot yet explain his grief, separation, and displacement.

---

### F6 — Teacher Intervention

His maths teacher, Mr Adeniranye, noticed and summoned his parents.

David Senior had previously visited the school and placed Abraham in the care of the maths and English teachers because those were core subjects.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE WITH RESTRAINT

**Privacy note:** Use "his maths teacher" unless author approves public use of Mr Adeniranye's name. The teacher is not an antagonist; he noticed and acted.

---

### F7 — David Senior Listens

David Senior asked Abraham what the problem was.

Abraham said he was tired of living in Akure.

David Senior's tone was not anger.

He trusted Abraham.

His tone was one of resignation to Abraham's decision, trusting him as usual.

David Senior told Abraham to choose any school he wanted.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE

**Structural note:** This is crucial for Ch.22 consistency. David Senior must not be rendered as neglectful or indifferent. He had placed Abraham in trusted care, saw the arrangement failing, listened, and gave Abraham choice.

---

### F8 — School Chosen in Hope

Abraham chose Adebayo Mokuolu College, Ogba, Lagos.

He chose it because it was the school attended by his childhood friends.

He hoped to join them there.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE WITH RESTRAINT

**Use note:** The important fact is that the school choice was an act of hope: return, friendship, continuity.

---

### F9 — The Friends Were Gone

The friends did not return from their holiday in America.

The Omolades had returned to America permanently without saying goodbye.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE WITH RESTRAINT / NAME-SENSITIVE

**Privacy note:** The Omolades are living/private individuals unless author confirms otherwise. Default prose should not name the family. Use one of:

- "the boys from America"
- "his childhood friends"
- "the family whose children he had hoped to join"

**Craft note:** The emotional fact matters more than the surname. Do not over-describe them.

## Later Akure / Lagos Continuation Source

Capture for future use only. Use selectively, and likely not in this chapter unless the chapter's structure later requires a short bridge.

### F10 — Running Away and Later Care Arrangements

Abraham eventually ran away from Akure to Lagos by himself in 1992.

He was persuaded to return.

David Senior arranged for him to live on campus with the Introductory Technology teacher, Mr Frank Charles.

That arrangement was not a good fit.

A kind bank manager, Mr Ade Ikuesan, through whom David Senior had been sending Abraham extra money to ease his suffering, took him in.

Mr Ade Ikuesan was extremely kind and generous to Abraham.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE WITH RESTRAINT / NAME-SENSITIVE

**Use note:** Do not use Mr Frank Charles or Mr Ade Ikuesan in the proposed chapter unless the chapter expands into the later 1992 return. Default: capture only.

---

### F11 — Academic Recalibration and University Path

When Abraham returned to Lagos, he deliberately repeated a class to recalibrate himself.

He passed his SSCE quite well.

He struggled for years to gain admission for Medicine and Surgery, the course David Senior wanted.

The family had a long line of healers.

Abraham eventually accepted Microbiology at the University of Lagos.

He was later offered Medicine and Surgery at Lagos State University, but by 1998 Abraham did not consider LASU the same standard as Unilag.

This is how his journey at Unilag began.

This explains why David was ahead of him and why David later left for London just as Abraham joined Unilag.

**Classification:** PUBLIC-SAFE WITH RESTRAINT

**Use note:** This is not the chapter's centre. Do not turn the chapter into a school chronology, medical-admission chapter, or Unilag origin story.

## Recommended Chapter Shape

### Movement 1 — After Jumoke, Away

Open shortly after Jumoke's death. Do not re-explain Ch.5.

Begin with consequence:

- Abraham left home.
- He did not see his family for a year.
- Akure became the place where childhood continued without home.

### Movement 2 — Mary's House

Render Mary fairly:

- no malice;
- trying to toughen him;
- chores and farm work;
- Abraham was not domesticated;
- Matthew beside him as her own son.

Show displacement without blame.

### Movement 3 — Aquinas and Silent Refusal

Show refusal physically:

- school hours;
- other absconding students;
- streets;
- still passing tests.

This is silent protest, not laziness.

### Movement 4 — Mr Adeniranye and David Senior

Teacher notices. Parents are summoned.

David Senior asks what is wrong.

Abraham says he is tired of living in Akure.

David Senior trusts him and lets him choose.

### Movement 5 — The Chosen School

Abraham chooses Adebayo Mokuolu College because of childhood friends.

He thinks he is choosing return, friendship, continuity.

The friends are gone.

The family whose children he had hoped to join had returned to America permanently without saying goodbye.

### Movement 6 — Optional Small Bridge

Only if structurally needed:

- this was not the last time someone would leave before Abraham was ready;
- no heavy foreshadowing;
- do not explain Ch.19;
- do not announce the theme.

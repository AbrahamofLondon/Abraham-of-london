# Ch.15 Source Recall — Funke

## What the chapter currently has

All confirmed facts are in the chapter:

- "Small mummy" self-title and the role she assigned herself
- Breadwinner: husband living with glaucoma for eleven years, she carried the household
- She warned Abraham directly: she had already decided the cost, she was "happy with the bargain"
- Doctor's visit with Rachael; hole-in-the-heart finding; instruction to stop working
- She went back to work
- She collapsed alone two days later
- The week already carried a substantial unnamed financial loss
- Mid-call prayer break during a business call
- Bayo's call: Funke was dead
- Abraham could not pay the medical bill
- Divorce papers served two days after her death
- The closing: "She had not stopped. She was gone."

The governing question the chapter holds: **What does it mean to lose the person who had quietly held the family's ordinary weight?**

## What is still missing

One ordinary scene with Funke alive — something she did, wore, carried, cooked, corrected, said, or refused. A physical moment in a specific place.

The chapter currently tells us what she was (breadwinner, fighter, loyal, small mummy) without showing her being it. The reader knows the facts of her life. They do not yet see her.

---

## Recall questions — answer any that have a real memory behind them

**Funke as person:**

1. What is the first image that comes when you think of Funke? Not what she meant to you — what do you see? Where is she? What is she doing?

2. What did she look like? Not a full description — one specific physical detail: her hands, her face, something she wore regularly, a gesture she made when she was making a point.

3. What did she sound like? Not her character — her voice. Was it quiet or loud? Did she use a specific phrase? Was there something she said often?

4. What did she do for work? Where did she go? What did she carry? What time did she leave in the morning?

**Ordinary moments:**

5. Was there a specific time she came to help Abraham? A visit, a phone call, something she brought, something she said or did? What happened? Where were you? What did she say?

6. Was there a moment where you saw her managing her own household — feeding her sons, managing her husband's condition, doing something ordinary that showed what her life cost her? What did you see?

7. Was there something she cooked, something she gave you, something you ate or drank with her? One specific instance.

8. Was there a moment when she made you laugh, or when she said something that surprised you? What was it?

**"Small mummy" in practice:**

9. Can you remember a specific time when she intervened — when she acted in the way she had assigned herself to act? What was happening? What did she do or say? Where were you?

10. When she told you she was "happy with the bargain" — do you remember where that conversation happened? What were you both doing? What exactly did she say? What did you say?

**Her relationship with the household:**

11. Did you ever see her with her husband or her sons in a way that showed the weight she was carrying? Not a crisis — something ordinary. A meal, a morning, a doorway.

12. What changed in the day-to-day after she died? Not what was lost in the large sense — what was different in an ordinary way? Something that had happened before that no longer happened. Something she had done that nobody else did.

**The last time:**

13. When did you last see Funke? What were you doing? What was she doing? Did you speak? What was said? What did she look like?

14. When did you last speak with her? By phone? In person? What was the conversation? Was there anything you did not say?

**What must not be said:**

15. Is there anything about Funke's situation — her husband's condition, her finances, her work, her relationship with the family — that would become legal argument if written? What must stay out of the chapter?

16. Is there anything about the timing of her death and the divorce filing that must not be stated directly — something that is true but would be read as accusation?

---

## What this recall will unlock

Any of the above that yields a physical image — a room, a gesture, a sentence, an object she was holding, something she left behind — becomes the chapter's ordinary scene.

The chapter does not need a long scene. It needs one moment where Funke is doing something ordinary and the reader can see her doing it. That is what allows her death to land as a loss rather than a fact.

---

## What NOT to supply

- Do not supply anything that would turn the chapter into legal argument or proof of hardship.
- Do not supply a full biographical account — the chapter is a grief hinge, not a portrait.
- Do not supply theological framing around her death — the chapter ends on loss, not meaning.
- Do not supply anything that makes her death an explanation for what followed — the divorce filing appears in the chapter but is not the chapter's thesis.
- Do not supply anything that positions Funke as a martyr — the chapter's restraint is its force.

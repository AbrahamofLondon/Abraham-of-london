# Chapter 2 — Isua to Agege

The road between Isua and Lagos is shorter now than it was. That is not the interesting thing about it.

The interesting thing is that there was a man who walked some version of it — the roads were routes then, paths worn into the earth by people who had gone before — and he walked toward something he had not named. He would not arrive at what he thought he was going toward. He would arrive at something else. This is usually how it goes with the journeys that matter.

Isua is in Ondo. The Adaramola family were of the kingmaker lineage there — not royalty, but the kind of family whose standing in the question of royalty had historically mattered. This is family memory, passed down with the seriousness such things carry when a family has held them long enough to stop needing to prove them. There are older stories too, oral ones, about connections to the court at Benin, to older authorities. Those stories belong to the category of oral inheritance: possible, significant to the people who carry them, not available for the kind of verification that external record provides. They are carried as what they are.

The man who began the movement was named Adarabioyo. He became Adaramola through the kind of administrative simplification that colonial record-keeping imposed on Yoruba names when the two systems met and one had the pen. The name he became means, roughly, the crown combines with wealth. He did not choose the simplification. He carried the result.

He was a tailor. That is the first trade recorded in family memory. He sewed for the British missionary who came through the region — the specific work of a craftsman meeting a stranger with requirements different from what the neighbourhood typically produced. In that proximity — cloth, needle, measurement, the particular demands of a man with a different faith and a different sense of what a garment should be — he became the first person in the Adaramola lineage to take Christianity seriously. Not to encounter it. To choose it. The family has always maintained this distinction. He was not converted in the passive sense. He decided. The faith that Iyalode would carry later, that David Senior would carry in his own register, that Abraham would carry through fire and courtroom and the long years of a contested fatherhood — it begins here, in a tailor's encounter with a missionary's world, in Isua, before the road was a road.

He was also a merchant. Kola nuts. Cocoa. He understood land — what it meant in that period to hold land, what it said about a family's capacity to carry its own future. He acquired it. He built. He understood, in the way of men who build things rather than possess them, that what you build outlasts you, that this is the point, and that this is also the risk, because what outlasts you must be carried by whoever comes next.

He died before the work was done.

This is the pattern. It will not be recognisable as a pattern for some time. From inside, it looks like: a man built, and was taken early, and what he left was handed forward to people who would carry it without always knowing what they were carrying or where it had come from.

Iyalode was from this lineage. She walked from Isua toward Lagos with her husband, and she carried what had been first held in this lineage by the founding ancestor: the faith, held now inside her also alongside the older spiritual knowledge, the two not in conflict in her, both real, both hers. Her husband built more. The movement continued. The founding ancestor had started something and handed it forward, and the handing forward continued through people who had not met him and would not meet him and carried what he had started regardless.

Abraham is not in this chapter. He is not born yet. But the name has already been given its shape. The faith has made its first appearance in the lineage. The work of becoming something — the cumulative work of Adarabioyo the tailor and merchant and landowner, of Iyalode and David Senior and everyone between them — has been started and is still being done.

The road between Isua and Lagos is shorter now. The inheritance is not.

# Chapter 4 — A Hearing Date

The screen did not look like a place where time could be taken.

It looked like every other Teams screen: a rectangle of light, a row of icons, a mute button, a camera button, a name appearing in the corner, the small pauses that belong to video calls when nobody is quite sure whether the next voice will come through clearly. There was no wood panel. No witness box. No raised bench visible from where Abraham sat. Just a digital room on the morning of 27 July 2023, and a father waiting to learn what would happen next.

The case was adjourned.

That was what they were told first.

Then they were told to wait to be called back.

There are kinds of waiting that can be explained to someone else, and kinds that cannot. Waiting for a bus is waiting. Waiting for a doctor is waiting. Waiting for a reply to an email is waiting. This was not like those. This was waiting while a child's time was somewhere inside the machinery, waiting while the next shape of fatherhood might be decided in a room Abraham could not enter, waiting while the screen remained ordinary and the meaning behind it did not.

He did not know how long the wait would be.

He knew only what he had been told: wait.

The room around him did not become dramatic because the moment mattered. That is one of the things people misunderstand about pressure. Often it arrives without a suitable setting. A man may be sitting at a table. A phone may be beside him. A screen may be open. The world may continue to make its usual small demands. The body still has to breathe. The hands still have to rest somewhere. The eyes still look at the same image, the same empty digital space, the same small signs that the institution has not forgotten him but has not yet called him back.

Ten minutes is not long until it is holding a child's future.

In those minutes, Abraham had no argument to make. No speech would reach the place where the direction of the case was being settled. No evidence could be placed on the table by force of will. No love could be converted into the category the institution needed in order to recognise it. He could only remain available. He could only keep the line open. He could only be there when the call came back.

This was one of the first lessons the process taught him: presence could be required and powerless at the same time.

If he disappeared, that would mean something.

If he remained, that might still not be enough.

So he remained.

The minutes did what minutes do when they are being counted by fear. They stretched. They repeated themselves. They made the ordinary visible. The screen. The silence. The small delay after every movement. The slight unreality of waiting inside technology for something as old as judgment. A father looking at a device, trying not to let his mind run faster than the facts.

He had learned by then that delay was not always a large thing. Sometimes it was a date moved. Sometimes it was a file passed. Sometimes it was a person who had not read enough history to understand why the next decision could not be treated as if nothing had happened before. Sometimes it was the loss of continuity, the need to begin again in front of someone new, the quiet cost of having to make the past legible one more time.

But in that moment, he did not have the full explanation. He had only the wait.

The call came back.

The original judge returned to sit.

That was the fact that changed the direction of the day. It did not arrive with music. It did not announce itself as mercy. It came through the same digital frame, through the same institutional language, through the same ordinary screen. Yet continuity mattered. Someone who understood the shape of the history was back in the place where that history could be held. What might have become another long detour did not become one that morning.

Abraham did not experience it as triumph.

Triumph would have been the wrong word. Nothing about that morning gave him back what had already been taken by time. Nothing made the process gentle. Nothing made the waiting harmless. The return of continuity did not turn machinery into love. It simply meant that, for that day, the machinery did not send him all the way back to the beginning again.

Sometimes that is what relief looks like inside an institution: not restoration, not vindication, not a clean answer, but the prevention of another loss.

The screen remained a screen.

The case remained a case.

His son remained the reason.

That was the separation Abraham kept feeling, even when he did not yet have language for it. The institution had categories. It had dates, directions, bundles, reports, waiting rooms, links, adjournments, listings, and records. It could ask whether a thing had been filed. It could ask who had attended. It could ask what should happen next in the process.

It could not ask what it meant for a father to count ten minutes as part of a child's life.

This did not make the institution evil. Abraham had no need to make it evil. Evil was too simple for what he was learning. Machinery does not have to hate you to grind you down. It only has to move according to the material it has been built to process. It can be careful and still incomplete. It can be necessary and still cold. It can be the only route available and still wound the person who must travel through it.

That was why the waiting mattered.

Not because it was the worst thing that happened.

Not because ten minutes could explain the years.

It mattered because it showed the shape of the years in miniature. A father present. A child absent from the room but present in every breath. A screen between the man and the institution. A direction possibly changing somewhere beyond his reach. The demand to wait. The refusal to leave.

After the hearing, there would be another date.

There was always another date.

That is how the years were built: not only from large decisions, but from mornings like this one, from links opened, from names called, from silence observed, from a man sitting where he was told to sit because love had taken the form of attendance.

Abraham had not yet reached the later language for what he was doing. He was not trying to become an emblem. He was not trying to prove a theory about fatherhood. He was trying to remain close enough to his son that his son would one day know he had not been abandoned.

So when the screen opened, he came.

When the case was adjourned, he waited.

When they called him back, he returned.

That was not victory.

It was presence.

And in those years, presence was often the only form of fatherhood the process allowed him to practice.

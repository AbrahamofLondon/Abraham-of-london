# Chapter 6 — The Fire and the Deal

He found himself at an entrance. It looked ancient — not golden, not decorated, not the kind of entrance any story had prepared him for. It looked ordinary, but the feeling it carried was not ordinary. He knew, without knowing how he knew, that what stood before him was a boundary. He was not afraid. The entrance was not threatening. It was simply there, and he was there, and the space between them felt like the most important distance he had ever stood inside.

Then he was moved away from it. Not by his own will. He was taken from the entrance and began to fall.

The darkness was endless. There was no light, no ground, no end. He could hear people screaming and crying. The sound did not come from one direction. It came from everywhere, as if the darkness itself was made of it. He knew it was hell. Not because anyone told him. Not because he saw flames or figures or any of the images he had been taught. He knew it the way a body knows temperature — without language, without argument, directly.

The thought in his heart was: this was hell.

And yet, inside that knowledge, he felt he could get out.

He does not know why that confidence was there. It was not based on anything he had done or earned. It was simply present, as if the capacity to leave had been placed inside the same space as the knowledge of where he was. The falling continued, but the knowing had shifted. He said a quiet prayer. Not a formal one. Not one he had been taught. A prayer that rose from the part of him that had recognised the entrance and understood the darkness and still believed there was something on the other side of both.

Right there, he got an answer.

He made a promise.

He does not remember the sentence as a transcript. He remembers the exchange as a demand and an answer — that he offered his life, and that something accepted the offer.

Then he woke.

The room was on fire. There was soot in the air, dark against the dark of the hour. This time it was not the darkness of the fall — it was fire, visible, close, already consuming the mattress he had been lying on. A mosquito coil on the table beside his bed had caught the bedding. The flame was moving along the surface, and the smoke was low and thick.

He was in the room he shared with Jumoke and Yemisi. Both were asleep in their beds. It must have been about five in the morning — the kind of hour when the world is still and fire becomes visible before it becomes understood, when a sound carries further than it should, when the body is closest to sleep and furthest from defence.

He does not remember deciding to move. He remembers only that he was already moving, that his body had begun the work before his mind had caught up to what the work required. He woke them. They ran to call their parents.

By the time the adults reached the room, Abraham had put out the fire.

He still does not remember exactly how he did it. He remembers the soot on his hands. He remembers the smell of the burnt mattress. He remembers his sisters standing in the doorway, awake now, and the mosquito coil still on the table, the source of it all, small and ordinary and capable of enough.

What he remembers most clearly is this: he had been at an entrance, had fallen through darkness, had heard what hell sounds like, had prayed, had been answered, had made a promise. Then he had woken into a room that was burning, and the promise was no longer abstract. It was attached to a mattress, to soot, to a mosquito coil, to his sisters' sleep, to a fire he had to meet with his hands.

The promise was this: his life, in service. All of it. A child made that deal, in a state he could not fully explain, before a fire that was real. He has been living inside it ever since. He has never tried to renegotiate.

# Chapter 8 — The Spiritual Covering

The people who came to see her did not always come for the same reason.

Abraham noticed this as a child, from the distances he typically observed her — from wherever the adults weren't sitting. She would be with her visitors. He would be elsewhere, watching. The visitors came and sat. They spoke or they did not speak. Something passed between them, from her to them, that Abraham could not name as a child and has not found a simpler name for since. What he could tell was that the people who left were different from the people who had arrived. Not transformed — adjusted. Settled to something.

She was Iyalode. She had lived in that authority long enough for it to be ordinary. She was very neatly dressed. She was particular about her meals, and these were not incidental facts but facts of the same kind as her authority — the way the surface of a thing tells you what is underneath. She was self-directed in ways that the household arranged itself around without question.

She was of traditional African spiritual persuasion. That is not an approximation — it is what she was, and what she knew herself to be, and what the people who came to sit with her knew they were sitting with. She came from a world where spiritual authority was real and located in specific people, and she was one of those people.

She also converted to Christianity through gospel witness. She did not abandon what she had carried before. She held both, and the holding was not a problem she was required to solve. It was the shape of her life, lived whole rather than in compartments.

She doubted Abraham. She thought he was the wrong child for the name he carried — too dark, too connected to the maternal line, not what the name had learned to expect. She had an experienced eye. She was not wrong to use it. Her error was the conclusion, not the perception.

She named Jesus as most powerful to a boy who was watching her from a distance, and carrying a name she doubted he was fit for, and would go on carrying it anyway.

She died at 118, in 2006.

Abraham has been working out what he inherits from her ever since. Not a legacy. Not a lesson. A person — irreducible, unresolved, real. A woman who doubted him and covered him and held two truths simultaneously without asking permission to do either. That is the inheritance: not what she taught him, but what she was, and the particular impossibility of reducing her to a single account.

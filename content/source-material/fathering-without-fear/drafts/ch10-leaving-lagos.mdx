# Chapter 10 — Leaving Lagos

The family had converged at David Senior's house before the airport.

Femi was there. Bayo. Their wives. Funke. Yemisi. Some nephews and nieces. The room was full of the kind of talk that fills a house before a departure — not heavy, not light, just present. They were catching up on lives, sharing stories, discussing relatives in London whom David Senior hoped Abraham might see. Someone mentioned a cousin who had been asking about him. Someone else laughed at a memory Abraham had almost forgotten. A nephew ran through the room chasing something only a child could see. The ordinary texture of a family sending one of its own into a distance they could not fully picture.

David Senior moved through the room with the quiet authority he carried naturally. He was not a man of many words in a crowd, but his presence organised the space. When he spoke, people listened. When he was silent, the room still knew where he was.

Abraham's mother moved through the room with the particular concern that only mothers seem to carry at moments like this. She was worried about the Nigerian food — the rice, the stew, the things that taste like home because they have never been tasted anywhere else. She asked whether he would miss them. Abraham said he would be fine, he could do without them. She was not convinced. She listed the dishes she thought he would crave. She wanted him to carry as much as he could. There was also the question of whether the food would be allowed through airport security. The family discussed it. They agreed that if the officials objected, his brother would return with the items. Abraham carried the food anyway. At the checkpoint, it was allowed. A small thing, but it set a pattern that would repeat itself many times over the coming years: provision arriving through ordinary channels, not through drama, just when it was needed.

His luggage was a black suitcase. One suitcase was just shoes. Abraham remembers not knowing why he liked buying shoes so much, because he only ever used a few. The shoes sat in the suitcase like a small monument to a habit he could not explain. He gave out almost all his suits and blazers, keeping only a few, because he thought he would not need them for classes. He was wrong about that — he would need them sooner than he knew, for interviews, for presentations, for the kind of life that required a jacket — but the gesture of leaving them behind was part of the departure. He was stripping down to what he thought the next phase required, letting go of what he believed he had outgrown.

Then David Senior stood.

He did not have cash to give. His younger brother gave Abraham two hundred and fifty dollars — a practical gift, the kind that meets a need without ceremony. But David Senior's gift was not cash. It was the blessing.

Abraham knelt. The room went quiet. The family stood around them — Femi, Bayo, their wives, Funke, Yemisi, the nephews and nieces who had been running moments before, now still. David Senior laid his hands on Abraham's head. The weight of them was ordinary — the weight of a father's hands, nothing more — but the silence in the room gave the gesture a gravity that Abraham would remember longer than the words. David Senior quoted Psalm 27:10 — *When my father and my mother forsake me, then the Lord will take care of me* — and then prayed it as provision. The sense of the prayer was this: unless Abraham had ever looked away from another person in need, he would never be stranded abroad; his needs would be met. David Senior's voice was the voice of a father sending his child into unknown territory, trusting God to provide and protect him. He was not reciting scripture. He was applying his own lifetime record — he had found this to be true; he was vouching for it; he was handing it over as the most reliable thing he knew about where his son was going. A man who had become a father at fifteen, who had raised his younger siblings alone, who had watched God provide in ways he could not have manufactured — that man was now standing over his son, speaking the same confidence into the next generation.

Abraham thought he understood the blessing at the time. He thought he knew what it meant to be sent with a prayer and a promise. But the understanding came slowly, over months, as provisions arrived — not always early, but always at the necessary moment. A church member noticed something in his expression during choir and later arranged a payment. A housemate provided the missing amount shortly before a deadline. A friend who had spent three days unable to stop thinking about Abraham gave him an envelope containing exactly what he needed. A former Lagos boss provided USD that covered the final instalment including charges. The money arrived. Not early. At the last moment, each time. The prayer was not a guarantee of comfort. It was a guarantee of presence.

Abraham's house was close to the airport. From his living room, he could see the private tarmac — the planes moving on the ground, the ordinary business of departure visible from where he sat. He left his luggage at home, went back to pick it up from Agege, and then drove straight to the airport. The drive took less than ten minutes. Femi and Abimbola accompanied him.

It was night. Lagos Murtala Muhammed International Airport. British Airways. The terminal was bright under the overhead lights — the kind of brightness that makes everything look ordinary, even a departure that will change a life. Check-in was normal. The queue. The counter. The weight of the black suitcase on the scale. Abraham was numb, as he often is during tense experiences. His body was present, going through the motions, but something in him had already left. No one cried that he saw. His mother looked sad. Goodbye was a handshake, a nod, the turning away.

After crossing security, Abraham remembers no strong dramatic feeling. He does not remember whether he looked back. The departure was not a scene from a film. It was a man walking through an airport corridor, carrying a black suitcase and a blessing he did not yet know how to hold.

The flight was British Airways. Night flight from Lagos to London. The cabin lights dimmed. People around him slept. Abraham sat with the weight of the departure still on him, the blessing still unabsorbed. He did not sleep. He sat in the half-dark of the cabin, watching the small screen map show the plane moving north across the continent, the distance growing between him and everything he had known. He thought about the food in his suitcase, the shoes he had packed for reasons he could not name, the suits he had left behind, the two hundred and fifty dollars in his pocket, the hands of his father on his head.

Heathrow arrival was early morning — around five or six, the exact time unconfirmed. The terminal was quiet in that particular way airports are at that hour, before the day's traffic fills the corridors. The air felt different — not colder, just different — the way air is when you have crossed a border you cannot see. Friends in Norwich had prepared him for cold weather, but it was not as cold as they had made him expect. The forecast had been worse than the reality, which seemed, in that first hour, like a small mercy.

The first room he stayed in was small and plain. A single bed with a white mattress. Red curtains that did not quite close, letting a strip of grey English light into the room. A desk with a black lamp. Shelves. A small bin. Plain white walls. Grey carpet. A small wooden chest. The room was not remarkable. It was simply where he was, in a new country, with a blessing he did not yet fully understand. He sat on the edge of the bed and looked at the room. The red curtains. The desk. The lamp. The silence of a place where no one knew his name. He had left a house full of family and arrived in a room that contained only what could be carried in a black suitcase.

The ticket had been paid for by a client of Abraham's last employer in Lagos. Abraham did not know the details of the arrangement — only that the ticket existed, that it was British Airways, that someone had covered it without being asked. The same client had also paid for a hotel in London for a few days. Abraham did not use the hotel. Visa delays had already cost him two weeks of orientation, and he needed to get to Norwich as soon as possible. The hotel went unused. A room in London, paid for, empty, while he travelled east toward a smaller room he had never seen.

After arrival, a Ghanaian woman helped him find the accommodation office. She did not know him. She simply saw someone who looked like he needed direction and gave it. Another man helped him find the business school, where staff arranged his dorm keys. Each step was someone who knew where he needed to go when he did not yet know himself. A person, an office, a building, a key.

On the first night on campus, a woman who knew him from Lagos tracked him down. She had heard he was coming and found him before he had even settled. She brought a cooked meal — the first food he had eaten in England that tasted like someone had made it for him. She told a man who also knew Abraham, and he came later that night. He bought Abraham a SIM card so he could make calls, a winter jacket because the one Abraham had brought was not warm enough, and gave him cash — the kind of practical help that meets a need before the person receiving it has fully recognised the need as urgent.

At the time, each help arrived as the next practical thing. A ticket. A key. A meal. A SIM card. A coat. Cash. Only later did the sequence begin to gather around his father's prayer — the prayer that said Abraham would never be stranded abroad, that his needs would be met. Provision did not arrive as spectacle. It arrived as a ticket, a meal, a SIM card, a coat, a few notes of cash, a key. Each one ordinary. Together, they formed a pattern he would spend years learning to read.

At the time, Abraham was working and felt he could not build his life the way he wanted. He wanted to see the world, understand what it had to offer, and recalibrate his position. That path later led toward the refinery venture that did not close. Looking back, the blessing became provision, progressively understood over months — not as a promise that everything would work, but as a promise that he would not be left alone while finding out whether it would.

He was being carried. That was real. What he was being carried toward — whether it was what he believed it was — the next several years would answer.

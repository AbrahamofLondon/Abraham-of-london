# Chapter 11 — A Post on Facebook

Nigeria was familiar when Abraham returned. He was not.

The streets knew what they had always known. The house knew its rooms. People still spoke in the registers he understood without translation. But his father was in the ground now, and the MBA had ended, and the man who had left for Norwich under a father's prayer had come back with a certificate and an absence.

Grief does not always announce itself as collapse. Sometimes it enters the ordinary day and sits beside everything else. You eat. You answer messages. You think about work. You think about what season of life might be opening, because the mind tries to keep moving even when the body knows something has been buried.

There was a refinery possibility in motion. There were signs, or what felt like signs, that another phase was beginning. Abraham had been reading patterns for a long time by then. The MBA had been full of provisions that arrived at the necessary moment. His father's death had come after visions he had not asked for. The burial had placed him in that strange post-funeral clarity where everything seems sharpened and unstable at once.

He posted on Facebook about his father's death.

Without expectation.

Most people respond to grief from outside it. They say what can be said. They are sorry. They pray. They use the sentences available to the living when they are standing at the edge of someone else's death and cannot enter it. There is nothing wrong with this. It is often all language can do.

This reply did not arrive that way.

A woman from his past responded. She did not offer only sympathy. She answered grief with grief. Her own father, she said, had died suddenly, in a way that left grief with no time to prepare.

The detail mattered.

Not because one father's death can be measured against another. It cannot. But because the sentence came with the claim of having paid for understanding. She was not speaking as a spectator to loss. She was presenting herself as someone who had stood inside the same kind of room, someone who knew what it meant for a father to be suddenly absent from the world that should still contain him.

That is why it landed.

Abraham was not reading the reply with the protections he would learn later. He was reading it as a grieving son, newly returned to Nigeria, his father's burial still near enough in time to make recognition feel like mercy. The exchange did not feel general. It felt specific. It did not feel like someone saying the right thing. It felt like someone speaking from a place that had cost her something.

He did not know the full shape of what was beginning. He knew only the part available to him then: that somebody had answered the open wound in him with an open wound of her own, and that this made the distance between them feel shorter than it had any right to be.

The invitation came from there.

She invited him back to the UK.

That sentence belongs to another kind of threshold. Not the MBA departure. That had been January 2016, under David Senior's Psalm 27:10 prayer, with David Senior alive and sending him out. Not the burial return. That had been September 2017, Abraham coming back to Nigeria to place his father in the ground. This was a second movement, later, after grief had become the language through which a new door opened.

He did not experience it as something to defend against. That would be a dishonest way to write the moment. The post was grief. The reply was recognition. The invitation was a door. At that point in the story, recognition is powerful when a man has just buried the person who recognised him before almost anyone else did.

So he listened.

The country around him was familiar. The future was not. A woman from his past had found the one place in him that was still uncovered and had spoken to it from what sounded like her own uncovered place. He had spent the previous season being carried through closed doors at the last moment. It was not difficult for him to believe that another door might be opening.

He had buried his father. Someone answered the grief. Then she asked him to come.

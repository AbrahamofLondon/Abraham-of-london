# Chapter 12 — Married by December

The date was on the form before the meaning of the date had caught up with him.

Lagos. 23 December 2017.

Not London. Not after a permanent relocation. Lagos. Before the March 2018 move that would finally place Abraham in the UK as a settled fact.

Speed is not always visible while one is inside it. From the inside it can feel like obedience, conviction, momentum, the long-awaited door opening after years of delay. Abraham had been trained by life to move when a path appeared. The path had often appeared late. Money had arrived at the last moment. Strangers had helped when his phone died. His father's verse had seemed to travel ahead of him. He knew what it meant to be carried.

He was less prepared for what it meant to be carried quickly.

After the Chapter 11 exchange, he went back to the UK. He used his own savings for the travel rather than accepting hers. That detail matters because it is the kind of detail a man can use to assure himself that he is still acting from strength. He was not being bought. He was not being carried by her money. He was stepping up. He was doing what men in his family had done before him: taking responsibility before all the information was available.

The official engagement was in Lagos in October 2017.

She said Abraham's friends could not attend. It was to be close family only, very few people. He accepted the condition. He told friends not to come, or had to turn them back, including one who had travelled from the UK to Nigeria. Then she brought approximately twenty people.

Abraham saw it. He saw enough to be troubled. He minimised it. Maybe it was family pressure. Maybe it was misunderstanding. Maybe this was how things were done. Maybe his friends were reading too much into it.

He kept walking.

Before the December ceremony, at the marriage registry in Lagos, another fact emerged. She had previously been married. He did not know it before the registry.

The temptation, writing this now, is to let the older man prosecute what the younger man minimised. That would be easier. It would also be less honest.

Two friends had already bought tickets for the December wedding, one from the United States and one from the United Kingdom. After hearing what happened around the engagement, they refused to attend. They saw danger Abraham did not yet know how to honour. Or perhaps he did know, and did not want the knowledge to govern him. Those are not identical. The second is harder to confess.

He married on 23 December 2017, in Lagos, Nigeria.

The sentence is plain because the decision was not. It carried grief, hope, spiritual interpretation, family hunger, the pressure of age and expectation, and the deep inherited instinct that commitment is what a man does when things become difficult. David Senior had raised siblings when death handed him a household. Abraham had grown up inside that pattern. Stepping back did not feel like wisdom. It felt like failure.

The story Abraham had built was not stupid. That matters. It had grief in it. It had faith in it. It had the desire for family in it. It had the need to believe that the door opened through shared bereavement was not an accident. It had his own self-image in it: a man who would not abandon something because it was complicated, a man who could absorb difficulty, a man who could make a life work by staying steady inside it.

Some of those things were noble in other contexts.

Here, they made him slow to stop.

The wedding happened. The photographs would show what photographs show: clothing, faces, ceremony, the arrangement of bodies into a public fact. A marriage had been entered. The warnings did not disappear because vows were spoken. They went into the marriage with him.

He did not yet know what they would cost.

He knew only that he had chosen, and that choice now had a date, a place, witnesses, and a future attached to it. Lagos. 23 December 2017. A man who had seen enough to pause chose instead to proceed.

The rest of the story would not begin because he saw nothing.

It would begin because he saw something and made it smaller than his hope.

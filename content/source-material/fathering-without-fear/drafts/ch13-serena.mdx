# Chapter 13 — Serena

Serena was walking beside him when the road changed the posting.

Awka South LGA, Anambra. Approximately 2002 or 2003. The year had the temporary seriousness national service gives to everything — Abraham was not home, but he had to live as if the place mattered. He was not yet settled into adulthood, but adulthood had already begun asking for an answer.

Serena had come from Ayamelum into Abraham's station because the conditions around her posting had become unsafe. She came from danger into the orbit where Abraham was serving, and she needed a transfer to Awka South.

One morning she asked him to walk with her. So he did.

They were not walking as lovers. They were two service members on a road, carrying the practical business of a transfer in the middle of a year that was supposed to teach them how to become useful adults. Serena moved with purpose. She was not vague about need. She had come from danger, and the need in front of her was not abstract.

Someone stopped them.

"Are you corps members?"

The question was ordinary. The effect was not. A stranger had noticed the khaki, the bearing, the temporary identity that rendered young graduates visible to officials and strangers alike. Serena did not waste the opening. She asked about the transfer. The person offered help. What had seemed to require process suddenly had a path. The transfer was effectively confirmed there and later formalised.

Abraham would think about that walk more than once in the years that followed. Not because it was miraculous. Because it was precise. The right road, the right stranger, the right question at the right moment. Whether another person would have named it differently is not the point. Abraham knew what it felt like: less like coincidence than arrangement.

Their life there did not stay strange. It became ordinary almost immediately, the way life does. Abraham travelled to Aba for fabrics and produced limited-edition shirts. He chose fabrics carefully — the scarcity was part of the appeal. If too many people could wear the same thing, the shirt lost its force. The pieces sold quickly, sometimes within three hours.

Serena sold women's clothing, underwear, jeans. Her stock moved quickly too. There was a small confidence in that kind of commerce: travel, fabric, customers, money returning fast enough to fund the next trip. They were young and industrious, building structure where the year had given them very little.

There was closeness, but the boundary remained. She had a long-term boyfriend — approximately fifteen years. Abraham knew enough to understand that whatever category he occupied was not simple. He did not yet know how to read the complexity cleanly. They never had sex. There was closeness and cuddling, and the chapter does not need to inflate what it was. It was real, and it was enough to keep him near something he could not yet name.

Then things began arriving that the ordinary world could not hold.

There were incidents where Serena appeared to know what people had said about her when she was not present. Things were said privately. She seemed to know them. Abraham did not have an explanation that caused the ordinary world to stop functioning. The fabrics still sold. Customers still came. Service still had its rhythms. But something was present that he could not account for, and it did not explain itself.

Then there was the journal.

They were lying on the floor in a friend's room. The room was ordinary — a floor, a door, the kind of temporary space national service produced. Serena had been updating her journal before Abraham arrived. It was open. She noticed it was open and asked him to pass it. Then she paused. She had been reading entries from previous years while he was there. She asked him to read one.

He thought it was a joke at first.

He read.

He froze.

The entry did not name him. That deepened the strangeness. It described a meeting, or a man, closely enough for him to stop reading as if the page had become aware of him. The entry disclosed something about a spirit husband — a figure Serena believed had claim over her. The journal had been written years before Abraham had entered her life. The entry was not a confession made to him. It was a record made before him. That was the part that would not leave him alone.

He did not know whether to call it prediction, prophecy, or something else. He knew only that the page had been written before he arrived, and that it described him closely enough to make the room feel suddenly still. The floor he was lying on had not changed. The door was still a door. But the room had become a different kind of room.

What followed the journal was not subtle.

A voice came to Abraham the next day and told him it would kill him for his wife. Abraham did not know whose voice it was. He did not know what framework to place it in. He knew only that the threat had arrived, that it was specific, and that it came after the journal. Two near-death experiences followed in the weeks after — incidents Abraham would later understand as the warnings he had been given and had not fully absorbed.

He had believed — with the confidence of a young man whose faith had not yet been tested by something that moved inside intimate territory — that God's protection was complete. That nothing could reach him if he was held. Serena's presence challenged that architecture. Not through argument. Through proximity to something Abraham's theology had not prepared him for. The protection he believed in was real, but it had not made him wise. It had made him confident, and confidence without discernment is its own form of danger.

He did not understand this clearly at the time. He understands it now only because of what it cost him.

In May 2003, Abraham nearly died. Dayo was looking after him when he stopped breathing. What happened in that interval — whether it was dream, vision, or the threshold of something the body does when it is leaving — he does not reduce to a single category. He was shown a road. He understood, in whatever language that space uses, that his life was attached to other lives and that the attachment was not finished. He was reminded of what had been spoken over him — the words that had arrived before, in other rooms, from other voices, about what his life was for. He came back. The first thing he asked for was food. The ordinary body, reasserting itself after whatever the other thing had been.

He does not make this into spectacle. It happened. He survived. The surviving was not earned by anything he did. It was given, the way the walk in Anambra had been given, the way the transfer had arrived, the way the money had appeared. Except this time the giving was not convenient. It was terrifying.

He moved away from Serena after that. Not cleanly. Not with the decisive clarity the situation should have required. But the distance came, because the closeness had shown him something about himself that he could not remain inside and survive.

What he carried from Serena was not a romance story. It was a road in Anambra, a transfer that opened while they were walking, fabric sold quickly, a journal written before him, a voice that threatened to kill him, and the knowledge that the faith he carried could keep him alive without making him wise.

He had been warned. He had not yet learned how to read a warning.

That education would come later, and it would not be gentle.

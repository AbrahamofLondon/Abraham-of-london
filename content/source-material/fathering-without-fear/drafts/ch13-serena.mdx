# Chapter 13 — Serena

Serena was walking beside him when the road changed the posting.

That is where the chapter has to begin. Not with the journal. Not with the voice. Not with what Abraham would later understand and not understand. With a road in Anambra, during national service, and a young woman moving beside him with a problem she needed solved.

Awka South LGA. Approximately 2002 or 2003. The year had the temporary seriousness national service gives to everything. Abraham was not home, but he had to live as if the place mattered. He was not yet settled into adulthood, but adulthood had already begun asking for proof.

Serena had come from Ayamelum into Abraham's station because the conditions around her posting had become unsafe. Civil-war conditions is the phrase the source carries. It is enough to say that she came from danger into the orbit where Abraham was serving, and she needed a transfer to Awka South.

One morning she asked him to walk with her.

So he did.

They were not walking as lovers. They were two service members on a road, carrying the practical business of a transfer in the middle of a year that was supposed to teach them how to become useful adults. Serena moved with purpose. She was not vague about need. She had come from danger, and the need in front of her was not abstract. She had to stay where safety had become possible.

Someone stopped them.

"Are you corps members?"

The question was ordinary. The effect was not. A stranger had noticed the khaki, the bearing, the temporary identity that made young graduates visible to officials and strangers alike. Serena did not waste the opening. She asked about the transfer. The person offered help.

What had seemed to require process suddenly had a path.

The transfer was effectively confirmed there and later formalised.

That is the scene as Abraham remembers it: not thunder, not prophecy, not romance. A road. A question. Serena standing inside the need and moving when a door opened.

Their life there did not become strange because the transfer was strange. It became ordinary again almost immediately, the way life does. Abraham travelled to Aba for fabrics and made limited-edition shirts. He chose fabrics carefully because the scarcity was part of the appeal. If too many people could wear the same thing, the shirt lost its force. The pieces sold quickly, sometimes within three hours.

Serena sold women's clothing, underwear, jeans. Her stock moved quickly too. There was a small confidence in that kind of commerce: travel, fabric, customers, money returning fast enough to make the next trip possible. They were young and industrious, building structure where the year had given them very little.

That is how she should enter the page. Walking. Asking. Selling. Moving.

Not as a spiritual case.

There was closeness. There was cuddling. There was no sex. That is enough to say. The memoir does not need to borrow heat from what did not happen.

She had a long-term boyfriend. Approximately fifteen years. Abraham knew enough to understand that whatever category he occupied was not simple. He did not yet know how to read the complexity cleanly. That is not an accusation. It is the shape of what he was inside.

There were incidents where Serena appeared to know what people had said about her when she was not present. Things were said. She seemed to know. Abraham did not have an explanation that made the ordinary world stop. The fabrics still sold. Customers still came. Service still had its rhythms.

Then there was the journal.

It had been written years before they met. That mattered to Abraham. It did not arrive as something composed for him or around him. It existed before him. In it, he encountered a disclosure about a spirit husband.

The next day, a voice threatened to kill Abraham for his wife.

His wife.

The phrase did not fit Abraham's category for Serena. That was part of the disturbance. The warning came in language that claimed a relationship Abraham had not claimed in that way and did not control. Two near-death experiences followed.

He underestimated the warning.

That sentence is not accusation. Not of Serena. Not of Abraham. It is the plainest description of what happened. He was young. He was drawn. He had already been formed by spiritual seriousness, but not every kind of spiritual danger arrives in the form one has been taught to recognise.

Years later, in 2013, after redundancy, Abraham reconnected with Serena. She tried to help him with work or business. She mentioned him to her husband. Her husband told her to cut ties and not mention it again. The chapter does not make him a villain for this. A husband can recognise a boundary from inside his marriage. Abraham may not have liked the sentence. He can still write it without prosecuting it.

That is the ethic this chapter requires. Serena remains protected by dignity. Her husband remains protected by dignity. Abraham's memory remains honest without converting her life into spectacle.

What he carried from Serena was not a romance story. It was a road in Anambra, a transfer that opened while they were walking, fabric sold quickly, a journal written before him, a voice using a word he had not used, and the uneasy knowledge that closeness can feel clearest before wisdom knows what to do with it.

The warning was given.

He did not know how seriously to take it until later.

# Chapter 14 — David Was Missing From the Wedding

David was the one who knew how to enter a room.

Abraham did not have that gift in the same way. He could think his way through a room. David could warm it. He was jolly, sociable, well dressed, easy with people in ways Abraham was not. Parties made sense to him. Friends gathered around him. Girls noticed him. None of this made him shallow. It made him David: bright in the ordinary registers, kind without needing to advertise kindness, alive to the social world Abraham often stood beside rather than inside.

When people noticed how different they were, David had an answer ready. He said the fun genes had gone to him and the serious genes had gone to Abraham. It was a joke, and it was not only a joke. He could say it without cruelty because the difference between them was not a wound. It was part of the pact of their brotherhood: one carried the lightness, one carried the weight, and neither of them needed the other to become him.

They were brothers. Full stop.

They had grown through childhood and school and university with a private certainty about each other. David was not an accessory to Abraham's life. He was one of the people before whom Abraham did not have to translate himself. In families, this is a form of wealth. Not money. Witness. Someone who knows the earlier version of you and does not require evidence that it existed.

They made a pact about marriage. Neither of them would say "I do" without the other's approval and presence. It was the kind of pact young men make with seriousness and laughter in the same breath, believing the future will present itself in ways their promise can manage. They did not write it down. They did not need to. It was known between them.

In 2003 Abraham was in Awka, in Anambra, during national service. His life there had its own ordinary texture: postings, roads, work, walking, the unfinished business of becoming a man in a place that had not chosen him and that he had not chosen in any full sense either. Then he saw a wedding.

It was his own wedding.

David was not there.

The vision did not explain itself. It did not need to. Because of the pact, David's absence had only one possible meaning. Abraham did not understand it as a vague warning or a mood or a fear. The structure of the promise interpreted the image for him. If Abraham was saying "I do" and David was absent, then something had happened that no ordinary reason could explain.

David, you were missing from the one place you had promised not to miss.

The next day Abraham left for Lagos.

He did not travel as a man hoping to be wrong in some dramatic way. He travelled as a man carrying an answer he had not yet heard spoken by anyone living. The road from Awka to Lagos became the space between knowing and being told. That is a difficult space to describe because it is neither ignorance nor confirmation. It is the body moving toward the sentence the body already fears.

When he reached his father's house, there was a crowd.

That was enough.

In Lagos, a crowd at a house is not always grief. It can be celebration, argument, committee, family business, an event thickening before anyone has explained it. But this crowd had the weight of mourning. Abraham saw it before anyone spoke. The house had become the kind of house people come to when a sentence has already entered it.

At the gate, Uncle Dejo came toward him.

Abraham did not ask what happened. He did not ask whether something was wrong. He asked the question the vision had already given him.

"Is David dead?"

Uncle Dejo answered in the register the family already had for Abraham. Triplets. Strange children. He already told you.

The sentence confirmed more than David's death. It confirmed that the family was not surprised Abraham knew before he was told. They had lived with their own account of him since birth — first of the triplets, child of return, the one David Senior had watched as if an old life had come back into a new body. In that family, Abraham arriving with knowledge did not require explanation. It belonged to the existing file the family kept in its own language.

David died on 5 May 2003, in Kent.

The date is plain. The loss is not.

Abraham had not travelled to Lagos to discover his brother was gone. He had travelled to let the world say what the pact had already said. There are deaths that arrive as news. There are deaths that arrive first as absence, and the news comes afterward, late, carrying paperwork for what the spirit has already delivered.

David had been the fun genes. The parties. The friends. The brother who made difference feel companionable instead of lonely. He had been the one who would not let Abraham enter marriage without being seen first by someone who knew him before marriage, before London, before courtrooms, before fatherhood, before any of the later sentences that would try to reduce him.

Now that witness was gone.

The brother was gone, and the pact had worked exactly as a pact should. It told the truth before the world was ready to deliver it.

# Chapter 14 — David Was Missing From the Wedding

David was the one who knew how to enter a room.

He was dark-skinned, shorter than Abraham, with a face that offered itself plainly. A lovely smile. Not the smile of a man performing warmth, but the smile of a man who was actually glad to be there. He wore his clothes with intention — not extravagantly, but with the understanding that what you wore was worth saying well. He did not spend on everything. He spent on the few things that mattered and wore them until they meant something.

He could warm a room. Abraham could think his way through one.

David was jolly, sociable, easy with people in ways Abraham was not. Parties made sense to him. Friends gathered around him. Girls noticed him. None of this made him shallow. It made him David: bright in the ordinary registers, kind without needing to advertise kindness, alive to the social world Abraham often stood beside rather than inside.

When he entered a room he did not announce himself. He simply arrived, and then the room was different — not because of noise or gesture but because of welcome. He made people feel that being in his company was an easy thing. They did not have to prepare for it. Some people are like this. David was specifically like this.

They were brothers. Full stop.

---

At Unilag, his world was its own thing. He was at Ozolua Quarters — private accommodation off campus, in the social world the university offered if you knew how to find it. Abraham was at Sodeinde Hall. He called it stinking Sodeinde. Two brothers, same university, two different countries.

David spent much of his time at Abbey's place. Abbey shared private rented accommodation with his brothers, and there was always something in motion when David arrived — somewhere they had come from, somewhere they were going, laughter already running before the reason had been named. Abbey and his brothers especially loved having David around. It was not unusual. Most people did.

The group had its own language. Abbey and his brothers had invented it — they called it caveman language. They called each other matey. Abraham still remembers one word: *pinchomi.* It meant girl. He does not remember the rest of the vocabulary. What he remembers is that the group had built a private world with its own speech, and David had been let inside that world as naturally as he was let inside any room he entered.

Nobody who knew them thought of them as anything other than brothers. The people who noticed how different they were also saw the similarities. Even the way people described them — David in black, which was how he was remembered, that specific combination — carried both things at once. The differences and the resemblances occupied the same sentence. The group understood this without needing it explained.

---

David had a running complaint about Abraham. It took the form of a joke, which was also not only a joke.

Girls found Abraham funny and clever. They noticed him. Abraham had no particular use for the attention. He believed in courtship, not dating. He was not confused about this. It was a conviction, and convictions did not require defending. He simply did not pursue what was being offered.

David found this almost painful to observe.

He had a phrase for it. He would look at Abraham with the particular expression — half amusement, half genuine exasperation — and say that Abraham was a waste of talent. That was the exact formulation. A waste of talent. The girls could see it, and apparently only Abraham could not.

Abraham laughed at this. He was not offended. He understood it as affection wearing the clothes of complaint. David was not saying Abraham should be different. He was saying he was watching talent go unused, and that this was a personal loss for David to witness, and that it was funny, and also that it was not entirely funny.

He said the fun genes had gone to him and the serious genes had gone to Abraham. This was his formulation of the difference between them — delivered with the particular lightness of a person who has named something true and does not want to make it heavier than it needs to be. It was a joke. It was not only a joke. He could say it without cruelty because the difference between them was not a wound. It was part of what made the brotherhood hold: one carried the lightness, one carried the weight, and neither of them needed the other to become him.

---

There was one thing David said that Abraham did not fully understand until years later.

He said Abraham was naive.

He did not say it to wound him. He said it with the gentleness of a person who sees something clearly and is not sure how to hand it across without breaking it. He was not calling Abraham stupid. He was identifying something Abraham had not yet learned to protect.

Other people saw the outside. They saw the wit, the conviction, the way Abraham held himself when something was asked of him. David saw what the outside was covering. He was the first person to say it plainly: beneath the strength there was something that had not yet learned to be careful.

Abraham did not know what to do with this at the time. He thought he was the lion. He interpreted David's protectiveness as unnecessary, because he did not yet understand what David was seeing.

He understood it later.

---

David was a practical thinker. He was always looking ahead — what would come next, what needed to be arranged, what a future might actually look like if you thought about it seriously rather than letting it arrive.

Abraham, after his bachelor's degree and national service, was planning to start a rabbit farm.

David thought differently.

He did not think Nigeria was the right place for Abraham. He did not say this as a criticism of Nigeria. He said it as a calculation about Abraham specifically: what Abraham needed, what the moment could offer, whether the two things matched. He was ahead of Abraham in the way that some people are ahead — not cleverer, but already standing at the place you are walking toward.

---

They were outside Moremi Hall when the conversation happened.

David had his own name for it. He called it De La Morems — with an affectation, with the particular inflection of a man giving a car park a title it had not earned and did not need and that somehow seemed right coming from him. De La Morems. It sounded like David: the familiar given a name that made it funnier than it was.

He was talking about Abraham's inexperience with girls. This was a conversation he returned to — not obsessively, but with the consistency of a man who has identified a problem and does not intend to let it be ignored. Abraham understood his own convictions about courtship. David understood something else: that Abraham's convictions, whatever their rightness, were leaving him without the preparation that experience gives.

At some point in this conversation, David said:

Make me this promise. Whenever you get married, you only say I do after I have nodded my head in approval.

Abraham laughed.

David said: I am serious. Promise me.

Abraham said, approximately: OK. You too.

David said: Deal.

It was said once. There was no ceremony around it. It was not written down.

But David's eyes when he said *I am serious* were not the eyes of a man completing a joke. The words had humour around them. The man underneath the words did not. He had asked for a specific right: to consider what he knew about Abraham, to look at who Abraham was choosing, and to give or withhold his approval before the sentence was spoken. Not just presence at a wedding. The nod. The act of judgment by someone who had known Abraham long enough to judge honestly.

It was not a small thing to ask for.

It was not a small thing to give.

---

In 2003 Abraham was in Awka, in Anambra, during national service. His life there had its own ordinary texture: postings, roads, work, walking, the unfinished business of becoming a man in a place that had not chosen him and that he had not chosen in any full sense either.

His mobile phone had no coverage where he was staying.

One day a friend suggested they visit a cyber café. Abraham did not feel he needed to go. He went anyway. When the friend finished his business, some paid time remained on the machine. The friend said Abraham might as well check his email.

Abraham was not expecting anything.

David had emailed. He had been trying to call but could not reach him.

Abraham replied with Serena's number. David called.

He was already making arrangements for Abraham to join him in London. He had been thinking it through. He explained what needed to be done, what Abraham needed to prepare, what the route would look like. He was not describing a vague possibility. He was describing a plan, and the plan was specific, and he had made it without being asked.

Abraham was planning a rabbit farm.

He listened. He does not know now what he would have said if more time had been available. He knows that David had already decided what Abraham needed next, and was calling from England to explain it, and that the line between a man's plan and his ending is something you cannot see until afterward.

That was the last time they spoke.

---

The Kano airport road is another memory.

David was going to England. This was the first time he had gone, and it would be the last. He went to continue his education. He worked long hours alongside studying. He sang Celine Dion songs in a car on the road to Kano airport, which was not his usual music — it belonged more to Abraham than to David — but that morning he sang along anyway, and no one remarked on it.

A question came.

Abraham does not know what to call it. It was not a thought he decided to think. It arrived. It was this: what if he never saw David again? The question had the feeling of suggesting death. He did not want it. He pushed it away.

The question came again.

Reluctantly, he asked David about his relationship with Jesus.

David looked at him. Abraham knew the look — the expression David had for Abraham's particular register of seriousness, the face of a person who loves you and finds your heaviness occasionally excessive.

David burst into laughter.

He said he was only going on holiday. He was not going to die. He would soon be back.

Abraham asked again.

David said: I am good. We are good.

He hugged Abraham.

They were quiet for a while.

After that, David resumed singing along with the Celine Dion song.

The road continued. The airport was coming. The song was Abraham's music and David's music both at once, and the question had been asked and answered and the answer had been given with laughter, which was the only answer David had available to him because he believed what he was saying, and Abraham held the answer and the song continued and then they arrived.

---

The wedding was chaotic.

Abraham did much of the running around by himself. Friends had distanced themselves. They came mostly out of courtesy. He had expected Duke as his best man — the de facto arrangement, never formally made. Duke had bought a ticket to the engagement. He came to the ceremony, but missed it: he had gone to pick up friends near the venue and was told to take them back. By the time the ceremony reached the point of the best man, Duke was not there.

The MC called Abraham up. She asked his best man to follow.

One friend stepped up.

Duke was in the room. He was standing near [FRIEND]. Abraham heard him say, quietly, with his back turned: *Best man, me?*

Three words. Abraham heard them. He said nothing. He held them for years.

No one at the wedding mentioned David.

His absence was carried inside Abraham. It did not surface in the ceremony. No one asked about it. People greeted him, they ate, they spoke, and somewhere inside all of that, David was not there, and no one knew to name it, and Abraham did not name it either.

---

At the altar, as the vows were being exchanged, Abraham remembered the pact.

He looked down.

David was not there.

He ran off the stage to look for him.

Then he realised it was not real.

A trance is a trance. He is not interested in explaining what it was or what it meant. It was vivid. It was the most vivid thing in the room. The altar and the vows and the missing David and the running — all of it precise and in order, and then the returning, and then the realisation.

But the pact had come back to him. In the middle of the ceremony, the pact had risen — *Make me this promise*, David had said, outside De La Morems, with the laughter still in the air — and Abraham had looked for the nod, and there was no nod, and the person who had asked for the right to give it was not anywhere in the room.

David, you were missing from the one place you had promised not to miss.

---

The next day Abraham left for Lagos.

He did not travel as a man hoping to be wrong. He travelled as a man carrying an answer he had not yet heard spoken by anyone living. The road from Awka to Lagos became the space between knowing and being told. That is a difficult space to describe, because it is neither ignorance nor confirmation. It is the body moving toward the sentence the body already fears.

When he reached his father's house, there was a crowd.

That was enough.

In Lagos, a crowd at a house is not always grief. It can be celebration, argument, committee, family business, an event thickening before anyone has explained it. But this crowd had the weight of mourning. Abraham saw it before anyone spoke. The house had become the kind of house people come to when a sentence has already entered it.

At the gate, Uncle Dejo came toward him.

Abraham did not ask what had happened. He did not ask whether something was wrong. He asked the question the vision had already given him.

Is David dead?

Uncle Dejo answered in the register the family already had for Abraham. Triplets. Strange children. He already told you.

It confirmed that the family was not surprised Abraham knew before he was told. They had lived with their own account of him since birth — first of the triplets, child of return, the one David Senior had watched as if an old life had come back into a new body. In that family, Abraham arriving with knowledge did not require explanation. It belonged to the existing file the family kept in its own language.

David died on 5 May 2003, in Kent.

Abraham had not travelled to Lagos to discover his brother was gone. He had travelled to let the world say what the pact had already said.

He had been the brother who made difference feel companionable instead of lonely. The one who could look at Abraham and see past what everyone else saw. The one who called Abraham naive with gentleness, who saw through the lion, who sat at a car park outside De La Morems and said *I am serious* with eyes that were not joking. The one who was already making arrangements for Abraham's future from England while Abraham was planning a rabbit farm. The one who sang along with the Celine Dion song on the road to Kano airport and said *we are good* and meant it, and was not wrong to mean it, because he was a young man in the middle of building a life and he believed he was coming back.

The one who would not let Abraham enter marriage without being seen by someone who had known him before marriage.

Now that witness was gone.

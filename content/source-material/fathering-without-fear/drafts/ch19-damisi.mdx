# Chapter 19 — Damisi

Damisi wanted to know why people always had to be with them.

It was a fair question, which made it harder.

The room had its rules. The time had its edges. Adults had arranged the conditions inside which father and son were allowed to be together, and Damisi, being a child and therefore still honest in the ways adults learn to complicate, looked at the arrangement and found the obvious flaw.

Why could they not just leave?

Then he offered the solution.

They could escape through the window.

It was funny because he was a boy. It was painful because he was not wrong to want the world to be simpler. Abraham did not need to turn the sentence into a theory. He did not need to make it evidence of anything. A child had looked at a room that limited him and imagined an exit. The humour was real. The longing was real. Both belonged to him.

That is where any honest chapter about Damisi has to begin.

Not with the case.

Not with Abraham's pain.

With the boy.

Damisi is very intelligent. He talks with a maturity that can make a person forget his age if they are not careful, and Abraham is careful. He does not want his son's quickness to become another burden placed on him. Intelligence in a child is still childhood. Maturity in a child is still childhood. A boy can say something that sounds older than him and still need the adults around him to remember that he is not old.

Damisi remembers things. He asks questions. He watches. He has a way of arriving at the centre of a matter without taking the adult route there. Sometimes he speaks about school and friends. He thinks some of his teachers are cool. Sometimes he mentions being alone, or not having someone to play with. Sometimes he talks as if the ordinary world of school, games, teachers, friends, and small rivalries is enough to fill the whole sky, which is how it should be for a child.

He is kind to people in the contact centre.

He is respectful.

He notices the adults around him without losing his own childlike directness. Abraham is proud of that, but pride is not the whole feeling. There is also a tenderness that comes from watching a child learn how to behave well inside arrangements he did not create. A father wants manners in his son. He does not want the son to need manners as armour.

Damisi wants more time.

Once he said, "I want to do this every Saturday."

There was no speech attached to it. No adult argument. No accusation. Just the arithmetic of a child who had discovered that one measure of time was not enough and had proposed the next obvious unit. Every Saturday. That was how he put it. Simple. Practical. Devastating because it was simple.

Abraham had to receive the sentence without making Damisi responsible for what it did to him.

That was part of fatherhood now: to let the boy speak freely and not turn the boy's freedom into the father's display of pain.

They played sports-car races. Damisi liked competing. He did not like losing, which Abraham understood well enough. A child who wants to win is alive to the game. Abraham did not let him win everything. That would have been easier in the moment and worse in the long run. He wanted Damisi to know the dignity of earning a win and the safety of surviving a loss.

When Damisi won, he enjoyed it.

Sometimes, after winning, he helped Abraham win some too.

That detail stays with Abraham. Not because it proves anything. Because it reveals the boy. Competitive, yes. Dramatic, yes. But not mean. Capable of wanting victory and still wanting his father included in joy.

He likes swimming and is in an elite group for his age. He likes pizza and dinosaurs. He likes jokes. He likes dancing. His favourite colour was red, then yellow. His favourite animal used to be snake; lately it has been tiger. He often prefers villains, the way many children do, drawn to the big gestures and the music of danger without carrying adult meanings into it. Scar from The Lion King can hold a child's imagination without requiring an adult diagnosis.

Abraham lets these details remain ordinary.

That is important.

A child should be allowed to like tigers without becoming a symbol.

He should be allowed to prefer villains without someone making a sermon out of it.

He should be allowed to love pizza, dinosaurs, jokes, dancing, speed, colour, competition, and escape plans through windows without every preference being recruited into an adult narrative.

Damisi is not an argument.

He is a boy.

Abraham taught him to skate. There is something honest about skating with a child. The body cannot pretend. Balance is either there or not there. The fear of falling has to be met at the level of knees, feet, hands, and trust. Abraham could stand near him, guide him, encourage him, let him try. He could not learn it for him. That is fatherhood in miniature: close enough to help, far enough to let the child discover his own strength.

Damisi tells Abraham he misses him.

Abraham tells him the same.

He tells Damisi he loves him all the time. He tells him there is nothing Damisi can do to make him love him more, and nothing he can do to make him love him less. Abraham says this deliberately. He does not treat it as decoration. A child should not have to earn the love that formed him. A child should not feel he has to perform goodness, cleverness, cheerfulness, or loyalty to keep a father close.

Abraham knows too much about inherited weight to be careless here.

David Senior became responsible too early. He carried what had to be carried, and Abraham honours him for it. But honour is not repetition. Abraham does not want Damisi to become an adult before his time. He does not want his son to inherit the family strength without also inheriting permission to be small, silly, disappointed, wrong, playful, needy, and loved.

Sometimes Damisi tries to stop Abraham from being sad.

That is the detail Abraham handles most carefully, even inside himself.

The tenderness of it is almost unbearable. A boy notices something in his father and wants to fix it. That can look beautiful if the adult is selfish enough to receive it only as comfort. Abraham cannot receive it that way. He is moved by it. He is proud of the kindness in his son. But he also knows a child should not become responsible for keeping his father whole.

So he tells him.

You are a child.

You do not need to make Daddy happy.

You do not need to be perfect.

Nothing you do can make me love you more or less.

The words are simple because the truth has to be simple enough for a child to carry without strain. Abraham cannot give him unrestricted time in the way he wants. He cannot change every room. He cannot make every adult arrangement disappear. But he can refuse to let Damisi believe that love is a wage paid for emotional labour.

That refusal is part of what Abraham is trying to pass on.

Not the fight.

Not the bitterness.

Not the adult explanations.

Presence. Gentleness. A love that does not make the child manage the parent. A father who tells the truth of affection without asking the child to hold the full truth of pain.

Damisi will not understand all of that now, and he should not have to.

For now, he should be allowed to be the boy who wants every Saturday. The boy who wants to escape through the window. The boy who races sports cars and does not like losing. The boy who likes dinosaurs and pizza, jokes and dancing, red and yellow, snakes and tigers. The boy who asks questions adults cannot answer cleanly. The boy who helps his father win sometimes after winning himself.

The boy.

That is the word Abraham keeps returning to.

Before any case.

Before any argument.

Before any inherited duty.

Before any adult has the chance to make him carry more than he should.

Damisi is a boy.

And Abraham's task is to love him in a way that lets him remain one for as long as childhood will allow.

# Chapter 20 — Love Does Not Fear

The sentence changed while he was writing.

Not in a church. Not during prayer. Not in the heat of a crisis where a man can mistake intensity for revelation. Abraham was at the page, preparing this book, writing down the years in the order they had not lived themselves, and a verse he had carried for a long time began to look different from the way he had once used it.

The page was ordinary.

That mattered.

There was no fire in the room. No voice. No trance. No sudden arrangement of circumstances arriving at the last possible moment. There was only the work of setting down what had happened and trying not to make the writing more certain than the life had been. He had written enough by then to see the pattern: provision at the edge of need, survival beside loss, doors opening when he had already prepared himself for them to close, people dying while he remained.

The pattern had once made him feel chosen.

It had also made him careless.

That second sentence was harder to write.

Abraham had not understood this at the time. When he was younger, and especially when the road seemed to keep forming beneath his feet just before he needed it, he had read provision as approval. If money arrived when the deadline was near, if a stranger helped when the path was blocked, if an impossible thing became possible at the final moment, he took it as confirmation that the direction itself had been endorsed.

Sometimes it was mercy.

It was not always permission.

That was the difference he had not known how to hold.

The same mistake entered his reading of love. He had known the verse: "There is no fear in love; but perfect love casteth out fear." He had believed it. He still believes it. But belief is not the same as understanding, and understanding is not the same at forty as it was when the verse first became useful to him.

There was a time when Abraham heard "love does not fear" and thought it meant warning signs should not stop him. If he loved, he should proceed. If he was afraid, he should overcome the fear by stepping forward. If there were red flags, then perhaps faith meant refusing to be governed by them. He had mistaken movement for courage. He had mistaken intensity for faith. He had mistaken the absence of hesitation for the presence of God.

The sentence was true.

His use of it was not.

Writing the book forced him to look again. Not at the verse first, but at the lives around it. Jumoke. David. Funke. David Senior. Damisi. The marriage. The fight. The years of being preserved without being spared. The question that had been forming under the story was not whether God had protected him. Abraham could not honestly say he had not been protected. He had survived too many crossings to pretend survival was accidental.

The question was what that protection meant when pain kept falling close to him.

There had been a fear in him that he barely wanted to name: that something guarded his life rigorously while failing to guard everyone he loved with the same force. That fear did not make him unbelieving. It made him human. A man can believe in God and still look at the graves behind him with questions that do not become smaller because he knows the right verses.

He had once tried to answer the fear by refusing to be afraid.

Now he saw the flaw.

Refusing fear is not the same as receiving wisdom.

Seeing a red flag and pausing is not lovelessness. It may be the first honest act love performs.

Love does not abolish wisdom.

Love gives courage to obey wisdom.

That was the correction.

It did not arrive as comfort. It arrived almost as rebuke, but not the kind that humiliates. The kind that gives a man back responsibility. Abraham could no longer blame love for the places where he had refused caution. He could no longer call every warning fear. He could no longer treat intensity as proof that the thing was holy. Love was larger than fear. It did not need to behave like panic.

Love could wait.

Love could test.

Love could say no.

Love could step back from a fight that did not need to be entered.

Love could also continue when stepping back was no longer the faithful thing.

That was the harder balance. If wisdom had taught him to avoid unnecessary battles, fatherhood had taught him that some battles cannot be avoided without becoming another kind of fear. Damisi existed. That changed the moral shape of everything. Abraham could not use wisdom as a clean name for retreat. He could not mistake caution for disappearance. A father does not prove love by entering every fire. He also does not prove wisdom by leaving his child to explain an absence.

So the correction did not make life simpler.

It made the burden truer.

Love was not recklessness. It was not denial. It was not pretending pain had been redeemed simply because Abraham could write about it now. Love did not make death less death. It did not make Jumoke's absence smaller, or David's, or Funke's. It did not turn the years with Damisi into a lesson that justified their cost. It did not say everything worked out.

It said hope had not been killed.

That is different.

Hope is not the same as optimism. Optimism wants the next page to be easier. Hope can remain when the next page is not. Hope does not deny death. It refuses to let death become the only interpreter.

That was as far as Abraham could go without lying.

He could not write that he understood every loss. He did not. He could not write that pain had made him grateful for pain. It had not. He could not write that the years had ended cleanly. They had not. But he could write that fear was no longer allowed to be the final authority over what love required of him.

Fear had torment.

He knew that part too well.

Fear had made him rush. Fear had made him interpret speed as destiny. Fear had made him treat warnings as enemies of faith. Fear had also tempted him, later, to withdraw, to protect what remained of himself by becoming unavailable to the child who needed him.

Love corrected both errors.

It told him not to call recklessness courage.

It told him not to call disappearance wisdom.

At the page, that was what finally became visible. The verse had not changed. The man reading it had been changed by the cost of his earlier reading. He no longer needed love to mean the absence of caution. He no longer needed wisdom to mean the absence of pain. He needed something harder and more durable: courage joined to wisdom, hope joined to truth, faith that could keep moving without pretending the road was safe.

The sentence changed while he was writing.

Or perhaps it had always been waiting for him to become the kind of man who could read it without using it to excuse what he wanted.

There is no fear in love.

Not because love closes its eyes.

Because love, once disciplined, can look directly at what should be feared and still choose what is true.

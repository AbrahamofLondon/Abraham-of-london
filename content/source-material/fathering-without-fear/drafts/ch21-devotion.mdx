# Chapter 21 — Devotion

He was asleep when he felt the tap on his back.

The story, as David Senior told it, began after his own father died. He was not yet the father Abraham would know. He was a son who had lost a father, a young man with siblings behind him and a house already altered by death.

In the dream, his father told him to sit up.

So he sat up.

The dead father told the living son that he was not alone. Then he asked for two promises. Make sure the siblings are well educated. Make sure the four siblings are never separated.

He also told him provision would come for their needs.

What Abraham received was family testimony, carried with the seriousness of a man who had lived by it. David Senior did not tell the story as decoration. He told it as the beginning of a charge. A father had died. A son had been tapped awake. A life had been given its shape.

That is where David Senior's devotion began.

Not in temperament.

In obligation.

The difference matters. Generosity can be occasional. Kindness can arrive with mood. Devotion, as David Senior lived it, had structure. It had people attached to it. It had names, fees, food, school, letters, journeys, siblings who could not be allowed to scatter, and needs that did not wait for the person responsible to feel ready.

He took the promises seriously.

He made education part of obedience. He made unity part of obedience. He did not describe responsibility as a feeling. He treated it as the thing his life had been handed. The provision his father had spoken of did not free him from labour. It gave him a way to keep labouring when the arithmetic did not look possible.

That was the pattern Abraham later inherited before he knew he was inheriting anything.

David Senior was not only a man who gave. He was a man who had been told to give an account. He carried his siblings not because he enjoyed carrying weight, but because the weight had been placed in his hands and he believed the placing had meaning.

Decades later, around 1983, the story returned in another form.

David Senior said his father visited him again. This time the matter was not the education of the siblings or their unity. It was burial. He was reminded that the burial rites had not been properly observed. He was told to prepare.

The instruction did not arrive with the money attached.

That is another part of the pattern.

He had to make plans before he could see provision. Within about three months, provision came by a route he could not have arranged. He went for an interview where about two hundred candidates were present. In that crowd was the ordinary humiliation of need: many people waiting, each carrying a reason the job mattered, each hoping to be seen.

Then a prior encounter returned.

Someone David Senior had previously met on his farm turned out to be connected to the person in authority over the interview. David Senior was called out from the crowd. He was placed in a room and told to wait.

He waited from about seven in the morning until about eight at night.

At the end of the day, he was told that he had effectively completed his first day at work because of his patience and attitude.

That job funded the burial ceremony.

It also enabled him to build a house in Isua.

A promise can sound large when it is spoken. It becomes real when it becomes fees paid, siblings kept, rites completed, walls raised, a place built in the village that carried the family's name and memory. David Senior's life kept turning invisible obligation into visible structure.

This is the kind of father Abraham later lost.

Abraham did not lose a symbol. He lost the person whose life had made responsibility feel normal before Abraham had language for it.

The school-fees woman came after he died.

She did not know he was dead. She came to collect the second half of her son's school fees. David Senior had committed himself to helping her. At the time, he had no job or pension. That is the part that stays. Not the amount. Not the gesture as public virtue. The shape of it: a man without settled income making himself responsible for a child's education because need had come near enough for him to answer.

The woman came expecting the promise to continue.

The man who made it was gone.

David Senior's devotion had outlived his body in the form of obligations other people still expected him to meet. Death had stopped him. It had not stopped the evidence of what he had been doing.

He died on 15 August 2017.

Abraham returned to Nigeria on 10 September 2017 to bury him.

The grief was discovering, again and again, that the man who had seemed ordinary because he was always there had been holding more than his children had known. After death, the hidden parts of a life begin to send messengers. A woman at the door. A story remembered. A debt of kindness. A house. A sibling still educated. A family still not scattered.

David Senior had not performed devotion.

He had arranged his life around it.

Abraham had seen pieces of this as a son. He had seen the steadiness, the prayers, the way his father could make responsibility sound like the obvious next thing rather than an exceptional act. But sons often receive a father's devotion as weather. It surrounds them before they understand its cost. They live inside it, complain inside it, depend on it, resist it, and only later discover that what felt like atmosphere was labour.

The dream after his father's death had become a life.

The later visitation had become a job, a burial, a house.

The promise had become education and unity.

Abraham lost the man who had made obedience look practical. He lost the person who had lived as though provision was not a reason to avoid responsibility but a reason to accept it before the route was visible.

He had been tapped awake.

He had sat up.

He had promised.

And for the rest of his life, as Abraham received the story, he lived as though the promises were still being heard.

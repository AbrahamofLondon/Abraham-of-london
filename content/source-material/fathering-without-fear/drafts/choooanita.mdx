# Chapter 19 — The Version in His Head

Abraham could still dance.

That mattered more than he would have admitted. Not because dancing solved anything, and not because the rhythm changed the facts of his life. It did not change the visa. It did not change the court orders. It did not change the rent arrears or the waiting or the disciplined humiliation of having to explain himself to systems that never saw the full man.

But for a few hours, on a dance floor near Oxford Street, he could remember that he had a body before he had a case.

The place was Be At One. February 14, 2024. Adults had gathered under coloured lights to network, drink, laugh too loudly, exchange names, and pretend the city had not spent the week exhausting them. Abraham moved easily through that room. He had been told often enough that he was a good dancer that he no longer argued with the evidence. The music found him. He did not chase it.

There were women there who laughed quickly and spoke with the generous ease of people far from home. Sheryl was American. Tamara was American. They drew him into the evening the way certain people do when they decide a stranger already belongs. Abraham could do that too. He could enter a room and make it feel less closed. He had spent years building, connecting, creating circles of people where London would otherwise have left everyone alone inside their separate weather.

This was part of the life the book might forget if it only followed the battles.

He was not always sitting before a judge. He was not always waiting outside a contact centre. He was not always hungry, praying, writing statements, answering letters, checking email, wondering whether another delay would swallow another year of his son’s childhood.

He was also a man in a white shirt, laughing under low light, moving his shoulders to music, meeting people, building community, still imagining a future family even after the first one had broken in his hands.

At forty-eight, that imagination was not a luxury. It was a question pressing against the wall of time. Legacy was not an abstract word to Abraham. It had a face. It had Damisi’s face. It had David Senior’s face. It had the unfinished weight of men who had built and died and left others to carry what remained. If Abraham said he believed in family, then he had to face the harder question: what kind of family was still possible now?

He did not answer that on the dance floor. Nobody answers that under coloured lights.

But he was alive enough to ask.

A few weeks later, Sheryl mentioned a candlelight dinner in East London. She was going with Jenny, another American. It was casual, the sort of invitation that arrives without ceremony and becomes important only when life is read backwards.

Abraham thought about Samina.

He had noticed her before. She carried herself with a guarded softness, as if part of her wanted to be reached and another part had learned the cost of being reachable. Abraham was drawn to that. He had not yet learned to distrust the part of himself that mistook guardedness for depth. He invited her to come with him.

She said yes.

The evening had the usual London arrangement: a restaurant table, brick walls, glasses catching light, people leaning across plates to hear one another properly. Abraham arrived with Samina. Sheryl and Tamara were there. Jenny joined them. The table filled.

Then Anita arrived.

At first she was only a motion near him. A bottle slipped, or nearly slipped. Abraham caught it by instinct. It was such a small thing that a wiser man might not have remembered it. But memory is not governed by size. He looked up, saw her, and something in him registered the moment before he gave himself permission to understand it.

There was a spark.

Not theatre. Not music swelling. Not destiny announcing itself from the ceiling. Just the small adjustment that happens when attention moves before the mind has approved it.

She apologised. He handed the bottle back. He was polite. He did not linger.

He was there with Samina.

That mattered to him. He had a code, even when life around him had become disorder. He did not scatter himself because another woman had appeared. He returned to the table, to the conversation he had chosen, to the direction he thought he was taking.

Later, Anita sat with the group. She was Tamara’s colleague. She was not a passing stranger after all. The evening continued. Abraham danced because Abraham danced. People laughed. Samina left first. Others left after her. The city began doing what London does late at night: dividing a table into trains, platforms, separate journeys home.

Abraham found himself walking with Anita.

At the station, he was clear with her.

He told her he was interested in Samina. He told her he was not looking elsewhere. He was open to friendship. It was not a grand speech. It was just the decent thing to say. He had learned enough from life to know that ambiguity is often cowardice dressed as charm.

Anita accepted it.

Abraham left believing he had behaved properly.

For a while, Samina remained the direction.

Then the pattern began to show.

She would speak with him on the phone while driving home from work. The calls could stretch. Her voice would loosen. She would talk until she reached home, and then the call would end. Not always abruptly, but consistently enough for Abraham to begin hearing the structure beneath it. After that, she was unavailable. Busy. Tired. Somewhere else.

He paid attention.

That mattered too. The younger Abraham might have explained everything away because explanation was easier than adjustment. This Abraham had suffered enough to know that affection without clarity can become a corridor with no door at the end. He understood, slowly, that Samina’s life was not free in the way he needed it to be free. She was still inside a situation that had not resolved itself, and Abraham was not the person who could resolve it for her.

Abraham did not need to become heroic there. He needed to leave.

So he did.

Not angrily. Not as a sermon. Not with the vanity of a man congratulating himself for discipline. He simply stepped back from a situation that had begun to require more blindness than wisdom.

Then Anita reappeared.

Or perhaps she had never fully left the corner of his mind.

They went dancing. At first it was friendship, or the shape of friendship. Abraham could still tell himself that. They met at Be At One again. He was careful. He was warm. He was interested. He was still trying to be a gentleman in a city that often rewarded vagueness more than honour.

He introduced her to the wider group — couples, singles, friends, people gathered around evenings out and ordinary laughter. Abraham was not merely looking for romance. He was trying to build a life with other people in it. A life where community could exist without everyone needing to be in crisis first. A life where a man could dance, eat, talk, walk someone to a station, and not always be bracing for impact.

That was part of the dream too.

Not only wife. Not only children. Not only legacy in the heavy ancestral sense.

Also table. Music. Friends. Food. People who checked in. People who remained.

One day Anita called. She had been going to Heathrow but no longer needed to. She remembered Abraham lived in Hounslow and wanted to say hello.

She drove down.

That is how some doors open: not as confession, not as pursuit, but as convenience with timing inside it.

They spent the evening together. Abraham spoke plainly about his life. He did that too early sometimes. He called it honesty, and much of it was honesty. But honesty can also be a hunger. A man who has been misread for long enough can begin offering his whole file to anyone who looks as if she might read it kindly.

He told her about the visa. About work. About the impossibility of working and the impossibility of not working. About Damisi. About the court-shaped discipline of fatherhood. About waiting. About trying to build while being legally and financially restrained.

Anita listened.

She did not run. That seemed important.

She told him something about herself too. She said she was dismissive avoidant.

Abraham did not know what that meant.

He heard the words, but they did not yet have teeth. Attachment theory had not entered his working vocabulary. He knew other language. He knew betrayal. He knew instability. He knew spiritual danger. He knew, by then, enough about certain kinds of darkness to believe that if someone was not cruel, not predatory, not openly destructive, then love and patience could carry the rest.

He was wrong.

Not because Anita was evil. That would be too simple, and simplicity is often the liar’s version of clarity.

He was wrong because he did not understand that some people do not have to intend harm in order to repeat it. Some people leave by instinct. Some people withdraw when closeness becomes real. Some people tell the truth about themselves early, and the listener is too busy building a future to hear the warning inside the truth.

Anita said, in effect, be careful.

Abraham heard playfulness.

They began dating.

Nothing else was working, so he watered that one living thing with more hope than it could bear.

At that time, Abraham had no right to work. He had no access to public funds. He was surviving on savings preserved only because he had stopped paying lawyers for hearings that would have consumed money he no longer had. The final contact order had been made. The material had gone to the Home Office. He believed, with the tired optimism of a man who needed one part of life to make sense, that the visa would soon be resolved.

He had learned to prepare for the worst, but even he could still be surprised by how long the worst could keep renewing itself.

After he paid his landlady the balance of the arrears that had gathered over the previous year, she gave him notice. No fault. She wanted the room back.

He took it the way he took most blows: first into the body, then into interpretation.

Maybe there was a blessing inside it. Maybe this was not disruption but rearrangement. Maybe God, providence, timing, whatever name could be given to mercy under pressure, was moving him closer to the life he was trying to build.

He found a penthouse room nine minutes from Anita.

Nine minutes.

A man under pressure can make theology out of distance.

He saw alignment. He saw the possibility of nearness. He saw a story beginning to arrange itself in his favour. He told Anita. He wanted her to see what he saw: that after years of being made to wait outside doors, a door had opened near the woman he was beginning to imagine beside him.

He did not yet know that proximity is not proof.

He moved closer.

In hindsight, he could see the mistakes more cleanly. He became too available. Too agreeable. Too eager to show that he could be safe, consistent, useful, understanding. He made himself predictable in the way anxious men often do when they are trying to earn permanence from someone whose nervous system reads permanence as pressure.

He told her everything.

There is a kind of openness that is noble. There is another kind that is bargaining.

Abraham did not know which one he was practising.

By October, Anita began to withdraw.

Not dramatically. That would have been easier. Drama gives a man something to answer. Withdrawal asks him to argue with absence.

The daily messages thinned. Calls became less certain. Replies came later. Energy cooled. Anita travelled to Kenya and stayed longer than expected. She postponed her return. Then postponed again. Abraham told himself there were reasons. There are always reasons if a man needs them badly enough.

By then he was broke. Two months behind on rent. Facing another eviction. Still without a visa. Still trying to remain close enough to Damisi for the boy to know his father had not gone anywhere.

Then Anita called from Heathrow.

She would call back, she said.

She did not.

The next day, there was an explanation. An Uber driver. A conversation. Something that had occupied the space where the promised call should have been.

The detail did not matter as much as the place it touched.

Abraham knew that place. The small place inside him where waiting had become familiar. Waiting for calls. Waiting for decisions. Waiting for permission. Waiting for people to remember that he existed after they left the room.

He should have known then that the old wound had entered the relationship before wisdom did.

But most wounds do not introduce themselves. They simply begin choosing.

On February 4, 2025, Anita said the one thing that ended the argument inside him.

She told him he was not being practical. She said, in substance, that focusing everything on Damisi did not make sense. He could move city. He could move country. He could find work elsewhere, stabilise himself, build his career, return later. His son would still be his son.

It was not shouted. It did not need to be.

Some sentences do not become severe because of volume. They become severe because they touch the line a man has already decided he will not cross.

Abraham had lost many things. He had lost money. He had lost rooms. He had lost years to waiting. He had lost reputation in rooms where he could not fully defend himself. He had lost ease. He had lost sleep. He had lost the right to be careless.

He would not lose presence.

Damisi was in London. Therefore Abraham stayed in London.

That was not strategy. It was fatherhood.

He could not explain it to Anita in a way that would make sense if it did not already make sense. How could he explain that a father’s location can become a promise? That a child may not understand visa restrictions, legal fees, arrears, or career positioning, but he understands arrival and absence? That after everything Abraham had endured to remain in the boy’s life, leaving the city for opportunity would have felt like helping the wound finish its work?

He ended the conversation.

He did not call her back.

After that, he began reading.

There is a special education available to a man with no job, no money, no visa, no relationship left to manage, and too much silence in the room. He can finally hear the pattern.

He typed the words she had given him months earlier. Dismissive avoidant.

Now the phrase had weight.

He read about distance disguised as independence. He read about closeness becoming threat. He read about people who could want love and fear the very dependency love awakens. He read about withdrawal, deactivation, the need to regain space when intimacy begins to feel like invasion.

Then he read about himself.

That was harder.

He read about anxious attachment. About abandonment. About children who learn to perform for closeness. About people who become too open, too available, too patient with inconsistency, because somewhere in them a younger self is still trying to persuade someone to stay.

He did not want his childhood to be part of the evidence. He had loved his childhood. He had defended it. He had been fed, educated, protected in the ways his family understood protection. His father had worked. His mother had held. The household had carried faith, ambition, order, and pride.

But memory is not disproved by gratitude.

A few months after Jumoke died, Abraham was sent to Aquinas. He did not return home for a year. His father was busy, always building, always carrying responsibilities that had begun long before Abraham was born. None of this had to be cruelty in order to leave a mark.

That was the part he had resisted.

A wound does not require a villain.

He began to understand that he had been choosing women from a place he had never examined. Women who were not fully available. Women whose distance gave him work to do. Women who made him prove patience, prove steadiness, prove that he would not leave, while some younger part of him hoped the performance would be rewarded with permanence.

He thought he was choosing love.

He was also choosing at the level of his wound.

The phrase did not comfort him. It accused him too cleanly.

Because if it was true, then Anita was not merely another disappointment. She was a mirror. Samina had been a mirror too. So had others before them. Each one different in biography, similar in the place they touched inside him. The same soul, he thought sometimes, though he knew that was not fair as fact. It was truer as feeling. Different faces. Same lesson.

And the lesson had followed him beyond romance.

By the time Abraham was truly broke, the group had thinned.

That is another thing poverty reveals. Not because people are wicked, but because convenience has a short memory. Invitations slowed when he could no longer attend. The rhythm of outings continued without him. The table he had helped gather still existed somewhere in the city, but he was no longer always in the photograph.

Olu remained.

Nene remained.

They did not make speeches about loyalty. They brought food. They checked in. They helped with sustenance. They came with the ordinary mercy of people who understood that love is not proved by language when someone cannot eat.

Abraham noticed that too.

Maybe his choosing had never been confined to women. Maybe he had also chosen friendships from the same wound — people who reflected the places in him still waiting to be chosen back. Maybe he had mistaken proximity for presence, laughter for loyalty, access for attachment.

That did not make the vanished people evil. It made the remaining people clearer.

The revelation did not arrive as triumph. It arrived with rent unpaid, the visa unresolved, another room under threat, and the phone quieter than it had been when the dancing started.

He sat in the penthouse he had once read as alignment.

The room had not lied. He had.

Not deliberately. Not foolishly in the cheap sense. He had lied the way hurting people lie when hope begins arranging facts into mercy before wisdom has finished reading them.

Nine minutes from Anita had not been providence. Or if it had been, it was not the kind he thought. It had brought him close enough to see himself.

On the table, there were the ordinary objects of a life under pressure: phone, papers, notices, the remnants of food brought by people who had not disappeared. London moved outside as if nothing sacred was happening. Trains ran. Messages went unanswered. Other people fell in love, paid rent, went to work, came home, made dinner, slept.

Abraham thought about Damisi.

That was where the chapter had to settle. Not with Anita. Not with attachment theory. Not with the cleverness of a man who had finally found language for old pain.

Damisi.

The boy was the line that had held.

When everything else invited Abraham to negotiate himself down — money, romance, career, loneliness, fear — fatherhood remained the part of him that refused. He might misread a woman. He might misread proximity. He might mistake silence for depth, warning for softness, distance for mystery. But he would not misread that.

His son was in London.

So he stayed.

And because he stayed, he began to see.

Not all at once. Not cleanly. Not in the way people describe healing after the wound has become a lesson fit for public use. He still missed the version of Anita he had built. His head knew she was not that woman. His heart lagged behind, still grieving a future that had never actually existed.

That was the embarrassment of it.

He had not only lost a relationship. He had lost the woman he had imagined into being — the one who would help him rebuild family, fill a home, stand near the legacy dream, make the long years of fighting feel less barren. Letting go of Anita required letting go of the architecture he had placed around her.

He was almost fifty. That made the grief sharper. Time had become less theoretical. The question of family was no longer a distant field. It was land under his feet, and the ground was narrowing.

Still, Abraham understood something in that room that he had not understood on the dance floor.

A man can survive public battles and still be governed by private wounds.

He can stand before systems and refuse to disappear, yet keep choosing people who require him to disappear quietly inside relationship.

He can call it love.

He can call it patience.

He can call it faith.

But if it teaches him to abandon himself in order to be kept, it is not love doing the teaching.

The phone lit up.

A message from Olu. Nene had made food.

Abraham looked at it for a while before touching the screen.

The mercy was not dramatic. It rarely was. It came through ordinary hands, ordinary stew, ordinary people who did not need him to perform strength before they would bring help.

He replied.

Then he sat still.

The room was not what he had imagined. The relationship had not become what he had hoped. The group had not remained as he had built it. The visa had still not come. The rent still had to be faced. Damisi still had to be reached through permission, timing, and the discipline of showing up.

But Abraham was beginning to read the wound.

That mattered.

Not because reading healed it immediately. It did not. But a wound that can be read can no longer choose entirely in secret.

He had thought the chapter was about Anita.

It was not.

It was about the version in his head — the woman, the future, the room, the community, the healed life — and the painful mercy of losing it before it became another prison.

He put the phone down.

Outside, London continued without explanation.

Inside, Abraham remained where he had chosen to remain.

Near his son.

Near the wound.

Near the truth.

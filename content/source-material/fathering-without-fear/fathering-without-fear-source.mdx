FATHERING WITHOUT FEAR
The Story They Thought They Knew

A Memoir
Abraham of London

SOURCE MATERIAL — WORKING DRAFT

## Overview

Three people died telling Abraham of London to live. His twin sister Jumoke, dead at seven, appeared to him in the kitchen on the day of her death and made him promise to fulfil a destiny he did not yet understand. His brother David — his closest companion since childhood, present at every major turn of his life — appeared to him in a trance the night before he too died, his absence from a wedding the only warning anyone received. His sister Funke died two days before his wife filed for divorce, a debt he still carries. Each death left him with the same instruction, delivered in different voices across four decades: something always happens. Do not disappear.

FATHERING WITHOUT FEAR is the story of what it costs a man to keep that promise — across a Lagos childhood shaped by ancestral expectation and reincarnation belief, a near-death experience at age eleven that left him bargaining with God in a burning room, a marriage in London built on what he now recognises as calculated isolation, and a seven-year family court battle to remain father to the British son he was nearly written out of the story of raising.

This is not a misery memoir, and it is not a courtroom thriller. It is a book about inheritance — what is passed down through blood, through grief, through unrelenting institutional indifference — and about a man who learned, scene by scene, death by death, that refusing to disappear is itself a form of faith.

   "Love does not fear, because fear has to do with punishment. The one
   who fears has not been made perfect in love." — 1 John 4:18, the
   verse he returns to throughout the book

## Comparable Titles

This book sits at the intersection of three distinct, commercially proven memoir traditions — and borrows its structural confidence from each:

- EDUCATED (Tara Westover) — for its proof that a story rooted in an extreme, almost unbelievable upbringing earns mainstream literary trust through precision of detail and restraint of judgment, not sensationalism.

- THE GLASS CASTLE (Jeannette Walls) — for its model of interwoven timelines, where childhood material and adult reckoning are braided rather than told in strict sequence, and for showing love and harm can coexist in the same family without contradiction.

- WHEN BREATH BECOMES AIR (Paul Kalanithi) — for proving that explicit, unapologetic faith and mortality can anchor a book to massive crossover commercial success when the prose is precise enough to earn the reader's trust before asking for their belief.

The white space this book occupies and no major recent bestseller has claimed: a West African spiritual inheritance narrative collides with the British family court system. No comparable title exists that combines ancestral African cosmology, documented near-death experience, and a contemporary UK legal battle for fatherhood. This is a genuinely uncrowded shelf.

## Why This Book, Why Now

Three converging cultural currents make this the right moment for this story:

- The fatherhood reckoning. Books and reporting on fathers' rights, parental alienation, and family court reform have moved from niche advocacy into mainstream conversation in both the UK and US. This book offers the first-person literary account that the policy conversation lacks.

- The ancestral-spiritual memoir resurgence. Readers are hungry for spiritual narratives that are specific rather than generic — rooted in a real lineage and real cosmology, not wellness-industry abstraction. This book offers Yoruba spiritual inheritance with total narrative specificity: Iyalode, reincarnation belief, named ancestors, a documented near-death bargain.

- The immigrant-father story has not yet been told from this angle. Existing immigrant memoirs largely centre the immigrant's own coming-of-age. This book centres what happens after — when the immigrant becomes a father fighting a Western institution for the right to remain one.

## Structural Architecture

The book is built on a single structural decision: two timelines, interwoven, each illuminating the other. The two threads converge structurally at the book's midpoint and again at its close: the reader comes to understand that the same forces — inheritance, prophecy, promise, refusal to disappear — that shaped the boy in Lagos are the exact forces sustaining the father in the London courtroom. The ancestral material is never decoration. It is the engine that explains why this particular man did not fold.

EARNING THE READER'S BELIEF — A NOTE ON THE SPIRITUAL MATERIAL

The book's supernatural material — the near-death experience, the visitations from Jumoke and David, the trance — is extraordinary, and it is true to the author's lived experience. The narrative strategy is not to lead with it, and not to apologise for it, but to earn it: the reader meets Abraham first as a precise, credible, self-aware narrator in the grounded present-day material (the courtroom, the bureaucracy, the specific texture of fatherhood under siege), so that by the time the book asks the reader to sit inside a trance the night before a confirmed death, that reader has already learned to trust this narrator's eye for detail and his honesty about his own blind spots. Conviction is presented as fact of the author's experience; interpretation is left, deliberately, to the reader.

## Part & Chapter Architecture

Approximately 90,000–100,000 words across four parts and twenty-eight chapters.

### Part One: The Named Child

Establishes the ancestral inheritance and the present-day stakes in the same breath.

- Chapter 1 — Something Always Happens
  (Present: a specific, concrete courtroom or contact-centre scene from
  2026, opening mid-action, establishing voice and stakes before any
  history is explained)

- Chapter 2 — Isua to Agege
  (Ancestral: Iyalode and her husband's 1940s journey on foot from Osun
  to Lagos; the tailor, the farmer, the young men he raised into
  trades; his death by chosen euthanasia)

- Chapter 3 — The Boy Who Was Already Old
  (Ancestral: birth as one of triplets, Iyalode's rejection, his
  father David's certainty, the early vocabulary, the oscillation
  between struggle and brilliance in school)

- Chapter 4 — A Hearing Date
  (Present: the procedural, bureaucratic texture of the family court
  system — its language, its waiting rooms, its quiet erasures)

- Chapter 5 — Jumoke
  (Ancestral: the triplet who died in infancy, Jumoke's death at
  seven, the kitchen visitation, the promise extracted from an
  eleven-year-old boy)

- Chapter 21 — The Spiritual Covering
  (Ancestral: Introduces Iyalode fully as a person — her beauty, her
  elegance, her standing as spiritually powerful in a Yoruba context
  where "Iyalode" denotes a military-and-spiritual female title.
  Establishes her as a force the family regarded as covering, even as
  she lived primarily for herself.)

- Chapter 22 — The Boy Who Became a Father at Fifteen
  (Ancestral: Iyalode's abandonment of her four children in Lagos and
  return to Ondo to remarry. Abraham's father — fifteen years old —
  raised his three younger siblings alone. The visitations from his own
  father, instructing him on how to raise them, with promised resources
  that reliably manifested. This chapter establishes the pattern of
  paternal visitation and provision-by-prophecy that recurs across the
  whole book, including in Abraham's own life.)

- Chapter 23 — The Name That Had to Be Powerful
  (Ancestral: Iyalode is the first person to speak the name of Jesus to
  Abraham as a child. The impression it made — that a name spoken with
  such weight by a woman already regarded as spiritually powerful had to
  denote something greater still. Plant this early; it is the seed of the
  faith that carries the entire book, and it comes from the same woman
  who once rejected him at birth.)

### Part Two: The Bargain

The near-death experience, the deepening of the ancestral mythology, and

- Chapter 6 — The Fire and the Deal
  (Ancestral: the dream-death, hell, the bargain with God, waking to a

- Chapter 27 — Norwich
  (Present, provisional placement in Part Two — pending author-confirmed

  Placement and content both pending; this is a reserved slot, not a
  final position.)

- Chapter 8 — The Girl Who Wrote About Him First
  (Ancestral: national service, Stephanie, the journal entry written

- Chapter 9 — Married by December
  (Present: the speed of the marriage, the choice to leave Nigeria and

- Chapter 10 — The Voice at Noon
  (Ancestral: the threat received the day after the first night with

The deaths that taught him what nothing could kill him meant for the

  (Ancestral: the trance, the vivid absence, the journey to Lagos, the

- Chapter 12 — Brothers
  (Ancestral: David was Abraham's brother. Full stop. His place in the
  family from childhood, what was sacred about a brotherhood that an
  entire school and university generation respected without question.)

- Chapter 13 — Pregnant and Discarded
  (Present: 2018, narrated with full legal and emotional care, the
  turn in the marriage, what can and cannot be said directly — handled
  through scene, consequence, and restraint rather than direct
  accusation)

- Chapter 14 — Funke
  (Ancestral: July 2019, the medical bill he could not pay, two days
  before the divorce filing, the guilt he still carries, the third
  angel)

- Chapter 15 — What the System Sees
  (Present: the early mechanics of the family court process, the first
  hearings, the slow realisation that fatherhood was now a case file)

- Chapter 24 — A Decade of Waiting to Die
  (Ancestral: Iyalode's eventual conversion to Christianity before her
  death. The last decade of her life, spent tired and ready to go.
  Abraham's inexplicable certainty that she would not die until she had
  seen him once more. NOTE: do not draft the specific final-meeting
  scene until Abraham has confirmed and described it directly — do not
  invent sensory detail for a scene not yet given. Hold this chapter at
  outline level only until that material is provided.)

- Chapter 25 — Iyalode at One Hundred and Eighteen
  (Ancestral: Her death in 2006, age 118. Frame this chapter around what
  is confirmed — her age, the year, the pattern of the decade prior —
  without fabricating the final visit. If the final-meeting material
  remains unconfirmed at time of drafting, this chapter should explicitly
  note in development comments that the central scene is pending author
  input, not invent one to fill the gap.)

### Part Four: Something Always Happens

The convergence: present-day fatherhood, the full weight of the
inheritance, and the theology of fearless love that the entire book has
been building toward.

- Chapter 16 — Seven Years
  (Present: the cumulative weight of the legal battle, told through
  specific hearings, specific small cruelties and small victories, not
  abstraction)

- Chapter 17 — Anita and What Comes After Ruin
  (Present: 2024, what it means to let himself be loved again after
  the system and the marriage had both taught him not to trust it)

- Chapter 18 — Damisi
  (Present: the specific, sensory, unguardedly tender material of who
  his son actually is — the chapter the whole book has been earning
  the right to write)

- Chapter 19 — Love Does Not Fear
  (Theological/ancestral synthesis: 1 John 4:18, what fearlessness
  actually means after a lifetime of near-deaths and warnings, the
  difference between fearlessness and recklessness, what he wishes he
  had learned sooner)

- Chapter 28 — Devotion
  (Ancestral: His father's death mid-prayer, household gathered for
  early devotion, the last words — calling on both Iyalode and Jesus in
  the same breath. Born Monday, 12 September 1932; died Monday,
  15 August 2017, age 84. Three decades in Lagos local politics fighting
  corruption, the cost to his fortune and standing, what Abraham believes
  was a death of heartbreak as much as anything physical. This chapter
  should be drafted with extreme care and is one of the emotional peaks
  of the entire book — flag for editorial sensitivity review before wide
  circulation of any draft.)

- Chapter 20 — The Father Who Refuses to Disappear
  (Present: closing chapter, written from inside the still-unresolved
  present of 2026, refusing a false resolution, ending on the promise
  kept rather than the battle won)

## Voice & Craft Approach

- Earned belief, not asserted belief. The supernatural material is narrated with the same precision and sensory grounding as the courtroom material — never softened into metaphor, never inflated into spectacle.

- Restraint around what cannot be told. Material constrained by court order or by protection of his son is handled through implication, structure, and consequence — never through vague gesturing that reads as evasion. Technique: end the scene at the threshold of the constrained event, then open the next scene on its aftermath already in motion — the reader feels the gap as weight, not as omission.

- Specificity over theme. Every chapter opens in a real, sensory, particular moment — a fire, a journal entry, a hearing date — never in summary or abstraction.

- The dead are characters, not symbols. Jumoke, David, and Funke are written with full personhood — their humour, their specific words, their ordinariness before their deaths. Technique: every chapter featuring one of the dead must include at least one mundane, unsentimental detail about them — what they found funny, a phrase they overused, something ordinary — before any account of their death or appearance. Grief earns its weight by first establishing a life, not a loss.

- No villain monologue. The marriage's collapse and the legal battle are narrated through what Abraham experienced and felt, not through speculative interiority assigned to others — preserving both legal safety and literary integrity.

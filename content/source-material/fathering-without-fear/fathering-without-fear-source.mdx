FATHERING WITHOUT FEAR
A Memoir of Faith, Fatherhood, and Inheritance

A Memoir
Abraham of London

SOURCE MATERIAL — WORKING DRAFT

## Overview

Three people died telling Abraham of London to live. His twin sister. His brother. His sister. Each left behind a different wound, but the same command: do not disappear.

His twin sister Jumoke, dead at seven, appeared to him in the kitchen on the day of her death and made him promise to fulfil a destiny he did not yet understand. His brother David — his closest companion since childhood, present at every major turn of his life — appeared to him in a trance the night before he too died, his absence from a wedding the only warning anyone received. His sister Funke died two days before his wife filed for divorce, a debt he still carries. Each death left him with the same instruction, delivered in different voices across four decades: something always happens. Do not disappear.

FATHERING WITHOUT FEAR is the story of what it costs a man to keep that promise — across a Lagos childhood shaped by ancestral expectation and reincarnation belief, a near-death experience at age eleven that left him bargaining with God in a burning room, a marriage in London built on what he now recognises as calculated isolation, and a seven-year family court battle to remain father to the British son he was nearly written out of the story of raising.

A man born into prophecy, grief, and ancestral expectation spends his life being told he must live — only to discover that fatherhood is the final test of whether he will disappear.

This is not a misery memoir, and it is not a courtroom thriller. It is a book about inheritance — what is passed down through blood, through grief, through unrelenting institutional indifference — and about a man who learned, scene by scene, death by death, that refusing to disappear is itself a form of faith.

## Comparable Titles

This book sits at the intersection of three distinct, commercially proven memoir traditions — and borrows its structural confidence from each:

- EDUCATED (Tara Westover) — for its proof that a story rooted in an extreme, almost unbelievable upbringing earns mainstream literary trust through precision of detail and restraint of judgment, not sensationalism.

- THE GLASS CASTLE (Jeannette Walls) — for its model of interwoven timelines, where childhood material and adult reckoning are braided rather than told in strict sequence, and for showing love and harm can coexist in the same family without contradiction.

- WHEN BREATH BECOMES AIR (Paul Kalanithi) — for proving that explicit, unapologetic faith and mortality can anchor a book to massive crossover commercial success when the prose is precise enough to earn the reader's trust before asking for their belief.

- THE RETURN (Hisham Matar) — for its meditation on fatherhood, absence, state power, and the unresolved wound of family history.

The white space this book occupies and no major recent bestseller has claimed: a West African spiritual inheritance narrative collides with the British family court system. No comparable title exists that combines ancestral African cosmology, documented near-death experience, and a contemporary UK legal battle for fatherhood. This is a genuinely uncrowded shelf.

## Why This Book, Why Now

The book's commercial strength is multiple markets, not one. Family court reform is a real and useful press hook, but it is the narrowest of several: literary memoir, African spiritual inheritance, Christian survival narrative, fatherhood under institutional pressure, immigrant manhood in the West, grief and destiny, and the cost of refusing erasure all apply.

Three converging cultural currents make this the right moment for this story:

- The fatherhood reckoning. Books and reporting on fathers' rights, parental alienation, and family court reform have moved from niche advocacy into mainstream conversation in both the UK and US. This book offers the first-person literary account that the policy conversation lacks.

- The ancestral-spiritual memoir resurgence. Readers are hungry for spiritual narratives that are specific rather than generic — rooted in a real lineage and real cosmology, not wellness-industry abstraction. This book offers Yoruba spiritual inheritance with total narrative specificity: Iyalode, reincarnation belief, named ancestors, a documented near-death bargain.

- The immigrant-father story has not yet been told from this angle. Existing immigrant memoirs largely centre the immigrant's own coming-of-age. This book centres what happens after — when the immigrant becomes a father fighting a Western institution for the right to remain one.

## Structural Architecture

The book is built on a single structural decision: two timelines, interwoven, each illuminating the other. The two threads converge structurally at the book's midpoint and again at its close: the reader comes to understand that the same forces — inheritance, prophecy, promise, refusal to disappear — that shaped the boy in Lagos are the exact forces sustaining the father in the London courtroom. The ancestral material is never decoration. It is the engine that explains why this particular man did not fold.

EARNING THE READER'S BELIEF — A NOTE ON THE SPIRITUAL MATERIAL

The book's supernatural material — the near-death experience, the visitations from Jumoke and David, the trance — is extraordinary, and it is true to the author's lived experience. The narrative strategy is not to lead with it, and not to apologise for it, but to earn it: the reader meets Abraham first as a precise, credible, self-aware narrator in the grounded present-day material (the courtroom, the bureaucracy, the specific texture of fatherhood under siege), so that by the time the book asks the reader to sit inside a trance the night before a confirmed death, that reader has already learned to trust this narrator's eye for detail and his honesty about his own blind spots. Conviction is presented as fact of the author's experience; interpretation is left, deliberately, to the reader.

## The Five Questions

The manuscript is governed by five recurring questions. Every chapter should serve at least one.

1. What does a man inherit before he understands himself?
2. What does death demand from the living?
3. What does faith mean when God preserves your life but not your comfort?
4. What does a father owe a child when the system rewards disappearance?
5. What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying the people still inside the story?

## Voice & Craft Approach

The governing register is controlled fire, not anger. The book must not read as a legal defence, a grievance document, a fathers' rights manifesto, a spiritual spectacle, or a revenge memoir. It should read as a man testifying under oath before God, history, his son, and himself — restrained, grave, precise, and devastating.

- Earned belief, not asserted belief. The supernatural material is narrated with the same precision and sensory grounding as the courtroom material — never softened into metaphor, never inflated into spectacle.

- Restraint around what cannot be told. Material constrained by court order or by protection of his son is handled through implication, structure, and consequence — never through vague gesturing that reads as evasion. Technique: end the scene at the threshold of the constrained event, then open the next scene on its aftermath already in motion — the reader feels the gap as weight, not as omission.

- Specificity over theme. Every chapter opens in a real, sensory, particular moment — a fire, a journal entry, a hearing date — never in summary or abstraction.

- The dead are characters, not symbols. Jumoke, David, and Funke are written with full personhood — their humour, their specific words, their ordinariness before their deaths. Technique: every chapter featuring one of the dead must include at least one mundane, unsentimental detail about them — what they found funny, a phrase they overused, something ordinary — before any account of their death or appearance. Grief earns its weight by first establishing a life, not a loss.

- Consequence, not accusation. Where legal risk exists, the manuscript shows what happened, what changed, what it cost, what documents show, what the narrator believed at the time, and what he understands now. It does not speculate about hidden motives in others. This is both the legal-safety rule and the literary one — restraint here makes the prose sharper, not weaker.

## Final Positioning Statement

Fathering Without Fear is a literary memoir about a man born under the weight of ancestral expectation, marked by death, preserved by faith, and forced by fatherhood to confront the oldest question of his life: whether he would disappear or stand.

Across Lagos, Norwich, London, family memory, spiritual warning, courtroom language, and the fragile tenderness of father and son, Abraham of London tells the story not as a victim pleading to be believed, but as a witness reclaiming the record.

This is not a misery memoir. It is not a legal complaint. It is not a parenting book. It is the story of inheritance under siege — and one father's refusal to let fear become the final author of his life.

The winning frame: not "a father fought the system," but "a man spent his whole life being told not to disappear — and fatherhood became the place where that command was finally tested."

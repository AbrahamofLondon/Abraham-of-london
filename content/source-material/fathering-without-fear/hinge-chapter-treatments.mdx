HINGE CHAPTER TREATMENTS — DEVELOPMENT, NOT DRAFTING

These are treatments, not prose. The goal is to know what each hinge
chapter is *for* before any sentence of it is written. Five chapters were
chosen because five hinges done well will reveal whether the book's shape
holds better than twenty-two chapters done thin: the present-day opener
(1), the spiritual covenant (6), the deepest collateral wound (15), the
merged emotional summit (21), and the close (22).

Chapter numbers follow the **final 22-chapter sequence** (post Norwich/
Devotion merge). The Five Governing Questions are quoted exactly from
"The Five Questions" in `fathering-without-fear-source.mdx`:

1. What does a man inherit before he understands himself?
2. What does death demand from the living?
3. What does faith mean when God preserves your life but not your comfort?
4. What does a father owe a child when the system rewards disappearance?
5. What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying the people still inside the story?

Two hard gates are flagged below and must not be drafted past until the
author supplies the missing material: **Chapter 1's courtroom image** and
**Chapter 15's mundane-before-grief detail for Funke**.

================================================================

### Chapter 1 — Something Always Happens

**Opening image:** *Provisional — flagged for author confirmation.* The
chapter needs a single, real, present-day (2026) image from a courtroom
or contact-centre: one concrete physical fact the reader sees before they
are told anything — a name read off a list, a strip-light, a child's coat
on the wrong hook, the specific sound of a door that only opens from one
side. No such detail has been confirmed. Do **not** invent courtroom
texture wholesale; supply one true detail and the image is built around it.

**The mundane-before-grief detail:** N/A — no death is introduced in this
chapter.

**Primary governing question served:** "What does a father owe a child
when the system rewards disappearance?" (confirmed against the
architecture, not assumed.)

**Secondary question (if any):** None primary. The governing sentence —
*something always happens* — seeds Question 2 for later, but Chapter 1
should not yet carry it.

**Craft risk to watch:** This chapter has no ancestral material to lean
on, so it lives or dies on the narrator's voice alone. Two specific
failures: (a) stating the governing sentence too early or too often, so it
reads as a slogan rather than a thing he has earned the right to say; and
(b) letting the courtroom register tip from *controlled fire* into
grievance or legal-brief, which forfeits the reader's trust on page one.

================================================================

### Chapter 6 — The Fire and the Deal

**Opening image:** The fire itself — or the half-second before waking, heat
and smoke arriving before comprehension. Start as close to the body as
possible: the physical danger first, the bargain and its theology only
after the room is real. The richest confirmed material in the book lives
here (the dream-death, the bargain for seventy-five years, waking into an
actual fire, pulling his sisters out before the parents reached the room);
use it directly, but let the deal surface *after* the smoke.

**The mundane-before-grief detail:** N/A — no one dies in this chapter.

**Primary governing question served:** "What does faith mean when God
preserves your life but not your comfort?"

**Secondary question (if any):** "What does a man inherit before he
understands himself?" — the vow becomes the covenant that organises the
rest of his life before he understands what he has agreed to.

**Craft risk to watch:** This is the book's most extraordinary early
claim, and it arrives before the reader has fully learned to trust the
narrator. The specific failure is inflation — rendering the dream-death or
the fire as spectacle or set-piece. Per the voice rule, it must be
narrated as plainly and sensorily as the courtroom material, or it reads
as a tall tale and the reader stops believing the rest.

================================================================

### Chapter 15 — Funke

**Opening image:** *WITHHELD — HARD GATE.* No opening image is to be
proposed for this chapter until the mundane-before-grief detail below is
supplied. Drafting an opener now would force the chapter to begin at the
death or the guilt, which is exactly the failure the craft rule exists to
prevent.

**The mundane-before-grief detail:** **MISSING — author input required.**
Confirmed material is all loss: Funke died two days before the divorce
filing, the medical bill he could not pay, the guilt he still carries as
debt. There is currently **no** ordinary, specific detail of Funke as a
living person — what she found funny, a phrase she overused, an ordinary
habit. The craft rule "the dead must live before they die" was written
specifically to keep this chapter from becoming only a grief-and-guilt
summary. This is a hard gate: one true living detail must come from the
author before the chapter is built.

**Primary governing question served:** "What does death demand from the
living?" (the debt he still carries — death's demand made literal in an
unpaid bill.)

**Secondary question (if any):** "What does faith mean when God preserves
your life but not your comfort?" — his life was bargained for; hers was
not paid for.

**Craft risk to watch:** Two named failures. First, grief-and-guilt
summary with no living Funke in it (gated above). Second, performance —
the skeleton's own warning is "guilt without melodrama and grief without
performance"; the unpaid bill is so loaded that any reaching for effect
will cheapen it. The restraint is the power here.

================================================================

### Chapter 21 — Devotion

**Opening image:** *Not to be fixed here — reserved for direct author
collaboration.* Per standing instruction, this chapter's first line should
not be attempted without the author present. What can be said is the *kind*
of image it needs: it must open at the **edge of knowing** — the Monday
morning in Norwich, the strange waking feeling that preceded any call,
*before* confirmation of the father's death arrives — because that is how
the author actually experienced it, and because the merge only works if the
chapter begins in the present timeline and crosses into the ancestral one
without a seam. The actual opening sentence is left to collaboration.

**The mundane-before-grief detail:** **Needed — flag.** The death-scene
material is exact and confirmed (born Monday, 12 September 1932; died
Monday, 15 August 2017, age 84; mid-prayer during family devotion; last
words "Iyalode protect me, Jesus, receive me"). But those are details of
the death, not of the life. Per the craft rule, David senior must be felt
as an ordinary, living man before he dies — one mundane detail, not the
record of his political sacrifice. Confirm one before drafting.

**Primary governing question served:** *Author decision required — two
candidates, both genuine.* "What does death demand from the living?" — and
equally, given the father's own life of sacrifice and loss, "What does a
man inherit before he understands himself?" Do not pick one unilaterally;
flag both for the author.

**Secondary question (if any):** Whichever of the two above is not chosen
as primary effectively functions as the secondary.

**Craft risk to watch:** The seam. This is the one chapter where the
present and ancestral timelines are not interwoven but *identical*, and the
merge of the old Norwich material into Devotion must read as one continuous
event, not two stitched halves. The specific failure is a visible join — a
paragraph break or tonal shift where the reader feels the edit. The last
words are also a spectacle risk: stated plainly, they devastate; framed,
they perform.

================================================================

### Chapter 22 — The Father Who Refuses to Disappear

**Opening image:** *Not proposed — emerges last.* Closing lines are rarely
discoverable before the chapters they close. This treatment deliberately
fixes no opening image; it will be found once chapters 1–21 exist.

**The mundane-before-grief detail:** N/A — no death is introduced; this is
the close.

**Primary governing question served:** *All five, consciously, in summary.*
This is the only chapter likely to serve every governing question at once —
inheritance (1), death's demand (2), faith without comfort (3), what a
father owes (4), and the cost of telling the truth (5). Noting this
explicitly is the point; do not force a single primary.

**Secondary question (if any):** N/A — see above.

**Craft risk to watch:** False resolution. The architecture is explicit:
the book does not end because the battle is won, but because the promise is
kept. The specific failure is a triumphant or valedictory close that
resolves what the book has spent twenty-one chapters refusing to resolve.
It must be written from inside the still-unresolved present of 2026 —
the promise kept, the fight ongoing, the father still there.

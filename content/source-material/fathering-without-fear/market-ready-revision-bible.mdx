## SECTION 1 — GOVERNING COMMERCIAL PROBLEM

The architecture is sound. The canon is clean. The protection is holding.

The remaining gap is this: the manuscript still risks being read as testimony rather than lived as memoir. The prose is accurate. In places it is not yet inhabited. A reader can admire the architecture without feeling the chair underneath it.

The revision converts the current draft from powerful testimony with literary architecture into literary memoir with commercial readability. These are not in conflict. The best memoirs are both.

**The problem in its simplest form:** the manuscript too often tells the reader what a scene means before the scene has had the chance to mean it. Declarations accumulate before scenes earn them. The reader is correctly informed but not yet fully placed.

**The solution is not to make the book lighter.** It is to make it more real.

## SECTION 2 — THE CENTRAL MOTIF

### The Room Where Love Is Forced to Wait

Do not use the courtroom as the central metaphor. The courtroom is too narrow and too dangerous — it risks making the book feel like legal grievance even when the prose is restrained.

The book's governing motif is larger:

**The Room Where Love Is Forced to Wait.**

This motif can carry the whole manuscript because it is present in every part:

| Room | Chapter | What it holds |
|------|---------|---------------|
| The tailor's room in Isua | Ch.2 | Where faith entered the lineage |
| Iyalode's room | Ch.8 | Where visitors left altered |
| The room where Abraham was seen and doubted | Ch.3 | Where the name was questioned |
| The room of childhood | Ch.5 | Where Jumoke followed him |
| The room of the fire | Ch.6 | Where the bargain was made |
| The sleeping room after his father's death | Ch.9 | Where David Senior's obligation began |
| The departure | Ch.10 | Where provision preceded and followed the leaving |
| The interview room, 7am–8pm | Ch.21 | Where devotion waited and was rewarded |
| The contact-centre room | Ch.17, 19 | Where fatherhood was permitted in fragments |
| The Teams waiting room | Ch.4 | Where a child's future was inside the machinery |
| The rented room after rejection | Ch.16 | Where fatherhood began outside the home |
| The room where Abraham writes | Ch.20 | Where the old verse looked different |
| The final ordinary room with Damisi | Ch.22 | Where love is not forced to wait |

**The State does not become the book's main character.**

**The room becomes the witness.**

### How to use the motif

Do not name it repeatedly. Do not make it a slogan. Do not write "this was another room where love was forced to wait." Let rooms recur naturally. Let the reader feel the pattern before they can articulate it.

Track in each room:
- who is present
- who is missing
- who has power
- what object is in the room
- what silence is in the room
- what the room cannot understand
- what love does inside the room despite what the room cannot understand

The motif should be felt, not announced.

## SECTION 4 — CORE REVISION RULES

### Rule 1 — Scene First

Every major claim must be replaced or supported by a physical scene.

If the draft says "He refused to disappear" — where do we see him refusing? A chair? A form? A child waiting? A father folding himself into silence? A door closing?

The reader should conclude "he refused to disappear" before the prose says it.

**A declaration may remain only if the scene has already earned it.**

No chapter should open with its conclusion unless the whole chapter is designed around delayed proof.

### Rule 2 — Sensory Specificity

Every chapter must have at least one specific ordinary detail that makes its central scene real.

Not decorative description. Not purple prose. Only details that place the reader in the room.

Acceptable detail types: room temperature, plastic chair, fan sound, food smell, old paper, traffic outside, fabric texture, train platform, computer screen glow, child's toy, contact-centre carpet, voice tone, clothing, waiting-room clock, kitchen object, phone battery, shoes, table, envelope, form, pen, chair.

**Do not invent details.** If source detail is missing, mark: `AUTHOR DETAIL NEEDED` and specify exactly what is needed.

Example: `AUTHOR DETAIL NEEDED: one ordinary physical detail of Jumoke before grief — object, habit, sentence, expression, or small domestic action.`

### Rule 3 — Self-Accountability

The narrator must not sound like a flawless witness.

Sharpen or add where Abraham admits:
- he misread a warning
- he wanted hope to be evidence
- he confused movement with courage
- he stayed too long inside a story he wanted to be different
- he made something smaller than his hope
- he wanted love to mean safety

**Do not add self-condemnation. Do not perform guilt.**

Use flat, specific accountability. The model:

> He saw enough to be troubled. He did not yet know how to obey the trouble.

This is the tone. The older man does not prosecute the younger man. He describes him accurately.

### Rule 4 — Reader Utility Without Self-Help

The book must give the reader something they can carry, but not as principles, frameworks, or lessons inside the story.

The utility layer emerges through recurring questions, not instruction:

- What does love require when leaving would be easier?
- What did I inherit before I understood it?
- What warning did I rename as fear?
- What room am I still waiting in?
- What does devotion look like when nobody is applauding?
- What does a father owe a child when the world makes absence easier?

These questions appear sparingly. They do not interrupt scenes. They may appear at chapter turns, section breaks, or quiet reflective endings. Never write "Principle 3" or "The lesson is."

**The reader must feel taught without being lectured.**

### Rule 5 — Ending Image

No chapter ends as argument.

The final paragraph of each chapter lands as image, question, or silence — not explanation.

**The final page of the book** must make the reader feel: *He is still here.* Do not say it until the image has made it unnecessary.

### Rule 6 — Trust the Reader

Do not over-translate Yoruba, Christian, family, legal, or Nigerian terms. The reader does not need a glossary inserted into the reading experience. A word earns its meaning from the sentence around it.

Do not compare Abraham to epic or literary figures. Do not make chapter titles thesis statements. Do not turn ordinary objects into overblown symbols.

**The reader is equipped. The book trusts them.**

### Rule 7 — The Dead Must Live Before They Die

Every person who dies in the book must be rendered with ordinary human detail before death or symbolism enters.

Jumoke: her particular way of following, the photograph, her silence.
David: how he entered a room.
Funke: treated in hinge file; do not reopen.
Iyalode: her meals, her neatness, the specific weight of her authority.
David Senior: the interview room, the siblings, the obligations carried without theatre.

**A person who enters the book only to be mourned has not been fully written.**

### Rule 8 — The Central Motif Is Felt, Not Named

Rooms recur. The reader notices. Do not interrupt the noticing.

## SECTION 5 — ANTI-BESTSELLER PROHIBITIONS

Never do any of the following:

1. Turn the manuscript into self-help
2. Insert principles, frameworks, or numbered lessons into emotional scenes
3. Add a Yoruba glossary inside the reading experience
4. Over-translate cultural, legal, spiritual, or family terms
5. Compare Abraham to epic, biblical, or literary figures in the narration
6. Create "just-so" childhood parables where events become too neat
7. Explain the final image
8. Make chapter titles thesis statements ("How the State Erases the Father"; "What David Taught Me About Fear")
9. Turn ordinary objects into overblown symbols
10. End a chapter by summarising what the chapter meant
11. Write as though the reader cannot read the scene without assistance
12. Add new claims about Damisi's psychological state
13. Add medical or assessment language about any person
14. Let the narrative become anti-mother even by omission
15. Let the narrative become anti-system in a lazy way — the system is machinery, not a villain

## SECTION 7 — SENSORY DETAIL STANDARDS

### What counts as an acceptable sensory detail

Physical, specific, undecorated. It must do work — place the reader in the scene, make the time real, give the abstract a surface to rest on.

**Examples that pass:**
- "The form had a box for 'relationship to child.' He wrote 'father.' The box was small."
- "The laptop screen made a rectangle of light in the room."
- "The carpet in the contact centre was blue and slightly worn at the centre."
- "The fabric from Aba was cotton, and the pattern moved when you held it up."
- "He had been sitting since seven in the morning. By the afternoon the room had changed its light."

**Examples that fail (purple prose):**
- "The late afternoon sun spilled through the window like liquid gold, bathing the room in amber warmth."
- "The chairs stood sentinel, witnesses to the drama of fathers and sons."
- "The air hung heavy with unspoken grief."

**Examples that fail (over-symbolism):**
- "The pen he used to sign the form was the same colour as the door he could not open."
- "The torn corner of the envelope held within it the fracture of his hope."

### Protocol for missing details

When sensory detail cannot be confirmed from source material, do not invent it. Mark: `AUTHOR DETAIL NEEDED` with a specific, narrow request. The request should name the exact category of detail needed, not ask generally.

Good: `AUTHOR DETAIL NEEDED: what was the carpet or floor of the contact-centre room like?`
Bad: `AUTHOR DETAIL NEEDED: more physical detail about the room.`

## SECTION 9 — MARKET-READY ACCEPTANCE GATE

A chapter is not market-ready until it passes all ten checks:

1. One carrying scene exists and is rendered in physical detail.
2. One ordinary sensory detail makes the carrying scene real.
3. The chapter does not explain what the scene already proves.
4. The narrator shows at least one human limitation where relevant — confusion, delay, misreading.
5. The chapter does not become self-help.
6. The chapter does not become sermon.
7. The chapter does not become legal file or grievance document.
8. The chapter does not use any person as symbol before person.

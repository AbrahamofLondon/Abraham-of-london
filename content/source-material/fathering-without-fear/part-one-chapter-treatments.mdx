PART ONE CHAPTER TREATMENTS — TRACK B

These treatments bring Chapters 2–5 to the same development standard
established by the hinge-chapter work. The format carries thirteen
elements rather than the hinge format's five because Part One chapters
must be understood in terms of their structural function within the
book's opening movement, not only their individual craft requirements.
A book earns reader trust or loses it in its opening chapters. Part
One treatments reflect the weight of that.

Chapter numbers follow the final 22-chapter sequence. The Five
Governing Questions are quoted exactly from "The Five Questions" in
`fathering-without-fear-source.mdx`:

1. What does a man inherit before he understands himself?
2. What does death demand from the living?
3. What does faith mean when God preserves your life but not your comfort?
4. What does a father owe a child when the system rewards disappearance?
5. What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying the people still inside the story?

Chapter 1 (Something Always Happens) remains held per author's
instruction and is not treated in this document. See hinge treatments
for the standing note.

================================================================

PART ONE STRUCTURAL ARGUMENT

Part One opens on the present-day pressure (Chapter 1, held), descends
into the ancestral world that shaped Abraham before he could name
himself (Chapters 2 and 3), returns to the present-day fight (Chapter
4), and closes on the first death that made that fight non-negotiable
(Chapter 5). The timeline alternates: present → ancestral, ancestral
→ present → ancestral.

The chapters within Part One do not interweave their timelines. Each
chapter is wholly present or wholly ancestral. The interweaving
described in the book's architecture applies to the structure of
individual chapters in the later parts of the book, not to Part One's
sequencing.

The reader who finishes Part One should understand, without being told:
that something about this man means he has always survived what should
have erased him; that the patterns making him who he is are older than
he is; that the institutional fight he is currently in is the most
recent form of a very old pattern; and that a seven-year-old girl died
commanding him not to disappear. The four treatments below must each
contribute one piece of that argument without forcing it to the
surface.

### Chapter 2 — Isua to Agege

**Opening image:** Not the arrival — the moment of turning. The founding
ancestor set out from Isua toward the old Oyo Kingdom, the great city
of Yoruba imagination, and somewhere along the way turned instead toward
Eko, now Lagos. The image is that turn: a man who aimed for one city
and chose another, carrying an ambition that was larger than its
destination. Not what the journey meant — what it was. A decision made
in motion, the new city not yet visible, the old aim already left behind.

**Chapter function:** To establish that Abraham's inheritance begins not
just before his birth but before his father's birth — and that what he
inherits is not achievement but the full weight of a founding act
interrupted. The founding ancestor made one generational turn that
contained four departures: departure from inherited land, departure
toward strange territory, departure into commercial ambition, and
departure into Christian faith. Each was left unfinished by his early
death. Iyalode returned to Isua. The children were left young. The next
generation inherited the ambition without the achievement, the name
without the land, the faith without its builder. The pattern is not
exceptional in this family — it is structural. Abraham does not begin
with a blank page. He inherits a name already carrying movement, risk,
commerce, faith, loss, and unfinished mandate. The reader must
understand this before they meet Abraham — because it is the pattern
his life has been asked to complete.

**Governing question:** Q1 — "What does a man inherit before he
understands himself?" This is the chapter that shows the inheritance
being constructed before the inheritor exists. Every other question
in the book traces back through this one.

**Factual spine:**
- Family origin: Isua Kingdom, south-eastern Ondo State (not Osun)
- Family name: Adarabioyo ("as beautiful as the old Oyo Kingdom"),
  later Adaramola ("wealth looks good on us") — the name transformation
  encodes the family's shift across generations, from beauty linked to
  Oyo-connection to prosperity claimed in Lagos
- The founding ancestor — referred to in family oral history as Abraham
  — set out from Isua with his wife toward the old Oyo Kingdom; turned
  instead toward Eko (Lagos); became a tailor to the British missionary,
  a cocoa and kola-nut merchant, and a landowner
- His early death left his children young; Iyalode returned to Isua
  after his death; what he had built did not pass cleanly to the next
  generation
- Family lineage traces to Isua Kingdom; in family oral history, to
  kingmaker descent — a claim involving the Oba of Benin lineage; treat
  as family oral history, not independently verified, do not state as
  external historical fact
- The founding ancestor converted to Christianity — in family oral
  history, the first in the family lineage to do so; treat as
  family-source material, not externally verified; his work as tailor
  to the British missionary likely created the conditions for this
  conversion, though the exact moment and circumstances are not yet
  confirmed
- Genealogical distance between the founding ancestor and David Senior
  (one generation removed, or two?) should be confirmed before drafting;
  it affects how the chapter speaks the founding ancestor's relationship
  to the narrator

**Emotional centre:** Not grief and not triumph — founding weighted by
loss. The chapter is warm but carries a shadow: something was built,
it was real, and it was lost too soon. The emotional ground is the gap
between ambition and completion — a man who reached far enough to build
something, but not far enough to secure it for his children. The reader
should feel both the reach and its cost, without the chapter turning
melancholy. What was built is still the foundation. The foundation held.

**Theological pressure:** The founding ancestor's conversion to
Christianity is this chapter's theological territory — not the centre
of gravity, but one of the four founding departures, and the one with
the longest consequence. His work as tailor to the British missionary
is the likely proximate cause; the conversion (in family oral history,
the first in the lineage) is the consequence. This is the origin point
of the Christian thread that Iyalode will transmit to young Abraham in
Chapter 7, that David Senior will carry into his deathbed words, and
that Abraham will place at the centre of his bargain in Chapter 6. The
connection between the trade and the conversion must be handled as
family-source material — not externally verified, not overdressed with
theological significance. It belongs in the chapter as one founding act
among several, not as the chapter's crown jewel.

Yoruba spiritual authority is also present here, implicit rather than
stated. The chapter holds both frames — the Yoruba ancestral world
and the founding ancestor's Christian conversion — without explaining
either or resolving the tension between them. They coexisted in the
family's founding act, as they coexist across the book's entire arc.
That coexistence is the theological pressure: not a problem to be
solved, not a theme to be announced, but a factual condition the
chapter carries lightly.

**Inheritance thread:** This is the root of the book's entire
inheritance argument. Migration as a founding act; ambition as a
founding act; early death and partial loss as a founding act. The
pattern — reach, build, die before completion, reset, the children
carry forward what wasn't finished — is what Abraham inherits. He does
not inherit land or title. He inherits the obligation to do in London
what his ancestor started in Lagos. The name Adaramola ("wealth looks
good on us") is a declaration not yet completed. The chapter plants
that incompleteness before the narrator is born.

**Fatherhood thread:** Implicit and early. The founding ancestor is the
first father-figure the book introduces — a man who built something for
his children and died before they were old enough to hold it. The
book's governing question about what a father owes his child is seeded
here in its most ancestral form: a father whose intention was clear and
whose early death was the gap between intention and delivery.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** N/A — no death is the chapter's central
event, though the founding ancestor's early death is part of the factual
spine. The principle applies in a structural sense: Iyalode must be felt
as a person — her drive, her authority, her particular quality in the
world — before she becomes a symbol or a spiritual covering. She is not
yet "Iyalode the ancestor." She is a woman who returned to Isua after
her husband died and did what had to be done.

**Craft risk:** Three specific failures:
(a) Abstraction — rendering the journey as symbolic migration rather than
as physical event. The road must be real before it is meaningful. The
chapter that opens on the weight of ancestral consequence before the
physical reality of a man choosing a road will lose the reader before
it has found them.
(b) Over-narrating Iyalode's significance before it is earned. The
reader has not yet met Abraham. They do not yet know why Iyalode
matters. She must enter as a woman who made decisions and kept going.
Her spiritual authority arrives through the book's arc, not through
announcement in Chapter 2.
(c) Ancestry inflation — rendering the family's oral history as settled
genealogy, confirmed lineage, or origin epic. The chapter carries the
founding story as memoir: a family's account of where it came from, not
a verified historical record. The Oba of Benin claim in particular must
not be stated as external fact; it is what the family says, and the
chapter holds it exactly there. The chapter's power comes from the
weight of the family's self-understanding, not from the confirmed
nobility of its provenance.

**Prohibited material:** Iyalode's relationship with Abraham and the
naming question belong in Chapter 3. Iyalode's personal faith portrait
and her transmission of the name of Jesus to young Abraham belong in
Chapters 7 and 8 — note the distinction: the founding ancestor's
conversion belongs in Chapter 2; Iyalode's personal faith life and her
role as transmitter belong later. Her full portrait in contradiction
belongs in Chapter 8. The Oba of Benin origin claim must not be stated
as independently verified historical fact. Chapter 2 holds the founding
act and its immediate consequences only.

**Ending movement:** Iyalode returns to Isua. The children are young.
What was built is not yet secured. The family is back at the beginning,
but not the same beginning — they carry the name, the ambition, and the
knowledge that Lagos was reachable. The ending note is not defeat; it
is a reset with memory. The family knows now what is possible. It will
take another generation to try again.

**Drafting readiness:** STRUCTURALLY READY. The chapter's founding
argument is now clear and the factual spine is confirmed. Two items
remain conditional: (1) the genealogical relationship between the
founding ancestor and David Senior — whether one generation or two —
should be confirmed before drafting, as this affects how the chapter
speaks of the founding ancestor's distance from the narrator; (2) any
specific physical detail from the family's account of the journey —
a road, a season, a place where the turn was made — would anchor the
opening image with the precision the voice rules require. Oral-history
claims (Oba of Benin connection) must not be stated as verified fact.

### Chapter 3 — The Boy Who Was Already Old

**Opening image:** Not the birth. Not the naming debate. A moment of
the child already in excess of what is expected — a specific word used
too early, a question that silences adults, something small and
particular that marks him as different not through tragedy but through
a gravity that precedes understanding. The reader meets the boy before
they are told what was said about him.

**Chapter function:** To establish that Abraham did not become this way
through experience. He arrived this way. The chapter establishes the
naming burden: a grandmother who doubts the fit, a father who sees
unfinished work, and a child who receives a name that behaves like an
assignment before he can speak it clearly. Doubt and recognition are
both present from the beginning. The reader should finish this chapter
understanding that to be named Abraham in this family was to be given
not a word but a mandate — and that the child had no say in accepting it.

**Governing question:** Q1 primarily — "What does a man inherit before
he understands himself?" — sharpened here to its most embodied form:
what is the cost of carrying a name built before you were born, given
with expectation, and received without permission? Q3 begins to seed
beneath it — "What does faith mean when God preserves your life but not
your comfort?" — because the naming burden is preserved before it is
comfortable.

**Factual spine:**
- Abraham born as one of triplets; one triplet was lost in infancy
- Iyalode's doubt: she thought Abraham was the wrong child to carry
  his grandfather's name and legacy — her husband (the founding
  ancestor) had been fair-skinned, lanky, and stylish; Abraham was
  dark; he appeared connected visually and spiritually to his mother's
  line of herbalists rather than to the Adaramola line
- David Senior's recognition: he believed Abraham was his own father's
  reincarnate — come back to finish the work of making the name greater
  in a strange land. This is the belief that gave Abraham his name and
  its weight.
- He spoke too early, questioned too deeply, survived too strangely
- The quality of his questions: no child should yet have language for
  what he asked

**Emotional centre:** Two readings, one child. Iyalode sees the surface:
this doesn't look right, this doesn't match. David Senior sees beneath
the surface: my father has returned to finish what he started. The child
arrives into a family where the grandmother doubts and the father
recognises, before the child has any language for either reading. The
chapter's emotional question is not "who was right?" but "what does it
feel like to arrive already carrying something that the people who love
you are already arguing over?" The boy is not primarily a victim of
doubt. He is primarily a weight-bearer who didn't choose the weight.

**Theological pressure:** David Senior's belief in reincarnation is
the load-bearing theology in this chapter — not Iyalode's. It is his
belief, held precisely, and the chapter must honour its specificity
without the text endorsing it as authorial fact. The book's strategy
applies: narrate plainly, without apology and without inflation. David
Senior believed this because of what he saw; what he saw was real to
him; the text does not adjudicate whether he was right. The reader is
left with both the weight of the belief and the freedom to interpret it.

**Inheritance thread:** The lost triplet is part of the inheritance.
Abraham is already marked by absence before he can name absence. The
founding ancestor's unfinished work, carried through David Senior's
belief, now lands in the body of a child who was born dark in a family
that expected fair-skinned. The inheritance thread in this chapter runs
from the founding ancestor through David Senior's recognition through
the name Abraham was given. The child inherits not a quality but an
expectation.

**Fatherhood thread:** Present in its earliest form. David Senior
looking at his son and seeing his own father: this is the fatherhood
thread at its most ancestral inversion — a father who is also a son
who is also a believer in return. David Senior's reading of Abraham
is an act of love that is also an act of loading. The book will spend
twenty-two chapters working out what that combination costs.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** No death in this chapter in the direct
sense. The principle applies in modified form: before the naming debate
arrives, before the grandmother's doubt and the father's recognition,
the reader needs one specific ordinary detail of the boy as a child —
something he said, something he did, the particular quality of his
questions. The chapter cannot open on what he represents. It must open
on what he is.

**Craft risk:** Three specific failures:
(a) Mystification — rendering the child's quality as atmospheric rather
than precise. The chapter works only if what he carries is described
with enough specificity that the reader can locate it, not merely sense
it. Vagueness reads as evasion; the supernatural material in this book
earns trust through exactness, not through impressionism.
(b) Collapsing the two readings into one verdict. Iyalode's doubt is
not wrong because David Senior's recognition is right. Both are
responses to something real in the child that neither can fully see.
The chapter must hold both readings without the narrative casting a
vote for either.
(c) Spiritual overclaiming — David Senior's belief in reincarnation
must be narrated as his belief, exactly, without the text offering
confirmation or editorial distance from it. The chapter neither proves
it nor dismisses it. It states it as the thing David Senior believed
and acted on.

**Prohibited material:** Do not render David Senior's belief as the
book's verdict on reincarnation — it is his reading, narrated precisely.
Do not make Iyalode's doubt the chapter's wound — she is reading what
she sees, and what she sees is real; her error is in the conclusion,
not the perception. The fire and the bargain (Chapter 6) — do not
anticipate. Jumoke's death (Chapter 5) — do not anticipate. Iyalode
as Christian (Chapters 7 and 8) — do not enter. The chapter sits inside
the unresolved. Leave it there.

**Ending movement:** The boy already named, already assigned, already
carrying something the family has not finished arguing about. Not
triumphantly, not tragically — weightedly. The ending note is: the name
has been given. No one asked whether he wanted it. He is what the family
needed him to be before he was old enough to offer a different answer.

**Drafting readiness:** READY, subject to two disciplines. (1) David
Senior's belief in reincarnation must be narrated as his belief — the
chapter carries it with complete fidelity to his reading without the
text endorsing or adjudicating it. (2) Iyalode's doubt must be carried
without making it the chapter's wound — she is not wrong to see what
she sees; her conclusion is the question, not the perception.

### Chapter 4 — A Hearing Date

**Opening image:** Not a decision being announced. Not a dramatic
courtroom moment. The machinery before the decision: a waiting room,
a name called, a door that opens and closes. The specific sensory
reality of institutional space — strip-lights, chairs, the particular
sound of a building that processes people as cases. A father present
in a room not designed to hold what he is there for.

Note: Chapter 1 (held) also opens in a courtroom or contact-centre
setting. When Chapter 1's opening image is eventually confirmed,
Chapter 4's opening image should be reviewed against it to ensure the
two present-day chapters are in distinct register — Chapter 1
establishes the governing sentence; Chapter 4 establishes what the
machinery of that fight looks like from inside it. They must not
duplicate each other.

**Chapter function:** To anchor the reader back in the present-day
timeline after the ancestral material of Chapters 2 and 3, and to
establish what the institutional fight actually looks like from inside
it. The court is not rendered as villain. It is machinery: it processes
risk, procedure, history, reports, delays. It does not process love.
That is the chapter's central observation, arrived at through scene and
consequence, never through editorial complaint.

**Governing question:** Q4 primarily — "What does a father owe a child
when the system rewards disappearance?" This is the chapter that shows
what the system is. The answer to Q4 will only be visible in contrast
to what the system does with a father's continued presence. Q5 runs
beneath it — "What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying
the people still inside the story?" — because the legal constraint that
operates throughout this book operates here at full force.

**Factual spine:**
- The family court machinery: hearings, filings, orders, reports,
  waiting rooms, delays, managed access
- A father's love translated into the procedural categories available
  to an institution built for different purposes
- The father present, repeatedly, in a system that cannot fully see
  what he is there to preserve
- The waiting: between dates, between decisions, between one form of
  managed contact and the next

The factual spine for this chapter must come entirely from the author's
own experience of these proceedings — his memory of what these spaces
felt like, what the procedure felt like from inside it. No court
documents. No third-party records. No material under legal protection.
The chapter's authority comes from the narrator's precision about his
own experience, not from the record.

**Emotional centre:** Controlled restraint under institutional pressure.
Not rage. Not grief. Not pleading. A man who has made a bargain with
God and is sitting in a waiting room being processed as a case, and
who has not broken under it. The emotional question of this chapter is
not "how does he survive this?" but "how does he remain present inside
it?" Presence despite procedure is the chapter's emotional register.

**Theological pressure:** Minimal at the surface, structural underneath.
The book has not yet made its full theological claim — that is Chapter
6 and later. But the gap is already visible to the reader who carries
the whole arc: a man who bargained with God for seventy-five years of
service is sitting in an institution that has no category for what a
bargain with God is. The chapter does not say this. The reader who
knows the full book will feel it. The gap is the theological pressure,
not the statement.

**Inheritance thread:** Present but not foregrounded. What Abraham
refuses to pass to Damisi is already being decided in these rooms —
not bitterness, not defeat, not the story the system is constructing
about him. That refusal is Part Four material. Here the inheritance
thread is the fact itself: this is what this man's fatherhood looks
like from the outside. The reader needs to see that before they can
understand what refusing to be defined by it will cost him.

**Fatherhood thread:** This chapter is the most direct introduction of
the fatherhood fight. Damisi should be felt as the reason the father
is in the room — without being made into evidence. The son is a child.
His existence is what the hearing is about; his personhood is what the
hearing cannot fully hold. He does not appear in this chapter as a
speaker or a scene. His presence is registered through its absence in
the system's categories.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** N/A in the death sense. A modified form
applies: before the institutional machinery is fully rendered, the
reader should feel one moment of what the relationship between father
and son actually is — something specific, ordinary, and real — set
against what the court's categories make of it. That ordinary texture
grounds the stakes. Without it, the chapter risks becoming about the
system rather than about what the system cannot hold.

**Craft risk:** Three specific failures:
(a) The legal-brief register — the chapter's most specific danger.
Every sentence about the court must come through scene and consequence,
never through procedural summary, legal advocacy, or list. The narrator
is testifying under oath before God, history, his son, and himself.
Not filing a motion.
(b) Making the system the villain. A reader who finishes this chapter
hating the court has been failed by the narrator. The system is
machinery; it processes what it can process. That is its nature, not
its evil. The restraint here is literary as well as legal: the chapter
that reaches for the reader's anger gives up the reader's trust.
(c) Damisi as exhibit. The son cannot function as evidence for the
father's case, even implicitly. He is a child who is protected by the
book's own craft rules. His love for his father and his father's love
for him are not demonstrated to the reader as proof. They are the
thing that is real, against which the court's categories appear
diminished.

**Prohibited material:** Court documents of any kind. Any third-party
authored record. Any reproduction or structural echo of material
subject to court order. Named parties in the proceedings beyond
Abraham. Legal argument or advocacy framing. The specific procedural
details of Abraham's case as a legal account — this is not a legal
account. Anything that reads as rehearsing the case rather than
testifying to the experience.

**Ending movement:** The hearing date passes. There will be another.
The father is still present. That is the chapter's final beat: he is
still here, the machinery continues, and presence is already a kind
of answer to what the system rewards. Not triumphant. Not resolved.
Continuing.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. One confirmed specific
sensory detail from the author's own memory of a family court
proceeding — not from any document, from memory alone — will unlock
the opening image and make the chapter immediately draftable. Without
it, the risk is generic institutional texture rather than earned
specificity. Possible sensory categories: the corridor, the chairs,
the waiting room, silence, security presence, lighting, smell, body
sensation, or a specific sound. One remembered detail from any of
these is all the chapter needs to open.

### Chapter 5 — Jumoke

**Opening image:** Jumoke always near. Not the kitchen. Not the day
she died. Not the appearance. Jumoke as she was when she was alive —
the person who was always with Abraham, always alongside him, who had
chosen him as her person before either of them had language for what
that meant. She was clingy, in the way of children who have found
their attachment and won't let it go. The chapter should open in her
presence: the specific texture of always being accompanied by someone
who has simply decided that you belong to them. Not the grief of her
absence — the fact of her presence, so ordinary and so complete that
it will take her absence to make it visible.

**Chapter function:** To introduce Jumoke as a person before she is
introduced as a loss. The chapter establishes the first of the three
deaths that commanded Abraham to live, and in doing so establishes
the book's central structural logic: the dying do not merely depart,
they instruct. The specific content of Jumoke's instruction — she died
so he could live to fulfil his destiny — is the sentence that organises
the rest of the book. But the chapter must earn that sentence by first
making the reader grieve the person, not the function.

**Governing question:** Q2 primarily — "What does death demand from
the living?" This is the question in its most direct, earliest form.
Jumoke's death makes the demand explicit in a way no subsequent death
can match for directness: she appeared and told him. Q1 runs beneath
it — what she demands of him is specifically about what he has already
been named to carry before he understood it.

**Factual spine:**
- Jumoke: Abraham's younger sister. NOT his twin. NOT part of the
  triplet set. A separate younger sister, who came after the triplets.
- Died at age seven.
- Appeared to Abraham in the kitchen on the day of her death.
- The sentence she left: she died so he could live to fulfil his destiny.
- She was very clingy to Abraham — always with him, always near,
  choosing him as her person.
- Abraham describes her as his "quiet first love."
- She came after the triplets, who were locally celebrated — gifted by
  companies and kings, featured in The Punch newspaper. Being the
  last-born girl in the shadow of celebrated children may have been a
  hard place to occupy. Abraham, looking at the only picture of them
  now, sees that she may have been an unhappy child. This is his
  retrospective reading; it is not a diagnosis, and the chapter must
  not treat it as one.
- Chronology confirmed: Jumoke was alive during the Chapter 6 fire.
  She was one of the two sisters who shared the room with Abraham
  during the fire incident. The book presents Chapter 5 before
  Chapter 6 for structural reasons; the events of Chapter 6
  chronologically precede Jumoke's death.

**Emotional centre:** She was always there. She chose him. He was her
person, and she was his "quiet first love." A child receiving a command
too large for a child to hold — but first, before the command, the
presence: the specific ordinary weight of someone who has simply decided
to love you before you know what love costs. The chapter's emotional
centre is not the death itself, or even the sentence that follows it.
It is the particular texture of what was there before either, and what
the death removed without warning or permission.

**Theological pressure:** The highest of any Part One chapter. This is
where the supernatural material enters the book at full weight for the
first time — the appearance in the kitchen, the sentence, the fact that
she came to give him an instruction she could not have given in life
because he did not yet know she was going to die. The strategy confirmed
in the source proposal applies absolutely: narrate plainly, without
apology and without inflation. The reader has been prepared by the
narrator's precision in Chapters 2, 3, and 4 to trust what they are
about to be told. Do not betray that trust by performing the moment.

**Inheritance thread:** Jumoke is the first transmission. What she
passes to Abraham is not material — it is obligation. She leaves him
a sentence about purpose that he will spend the rest of his life
working out. The inheritance thread in this chapter is obligation
itself, in its earliest form, arriving before he is old enough to
understand what it means.

**Fatherhood thread:** Distant but present as the faintest forward
echo. Abraham is a child in this chapter. The command to live and to
fulfil his destiny will eventually mean — will only fully mean — that
he is a father who refuses to disappear. But the chapter should not
reach for that connection. Jumoke's death is a child's death. The
promise it produces is a child's promise, made in a kitchen. Let it
remain that. The book will draw the line in its own time.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** GATE UNLOCKED. The ordinary-before-grief
detail is confirmed: Jumoke's quiet, clingy attachment to Abraham —
always there, always with him, choosing him as her person before she
had the language for it. The phrase "quiet first love" is the emotional
key. The chapter should open in her presence, in her ordinary habit of
being near him, before it moves toward the kitchen and what arrived
there.

**Craft risk:** Five specific failures:
(a) The kitchen appearance as spectacle — the danger is rendering the
appearance as a supernatural event rather than as an experience. Jumoke
does not arrive as an effect. She arrives the way these things arrive
in a life: without announcement, in a room that is already real, to a
person who is not prepared. The narration must stay inside Abraham's
experience — what he perceived, in its exact strangeness — not at a
remove that makes the event cinematic.
(b) The sentence as the chapter's climax — "she died so he could live
to fulfil his destiny" is the chapter's centre, not its crescendo. The
danger is building toward it dramatically so that it lands as
revelation. It should arrive the way something inevitable arrives: not
as a surprise, but as a confirmation of something that has been true
all along, now spoken aloud for the first time.
(c) Premature interpretation — the chapter should not tell the reader
what this means for Abraham's life, what it will cost him, what it
explains about the chapters ahead. The sentence is given. The reader
is left inside it with Abraham. The interpretation belongs to the rest
of the book.
(d) Sentimental inflation — "quiet first love" is already a phrase of
extraordinary weight. The craft discipline is to let the ordinary
attachment carry the grief without the prose underlining it. State what
she was. Do not tell the reader what to feel about the loss of it.
(e) Retrospective over-reading — Abraham's sense, from a photograph,
that she may have been an unhappy child is his reading now, looking
back. The chapter holds this lightly as a shadow — not diagnosed, not
amplified, not used to reshape her into a figure of pathos. She was
what she was: the person always near him. Let that be the chapter's
truth, not a reconstruction built from grief.

**Prohibited material:** No comparison to David's death or Funke's
death — those chapters will draw the pattern; this chapter is still
inside the first death, which must be felt on its own terms. No
anticipation of the fire or the bargain (Chapter 6) — though the
drafter should know Jumoke was alive during that fire. No theological
framework for the appearance — the book does not adjudicate what the
appearance was or wasn't; it is the narrator's experience, stated as
fact, open to the reader's interpretation. No use of Jumoke as symbol
in this chapter — she is a person here, and she stays a person.

**Ending movement:** Abraham holding the sentence Jumoke left. Not
understanding it. Not yet acting on it. The chapter does not move
forward. It holds. The ending beat is the weight of a command given
to someone too young to know what it requires — and the silence of
that kitchen, or its echo, after she is gone.

**Drafting readiness:** READY, with caution. The mundane-before-grief
gate is filled. The chronology is confirmed. The chapter is draftable.
Two disciplines govern the draft: grief handled plainly (no
sentimentalisation of "quiet first love"); retrospective readings (the
photograph, the possible unhappiness) held as shadows, not diagnoses.

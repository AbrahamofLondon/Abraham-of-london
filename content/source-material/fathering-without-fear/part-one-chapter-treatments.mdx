PART ONE CHAPTER TREATMENTS — TRACK B

These treatments bring Chapters 2–5 to the same development standard
established by the hinge-chapter work. The format carries thirteen
elements rather than the hinge format's five because Part One chapters
must be understood in terms of their structural function within the
book's opening movement, not only their individual craft requirements.
A book earns reader trust or loses it in its opening chapters. Part
One treatments reflect the weight of that.

Chapter numbers follow the final 22-chapter sequence. The Five
Governing Questions are quoted exactly from "The Five Questions" in
`fathering-without-fear-source.mdx`:

1. What does a man inherit before he understands himself?
2. What does death demand from the living?
3. What does faith mean when God preserves your life but not your comfort?
4. What does a father owe a child when the system rewards disappearance?
5. What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying the people still inside the story?

Chapter 1 (Something Always Happens) remains held per author's
instruction and is not treated in this document. See hinge treatments
for the standing note.

================================================================

PART ONE STRUCTURAL ARGUMENT

Part One opens on the present-day pressure (Chapter 1, held), descends
into the ancestral world that shaped Abraham before he could name
himself (Chapters 2 and 3), returns to the present-day fight (Chapter
4), and closes on the first death that made that fight non-negotiable
(Chapter 5). The timeline alternates: present → ancestral, ancestral
→ present → ancestral.

The chapters within Part One do not interweave their timelines. Each
chapter is wholly present or wholly ancestral. The interweaving
described in the book's architecture applies to the structure of
individual chapters in the later parts of the book, not to Part One's
sequencing.

The reader who finishes Part One should understand, without being told:
that something about this man means he has always survived what should
have erased him; that the patterns making him who he is are older than
he is; that the institutional fight he is currently in is the most
recent form of a very old pattern; and that a seven-year-old girl died
commanding him not to disappear. The four treatments below must each
contribute one piece of that argument without forcing it to the
surface.

### Chapter 2 — Isua to Agege

**Opening image:** The walk itself, or the moment of departure — two
people setting out from Isua before Abraham exists, before the family
takes its shape in Lagos. The image should be as close to the body as
possible: the physical fact of leaving, the road before arrival, the
city not yet visible. Not what the walk means — what the walk is. Two
people in motion, with nothing ahead confirmed, carrying something
they do not yet have words for.

**Chapter function:** To establish that Abraham's inheritance begins
before his birth. The patterns that govern the book — migration,
labour, sacrifice, the giving up of one form of life to build
something elsewhere — are structural to the family line, not peculiar
to Abraham's generation. The reader must understand this before they
meet Abraham: the forces he lives inside are not new. They are old
enough to have a geography.

**Governing question:** Q1 — "What does a man inherit before he
understands himself?" This is the chapter that shows the inheritance
being constructed before the inheritor exists. Every other question
in the book traces back through this one.

**Factual spine:**
- Iyalode and her husband walk from Isua (Osun) to Lagos
- The husband: tailor, farmer, builder of men — the first figure in
  the family's pattern of sacrifice
- Iyalode: the family's spiritual authority; eventually Christian;
  dies at 118 in 2006
- The family line established in Lagos as the outcome of a decision
  made before any of the book's central events

**Emotional centre:** Not grief and not triumph — founding. The feeling
of something beginning under difficult conditions before anyone knows
what it will become. The chapter is warm but unsentimental. These are
people under pressure who have made a decision and are carrying it
forward. The reader should feel the weight of the walk, not yet the
weight of what it will cost the generations that follow it.

**Theological pressure:** Yoruba spiritual authority is present here,
implicit rather than stated. Iyalode carries something the reader will
learn more about in Chapters 7 and 8. For now it is enough that she is
the kind of person whose decisions shape what comes after her. The
Christian conversion is not this chapter's territory. The chapter holds
the ancestral spiritual world without naming or explaining it — let it
be present as fact, not as framework.

**Inheritance thread:** This is the root of the book's entire
inheritance argument. Migration as a founding act; sacrifice as a
founding act; labour without guarantee of reward as a founding act.
Abraham inherits all three — they are in the family's bones before he
is in his mother's womb. The chapter establishes the pattern that will
recur across the whole book: a person gives up their own security for
the sake of something that will only be understood by the generation
after them.

**Fatherhood thread:** Implicit and early. Iyalode's husband is the
first father-figure the book introduces — a tailor, farmer, builder of
men. The book's governing question about what a father owes his child
is seeded here in its most ancestral form, before fatherhood and child
have been introduced as live categories in the present-day timeline.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** N/A — no death in this chapter. The
principle applies in a structural sense: Iyalode must be felt as a
person — her drive, her authority, her particular quality in the world
— before she becomes a symbol or a spiritual covering. This chapter
carries that responsibility. She is not yet "Iyalode the ancestor."
She is a woman who made a decision and took a walk toward a city.

**Craft risk:** Two specific failures:
(a) Abstraction — rendering the walk as symbolic migration rather than
as physical event. The road must be real before it is meaningful. A
chapter that opens on the weight of ancestral consequence before the
physical reality of two people on a road will lose the reader before
it has found them.
(b) Over-narrating Iyalode's significance before it is earned. The
reader has not yet met Abraham. They do not yet know why Iyalode
matters. She must enter as a woman who made a decision. Her spiritual
authority arrives through the book's arc, not through announcement
in Chapter 2.

**Prohibited material:** Iyalode's rejection of Abraham belongs in
Chapter 3. Her Christian conversion and the name of Jesus belong in
Chapter 7. Her full portrait in contradiction — beautiful, commanding,
spiritually feared, self-directed, absent, powerful — belongs in
Chapter 8. Her death at 118 is a beat in another chapter, not this
one. Chapter 2 holds the founding act only.

**Ending movement:** The family arrives in Lagos. The future is not
yet readable. The ending note is the particular quality of people who
have taken root in new ground and do not yet know what will grow. Not
arrival as triumph — arrival as beginning. The reader feels the family
finding its first position in the city that will hold the next several
generations of the story, without yet knowing that is what is happening.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. The structural and
emotional material is confirmed. One gap: specific physical details of
the walk — season, approximate period, whether they walked the full
distance or whether "the walk" is the memoir's compressed rendering of
a longer migration — are not confirmed in the source material. The
chapter can be drafted around the confirmed facts. One grounding
physical detail from the author, however small, will anchor the opening
image with the precision the voice rules require.

### Chapter 3 — The Boy Who Was Already Old

**Opening image:** Not the birth. Not the rejection. A moment of the
child already in excess of what is expected — a specific word used too
early, a question that silences adults, something small and particular
that marks him as different not through tragedy but through a gravity
that precedes understanding. The reader meets the boy before they are
told what to make of him.

**Chapter function:** To establish that Abraham did not become this way
through experience. He arrived this way. The chapter also establishes
the early paradox at the book's core: the child who is rejected as
spiritually threatening is the one who carries the family's most
important unfinished spiritual business. Rejection and recognition are
part of the same movement. The reader should finish this chapter
holding both without resolution.

**Governing question:** Q1 primarily — "What does a man inherit before
he understands himself?" This is the question at its most direct: a
child who carries something he has not been given and cannot yet name.
Q3 begins to seed beneath it — "What does faith mean when God preserves
your life but not your comfort?" — because the spiritual covering and
the spiritual threat can coexist in the same child from birth. The
coexistence is the chapter's unspoken argument.

**Factual spine:**
- Abraham born as one of triplets; one triplet was lost in infancy
- Iyalode's rejection: she saw in him a troubling spiritual resemblance
  (to what or whom she believed is not yet confirmed — flagged below)
- He spoke too early, questioned too deeply, survived too strangely
- The quality of his questions: no child should yet have language for
  what he asked

**Emotional centre:** The gap between being misread and being genuinely
seen. Iyalode's reading of Abraham is not simply wrong — she perceives
something real in him. Her error is not in what she sees but in what
she concludes from it. The chapter's emotional question is not "why
did she reject him?" but "what does it feel like to carry something
real that the people around you don't have words for either?" The boy
is not primarily a victim of rejection. He is primarily a mystery —
to them and to himself.

**Theological pressure:** Yoruba belief in spiritual resemblance and
reincarnation is load-bearing here. Iyalode's rejection is not
superstition to be dismissed by the narrative — it is a reading,
however wrong in its conclusion, of something genuinely present in the
child. The book does not adjudicate between spiritual frameworks. It
holds both: this child carries something real; what Iyalode concludes
from that reality is not the book's final word. The reader needs to
feel that something is being perceived, even if the interpretation is
contested.

**Inheritance thread:** The lost triplet is part of the inheritance.
Abraham is already marked by absence before he can name absence. The
one who was lost is never named and does not appear, but is part of
the context in which Abraham exists — an absence that is structurally
present. The chapter carries this lightly: not dwelt on, but there.

**Fatherhood thread:** Present as contrast and earliest seed. Abraham
is a child who has arrived in a family that does not have full
categories for what he is — not because they don't love him, but
because what he carries exceeds what they know how to hold. The
fatherhood question in its earliest form is already here: what does
a family owe a child it cannot fully read? The book will spend
twenty-two chapters building the answer.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** No death in this chapter in the direct
sense. The principle applies in modified form: before the spiritual
weight of the chapter arrives, before the rejection and its framing,
the reader needs one specific ordinary detail of the boy as a child —
something he said, something he did, something small and specifically
his. The chapter cannot open on what he represents. It must open on
what he is.

**Craft risk:** Two specific failures:
(a) Mystification — rendering the child's quality as atmospheric
rather than precise. The chapter works only if what he carries is
described with enough specificity that the reader can locate it, not
merely sense it. Vagueness reads as evasion; the supernatural material
in this book earns trust through exactness, not through impressionism.
(b) Making Iyalode's rejection the chapter's wound. She is not the
villain of Chapter 3. She is a woman making a reading — a reading with
authority, even in its error. The chapter must honour both her
spiritual seriousness and the specific mistake of her conclusion
without collapsing the tension between them. She will be rendered in
full in Chapter 8; here she is present but not yet explained.

**Prohibited material:** The fire and the bargain (Chapter 6) — do not
anticipate. Jumoke's death (Chapter 5) — do not anticipate. Iyalode
as Christian (Chapters 7 and 8) — do not enter. The proof of
Abraham's life as retrospective correction of Iyalode's reading — do
not draw that line here. The chapter sits inside the unresolved. Leave
it there.

**Ending movement:** The boy already formed into something the family
cannot easily hold. Not triumphantly, not tragically — ambiguously.
The ending note is: this is who he is, and no one yet has words for
it, including him. The chapter's last beat should feel like the pause
before a sentence that hasn't been spoken yet.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY, with one gate. What
specifically Iyalode believed she saw in Abraham — what the "troubling
spiritual resemblance" was, to whom or what she understood him to be
spiritually similar — is not confirmed in the source material. The
chapter can approach but cannot fully draft the rejection scene without
that specific belief named. It is the chapter's theological
load-bearing detail. Confirm with author before drafting.

### Chapter 4 — A Hearing Date

**Opening image:** Not a decision being announced. Not a dramatic
courtroom moment. The machinery before the decision: a waiting room,
a name called, a door that opens and closes. The specific sensory
reality of institutional space — strip-lights, chairs, the particular
sound of a building that processes people as cases. A father present
in a room not designed to hold what he is there for.

Note: Chapter 1 (held) also opens in a courtroom or contact-centre
setting. When Chapter 1's opening image is eventually confirmed,
Chapter 4's opening image should be reviewed against it to ensure the
two present-day chapters are in distinct register — Chapter 1
establishes the governing sentence; Chapter 4 establishes what the
machinery of that fight looks like from inside it. They must not
duplicate each other.

**Chapter function:** To anchor the reader back in the present-day
timeline after the ancestral material of Chapters 2 and 3, and to
establish what the institutional fight actually looks like from inside
it. The court is not rendered as villain. It is machinery: it processes
risk, procedure, history, reports, delays. It does not process love.
That is the chapter's central observation, arrived at through scene and
consequence, never through editorial complaint.

**Governing question:** Q4 primarily — "What does a father owe a child
when the system rewards disappearance?" This is the chapter that shows
what the system is. The answer to Q4 will only be visible in contrast
to what the system does with a father's continued presence. Q5 runs
beneath it — "What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying
the people still inside the story?" — because the legal constraint that
operates throughout this book operates here at full force.

**Factual spine:**
- The family court machinery: hearings, filings, orders, reports,
  waiting rooms, delays, managed access
- A father's love translated into the procedural categories available
  to an institution built for different purposes
- The father present, repeatedly, in a system that cannot fully see
  what he is there to preserve
- The waiting: between dates, between decisions, between one form of
  managed contact and the next

The factual spine for this chapter must come entirely from the author's
own experience of these proceedings — his memory of what these spaces
felt like, what the procedure felt like from inside it. No court
documents. No third-party records. No material under legal protection.
The chapter's authority comes from the narrator's precision about his
own experience, not from the record.

**Emotional centre:** Controlled restraint under institutional pressure.
Not rage. Not grief. Not pleading. A man who has made a bargain with
God and is sitting in a waiting room being processed as a case, and
who has not broken under it. The emotional question of this chapter is
not "how does he survive this?" but "how does he remain present inside
it?" Presence despite procedure is the chapter's emotional register.

**Theological pressure:** Minimal at the surface, structural underneath.
The book has not yet made its full theological claim — that is Chapter
6 and later. But the gap is already visible to the reader who carries
the whole arc: a man who bargained with God for seventy-five years of
service is sitting in an institution that has no category for what a
bargain with God is. The chapter does not say this. The reader who
knows the full book will feel it. The gap is the theological pressure,
not the statement.

**Inheritance thread:** Present but not foregrounded. What Abraham
refuses to pass to Damisi is already being decided in these rooms —
not bitterness, not defeat, not the story the system is constructing
about him. That refusal is Part Four material. Here the inheritance
thread is the fact itself: this is what this man's fatherhood looks
like from the outside. The reader needs to see that before they can
understand what refusing to be defined by it will cost him.

**Fatherhood thread:** This chapter is the most direct introduction of
the fatherhood fight. Damisi should be felt as the reason the father
is in the room — without being made into evidence. The son is a child.
His existence is what the hearing is about; his personhood is what the
hearing cannot fully hold. He does not appear in this chapter as a
speaker or a scene. His presence is registered through its absence in
the system's categories.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** N/A in the death sense. A modified form
applies: before the institutional machinery is fully rendered, the
reader should feel one moment of what the relationship between father
and son actually is — something specific, ordinary, and real — set
against what the court's categories make of it. That ordinary texture
grounds the stakes. Without it, the chapter risks becoming about the
system rather than about what the system cannot hold.

**Craft risk:** Three specific failures:
(a) The legal-brief register — the chapter's most specific danger.
Every sentence about the court must come through scene and consequence,
never through procedural summary, legal advocacy, or list. The narrator
is testifying under oath before God, history, his son, and himself.
Not filing a motion.
(b) Making the system the villain. A reader who finishes this chapter
hating the court has been failed by the narrator. The system is
machinery; it processes what it can process. That is its nature, not
its evil. The restraint here is literary as well as legal: the chapter
that reaches for the reader's anger gives up the reader's trust.
(c) Damisi as exhibit. The son cannot function as evidence for the
father's case, even implicitly. He is a child who is protected by the
book's own craft rules. His love for his father and his father's love
for him are not demonstrated to the reader as proof. They are the
thing that is real, against which the court's categories appear
diminished.

**Prohibited material:** Court documents of any kind. Any third-party
authored record. Any reproduction or structural echo of material
subject to court order. Named parties in the proceedings beyond
Abraham. Legal argument or advocacy framing. The specific procedural
details of Abraham's case as a legal account — this is not a legal
account. Anything that reads as rehearsing the case rather than
testifying to the experience.

**Ending movement:** The hearing date passes. There will be another.
The father is still present. That is the chapter's final beat: he is
still here, the machinery continues, and presence is already a kind
of answer to what the system rewards. Not triumphant. Not resolved.
Continuing.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. One confirmed specific
sensory detail from the author's own memory of a family court
proceeding — not from any document, from memory alone — will unlock
the opening image and make the chapter immediately draftable. Without
it, the risk is generic institutional texture rather than earned
specificity. One real remembered detail is all the chapter needs to
open.

### Chapter 5 — Jumoke

**Opening image:** Jumoke alive. Not the kitchen. Not the day she died.
Not the appearance. Jumoke as she was before any of that — a specific
moment of her being ordinary, particular, herself. Something she said.
A game she played. A habit. The particular way she inhabited being
alive at whatever age this moment belongs to. The opening image of
this chapter is the most load-bearing application of the book's craft
rule — the dead must live before they die — because everything the
book asks of the reader in relation to Jumoke's death depends on
whether they first believed in her life.

The opening image cannot be provided by this treatment. It must come
from the author. See the mundane-before-grief gate below.

**Chapter function:** To introduce Jumoke as a person before she is
introduced as a loss. The chapter also establishes the first of the
three deaths that commanded Abraham to live, and in doing so
establishes the book's central structural logic: the dying do not
merely depart, they instruct. The specific content of Jumoke's
instruction — she died so he could live to fulfil his destiny — is
the sentence that organises the rest of the book. But the chapter
must earn that sentence before it arrives.

**Governing question:** Q2 primarily — "What does death demand from
the living?" This is the question in its most direct, earliest form.
Jumoke's death makes the demand explicit in a way no subsequent death
can match for directness: she appeared and told him. Q1 runs beneath
it — what she demands of him is specifically about what he has already
been named to carry before he understood it.

**Factual spine:**
- Jumoke: Abraham's younger sister. NOT his twin. NOT part of the
  triplet set. A separate younger sister.
- Died at age seven.
- Appeared to Abraham in the kitchen on the day of her death.
- The sentence she left: she died so he could live to fulfil his
  destiny.
- She must be established as a living person before any of the above
  is introduced.

Chronological note for drafting: The source material indicates that
the fire and bargain (Chapter 6) occur when Abraham is eleven. Whether
Jumoke's death preceded or followed the fire chronologically — and
therefore whether Jumoke was among the sisters Abraham saved during
the fire — should be confirmed by the author before the chapter is
drafted. The book presents Chapter 5 before Chapter 6 for structural
and thematic reasons, not necessarily chronological ones. Do not
assume the sequence; confirm it.

The specific ordinary detail that establishes Jumoke alive is NOT in
the source material and must not be invented.

**Emotional centre:** A child receiving a command too large for a child
to hold, and then holding it anyway, because he has no choice. Not the
death itself — that arrives as part of the chapter but is not its
centre. The centre is what follows: a boy standing with a sentence
that has just rewritten his entire future, in a kitchen or its
aftermath, in the stillness of not yet knowing what it requires.

**Theological pressure:** The highest of any Part One chapter. This is
where the supernatural material enters the book at full weight for the
first time — the appearance in the kitchen, the sentence, the fact
that she came to give him an instruction she could not have given in
life because he did not yet know she was going to die. The strategy
confirmed in the source proposal applies absolutely: narrate plainly,
without apology and without inflation. The reader has been prepared
by the narrator's precision in Chapters 2, 3, and 4 to trust what
they are about to be told. Do not betray that trust by performing the
moment.

**Inheritance thread:** Jumoke is the first transmission. What she
passes to Abraham is not material — it is obligation. She leaves him
a sentence about purpose that he will spend the rest of his life
working out. The inheritance thread in this chapter is obligation
itself, in its earliest form, arriving before he is old enough to
understand what it means.

**Fatherhood thread:** Distant but present as the faintest forward
echo. Abraham is a child in this chapter. The command to live and to
fulfil his destiny will eventually mean — will only fully mean — that
he is a father who refuses to disappear. But the chapter should not
reach for that connection. Jumoke's death is a child's death. The
promise it produces is a child's promise, made in a kitchen. Let it
remain that. The book will draw the line in its own time.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** FULL HARD GATE. This chapter cannot be
opened without one author-confirmed ordinary detail about Jumoke alive.
A phrase she used. Something she found funny. A habit. Something
physical — the way she ran, a game, what she called him. The detail
must be specific enough that a reader would recognise her as an
individual rather than as a function in the story. It must come from
the author's memory, not from inference or craft instinct.

This is the strictest application of the mundane-before-grief gate
in Part One. Chapter 15 (Funke) required the same gate; "small mummy"
was the detail that filled it. Chapter 5 requires the equivalent for
Jumoke. The chapter will not be attempted without it.

**Craft risk:** Three specific failures:
(a) The kitchen appearance as spectacle — the danger is rendering the
appearance as a supernatural event rather than as an experience.
Jumoke does not arrive as an effect. She arrives the way these things
arrive in a life: without announcement, in a room that is already
real, to a person who is not prepared. The narration must stay inside
Abraham's experience — what he perceived, in its exact strangeness —
not at a remove that makes the event cinematic.
(b) The sentence as the chapter's climax — "she died so he could live
to fulfil his destiny" is the chapter's centre, not its crescendo. The
danger is building toward it dramatically, so that it lands as
revelation. It should arrive the way something inevitable arrives:
not as a surprise, but as a confirmation of something that has been
true all along, now spoken aloud for the first time.
(c) Premature interpretation — the chapter should not tell the reader
what this means for Abraham's life, what it will cost him, what it
explains about the chapters ahead. The sentence is given. The reader
is left inside it with Abraham. The interpretation belongs to the rest
of the book.

**Prohibited material:** No comparison to David's death or Funke's
death — those chapters will draw the pattern; this chapter is still
inside the first death, which must be felt on its own terms. No
anticipation of the fire or the bargain (Chapter 6). No theological
framework for the appearance — the book does not adjudicate what the
appearance was or wasn't; it is the narrator's experience, stated as
fact, open to the reader's interpretation. No use of Jumoke as symbol
in this chapter — she is a person here, and she stays a person.

**Ending movement:** Abraham holding the sentence Jumoke left. Not
understanding it. Not yet acting on it. The chapter does not move
forward. It holds. The ending beat is the weight of a command given
to someone too young to know what it requires — and the silence of
that kitchen, or its echo, after she is gone.

**Drafting readiness:** HARD GATE. The chapter cannot be opened
without one confirmed ordinary detail about Jumoke alive. All other
material is confirmed and in place. The gate is singular and specific.
When the author provides the detail, the chapter is immediately ready
to draft.

PART ONE OUTSTANDING ITEMS

1. **Chapter 1 (Something Always Happens)** — HELD per author boundary.
   No task attached. Raise when ready; not before.

2. **Chapter 2 (Isua to Agege)** — CONDITIONALLY READY. One physical
   detail from the walk itself — season, period, or any small grounding
   particular — would anchor the opening image with the precision the
   voice rules require.

3. **Chapter 3 (The Boy Who Was Already Old)** — CONDITIONALLY READY.
   Gate: what specifically Iyalode believed she saw in Abraham —
   the nature of the "troubling spiritual resemblance" — must be
   confirmed before the rejection scene can be fully drafted.

4. **Chapter 4 (A Hearing Date)** — CONDITIONALLY READY. One confirmed
   specific sensory detail from the author's own memory of a family
   court proceeding — not from any document — will unlock the opening
   image and make the chapter immediately draftable.

5. **Chapter 5 (Jumoke)** — HARD GATE. One confirmed ordinary detail
   about Jumoke alive — a phrase, a habit, something specifically her
   — is required before the chapter can be opened. Cannot be invented
   or inferred.

6. **Chapter 5 — chronological note** — Confirm relative chronology
   of Jumoke's death and the fire/bargain (Chapter 6), specifically
   whether Jumoke was alive during the fire at Abraham's age eleven.
   The book presents Ch.5 before Ch.6 for structural reasons; the
   drafting of Chapter 5 should know whether the events are presented
   out of chronological order, and if so, how the draft handles the
   anachronism.

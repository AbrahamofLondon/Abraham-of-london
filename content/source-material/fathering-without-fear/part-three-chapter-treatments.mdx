PART THREE CHAPTER TREATMENTS — TRACK B

Part Three is *Love, Death, and Collateral Damage*. Its function is
to move Abraham from the London arrival of Chapter 10 through the
full arc of attraction, marriage, warning, grief, and collapse —
arriving at the threshold of Part Four's legal and systemic fight.
The danger throughout is heat. This section must not become
accusation, melodrama, litigation, or therapy transcript. Its job
is to preserve emotional truth while keeping the narrator morally
credible.

Chapter 15 (Funke) is the grief/premonition hinge inside Part Three.
It is fully treated in `hinge-chapter-treatments.mdx` and is not
re-treated here. This file covers Chapters 11, 12, 13, 14, and 16.

Chapter numbers follow the final 22-chapter sequence. The Five
Governing Questions are quoted exactly from "The Five Questions" in
`fathering-without-fear-source.mdx`:

1. What does a man inherit before he understands himself?
2. What does death demand from the living?
3. What does faith mean when God preserves your life but not your comfort?
4. What does a father owe a child when the system rewards disappearance?
5. What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying the people still inside the story?

================================================================

PART THREE STRUCTURAL ARGUMENT

Part Three covers the period from Abraham's London arrival (late in
Chapter 10, Part Two) through the collapse of his marriage and the
beginning of the fatherhood fight. The timeline is not strictly
linear. Chapter 13 (Stephanie) is placed after the marriage chapters
(11–12) as a retrospective, functioning as earlier-formation material
that the reader needs to understand why Abraham was vulnerable in the
London relationship. The insertion follows the book's existing
structural logic — the same interweaving of ancestral/prior material
with present-day events used throughout the book.

The section must pass four tests throughout:

**1. No legal contamination.** Do not import court documents, Home
Office records, transcripts, or third-party legal records. Part Three
can point toward the legal fight ahead, but it must not become a
legal bundle. The legal material is Part Four's territory.

**2. No revenge prose.** Where relational harm appears: state what
happened; avoid theatrical judgment; avoid name-calling; avoid proving
too much; do not write as if arguing before a judge. The reader must
feel the damage without feeling manipulated.

**3. No saint/villain flattening.** Abraham must not appear as an
innocent hero. The other parties must not appear as cartoon antagonists.
Where Abraham was confused, hopeful, naïve, stubborn, spiritually
pressured, or self-deceiving, the treatments must leave room for that.
The memoir's authority comes from this restraint.

**4. No grief spectacle.** Chapter 15 is the Part Three grief hinge.
Do not dilute it by spreading the same grief logic across adjacent
chapters. Each loss in Part Three — David, Funke — must have its own
distinct weight and its own distinct register.

The reader who finishes Part Three should understand, without being
told: that the relational collapse was not a clean story of victim
and villain; that every major death and departure arrived in a man
already carrying an enormous prior load; that the fatherhood fight
now beginning in Part Four has been earned by everything the previous
fifteen chapters established; and that the bargain with God made in
Chapter 6 will now be tested in its hardest terrain.

CARRY-FORWARD NOTE — Q5 GOVERNS PART THREE

Question 5 — "What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying
the people still inside the story?" — operates as the governing
craft constraint throughout Part Three. All factual material about
the marriage, the relational collapse, and the people involved must
be derived from Abraham's own memory and approved source material.
No court documents. No Home Office records. No third-party legal
accounts. No speculation about other people's internal states or
motivations beyond what Abraham directly perceived.

This constraint is not weakness. It is the source of the memoir's
authority. The reader who is given the consequence without the
accusation arrives at their own verdict with far more conviction
than the reader who has been argued at.

### Chapter 11 — A Post on Facebook

**Opening image:** Not the post. The state Abraham is in when he
encounters it — what he is looking for, or not looking for, in the
moment the connection begins. The specific quality of life in London
in those first weeks: a new city that has not yet become familiar,
grief recently arrived from Lagos, a man trying to build something
from ground level. Not romance as subject. The human condition of
the person who is about to step toward something. The image opens
before the connection, in the ordinary life that preceded it.

**Chapter function:** To establish how Abraham entered this
relationship — what he wanted, what he thought he saw, what made
him vulnerable to the speed that followed. The chapter must make
the reader understand why Abraham moved toward this connection
without making the connection look obviously doomed from the first
page. The reader must feel what Abraham felt at the time, not the
narrator's retrospective judgment about what he should have felt.
The chapter earns the later chapters by first making the beginning
real.

**Governing question:** Q1 runs beneath the surface — "What does
a man inherit before he understands himself?" The vulnerability
Abraham brings to this moment is not random. It is the vulnerability
of a man who has just lost his father, left his country, carries a
mandate he has never been able to put down, and has a deep-coded
instinct to provide and protect that can misfire in romantic
territory. Q5 governs the craft absolutely throughout.

**Factual spine:**
- August 2017: Abraham arrives in London (Chapter 10)
- The connection begins through Facebook — a post, what it was, what
  led to it — specific details must be confirmed with author; the
  factual spine here comes from Abraham's memory, not from any legal
  record or third-party document
- He has just left Lagos carrying grief (David Senior's death, August
  2017), ambition, and the illusion of a cleaner future
- What he initially saw or felt in the connection: what seemed
  providential, what felt like recognition, what spoke to his hunger
  for belonging in a new city — must be confirmed with author
- What Abraham later identified (retrospectively) as warning signs:
  mirroring, speed, emotional exhaustion, the danger of being seen at
  the exact moment of maximum vulnerability — these are retrospective
  readings, not necessarily visible at the time; the chapter must
  hold this distinction

**Emotional centre:** The specific quality of vulnerability that
comes from arriving in a new country, newly bereaved, carrying a
destiny you've never been able to put down, and then feeling seen —
or feeling seen for the first time in that new place. The chapter's
emotional ground is not romantic naivety. It is the particular
openness that comes from grief, dislocation, and a long inheritance
of believing something important is coming. That openness is real
and human; the chapter holds it without mockery.

**Theological pressure:** The word "providential" (from the Prose
Skeleton's description of this chapter) is the theological pressure.
Abraham initially experienced the connection as something that felt
like it might be aligned with what God had preserved him for. The
chapter carries this as what he believed at the time — not mocked,
not confirmed, not refuted. The gap between what he believed and
what he later understood is present in the background, not
foregrounded.

**Inheritance thread:** The founding ancestor set out toward Oyo
and found himself in a different destination. The pattern of moving
toward what looks like the right place — and the discovery that the
destination is not what the departure promised — is the family's
deepest structural truth. Chapter 11 carries this inheritance lightly.
Abraham moves toward this connection as a man who inherited the
instinct to depart toward what seems right. The chapter does not
name the inheritance pattern. The reader who has carried Parts One
and Two will feel it.

**Fatherhood thread:** Absent at this stage. This chapter precedes
fatherhood. Damisi is in the unreached future. The chapter does not
reach for him.

**Mundane-before-rupture gate:** Before anything about the
relationship's arc — before the connection, before the speed, before
any retrospective judgment — one specific ordinary detail of Abraham's
life in London in those first weeks. Not grief, not destiny, not
romance. Something specific and small that makes him a person in a
city: what the flat was like, what he ate, what he did on a particular
morning, how the city looked before it became familiar. One confirmed
detail from author.

**Craft risk:**
(a) Romantic simplification — making the relationship look obviously
doomed from the chapter's first page. The reader must understand why
Abraham moved toward this, not merely observe him moving. The chapter
is inside his experience, not above it.
(b) Retrospective over-judgment — the retrospective narrator's
understanding of what the connection was must not shade the chapter's
narration of how it felt. The older narrator can hold the retrospective
lens, but it must sit lightly over the younger man's experience.
(c) Legal contamination — the relationship's later legal dimension
must not leak into this chapter. No court language, no documentation
references, no framing that prepares a legal account rather than a
testimony.

**Prohibited material:** Court documents, legal records, third-party
accounts, Home Office records. Any characterisation of the other
person as villain or antagonist. The marriage (Chapter 12). David's
death or Funke's death (later chapters). Any theological verdict
on whether the connection was or was not providential.

**Ending movement:** The connection made. Abraham invested. The
chapter ends at the point where what is being entered feels real
and promising — even if the reader suspects it will not hold. The
ending beat is Abraham moving toward something he believes in,
before the chapter corrects him.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. Two gates: (1) the
specific nature of what Abraham saw or felt in the initial connection
— what seemed providential, what spoke to his vulnerability — must
be confirmed with author; (2) one specific ordinary detail of
Abraham's London life in those first weeks fills the mundane-before-
rupture gate.

### Chapter 12 — Married by December

**Opening image:** Not the wedding. Not the ceremony. The specific
moment when Abraham understood — or did not yet understand — the
velocity at which everything had moved. August arrival, December
marriage: four months. The image is the speed itself, made physical:
a calendar, a date on a form, the particular feeling of having made
an irreversible decision in a city that has not yet become fully
real. Not judgment — scale. A man measuring the distance between
where he was and where he now is.

**Chapter function:** To show how quickly an entire future can be
rearranged before a man fully understands what he has entered. The
chapter is not about stupidity or romance alone — it is about
velocity: how the human capacity for hope, need, and pattern-
recognition can commit a person to something whose full nature they
cannot yet see. The chapter must make the reader understand the
marriage without excusing its haste, and hold Abraham's complexity
inside it — his inherited instinct to step up and commit — without
collapsing him into a fool or a victim.

**Governing question:** Q1 runs beneath — "What does a man inherit
before he understands himself?" The helper/provider pattern
inherited from David Senior's fifteen-year-old formation now operates
in a context it was not built for. Q5 governs: the truth told here
must not destroy the people still inside the story.

**Factual spine:**
- August 2017: Abraham arrives in London
- December 2017: Abraham is married — approximately four months later
- The specific sequence of what accelerated the decision between
  August and December must be confirmed with author
- The first signs of fracture (held as retrospective observation):
  what the chapter reveals, with restraint, about what was visible
  only in hindsight
- The hope that kept Abraham invested through early warning signs
- Whether and how any sense of spiritual confirmation shaped the
  decision — what Abraham believed at the time, held as his belief

**Emotional centre:** The specific difficulty of distinguishing love,
rescue, loyalty, and fear in real time — and of trusting a pattern
of conviction that has served you in other contexts. Abraham believed
something. That belief moved with velocity. The chapter's emotional
ground is what happens when the instinct to commit, inherited from
a lineage of people who committed without full information, runs
into a context it cannot correctly read.

**Theological pressure:** The inherited pattern of stepping up and
committing without complete knowledge — the founding ancestor's
departure toward Oyo, David Senior's fathering of his siblings at
fifteen — now operates in the register of marriage. Whether Abraham
experienced spiritual confirmation of the marriage must be confirmed
with author; whatever he believed must be held as his belief at the
time, not as the book's endorsement.

**Inheritance thread:** David Senior committed without preparation
at fifteen and found provision. The founding ancestor set out without
knowing his destination and arrived somewhere real. Abraham commits
here in the same inherited posture — without full information, with
conviction, with the instinct that stepping up is always the right
move. The pattern is accurate in its domain and misfiring here. The
chapter carries this lightly.

**Fatherhood thread:** Damisi is in the future of this marriage.
The reader knows this; the chapter does not reach for it. What the
chapter establishes is the trajectory being set: a man who commits
unconditionally will later be asked to remain present in the aftermath
of that commitment's collapse. The fatherhood thread is present as
potential energy, not yet released.

**Mundane-before-rupture gate:** Before any retrospective fracture
or judgment, one specific ordinary detail of the early marriage —
something real and specific that was genuinely between them, something
that made the marriage feel like something. The relationship must be
real before it can be lost. One confirmed detail from author.

**Craft risk:**
(a) Retrospective over-explanation — the chapter must be narrated
from inside the experience first. The older narrator can hold the
retrospective understanding, but it must not swamp the younger man's
lived experience of what he believed he was doing.
(b) Victim-performance — the chapter that builds to "and this is
why I didn't know better" begs for sympathy in the wrong register.
State what happened, what was believed, what was later understood.
Not: witness that I was wronged.
(c) Villain construction — the person Abraham married must not
appear as a cartoon antagonist here or anywhere in Part Three. The
memoir's authority requires moral seriousness about all parties.

**Prohibited material:** Court documents, legal records, third-party
assessments of the marriage. Any framing that argues a legal case.
The collapse of the marriage (Chapter 16). Funke's death (Chapter
15). David's death (Chapter 14). The chapter stays inside the
marriage's early phase only.

**Ending movement:** The marriage entered. The velocity not yet
legible as a problem. The chapter ends with Abraham inside a
commitment he believes in, at the stage where the full nature of
what he has entered has not yet resolved. The reader holds the
knowledge of what comes. The chapter holds Abraham where he is.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. Two gates: (1) the
specific sequence of what happened between August and December 2017
that accelerated the marriage decision must be confirmed with author;
(2) one specific ordinary detail of the early marriage fills the
mundane-before-rupture gate.

### Chapter 13 — Stephanie

**Opening image:** Not Stephanie. Not desire. A specific moment from
the national service period — where Abraham was stationed, what the
work or placement was, what the physical environment felt like —
that grounds the chapter before the relationship arrives. A young
man in a place not entirely his own, on an obligation not of his
choosing, finding his way through a structure imposed from outside.
The ordinary texture of national service life: the particular quality
of that placement, that season, that role. Not romance. The room
before romance.

**Chapter function:** To illuminate, through a formative earlier
encounter, the romantic and spiritual inheritance Abraham brought
to London. The chapter is placed after Chapters 11 and 12 not
because it happened after them — it happened earlier, during national
service in Nigeria — but because the reader needs this prior
formation to understand the London vulnerability more fully. It
is a retrospective chapter: the book steps back to show where
Abraham's patterns in romantic territory were first shaped. After
the speed of Chapters 11–12, Chapter 13 gives the reader the earlier
experience that makes that speed legible.

The chapter also establishes the specific kind of spiritual pressure
Abraham encountered in intimate territory: spirit-husband disclosure,
warning, the gap between what he felt and what was actually present.
This is the first time the book places spiritual danger inside
romantic attachment. It will not be the last.

**Governing question:** Q3 primarily — "What does faith mean when
God preserves your life but not your comfort?" — because the
Stephanie encounter placed Abraham inside a form of spiritual danger
he underestimated, and the discomfort of that encounter was part of
his formation. Q1 runs beneath it: what does a man inherit before
he understands himself? The patterns of how Abraham moved in
romantic territory — what he was drawn toward, what he dismissed,
what he underestimated — were formed before London.

**Factual spine:**
- National service period: NYSC (Nigerian Youth Service Corps),
  typically post-university; the specific state or placement must
  be confirmed with author
- Stephanie: a person Abraham encountered during this period
- The relationship that developed between them: nature, duration,
  what it was — must be confirmed from Abraham's memory
- Her journal: the specific nature of what it contained or what
  Abraham encountered in it — must be confirmed with author before
  treating as settled fact
- The spirit-husband disclosure: what this was, in what context
  it was made, what Abraham understood at the time — must be
  confirmed; do not speculate about its spiritual status
- The warnings that followed: who gave them, in what form, what
  Abraham did with them — must be confirmed
- What Abraham "underestimated" in this encounter (from Prose
  Skeleton)

All factual material comes from Abraham's own memory and approved
source material. No speculation about Stephanie's internal states
or motivations. No construction of her as a villain.

**Emotional centre:** The specific quality of youthful misreading —
not stupidity, but the blindness that comes from desire, inexperience
in spiritual discernment, and the instinct to rescue rather than to
protect oneself. Abraham was drawn toward something and did not
yet have the vocabulary to read what was in it. The chapter's
emotional ground is the experience of being inside an encounter
whose real nature you cannot yet see — and the specific texture
of understanding, years later, what the warning had been and how
completely you missed it.

**Theological pressure:** The spirit-husband disclosure and the
warnings that follow are this chapter's theological territory.
Abraham encountered, in the Stephanie relationship, a form of
spiritual interference his earlier formation had not prepared him
for. The book handles this the way it handles all spiritual material:
narrate what happened, what was believed, what was warned, what
was later understood. Do not adjudicate between spiritual
frameworks. Do not dismiss what was reported. Do not make it
theatrical.

**Inheritance thread:** The Stephanie encounter is where a blind
spot in Abraham's spiritual discernment was first formed — or first
revealed. What he failed to read here, he brought forward to London.
The inheritance thread in Chapter 13 is negative formation: the
specific gap in Abraham's readiness for what romantic territory
could contain. He arrived in London already carrying this gap.

**Fatherhood thread:** Absent at this stage. This is a young man's
story from a time before fatherhood was a category in his life.
The chapter does not reach forward.

**Mundane-before-rupture gate:** The national service placement as
ordinary life: what the assignment was, what the community looked
like, what a typical day contained — before the relationship
arrives. One specific grounding detail from that period (not about
Stephanie, not about the spiritual material). Confirmed with author.

**Craft risk:**
(a) Spiritual overclaiming — the spirit-husband material is specific
and serious; the chapter reports what Abraham understood to be
happening, not what the book pronounces as metaphysical fact. The
reader is not asked to agree; they are asked to understand what
Abraham encountered and how he processed it.
(b) Making Stephanie a villain — she is a person from Abraham's
past who carried something he was not prepared for. The chapter does
not reduce her to that thing. She must exist as a person even if
the chapter's focus is on what Abraham encountered rather than who
she was.
(c) Over-drawing the line to London — the reader will draw the
connection from Stephanie to the London relationship without
assistance. The chapter must not draw it for them. State what
happened. Let the resonance travel forward without annotation.

**Prohibited material:** Stephanie's internal states or motivations
beyond what Abraham directly knew or perceived. Court records or
legal material of any kind. Any direct connection named between the
Stephanie story and later legal proceedings. Any construction of
Stephanie's journal contents as confirmed spiritual diagnosis rather
than as what Abraham encountered at the time. Any framing that
treats the spirit-husband disclosure as proof of any theological
position about spiritual marriage.

**Ending movement:** The Stephanie chapter closes on what Abraham
carried away from it: what he understood, and — more important —
what he did not yet understand. The warning was given. It was held
lightly, not fully absorbed. The reader feels the resonance forward
without the chapter naming it.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. Three gates: (1) the
specific nature of what was in Stephanie's journal and what the
spirit-husband disclosure was — must be confirmed with author before
drafting; (2) the specific warnings given after the disclosure
— who gave them, what form they took — must be confirmed; (3) one
grounding ordinary detail of the national service period. All three
needed before the chapter can be opened.

### Chapter 14 — David Was Missing From the Wedding

**Opening image:** Not David missing. Not the wedding. David present:
a specific ordinary detail of who David was to Abraham — the
companion who stood beside him through childhood, school, university,
every private crossing of his life. Not his absence; his presence.
Not what his death demanded; who he was. Something small and specific
that makes David a person before the chapter reveals what the chapter
is actually about. One ordinary detail of David alive, held in a
brother's memory.

**Chapter function:** To render David as the companion — the brother,
full stop — and to hold his absence from a wedding in a trance as
the warning it was, and his death as the confirmation it became.
The chapter's primary obligation is to the person: David must live
before he dies. The craft rule applies absolutely here. The chapter
establishes David as the one who knew the same history Abraham
knows from inside the same childhood — the specific kind of witness
only a sibling can be. When he dies, Abraham loses that witness.
The chapter must make the reader feel what is being lost before it
shows the loss.

**Governing question:** Q2 primarily — "What does death demand from
the living?" — but not yet in its most developed form. David's death
makes a demand of Abraham. The chapter begins to register what that
demand is. The full weight of three deaths together and what they
collectively commanded is Part Four's territory; here the question
is present as the beginning of its answer. Q1 runs beneath: what
does Abraham carry after the companion of the full inheritance is
gone?

**Factual spine:**
- David: Abraham's brother — full stop (canon: never qualified,
  never "cousin," in any file under any circumstance)
- The nature of their brotherhood: the companion through childhood,
  school, university, and every private crossing — specific details
  of this companionship must be confirmed with author before the
  opening image can be drafted
- David was in a trance during or around a wedding — the specific
  wedding (whose, when, what the occasion was) must be confirmed
  with author
- David's absence in that state was the warning; his death was
  the confirmation
- David appeared to Abraham in a trance on the day of his death —
  the specific nature of this appearance, what Abraham experienced,
  what David communicated if anything — must be confirmed with author
- When David died: approximate date or period must be confirmed

**Emotional centre:** Brotherhood before loss. The specific quality
of losing a brother — which is not the same as losing a sister, a
parent, or a companion. A brother is a parallel self: the one who
grew up alongside you, who knows the same history from the same
angle, who has been your witness across the full inheritance. To
lose a brother is to lose the person who knew you from inside the
same story. The chapter must honour that specific kind of loss
before it becomes anything else.

**Theological pressure:** David's appearance in a trance on the day
of his death is this chapter's supernatural territory. The pattern:
Jumoke appeared in the kitchen on the day of her death; David appears
in a trance on the day of his. The book handles both the same way.
Narrate plainly, without apology and without inflation. What Abraham
experienced is stated as what he experienced. The reader is not
asked to adjudicate whether the trance was "really" a communication
or a coincidence of timing. It is stated as the thing that happened.

**Inheritance thread:** David's death removes the last peer-witness
of Abraham's full formation. The reader has met Abraham's grandmother,
father, sisters, and the ancestral line behind them. David is the
one who knew all of that from inside the same childhood. After his
death, Abraham carries the inheritance without its closest human
witness. What is handed on from Chapter 14 is not an instruction
(as Jumoke gave one) but an absence — the specific absence of being
the last one who knew it all from inside.

**Fatherhood thread:** Forward-pointing but not yet arrived. The
wedding from which David was absent is connected to the relational
world of Abraham's marriage. The trance/absence is a warning within
that context. The chapter does not resolve what the warning was
about — that is Part Three's arc, unfolding in the chapters that
surround Chapter 14. The fatherhood thread is present only as
potential energy: the man who is losing his brother is moving
toward a fight that will require exactly the kind of witness David
was.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** The gate applies absolutely. Before
the trance, before the wedding, before the death — one specific
ordinary detail of David alive: something he said, a habit of his
company, a quality of how he moved in Abraham's life. The chapter
cannot open on his absence. It must open on his presence.

**Craft risk:**
(a) Grief spectacle — two grief-events in this chapter (the trance
and the death). Neither must be theatrical. The brother must be felt
before the loss is rendered; the death must register as weight, not
as drama.
(b) Pre-emptive meaning-making — David's death is part of the book's
structural argument about three people dying and commanding Abraham
to live. The chapter must not make his death into a neatly labelled
station on that argument. Let it be what it is: the loss of a
specific brother, in specific circumstances.
(c) Bleeding into Chapter 15 — Funke's death follows immediately.
Chapter 14 must be David's chapter. His death and her death are two
distinct losses with two distinct weights. They must not bleed into
each other. Chapter 14 ends before Chapter 15 begins, and the reader
must feel the difference.

**Prohibited material:** Funke — her chapter immediately follows;
let Chapter 14 be David's. The full account of what David's death
demanded of Abraham (Part Four territory, developed across the book's
full arc). Speculation about what the trance meant beyond what
Abraham believed and experienced at the time. Any use of the wedding
as occasion to characterise the marriage (that belongs in Chapters
12 and 16).

**Ending movement:** David is dead. The warning has become the
confirmation. The chapter ends in the specific weight of that
confirmation: not grief as performance, not grief as escalation
toward Funke's death, not a transition sentence that rushes toward
Chapter 15. The ending beat is Abraham alone with what David's death
means — which is not yet fully legible. Something irreplaceable has
been taken. The chapter ends before the full reckoning.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. Three gates: (1) one
specific ordinary detail of David alive — who he was, how he moved
in Abraham's life, one particular thing — must be confirmed with
author before the mundane-before-grief gate can be filled; (2) the
specific wedding David was absent from — whose, when — must be
confirmed; (3) the specific nature of the trance/appearance on the
day of David's death — what Abraham experienced, what David
communicated if anything — must be confirmed.

### Chapter 16 — Pregnant and Discarded

**Opening image:** Not the divorce filing. Not the legal machinery.
The specific texture of being in the middle of collapse while the
world keeps demanding that you function. August 2017 arrival,
Chapter 15's grief still carried, a new child either expected or
recently born, the structure that was supposed to be home beginning
to close. Not accusation, not documentation — the specific physical
or emotional weight of holding a newborn obligation while everything
built around it dismantles. The image opens in the body, in the
ordinary demands of daily life, before it names what is happening.

**Chapter function:** To show the aftermath and collateral damage
following Chapter 15 and the beginning of the marriage collapse,
handling both through consequence, not accusation. The chapter shows
what changed, what it cost, what Abraham understood then and what
he understands now. It does not speculate where the record cannot
safely go. This chapter is the transition from Part Three to Part
Four: it ends the relational arc and opens the legal/systemic fight.
The reader who finishes Chapter 16 should understand that the
marriage ended, a son exists, the system is about to become the
arena, and the father who refuses to disappear is about to face
the most demanding test of what that refusal costs.

**Governing question:** Q4 moves from background to foreground for
the first time — "What does a father owe a child when the system
rewards disappearance?" The moment Damisi's existence is fully
established in the context of a collapsing marriage, Q4 becomes
the book's active question. Q5 governs the craft absolutely:
what does it cost to tell the truth without destroying the people
still inside the story?

**Factual spine:**
- The marriage collapse: what changed, what became undeniable, when
  the collapse became evident — confirmed from Abraham's own memory;
  no legal documents
- Funke died two days before the divorce filing was lodged (from
  canon): the proximity of her death and the legal beginning of the
  collapse must be present in the chapter
- Damisi: his existence within this context — when he was born or
  expected, what his presence meant for the shape of the collapse
  — must be confirmed with author; specifically, how the pregnancy
  and birth relate to the collapse timeline
- What Abraham understood at the time vs. what he understands now:
  the chapter holds both without collapsing them
- The specific form the collapse took (departure, filing, change of
  access) — confirmed from Abraham's memory; no legal documents
- The title "Pregnant and Discarded" is the author's framing; the
  colloquial weight of "discarded" names an experience, and the
  chapter should carry that weight without building a legal case
  from it

**Emotional centre:** The particular density of holding everything
that has happened — Funke dead, marriage ending, new life beginning,
old country behind, new system ahead — simultaneously, without any
of it resolving into a single feeling. Not pure grief (that is
Chapter 15's register). Not pure anger (the wrong register for this
memoir's voice). The emotional ground of Chapter 16 is the specific
heaviness of being in the rubble of something you committed to, in
a country not your origin, with a child whose existence makes
leaving impossible. The reader must feel this complexity without
being handed a verdict.

**Theological pressure:** The bargain with God (Chapter 6) is now
in its hardest terrain. Abraham committed to seventy-five years of
service and was preserved. He is being preserved in circumstances
that do not look like preservation from inside them. The theological
pressure is not resolution — it is the gap between the vow made in
Chapter 6 and the reality in which that vow must now be lived. The
chapter does not resolve this gap. It holds it. The reader who knows
the full arc will feel it.

**Inheritance thread:** The founding ancestor built something and
died before his children could hold what he built. David Senior
stepped up into loss and raised what he was given to raise without
being prepared for it. Abraham is now in the same pattern — but in
London, not Lagos; in a legal system, not a family compound; with a
son to protect, not siblings to raise. The inheritance thread in
Chapter 16 is the continuity of the pattern: the man who must do
in a strange city what the generation before him could not finish.

**Fatherhood thread:** Fully present for the first time. Damisi
exists. The marriage is collapsing around that existence. What
Abraham owes Damisi is no longer an abstraction or a potential;
it is the active, daily, costly question of his life. Chapter 16
is where the fatherhood thread becomes the book's central thread.
Everything the previous fifteen chapters established has been
building toward this moment. The chapter must show the beginning
of that weight without yet showing how Abraham will carry it —
that is Part Four's territory.

**Mundane-before-rupture gate:** Before the collapse is named or
shown — before the filing, the departure, the legal beginning —
one specific ordinary detail of daily life with Damisi. Something
small and specific that makes the father-son relationship real before
the institutional apparatus arrives around it. A moment with the
child, not a demonstration for an audience. One confirmed detail
from author.

**Craft risk:**
(a) Victim-performance — the chapter must show what happened and
what it cost, not argue that Abraham deserves sympathy. The reader
who has been with Abraham across fifteen chapters knows what he has
carried. The chapter trusts that knowledge rather than performing
grief or grievance.
(b) Revenge prose — this is the chapter where the temptation to
name, accuse, and characterise is highest. The memoir's legal
exposure and the emotional weight of the collapse make restraint
here the hardest and most important craft requirement. Show
consequence. State fact. Do not assign motive. Do not write as
if arguing before a judge.
(c) Aftermath blur — a chapter that becomes vague grief fog after
the specificity of Chapter 15 would fail both chapters. Chapter 16
needs concrete consequence: what specifically changed, what
specifically was lost, what specifically Damisi's existence demanded
of Abraham in the aftermath.

**Prohibited material:** Court documents, Home Office records,
third-party legal accounts, material subject to court order.
Any characterisation of the other person that functions as legal
argument. Specific allegations. The full scope of the legal
battle (Part Four). Any scene in which Damisi functions as
evidence rather than as a child. The chapter shows the beginning
of the fight; Part Four shows the fight itself.

**Ending movement:** The marriage ended. The legal machinery
beginning. Abraham still present — still in London, still holding
Damisi, still carrying the promise that was made before any of
this was foreseeable. The ending beat is not resolution. It is
the threshold of Part Four: the father who refuses to disappear
about to enter the system that rewards disappearance. The chapter
ends at the beginning of the longest part of the fight.

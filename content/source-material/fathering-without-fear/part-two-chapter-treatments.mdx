PART TWO CHAPTER TREATMENTS — TRACK B

Part Two is *The Bargain with God*. Its function is to establish the
theological inheritance behind Abraham's childhood — the fire, Iyalode,
family fear, faith, protection, and spiritual burden — and to clarify
that the spiritual life the book depicts is not phenomenon in isolation
but inheritance under pressure. The part must not become sensational
spiritual memoir. The tone remains plain, disciplined, concrete, and
morally serious throughout.

Chapter 6 (The Fire and the Deal) is the hinge chapter that opens Part
Two. It is fully treated in `hinge-chapter-treatments.mdx`. These
treatments cover Chapters 7–10.

Chapter numbers follow the final 22-chapter sequence. The Five
Governing Questions are quoted exactly from "The Five Questions" in
`fathering-without-fear-source.mdx`:

1. What does a man inherit before he understands himself?
2. What does death demand from the living?
3. What does faith mean when God preserves your life but not your comfort?
4. What does a father owe a child when the system rewards disappearance?
5. What does it cost to tell the truth without destroying the people still inside the story?

================================================================

PART TWO STRUCTURAL ARGUMENT

Part Two takes the reader from the spiritual covenant of Chapter 6 into
the full theological inheritance that gave Abraham the formation to make
such a covenant and to carry it forward. Chapter 6 is the hinge: the
bargain made. Chapters 7–10 are what the bargain was built on and what
it immediately cost.

The Christian thread must be handled with precision across Part Two.
The founding ancestor (Chapter 2) was the first known family convert
to Christianity — the origin point of the faith line. Iyalode's
Christianity in Chapters 7 and 8 is therefore not the family's first
entry into Christian faith. It is a return to, or continuation of,
the faith her husband brought into the family. She is the woman who
transmits the name of Jesus to Abraham with weight — but she does so
as a person who is herself carrying a faith already present in the
family before her. This distinction shapes how Chapters 7 and 8 must
be written: Iyalode is not the origin. She is the living embodiment of
a thread that runs back through her husband and forward through her
grandson.

This must be handled quietly. Not as a symmetry to be announced. Not
as theology to be explained. As a condition of the inheritance that
the reader carries in the background.

The reader who finishes Part Two should understand, without being told:
that the faith Abraham carries was not chosen from a menu but received
as inheritance and encounter; that the people who shaped him are
irreducible; that the pattern of the dead making demands on the living
is not unique to Abraham's generation but is the family's deepest
structural truth; and that the departure for London in Chapter 10
carries everything Part Two has established, across an ocean, into a
world not designed to hold it.

CARRY-FORWARD NOTE — IYALODE'S CHRISTIANITY

This note governs all treatment of Iyalode's faith in Chapters 7 and 8,
and should be referenced whenever Iyalode's Christianity appears
anywhere in the manuscript or its source material.

**Iyalode's Christianity is not the family's first entry into Christian
faith.** The founding ancestor — Iyalode's husband — was (in family oral
history) the first in the lineage to convert. His work as tailor to the
British missionary is the likely proximate context for that conversion,
though the specific moment and circumstances are not yet confirmed.

Iyalode's personal relationship to Christianity is therefore best
understood as a continuation of, or return to, a faith already present
in the family through her husband. She did not bring Christianity into
the family; she carried it forward through a long life — 118 years —
during which she held both Yoruba spiritual authority and Christian
faith simultaneously.

What this means for the writing:
- Do not frame Iyalode as the family's Christian origin. She is its
  living embodiment through her generation.
- Do not make her conversion (if it can be called that, given the
  founding ancestor's prior conversion) feel like the start of something.
  It is the continuation of something.
- Do not collapse the Yoruba ancestral authority and the Christianity
  into conflict needing resolution. Iyalode held both. The book holds
  both. The coexistence is the point.
- Do not annotate the connection between the founding ancestor's
  conversion and Iyalode's faith in the manuscript itself. Let the
  reader feel the thread across chapters without the narrator naming it.

### Chapter 7 — The Name That Had to Be Powerful

**Opening image:** Not a scene of religious instruction. Not a prayer
meeting. A specific moment when Iyalode speaks the name of Jesus in a
way that registers differently from every other time Abraham has heard
it. Not what she says around the name — the name itself, and the
particular quality of it in her mouth, from this woman, in this moment.
The same name Abraham has heard before, now arriving with unmistakable
weight. Something shifts. The chapter opens inside that shift.

**Chapter function:** To establish the chain of transmission by which
Christianity reaches Abraham as lived experience rather than background
religion. The chapter does two things simultaneously: it shows how
faith was received experientially (a name spoken with weight by a
specific person in a specific moment), and it establishes the first
great paradox of Abraham's spiritual inheritance — that the woman who
doubted his belonging at birth (Chapter 3) is the same woman through
whom the power that will preserve him arrives. The family's founding
Christian thread (from the founding ancestor) now lands in Abraham's
body through Iyalode's voice. He does not yet know what he is
receiving. He knows only that something was given.

**Governing question:** Q3 primarily — "What does faith mean when God
preserves your life but not your comfort?" — this is the question in
its earliest experiential form. Abraham receives something in Chapter
7 before he understands what it is. He will not fully understand it
until Chapter 6 (the bargain) confirms it under fire. Chapter 7 plants
what Chapter 6 will prove — faith arrives before the test, not after.

**Factual spine:**
- Iyalode is the first person to speak the name of Jesus to Abraham
  with unmistakable weight — the specific occasion, what she said
  around the name, and what Abraham experienced must be confirmed
  with author before drafting
- She is the same woman who rejected him at birth as the wrong child
  (Chapter 3), making the transmission source paradoxical — the person
  who doubted his belonging becomes the voice of his spiritual
  inheritance
- The founding ancestor's conversion (Chapter 2, family oral history)
  is the background of Iyalode's faith; she carries a Christian thread
  already present in the family before her, not one she originated
- The nature of the transmission: was it a prayer spoken over him, a
  rebuke, a declaration, an ordinary moment that carried extraordinary
  weight? To be confirmed with author — this is the chapter's central
  scene.

**Emotional centre:** The paradox of the source. The person from whom
a vital transmission comes is not the person one would have chosen.
Faith arriving through Iyalode — who first doubted Abraham — makes
the transmission both more complex and more accurate. It is not
comfort. It is weight. He does not receive it as warmth but as charge.
The emotional question of the chapter is not "what did Iyalode give
him?" but "what does it mean to receive something important from
someone who first refused to see you?"

**Theological pressure:** The founding ancestor's Christianity is the
chapter's background condition, not its foreground argument. What
Iyalode transmits to Abraham is not the family's first act of faith —
it is the family's faith in living form, carried across a generation
by the woman who outlived the one who first received it. The chapter
handles this by not naming it: the connection between the founding
ancestor's conversion and Iyalode's transmission is present in the
factual record, not in the narrative's editorial commentary. What
Abraham receives is the name. The history behind the name belongs to
the reader who has carried Chapter 2.

**Inheritance thread:** The name of Jesus travels a specific path:
founding ancestor (first convert, family oral history) → Iyalode
(carrier of the faith thread through her marriage, her widowhood, and
her long life) → young Abraham (receives the name as experiential
weight before he has the vocabulary for it). Three generations of the
same thread, each receiving it differently. The chapter's inheritance
argument is simple: faith is not self-generated. It arrives through
people — often through imperfect people, sometimes through people who
have also wounded you.

**Fatherhood thread:** Distant but present. The woman who doubted
Abraham's belonging as a child becomes the voice that hands him his
spiritual inheritance. David Senior believed Abraham carried his own
father's soul (Chapter 3); Iyalode, in this chapter, transmits the
faith that same grandfather's soul first carried. The child in Chapter
7 is not yet a father. But what he receives here will govern how he
fathers: the capacity to stay present inside something that was given
before he understood it.

**Mundane-before-miracle gate:** Before the weight of the name arrives,
the reader needs one specific ordinary detail of Iyalode as a human
presence in Abraham's life. Not her authority, not her rejection, not
her grandeur. Something specific and small — something she said or did
in the ordinary run of things — that makes her a person in a room before
she becomes the voice of something larger. The gate must be filled
before the chapter opens on the transmission.

**Craft risk:**
(a) Making the transmission evangelical — the chapter is about the
weight of a name, not about a conversion moment. Abraham does not
convert in Chapter 7; he is already in a Christian family by lineage.
He receives. The distinction matters: receiving is not adopting for
the first time. The chapter must carry that distinction without
explaining it.
(b) Sentimentalising Iyalode — she is still the woman who doubted him.
The transmission does not redeem the doubt; it coexists with it.
Chapters 7 and 8 together render her in full contradiction. Chapter
7 alone must not collapse into a redemption narrative for a character
the reader has known since Chapter 3 as a doubter.
(c) Overloading the theological cargo — Chapter 7 plants a seed.
Everything that grows from that seed belongs to later chapters. The
temptation is to annotate the transmission with its consequences. The
chapter must resist: state what happened, state what Abraham received,
leave the meaning to the arc.

**Prohibited material:** Iyalode's full portrait in contradiction
(Chapter 8 — these chapters are sequential and must not collapse into
each other). The fire and the bargain (Chapter 6 — the hinge, already
treated; do not anticipate it from Chapter 7). David Senior's own
faith and his deathbed words (Chapter 21 territory). The founding
ancestor's conversion story as a scene (Chapter 2 territory — background
condition in Chapter 7, not narrative event). Any doctrine explaining
what the name of Jesus means theologically.

**Ending movement:** The name received. Abraham does not yet understand
what to do with it. The chapter ends with the weight of something given
before he has the context to understand its meaning — not religious,
not dramatic, not resolved. The way something arrives before the person
knows they need it. The chapter's last beat is the holding of that
weight in a child's body, before the test that will confirm it.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. One gate: the specific
moment when Iyalode speaks the name with weight — what Abraham
remembers, what she said, what the occasion was, what made it
different from all other uses of the name — must be confirmed with
author before drafting. This is the chapter's opening image and its
central beat. Without it the chapter has no anchor.

### Chapter 8 — The Spiritual Covering

**Opening image:** Not a portrait. Not a list. A specific moment in
which Iyalode is all of her contradictions at once — something she
does or says that requires holding her beauty, her authority, her
capacity for rejection, and her eventual faith in the same frame,
simultaneously, without resolution. Not a scene that explains her.
A scene that holds her. One moment that contains the whole person.

**Chapter function:** To render Iyalode in full — not as symbol, not
as saint, not as villain, but as inheritance embodied. The chapter
does not explain her contradictions. It holds them. The reader must
leave Chapter 8 understanding that what Abraham inherits from Iyalode
is not a lesson or a principle but a person — one who could doubt and
transmit, reject and cover, fear and protect, all at once, without
resolving those contradictions into a coherent story about herself.
The chapter establishes Iyalode as the book's deepest portrait of
what inherited contradiction looks like from inside a life.

**Governing question:** Q1 primarily — "What does a man inherit before
he understands himself?" — answered here at its most human level:
he inherits a person. Not a legacy, not a named quality, not a
spiritual principle. A specific woman with specific contradictions,
some of which she never resolved, all of which became part of what
Abraham carries. Q3 runs beneath it — "What does faith mean when God
preserves your life but not your comfort?" — because Iyalode is the
portrait of how faith coexists with everything else in a life without
simplifying any of it.

**Factual spine:**
- Iyalode: beautiful, commanding, spiritually feared, self-directed,
  absent, powerful, and eventually Christian (the exact qualities
  listed in the Prose Skeleton)
- She rejected Abraham at birth as the wrong child (Chapter 3)
- She spoke the name of Jesus to him with unmistakable weight
  (Chapter 7)
- She died at 118 in 2006
- Her Christianity is a continuation of the faith her husband (the
  founding ancestor) brought into the family — not her own first entry
  into Christian faith. She carries the faith as its living embodiment
  through a long life, alongside — not instead of — her Yoruba
  spiritual authority.
- Specific physical details of Iyalode — how she moved, what she wore,
  how she held authority in a room, particular things she said or did —
  must be confirmed with author before drafting; these are the chapter's
  grounding particulars
- How she held both Yoruba spiritual authority and Christian faith
  simultaneously — what she said or did that showed both at once —
  must be confirmed; this is the chapter's theological centre

**Emotional centre:** The challenge of loving and inheriting a person
who is irreducible. The chapter must earn both the rejection (which the
reader has carried since Chapter 3) and the covering (which the reader
has been told she provides). These do not resolve into a narrative arc.
They coexist. The emotional register is not grief or anger or love —
it is the particular feeling of being shaped by someone you never fully
understood and who never fully understood you. The reader must finish
this chapter neither loving nor hating Iyalode, but holding her. That
is the chapter's emotional demand.

**Theological pressure:** Iyalode holds Yoruba spiritual authority and
Christian faith simultaneously, across a life of 118 years, without
apparently needing to resolve the contradiction between them. The
chapter does not resolve it either. She is "spiritually feared" in
both registers: feared because of what she carries from the ancestral
world, feared because of how she carries the Christian faith. The
chapter should show that she held both — through scene and detail, not
through editorial interpretation. The theological instruction is the
coexistence itself. The reader is not asked to adjudicate.

The founding ancestor's prior conversion (Chapter 2) is present as
background condition. Iyalode did not originate the family's Christian
faith; she embodied it through her person. That distinction does not
need to be stated — it is carried by what the reader already knows from
Chapter 2 and will feel in how Chapter 8 renders her.

**Inheritance thread:** This chapter is the densest inheritance thread
in Part Two. What Abraham inherits from Iyalode cannot be listed. It
is the full weight of her person — her rejection included, her covering
included, her Yoruba authority included, her Christianity included, her
death at 118 included. The chapter establishes that the inheritance
question (Q1) is not resolved by understanding what you inherit. It
is complicated by it. The more fully Abraham understands Iyalode, the
more he understands that she cannot be reduced to what she gave him.

**Fatherhood thread:** The fatherhood thread in this chapter runs
through David Senior as mediating link. He is the connection between
Iyalode and Abraham — through his belief (Chapter 3) Abraham is placed
in relation to the founding ancestor, and through Iyalode the faith is
transmitted. David Senior's own full complexity arrives in Chapter 9
and Chapter 21; here he is present as the generational conduit between
the founding ancestor's faith and Abraham's reception of it. What does
it mean to father a child who must also inherit what you received from
your own complicated mother? That question belongs to David Senior and
surfaces only obliquely here.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** Iyalode dies at 118 in 2006, but her
death is not this chapter's central event. The principle applies in
modified form: before her full contradiction arrives, before her
spiritual authority and her rejections and her faith are rendered, the
reader needs one specific ordinary detail of Iyalode as a particular
human being — not her grandeur, not her rejection, not her power.
Something specific and small that makes her a person before she becomes
a force. One detail, confirmed by the author.

**Craft risk:**
(a) Hagiography — rendering Iyalode with such grandeur that she becomes
a figure rather than a person. She must not be treated as an
ancestor-saint. The chapter must hold her irreducibility without
elevating her into myth. She was a woman. That is the most accurate
account of what she was.
(b) Condemnation — letting the rejection from Chapter 3 colour the full
portrait so that Iyalode becomes primarily a figure of having-wounded.
She is more than that, and the chapter must show the more without
excusing the wound.
(c) Symbolic over-tidiness — making the fact that she eventually holds
Christian faith feel like a redemptive arc for the rejection. It is
not. The Christianity does not cancel the rejection, and the rejection
does not cancel the faith. They are both true of the same woman. The
chapter refuses to let either cancel the other.
(d) Flattening the spiritual plurality — Iyalode is "spiritually feared"
in the sense that she holds authority in the Yoruba ancestral world.
The chapter must not reduce her to only the Christian frame. She holds
both. A chapter that only renders her Christian faith has rendered half
of her.

**Prohibited material:** Iyalode's death as the chapter's centre or
climax (it is a fact in the factual spine; it is not the chapter's
emotional event). Any resolution of her contradiction into a coherent
arc. The Oba of Benin lineage claim (family oral history only — carry
with restraint, or not at all, in this chapter). Explicit theological
interpretation of how she held Yoruba and Christian frames: the chapter
shows it through what she did, not through editorial analysis. The
founding ancestor's conversion story (Chapter 2 territory — present
here only as background condition).

**Ending movement:** Not her death. Not a redemption. Something that
leaves the reader holding the full weight of her without release. The
ending beat should feel like the moment after a long look at a
photograph — the person has refused to be fully known, and the reader
accepts that refusal as the most accurate account of what she was. She
ends the chapter unresolved. That is the inheritance.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. Two gates: (1) specific
physical details of Iyalode — how she moved, what she wore, how she
held authority in a room, particular things she said in ordinary life —
must be supplied by author before drafting; (2) the specific way she
held both Yoruba spiritual authority and Christian faith — what she
said or did that showed both at once — must be confirmed before the
theological pressure can be drafted accurately. Without these, the
chapter risks becoming abstract rather than rendered.

### Chapter 9 — The Boy Who Became a Father at Fifteen

**Opening image:** David Senior at fifteen. Not grief, not heroism.
The specific ordinary pressure of a boy who woke up one morning as a
child and will not go to sleep as one. Something small and particular —
the first morning he had to decide something no one had asked him to
decide before. A decision no fifteen-year-old should be making, made
without the vocabulary for what it costs. Not the weight itself — the
moment the weight landed.

**Chapter function:** To establish the paternal pattern Abraham will
inherit. David Senior's formation at fifteen — abandoned into
responsibility, stepping up without preparation, guided across the
gap by his dead father — is the direct ancestral antecedent of
Abraham's own formation. The chapter shows the pattern before Abraham
is born: a man who gave everything away because he was given everything
to carry, who found provision arrived, who learned that stepping up
was the only available identity. When the reader watches Abraham refuse
to disappear in Parts Three and Four, they should feel the echo of
David Senior at fifteen refusing to disappear from his siblings' lives.
The pattern is not coincidence. It is inheritance.

**Governing question:** Q1 primarily — "What does a man inherit before
he understands himself?" — answered here through the father's
experience before the narrator is born. Q2 secondarily — "What does
death demand from the living?" — because David Senior's dead father
makes a demand across the gap, exactly as Jumoke will later make a
demand of Abraham. The pattern of the dead instructing the living is
established here as a family structure, not as Abraham's singular
experience.

**Factual spine:**
- David Senior abandoned into responsibility at fifteen when Iyalode
  left for Ondo (specific circumstances — what happened, who left, when,
  what the household held at that point — to be confirmed with author)
- He dropped out of formal schooling to raise his younger siblings
- Continued education by correspondence — provider unverified (possibly
  Rapid Results College, UK-based; do not state as confirmed fact)
- Was supported by a middle-class family who helped him with the cost
  of raising the children
- One sibling later attended Yaba College of Technology before being
  supported to study in England
- David Senior's dead father — the founding ancestor Abraham, the
  family's first Christian convert — appeared to him during this period,
  promising provision that arrived when needed
- The specific nature of the appearance, what the founding ancestor
  said, and what provision came must be confirmed with author before
  drafting; this is the chapter's supernatural event

**Emotional centre:** The weight that arrives before a boy is ready
for it, and the discovery that he can carry it anyway. Not heroism —
necessity. David Senior does not step up because he is exceptional.
He steps up because there is no one else. The chapter's emotional
ground is the particular loneliness of responsibility without
preparation, and the particular steadiness that comes from discovering
you can bear what you did not choose. The reader should finish the
chapter understanding how David Senior became who he was — not through
a character flaw or a virtue, but through the pressure that formed him.

**Theological pressure:** David Senior's dead father — the founding
ancestor Abraham, the first Christian in the family — appears to guide
him. The family's Christian thread, which the founding ancestor began
and Iyalode embodied, now manifests in a third form: the dead providing
for the living across the gap. The chapter handles this with the same
discipline the book applies to all its supernatural material: narrate
plainly, without apology, without inflation. David Senior believed his
father appeared. Provision arrived. The text does not adjudicate between
the belief and the provision. It states both as facts of David Senior's
experience and leaves the reader to hold them.

The connection between the founding ancestor's faith and his appearance
to David Senior does not need to be stated editorially. The reader who
has carried Chapter 2 will feel it. The chapter holds the appearance
as David Senior's experience, not as the book's theological argument.

**Inheritance thread:** The most direct inheritance thread in Part Two.
The pattern David Senior learns at fifteen — give everything, step up,
be guided by something beyond what you can see, find that provision
arrives — is the pattern Abraham will be asked to live in London, in a
courtroom, as a father under institutional pressure. Chapter 9 plants
the inheritance before Abraham is born. The structural echo with Chapter
21 (Devotion) — noted in the hinge treatments — runs through this
thread: David Senior at fifteen inheriting his dead father's mandate;
Abraham at forty-something inheriting David Senior's mandate; the same
movement, forty years apart, in the same family.

**Fatherhood thread:** This chapter is about fatherhood in its most
ancestral form: a boy who becomes a father-figure before he becomes a
man, who learns to father by being fathered by a ghost, who discovers
that the dead can still provide. David Senior's formation as a father-
figure — his capacity to give without guarantee, to step up without
being prepared, to find provision in impossible circumstances — is the
template for the book's central fatherhood question. The reader meets
Abraham's father before they understand fully what his father gave him.

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** David Senior's dead father must not
arrive at the chapter's opening. Before the appearance, before the
provision, before the pattern — one specific ordinary detail of the
boy David Senior in the daily texture of a life now carrying what it
was not built to carry. Not the weight. The person under the weight.
One confirmed detail from author before drafting.

**Craft risk:**
(a) Hagiography of David Senior — he is being rendered with
extraordinary qualities here. The chapter must hold his ordinariness
inside his remarkable formation. He is a boy doing what he has to
do. Not a hero enacting a destiny.
(b) Making the dead father's appearance theatrical — the founding
ancestor appearing to David Senior must be narrated with the same
sobriety as all the book's other supernatural material. It is not
a set piece. It is what David Senior experienced, reported as fact.
(c) Summarising rather than showing — the chapter's risk is becoming
an explanation of David Senior's character rather than a scene of it.
The inheritance thread must emerge from the chapter's material, not
from editorial annotation. Let the reader draw the line to Abraham.

**Prohibited material:** David Senior's death (Chapter 21 — the
structural echo is intentional, but this chapter must not reach
forward to it). His political work in Lagos (covered elsewhere in
the arc). The specific financial losses of the founding ancestor
(Chapter 2 territory). Any statement that presents the dead father's
appearance as historically provable rather than as David Senior's
reported experience.

**Ending movement:** The boy who arrived at fifteen without a map is
standing later — at seventeen, or twenty, or twenty-five — having
managed. The younger siblings are accounted for. Something has held.
The ending beat is not triumph. It is the particular quiet of having
carried what you were given to carry, and still being there. The reader
should feel: this is where the pattern was set. Everything Abraham
will later do in London is here in embryo, in a boy in Lagos, doing
what had to be done.

**Drafting readiness:** CONDITIONALLY READY. Two gates: (1) the
specific circumstances of David Senior's abandonment at fifteen —
what exactly happened, who left and when, what the household looked
like — must be confirmed before drafting; (2) the specific nature of
the founding ancestor's appearance — what he said, what the provision
was, how it arrived — must be confirmed before the supernatural
material can be drafted with the precision the voice rules require.

### Chapter 10 — Leaving Lagos

**Opening image:** A threshold. Not the decision to leave — the going
itself. August 2017. A specific sensory detail of departure: an airport,
a bag, a room now empty, the city beginning to recede. Not what Abraham
carries emotionally — what he carries physically. The emotional cargo
becomes visible through the physical fact of departure. The chapter
opens in the body, not in the meaning.

**Chapter function:** To bridge the ancestral and childhood world of
Parts One and Two with the London world of Parts Three and Four.
Chapter 10 does not introduce new characters or new events. It shows
what Abraham takes with him when the world the book has spent nine
chapters building ends and the world the book will spend the next
twelve chapters testing begins. The chapter is the crossing point.
Everything Parts One and Two have established now becomes portable.
The chapter's function is to make the reader feel the weight of what
is being carried — without naming it.

Note on timing: David Senior died on 15 August 2017. Chapter 10 is set
in August 2017. The grief Abraham carries when leaving Lagos is likely
connected to his father's death, either immediately preceding the
departure or part of the accumulated weight of that summer. The
relationship between Chapter 21 (Devotion — the full rendering of David
Senior's death) and Chapter 10 (the departure) should be confirmed
with author: does the departure follow the death? Precede it? Is the
departure emotionally organised around the death in ways the chapter
needs to hold or defer? The answer will shape the chapter's opening
image and emotional register. (Chapter 21 is the book's emotional
summit on the death; Chapter 10 is the departure that carries the grief
— the two chapters serve different functions and must not duplicate
each other.)

**Governing question:** Q1 runs beneath the surface — "What does a man
inherit before he understands himself?" He is leaving, but what he
carries is not luggage. It is everything the previous nine chapters
have established: the name, the founding mandate, the bargain with God,
the faith received through Iyalode, the pattern learned from David
Senior at fifteen, the grief of Jumoke, the weight of a purpose he
accepted before he understood it. The chapter does not name any of
this. The reader feels it. Q4 begins to shadow from ahead — fatherhood
is where he is going, and London is where the question will become
unavoidable.

**Factual spine:**
- August 2017: Abraham departs Lagos for London
- He carries grief — connected to David Senior's recent death (date
  of death: 15 August 2017; relationship to departure timing to be
  confirmed), and to the accumulated griefs of the whole arc
- He carries ambition and uncertainty, and (per Prose Skeleton)
  "the illusion that relocation will give him a cleaner future"
- The specific details of the departure — what he did in the days
  before, what the airport or journey felt like, what the last things
  were — must be confirmed with author; these are the chapter's
  grounding particulars
- What was already waiting in London that the Lagos departure did not
  leave behind

**Emotional centre:** The gap between what departure feels like and
what it does. A man leaving one place does not leave what that place
made him. The emotional ground of Chapter 10 is the specific feeling
of moving while carrying everything — the mobility without the release.
Not nostalgia for Lagos. Not hope for London. The complex, specific
weight of departure that is not escape, even when it feels like one.
A man crossing a distance and finding the distance crossed nothing
essential.

**Theological pressure:** Minimal at the surface. The bargain with God
(Chapter 6) governs everything but is not the chapter's explicit
subject. The theological register here is the question underneath the
departure: was this part of the covenant's shape? Did the seventy-five
years of service include this crossing? The chapter does not answer.
It carries the question in the luggage. The reader who knows the full

**Inheritance thread:** Chapter 10 is the inheritance thread crossing

unfinished work, Iyalode's faith transmission, David Senior's pattern,
the obligations placed by the dead — now travels with him. The chapter

**Mundane-before-grief gate:** No death in this chapter, but grief is

**Craft risk:**
(a) Retrospective framing — narrating the departure with the knowledge
of what London will bring, so that the reader knows from the chapter's
first page that this is a departure toward catastrophe. The chapter
must be narrated from inside the departure: what Abraham knew then,
what he hoped then, what he did not yet know. The retrospective
knowledge is the narrator's; the experience must remain the young
man's.
(b) Nostalgia for Lagos — making Lagos feel like the paradise that
London will shatter. Lagos is not a paradise. It is where everything
in Parts One and Two happened. Departure does not sanctify the place
being left.
(c) Thematic overloading — "the old story follows him across borders"
is the chapter's emotional truth, but it cannot be stated or implied
before the reader has experienced it through Parts Three and Four.
Chapter 10 carries the seed; the later chapters reveal the fruit.
The chapter must resist naming what the departure means before the
reader has been given the means to understand it.

**Prohibited material:** David Senior's death as a rendered scene
(Chapter 21's territory — Chapter 10 carries the grief, Chapter 21
carries the event). The marriage and its collapse (Chapter 12 onward).
Damisi (Chapter 19's territory). Any summary of what London will
bring. Court proceedings (Part Three onward). The chapter holds only
the departure and what is carried in it.

**Ending movement:** Departure achieved. The old world receding. The
new world not yet visible. The ending beat is not arrival — it is the
specific quality of being in-between: neither in the place left nor
yet in the place approaching, carrying everything inherited, moving
toward everything unknown. The reader should feel the crossing as
genuine uncertainty, not as a setup for what comes. The chapter ends
mid-crossing, and that is the right place to end it.

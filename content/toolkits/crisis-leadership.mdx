# CRISIS LEADERSHIP TOOLKIT

**(A+ Standard — Pressure Decision Architecture)**

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Crisis does not create leadership failure.
It exposes it.

Every weakness tolerated in calm conditions becomes a fracture under pressure.
Unclear authority becomes paralysis. Cultural drift becomes chaos. Governance gaps become liabilities.

This toolkit provides:

```txt
Rapid Decision Framework — structured decision-making under time compression
24h/72h/7d Sequencing — phased crisis response architecture
Stress-Test Playbooks — pre-built response protocols for common crisis types
Command Authority Protocol — who decides what, when, and with what constraints
```

It does not teach leaders how to feel during crisis.
It teaches them how to **decide**.

The standard is simple:

```txt
Under pressure, every decision must have:
  An owner
  A deadline
  A scope
  A reversibility classification
```

Without these four elements, a decision under pressure is a gamble.
This toolkit eliminates gambling.

---

## 2. Strategic Context

Crisis arrives in three forms:

```txt
1. Sudden shock — no warning, immediate impact
2. Escalating threat — signals present but unaddressed
3. Chronic failure — slow decay that reaches a tipping point
```

Leadership response to each form typically fails in the same way:

```txt
Sudden shock → panic-driven decisions, no structure
Escalating threat → delay, hoping the threat resolves itself
Chronic failure → denial, followed by too-late intervention
```

The common thread:

```txt
Decision-making degrades under pressure because no system exists
to hold it together.
```

In calm conditions, organisations tolerate ambiguity.
Under pressure, ambiguity kills.

This toolkit installs the decision architecture **before** crisis arrives.
It is designed to be activated, not created, under pressure.

### 3.1 Rapid Decision Framework

**Purpose:** Make sound decisions under extreme time compression.

**Decision Classification Under Pressure:**

```txt
TYPE A — Irreversible, high-impact
  → Requires: senior authority, evidence review, explicit sign-off
  → Timeline: minimum deliberation before commitment

TYPE B — Reversible, high-impact
  → Requires: senior authority, rapid assessment
  → Timeline: decide fast, monitor, reverse if needed

TYPE C — Irreversible, low-impact
  → Requires: delegated authority, documented rationale
  → Timeline: decide and move

TYPE D — Reversible, low-impact
  → Requires: any competent authority
  → Timeline: decide immediately
```

**Rapid Decision Protocol:**

```txt
Step 1: Classify the decision (A/B/C/D)
Step 2: Identify the owner (who has authority for this class)
Step 3: Set the deadline (when must this be decided by)
Step 4: Gather minimum viable evidence (not complete evidence—minimum)
Step 5: Decide
Step 6: Document rationale (30 seconds, written)
Step 7: Communicate to all affected parties
Step 8: Set review trigger (when to reassess)
```

**Key Principle:**

```txt
A good decision made on time is superior to a perfect decision made too late.
Under crisis conditions, speed of decision is itself a strategic asset.
```

**Anti-Patterns to Eliminate:**

```txt
"Let's wait for more information" — on TYPE B/C/D decisions
"We need everyone's input" — during acute crisis
"Can we schedule a meeting?" — when the deadline is hours away
"Who should really decide this?" — authority must be pre-assigned
```

### 3.2 The 24h/72h/7d Sequencing Model

**Purpose:** Structure crisis response into phased time horizons.

**Hour 0–24: Containment**

```txt
Objective: Stop the bleeding. Limit exposure. Establish command.

Actions:
  1. Activate Crisis Command Authority (single decision-maker identified)
  2. Assess immediate impact (people, operations, finances, reputation)
  3. Contain — prevent the crisis from expanding
  4. Communicate — internal first, then external (one voice, one message)
  5. Document all decisions and rationale in real-time

Deliverables:
  — Situation Assessment (1-page maximum)
  — Containment Actions Log
  — Communication issued (internal + external)
  — Decision Authority confirmed and published
```

**Hour 24–72: Stabilisation**

```txt
Objective: Restore operational baseline. Assess full damage. Plan recovery.

Actions:
  1. Complete damage assessment (comprehensive, not rushed)
  2. Identify root cause (preliminary — not final)
  3. Stabilise operations (restore minimum viable function)
  4. Engage stakeholders (board, regulators, partners, clients)
  5. Establish recovery priorities (what matters most)

Deliverables:
  — Full Damage Assessment Report
  — Preliminary Root Cause Analysis
  — Operational Stabilisation Confirmation
  — Stakeholder Communication Log
  — Recovery Priority Matrix
```

**Day 3–7: Recovery Architecture**

```txt
Objective: Design the recovery. Assign ownership. Set timelines.

Actions:
  1. Finalise root cause analysis
  2. Design recovery plan (structural, not cosmetic)
  3. Assign recovery owners for each workstream
  4. Set 30/60/90 recovery milestones
  5. Install monitoring and escalation triggers
  6. Conduct initial lessons-learned capture

Deliverables:
  — Root Cause Analysis (final)
  — Recovery Plan with owners and deadlines
  — 30/60/90 Recovery Timeline
  — Monitoring Dashboard activated
  — Lessons-Learned Register (initial)
```

**Pressure Communication Protocol:**

```txt
Internal communication must be factual, brief, and directive.
No motivational language during crisis.
External communication must be measured, consistent, and authoritative.
One spokesperson. One message. One cadence.
The gap between internal and external messaging is where trust dies.
```

### 3.3 Stress-Test Playbooks

**Purpose:** Pre-built response protocols for the most common institutional crises.

**Playbook 1 — Revenue Shock**

```txt
Trigger: Revenue decline ≥20% in single quarter or sudden loss of major client/contract
Response Sequence:
  1. Cash flow impact assessment (24h)
  2. Cost containment activation (48h)
  3. Revenue recovery strategy (72h)
  4. Stakeholder communication (24h)
  5. Structural review — is this cyclical or terminal? (7d)
```

**Playbook 2 — Leadership Loss**

```txt
Trigger: Sudden departure, incapacity, or removal of senior leader
Response Sequence:
  1. Activate interim authority (immediate)
  2. Communicate to organisation (24h)
  3. Assess institutional dependencies on departed leader (48h)
  4. Activate Succession Engineering Toolkit if no plan exists (72h)
  5. Stabilise team morale and decision continuity (7d)
```

**Playbook 3 — Regulatory Intervention**

```txt
Trigger: Investigation, enforcement action, or compliance failure
Response Sequence:
  1. Legal counsel activation (immediate)
  2. Document preservation order (immediate)
  3. Internal fact-finding (48h)
  4. Board notification and authority escalation (24h)
  5. Regulatory engagement strategy (72h)
  6. Remediation plan (7d)
```

**Playbook 4 — Reputational Crisis**

```txt
Trigger: Public exposure of failure, misconduct, or institutional weakness
Response Sequence:
  1. Narrative assessment — what is true? (immediate)
  2. Single spokesperson designation (immediate)
  3. Internal communication — truth first (24h)
  4. External response — factual, brief, accountable (24h)
  5. Root cause investigation (72h)
  6. Corrective action plan published (7d)
```

**Playbook 5 — Operational Breakdown**

```txt
Trigger: System failure, supply chain disruption, or process collapse
Response Sequence:
  1. Impact containment (immediate)
  2. Workaround activation (24h)
  3. Root cause identification (48h)
  4. Permanent fix design (72h)
  5. Resilience upgrade to prevent recurrence (7d)
```

## 4. Diagnostic Signals

Signals that crisis leadership capability is inadequate:

```txt
DECISION_PARALYSIS        — leadership unable to decide under time pressure
AUTHORITY_CONFUSION       — unclear who commands during crisis
COMMUNICATION_FRAGMENTATION — multiple messages, no single narrative
CONTAINMENT_FAILURE       — crisis expands because initial response is too slow
BLAME_DISPLACEMENT        — energy directed at fault-finding, not problem-solving
PROTOCOL_ABSENCE          — no pre-built response exists for common scenarios
```

**Severity Classification:**

```txt
LOW        — minor gaps in crisis readiness; correctable with planning
MEDIUM     — no formal crisis protocols; leadership untested under pressure
HIGH       — previous crisis response failed; no improvements made
CRITICAL   — active crisis with no decision architecture in place
```

---

## 5. Failure Modes

Crisis leadership fails in predictable patterns:

```txt
1. Committee response — crisis managed by group consensus instead of command authority
2. Information hoarding — leaders wait for complete information that never arrives
3. Narrative loss — external narrative controlled by others while leadership deliberates
4. Scope creep — crisis response expands to address everything, resolving nothing
5. Hero dependency — one person carries the entire response without structural support
6. Post-crisis amnesia — no lessons captured; same failure repeats
```

The most dangerous failure:

```txt
"We've never had a crisis before, so we don't need a plan."
```

This is not confidence. It is structural negligence.

## 6. Application Playbook

### Step 1 — Install Crisis Command Authority

**Action:** Pre-designate who commands during each crisis type. Document and publish.

**Deliverable:** Crisis Command Authority Register (crisis type → commander → scope → constraints).

### Step 2 — Classify Decision Types

**Action:** Train leadership team on A/B/C/D decision classification. Practice under simulation.

**Deliverable:** Decision Classification Guide distributed to all senior leaders.

### Step 3 — Build 24h/72h/7d Templates

**Action:** Create phased response templates for the institution's top 5 risk scenarios.

**Deliverable:** 5 Stress-Test Playbooks, customised and pre-approved by board.

### Step 4 — Conduct Crisis Simulation

**Action:** Run annual crisis simulation. Test decision speed, authority clarity, and communication.

**Deliverable:** Simulation Report with performance scores and improvement targets.

### Step 5 — Install Monitoring Triggers

**Action:** Define early warning signals for each crisis type. Connect to escalation protocol.

**Deliverable:** Crisis Early Warning Dashboard integrated with governance monitoring.

### Step 6 — Review and Iterate

**Action:** After any real crisis or simulation, conduct structured debrief within 14 days.

**Deliverable:** Lessons-Learned Register. Updated playbooks. Revised authority if needed.

## 7. Board-Level Questions

```txt
Who commands if crisis strikes tonight?
Can our leadership team make sound decisions under extreme time pressure?
Do we have pre-built response protocols for our top 5 risks?
When did we last test our crisis response—and what did we learn?
Are we confusing the absence of crisis with the presence of readiness?
What is our 24-hour containment capability—and has it been validated?
How many decisions can we make simultaneously under pressure without quality degradation?
```

If the board cannot answer these questions with specifics, the institution is unprotected.

## 8. Metrics & Measurement

**Crisis Readiness Metrics:**

```txt
Crisis Command Authority documented (yes/no)
Stress-Test Playbooks complete (count out of 5)
Crisis simulation conducted in last 12 months (yes/no)
Decision classification training completed (% of senior leaders)
Early warning triggers installed (count)
```

**Crisis Response Metrics (post-event):**

```txt
Time to first decision (hours)
Time to containment (hours)
Communication clarity score (single narrative maintained: yes/no)
Decision quality under pressure (% of decisions that held on review)
Recovery timeline vs plan (variance in days)
Lessons captured within 14 days (yes/no)
```

**Success Threshold:**

```txt
First decision within 2 hours of crisis onset
Containment within 24 hours
Single narrative maintained throughout
≥80% of pressure decisions validated on post-crisis review
Recovery within 10% of planned timeline
```

## 9. Case Application (Modelled)

### Situation

Technology company. 600 staff. Major client representing 35% of revenue
terminates contract with 30 days notice.

### Crisis Classification

```txt
Revenue Shock (CRITICAL)
Playbook 1 activated
```

### Response Executed

```txt
Hour 0–24 (Containment):
  CEO designated Crisis Commander
  Cash flow impact assessed: 4-month runway without replacement revenue
  Cost containment measures identified (not yet activated)
  Internal communication: transparent, factual, non-alarmist
  Board notified within 6 hours

Hour 24–72 (Stabilisation):
  Full damage assessment completed
  Root cause: over-concentration risk (single client dependency)
  Operations stabilised — non-dependent revenue streams confirmed
  Client engagement: professional exit negotiated (60-day transition, not 30)
  Recovery priorities set: diversification + cost optimisation

Day 3–7 (Recovery Architecture):
  Revenue diversification plan designed (3 new pipeline targets)
  Cost reduction: 12% operational savings identified without layoffs
  30/60/90 recovery milestones set
  Board recovery dashboard activated
  Lessons-learned: client concentration policy revised (max 20% single client)
```

### Outcome (90 days)

```txt
Revenue gap: 60% closed through new pipeline conversion
Cost savings: 12% implemented on schedule
Cash runway extended from 4 months to 9 months
Client concentration: no single client above 22%
Team retention: 96% (no crisis-driven departures)
Repeat risk assessment: concentration risk downgraded from CRITICAL to LOW
```

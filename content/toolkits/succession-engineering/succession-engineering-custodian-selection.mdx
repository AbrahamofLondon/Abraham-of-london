# SUCCESSION ENGINEERING — CUSTODIAN SELECTION PROTOCOL

**(A+ Standard — Institutional Leadership Selection System)**

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Most leadership selection processes are designed to identify the candidate who interviews best, not the leader who will perform best under the specific conditions the institution faces.

The result is a systematic bias toward:
- Charisma over judgment
- Experience over adaptability
- Familiarity over capability
- Confidence over self-awareness

This protocol replaces subjective selection with structured assessment across five dimensions:

```txt
Competence Assessment — can they do the work?
Judgment Assessment — can they decide when the answer is unclear?
Cultural Assessment — will they preserve what must be preserved?
Strategic Assessment — can they take the institution where it needs to go?
Resilience Assessment — will they survive the pressure?
```

---

## 2. Strategic Context

Selection processes fail in seven predictable patterns:

```txt
1. Charisma bias — the best interviewer is selected, not the best leader
2. Familiarity bias — the known candidate is selected over the better candidate
3. Timing bias — the available candidate is selected over the right candidate
4. Comparison error — candidates compared to each other instead of to the role requirements
5. Confidence error — confidence mistaken for competence
6. Experience error — past success in different conditions assumed to predict future success
7. Cultural fit error — cultural fit prioritised over cultural contribution
```

### The Selection Decision Quality Curve

```txt
Unstructured selection (interview + gut feel):
  Success rate: 30-40%
  Cost of failure: Very high (wrong leader for 2-4 years)

Structured selection (competency-based interview + assessment):
  Success rate: 50-65%
  Cost of failure: High (wrong leader selected despite process)

Rigorous selection (multi-dimensional assessment + simulation + reference verification):
  Success rate: 70-85%
  Cost of failure: Moderate (gaps identified before selection)

Engineered selection (DNA mapping + role design + multi-dimensional assessment + simulation + probation):
  Success rate: 85-95%
  Cost of failure: Low (gaps identified and addressed before full transition)
```

## 3. Core Model — Custodian Selection Framework

### 3.1 The Five Assessment Dimensions

```txt
Dimension 1 — Competence
  What to assess:
    — Technical capability for the role
    — Relevant experience (quality, not just quantity)
    — Track record of delivery
    — Domain knowledge depth
  Assessment methods:
    — Capability interview (structured, behaviour-based)
    — Work sample test (real problem, real solution)
    — Reference verification (specific, not generic)

Dimension 2 — Judgment
  What to assess:
    — Decision quality under uncertainty
    — Pattern recognition (can they identify what matters?)
    — Learning ability (do they learn from mistakes?)
    — Intellectual honesty (do they acknowledge what they do not know?)
  Assessment methods:
    — Case study analysis (real institutional problem, no right answer)
    — Post-decision review (analysis of a past decision they made)
    — Scenario walkthrough (how would they handle a specific future challenge?)

Dimension 3 — Cultural Stewardship
  What to assess:
    — Understanding of institutional values (not just agreement)
    — Commitment to preserving institutional identity
    — Ability to evolve culture without destroying it
    — Self-awareness about their own cultural impact
  Assessment methods:
    — Values interview (how do they interpret and apply institutional values?)
    — Cultural scenario (how would they handle a values conflict?)
    — Stakeholder feedback (how are they perceived by those who know them?)

Dimension 4 — Strategic Capacity
  What to assess:
    — Ability to set direction (not just execute)
    — Understanding of competitive dynamics
    — Capacity for long-term thinking
    — Ability to make strategic trade-offs
  Assessment methods:
    — Strategy presentation (their view of the institution's future)
    — Competitive analysis (their understanding of the market)
    — Trade-off exercise (specific strategic choices with consequences)

Dimension 5 — Resilience
  What to assess:
    — Ability to perform under pressure
    — Recovery from failure (specific examples)
    — Sustainability (can they sustain high performance over years?)
    — Support systems (do they have the personal infrastructure for the role?)
  Assessment methods:
    — Pressure interview (structured stress test)
    — Failure analysis (specific past failure and what they learned)
    — Personal sustainability assessment (support systems, health, balance)
```

### 3.2 Scoring System

```txt
Each dimension is scored 1-5:

1 — Significant gap. Would be a liability in this dimension.
2 — Below required level. Development needed before transition.
3 — Meets requirements. Capable in this dimension.
4 — Exceeds requirements. Strength in this dimension.
5 — Exceptional. Would raise institutional capability in this dimension.

Minimum thresholds:
  — No dimension below 2
  — At least 3 dimensions at 3 or above
  - Judgment must be at 3 or above (non-negotiable)
  - Resilience must be at 3 or above (non-negotiable)
```

## 4. Selection Process Architecture

### 4.1 Phase 1 — Role Design (Before Search)

```txt
Define what the role actually requires, not what the previous leader did:

  — Current state: What is the institution's condition?
  — Required state: Where must the institution go?
  — Critical challenges: What specific challenges will the leader face?
  — Required capabilities: What capabilities are needed for those challenges?
  — Non-negotiable criteria: What must the leader have (minimum thresholds)?
  — Desirable criteria: What would be valuable but is not essential?
```

### 4.2 Phase 2 — Sourcing

```txt
Cast a wide net. The best candidate may not be looking.

  — Internal candidates (known quantity, cultural knowledge)
  — External candidates (fresh perspective, new capabilities)
  — Non-obvious candidates (different industry, different background)
  — Candidate quality over candidate availability
```

### 4.3 Phase 3 — Assessment

```txt
Multi-dimensional assessment across all five dimensions:

  — Structured interviews (behaviour-based, not hypothetical)
  — Work samples (real problems, real solutions)
  — Case studies (institutional-specific scenarios)
  — Reference verification (structured, specific, verified)
  — Stakeholder feedback (360° where possible)
  — Psychometric assessment (where appropriate)
```

### 4.4 Phase 4 — Deliberation

```txt
Structured comparison against role requirements, not against other candidates:

  — Score each candidate against each dimension
  — Identify strengths and gaps for each
  — Assess gap-closability (can gaps be addressed through development?)
  — Consider team composition (how will this leader complement the existing team?)
  — Make decision based on evidence, not instinct
```

### 4.5 Phase 5 — Transition

```txt
Structured transition with verification:

  — Knowledge transfer (Leadership DNA Mapping)
  — Authority transfer (phased, verified)
  — Relationship transfer (stakeholder introduction and endorsement)
  — Probation period (90 days with clear success criteria)
  — Board verification (formal review at 90 days)
```

## 5. Diagnostic Signals

Signals that custodian selection is needed or failing:

```txt
SELECTION_CHARISMA_BIAS     — best interviewer selected, not best leader
SELECTION_FAMILIARITY_BIAS  — known candidate selected over better candidate
SELECTION_GAP_MISALIGNMENT  — selected leader lacks critical required capability
SELECTION_CULTURE_MISFIT    — selected leader does not preserve institutional culture
SELECTION_FAILURE_PATTERN   — same selection mistakes repeated across appointments
```

---

## 6. Failure Modes

```txt
1. Charisma selection — best interviewer selected. Leadership performance does not correlate with interview performance.

2. Familiarity selection — known internal candidate selected without rigorous assessment. Gaps are known but not addressed.

3. Comparison error — candidates compared to each other instead of to role requirements. The best of a weak field is selected.

4. Confidence error — confident candidate mistaken for competent candidate. Overconfidence conceals capability gaps.

5. Experience error — past success in different conditions assumed to predict future success. Context change invalidates experience.

6. Cultural fit error — candidate who fits current culture selected over candidate who can evolve culture. Institution stagnates.
```

## 7. Application Playbook

### Step 1 — Design the Role

Define current state, required state, critical challenges, and required capabilities.

**Deliverable:** Role Design Document

### Step 2 — Define Assessment Criteria

Set minimum thresholds and scoring criteria for all five dimensions.

**Deliverable:** Assessment Criteria Matrix

### Step 3 — Assess Candidates

Run multi-dimensional assessment for each candidate. Score against criteria.

**Deliverable:** Candidate Assessment Scores

### Step 4 — Deliberate and Select

Compare candidates against role requirements. Identify gaps and gap-closability.

**Deliverable:** Selection Recommendation with Rationale

### Step 5 — Plan Transition

Design knowledge transfer, authority transfer, and relationship transfer plan.

**Deliverable:** Transition Plan

### Step 6 — Verify Selection

At 90 days, conduct formal review. Is the selected leader meeting success criteria?

**Deliverable:** 90-Day Verification Report

## 8. Board-Level Questions

```txt
Are we selecting the best leader or the best interviewer?

What is our selection process's track record — how many of our last 5 leadership appointments succeeded?

Do we assess judgment and resilience — or just competence and confidence?

Are we comparing candidates to the role requirements or to each other?

What gaps did our last selected leader have — and did we address them before transition?

What is the cost of a failed leadership selection — and have we priced it?
```

---

## 9. Metrics & Measurement

```txt
Selection Success Rate     — % of selected leaders meeting 90-day success criteria (target: >85%)
Assessment Coverage        — % of selection decisions using all 5 dimensions (target: 100%)
Gap Closure Rate           — % of identified gaps addressed before transition (target: >90%)
Post-Selection Performance — leader performance at 6/12/18 months vs. role requirements
Selection Process Compliance — % of selections following full protocol (target: 100%)
Failed Selection Cost      — total cost of failed selections (financial + cultural + strategic)
```

## 12. Final Position

The most important decision an institution makes is who leads it next.

Most institutions make this decision with less rigour than they apply to a mid-level hire.

The cost is not the wrong leader for 2-4 years. It is the institutional trajectory that the wrong leader sets — and the years required to correct it.

Select with rigour. The institution's future depends on it.

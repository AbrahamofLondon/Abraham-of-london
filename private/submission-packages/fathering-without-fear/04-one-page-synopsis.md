# One-Page Synopsis — Fathering Without Fear

The book opens with a phone call. Abraham Adaramola is standing in his flat in Hounslow, west London, when the call arrives. It carries the kind of news that reorganises everything that came before it and everything that will follow. The call is not explained immediately. Instead, the book moves backward — into the family, the faith, and the inheritance that made Abraham the man holding the phone.

He was born in Isua, a small town in Edo State, Nigeria, into a Yoruba family whose Christianity arrived through a tailor's encounter with a missionary's world. His great-grandmother Iyalode lived to 118. His father, David Senior, became a father at fifteen — not by choice but by circumstance — and carried that responsibility without complaint for the rest of his life. Abraham's name was chosen to be powerful, because the family understood that names do work in the world.

As a boy, Abraham watched his father's authority and his grandmother's faith and began assembling his own sense of what fatherhood required. When he was a child, a girl called Jumoke followed him everywhere with a devotion he did not understand until years later, when a fire destroyed the family home and his father stood in the wreckage making a deal with God. The fire did not end the family. It remade the terms.

Abraham left Lagos for London. His brother David — bright, sociable, loyal, the one who had already planned Abraham's future from England — was missing from Abraham's wedding. Abraham searched for him at the altar in a trance and then came back to the ceremony. The next day he travelled to Lagos and learned what the vision had already told him. David had died in Kent.

Shortly after, his sister Funke — who had appointed herself his protector and called herself "big mummy" — collapsed at work and died. Two days later, Abraham was served with divorce papers. He could not pay her medical bill.

Abraham's second marriage brought fatherhood, but fatherhood did not begin inside a home. He was told to leave. He stayed elsewhere, carried the weight of expulsion, and refused the exit offered to him — not as heroism, but because a child had changed the meaning of the door.

What followed were seven years of supervised contact. Abraham saw his son Damisi in rooms arranged by institutions — rooms with supervisors, time limits, and files that could record that a visit took place but could not record what happened inside it. A boy corrected his father's Yoruba. A boy told his father "I am not your bro — I am your son." A boy played number 7 because the other children preferred a different goalkeeper. Abraham's task in those rooms was to love him without making the love performative, and to let the child remain a child.

The book does not narrate the legal process in detail. It does not name the other parties. It does not present allegations or invite the reader to judge. It holds the institutional pressure as weather — something Abraham moved through rather than something he prosecuted on the page.

In the later chapters, Abraham tries to rebuild ordinary adult life alongside the institutional pressure — social life, community, the attempt to imagine a future family while the system still holds his son. A relationship tests his fatherhood commitment in the most ordinary way possible: when he is told he could move city or country for work instead of staying in London for his son, he ends the conversation. He does not call back. The choice is not dramatic. It is a quiet refusal to trade presence for practicality.

The book closes in a room — a dining table, a laptop, a paper moved aside, a document saved. Everything the book has carried is present without being displayed. The room is ordinary. The ordinariness is the point.

*Fathering Without Fear* is a compressed literary memoir about inheritance, faith, grief, and the cost of remaining present. It is approximately 45,000 words across twenty-three chapters.

# Expansion Source Capture Plan — Fathering Without Fear

**Date:** 2026-06-23
**Current clean prose:** ~38,092 words (22 chapters)
**Target:** 50,000–55,000 words
**Possible upper range:** 55,000–60,000 only if author source material supports real scene-level expansion
**Total estimated expansion:** 12,000–17,000 words across 7 priority chapters

**Governing principle — MASTERPIECE-PRESSURE STANDARD**

Do not treat market-ready expansion as word-count inflation.

The objective is not to make the manuscript longer. The objective is to make every chapter more necessary, more embodied, more specific, more pressurised, and more unforgettable.

A revision may:
- expand;
- compress;
- restructure;
- cut;
- restore;
- withhold;
- or leave unchanged.

The decision must be based on literary force, not target word count.

**Rules:**
1. No chapter expands unless the added material makes the chapter better.
2. No chapter remains expanded if compression makes it stronger.
3. No bloated prose.
4. No inventory.
5. No explanatory padding.
6. No "beautiful" paragraph that does not increase pressure.
7. No market-body expansion that weakens the book's authority.

**Chapter test before any revision:**
1. What is the real question of this chapter?
2. What is the chapter currently pretending to be about?
3. What must the reader carry away for days?
4. Which material increases pressure?
5. Which material merely increases word count?
6. Which sentence must survive?
7. Which sentence is secretly the chapter's ending?
8. What must not be explained?

**Application to this plan:** The priority chapters below are priority because they must become stronger — not because they must be longer. Every expansion listed here is contingent on the chapter test above. If source material arrives and the test says compress, we compress. If the test says cut, we cut. The target ranges are directional, not contractual.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Source Status

### 1. Ch.10 — Leaving Lagos (Current: 623 | Target: 2,000–3,000)

**Why expansion is needed:** The departure from Nigeria is a major threshold in the book. At 623 words, it reads as a summary of a departure rather than the departure itself. The reader needs to feel what it cost to leave — what was left behind, what was carried, who was there, what the moment looked and felt like.

**What must be preserved:** The compression. The sense that departure was quick, almost abrupt. The knowledge that the old story followed him across borders.

**What must not be added:** Legal chronology. Later marriage/court framing. Explanation of what the departure would lead to. Grief essay about leaving home.

**Scene/sensory anchors needed:**
- The room or place where Abraham packed or prepared
- What he carried (physical objects — bag, documents, clothes)
- Who saw him off, if anyone
- The moment of leaving — the door, the threshold, the vehicle
- The journey itself — airport, road, the last view of familiar ground
- What he felt in his body — not what he thought it meant

**Safety/privacy/legal constraints:** None. This chapter precedes all legal material.

**Expansion verdict:** SOURCE-NEEDED

**Exact author-source questions:**

1. Where were you living in the weeks before you left Lagos? What did that room or house look like?
2. What did you pack? What physical objects did you carry with you to London?
3. Who, if anyone, saw you off at the airport or at the point of departure?
4. What do you remember about the journey itself — the airport, the plane, the arrival?
5. What was the last thing you saw of Nigeria as you left?
6. What did you feel in your body at the moment of departure — not what you thought it meant, but the physical sensation?
7. Was there a specific object, photograph, or document you remember holding or carrying?
8. What was the weather like that day?
9. Do you remember what you were wearing?
10. Was there a moment when you almost didn't go?

### 2. Ch.8 — Spiritual Covering (Current: 674 | Target: 1,500–2,500)

**Why expansion is needed:** Iyalode is one of the book's most important figures — the spiritual covering, the one who spoke Jesus' name first, the woman who lived to 118. But at 674 words, she is rendered as a concept rather than a person. The chapter needs scenes of Iyalode as a living woman — her voice, her presence, her contradiction.

**What must be preserved:** The dignity. The refusal to simplify her into saint or villain. The sense that she was both powerful and self-directed, both rejecting and covering.

**What must not be added:** Ancestry prestige. Genealogical expansion. Symbolic inflation of her spiritual role. Explanation of what she "meant."

**Scene/sensory anchors needed:**
- Iyalode's physical presence — her voice, her bearing, her clothing
- A specific memory of her in daily life — cooking, speaking, sitting, moving
- The room or space she occupied
- Her contradiction rendered through scene, not explanation

**Safety/privacy/legal constraints:** None. Iyalode died in 2006 at age 118.

**Expansion verdict:** SOURCE-NEEDED

**Exact author-source questions:**

1. What do you remember about Iyalode's physical presence — her voice, her height, her bearing, the way she moved?
2. Do you have a specific memory of her in an ordinary moment — cooking, speaking, sitting, walking?
3. What was the room or space she most often occupied?
4. What did she wear? Was there a particular garment or accessory you associate with her?
5. Do you remember a specific thing she said to you — not about spiritual matters, just something ordinary?
6. What did it feel like to be in her presence as a child?
7. Was there a moment when you saw her contradiction directly — her power and her self-direction in the same scene?
8. What do you remember about her voice when she spoke about spiritual things versus ordinary things?

### 3. Ch.5 — Jumoke (Current: 643 | Target: 1,500–2,200)

**Why expansion is needed:** Jumoke is the first death in the book and the first visitation. At 643 words, she appears almost entirely as a symbol — the twin who died, the kitchen visitation. The reader needs to meet her as a living child first, so that the loss lands before the visitation arrives.

**What must be preserved:** The kitchen visitation. The promise extracted. The restraint around the supernatural.

**What must not be added:** Sentimentality. Excessive detail about the death itself. Theological explanation of the visitation.

**Scene/sensory anchors needed:**
- Jumoke as a living child — one ordinary moment, one mundane detail
- The kitchen — the room where the visitation occurred
- The day of her death — what Abraham was doing when he learned

**Safety/privacy/legal constraints:** None. Jumoke died in childhood.

**Expansion verdict:** SOURCE-NEEDED

**Exact author-source questions:**

1. What is one ordinary thing you remember about Jumoke as a living child — something she found funny, a phrase she used, a game you played?
2. What did the kitchen look like on the day she appeared to you? The light, the time of day, where you were standing or sitting?
3. What were you doing when you learned she had died?
4. Do you remember her voice? What did it sound like?
5. Was there a particular object or place in the house that you associate with her?
6. What did you feel in your body when she appeared — before you understood what was happening?

**Scene/sensory anchors needed:**
- The boolekaja — what it looked like, sounded like, smelled like
- The road from Osun to Lagos — what they saw, how long it took, where they rested
- Abraham Adaramola's tailoring shop or workspace

**Safety/privacy/legal constraints:** None. This is family oral tradition, not legally protected material.

**Expansion verdict:** SOURCE-NEEDED

**Exact author-source questions:**

1. What do you know about the boolekaja — what it looked like, how many people it carried, what the journey felt like?
2. Do you have any family memory of the walk from Osun to Lagos — how long it took, where they rested, what they saw?
3. Was there a specific story about Abraham Adaramola's tailoring shop or workspace?
4. Do you know what the missionary's garment looked like — the specific thing Abraham Adaramola sewed?
5. Is there a family story about the moment Abraham Adaramola decided to follow the missionary to Lagos?
6. What do you know about his life in Lagos — where he lived, how he worked, what he built?

### 5. Ch.9 — Father at Fifteen (Current: 940 | Target: 1,800–2,700)

**Why expansion is needed:** David Senior's childhood — becoming a father at fifteen, raising his siblings alone, the visitations from his own father — is one of the most extraordinary stories in the book. At 940 words, it is compressed to the point of feeling like a summary of an extraordinary life rather than a rendering of it.

**What must be preserved:** The pattern of paternal visitation and provision-by-prophecy. The dignity. The refusal to sentimentalise.

**What must not be added:** Psychological analysis of David Senior. Comparison to Abraham's own story. Explanation of what the pattern "means."

**Scene/sensory anchors needed:**
- David Senior as a fifteen-year-old — what he looked like, how he carried the responsibility
- The younger siblings — who they were, what they needed
- The visitations from his own father — what was said, how the provision arrived
- The ordinary texture of a fifteen-year-old raising children

**Safety/privacy/legal constraints:** None. David Senior died in 2017.

**Expansion verdict:** SOURCE-NEEDED

**Exact author-source questions:**

1. What do you know about what David Senior looked like at fifteen — his bearing, his voice, how he carried the responsibility?
2. Who were the younger siblings he raised? What were their names, ages?
3. Do you know a specific story about how he managed — food, money, school, the household?
4. What do you know about the visitations from his own father? What was said? How did the provision arrive?
5. Is there a specific object, place, or phrase you associate with David Senior's early fatherhood?
6. Did he ever speak about that time in his life? What did he say?

**What must be preserved:** The dignity. The refusal to melodramatise. The medical bill detail. The timing — two days before the divorce filing.

**What must not be added:** Accusation against anyone for her death. Legal chronology of the divorce. Sentimental deathbed detail.

**Scene/sensory anchors needed:**
- Funke as a living person — her voice, her humour, her determination
- A specific memory of her — a conversation, a gesture, an ordinary moment
- The phone call or moment Abraham learned she had died
- The medical bill — the specific number or object

**Safety/privacy/legal constraints:** None. Funke is deceased. But the timing (two days before divorce) must be handled with care — it should not become an accusation.

**Expansion verdict:** SOURCE-NEEDED

**Exact author-source questions:**

1. What is one ordinary thing you remember about Funke — something she found funny, a phrase she overused, a gesture?
2. Do you remember a specific conversation where she told you she would fight for you?
3. What were you doing when you learned she had died? Who told you?
4. Do you remember the medical bill — the specific number, the piece of paper, where you saw it?
5. What did Funke look like? Her voice, her bearing, the way she moved through a room?
6. Is there a specific object or place you associate with her?

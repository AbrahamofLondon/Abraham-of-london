**FATHERING WITHOUT FEAR — Chapter Outline**
*Abraham of London*

**Chapter 1 — Hounslow Call** (1,541 words)
The book opens in February 2021. Abraham is in his room in Hounslow when the court calls without notification. He is not prepared. But he stays on the line — because the alternative is absence, and absence has its own danger. The book begins here, then moves backwards.

**Chapter 2 — Isua to Agege** (976 words)
The family line. Abraham's ancestor Adarabioyo was a tailor in Isua, in Ondo state, who sewed for a British missionary and became the first in the family to take Christianity seriously. His wife Iyalode walked from Osun to Lagos beside him. She lived to 118. The chapter establishes what Abraham carries before he knows he carries it.

**Chapter 3 — The Boy Who Was Already Old** (1,327 words)
Abraham's grandfather, Abraham Adaramola — the tailor's son — takes up his father's trade and name. He builds, raises a family, and dies before his work is finished. The chapter begins the account of naming as inheritance: the name came from wealth and from the family's memory of what wealth was supposed to mean.

**Chapter 4 — A Hearing Date** (1,227 words)
A brief, deliberately spare chapter. A hearing date reduces a father's life to ten minutes on a screen. The chapter holds institutional compression as form: the brevity is the experience.

**Chapter 5 — Jumoke** (1,380 words)
Abraham's youngest sister, who followed him everywhere as a child. She died at seven. The chapter is not an elegy; it is a portrait of a living child, rendered whole before she is lost, so that her absence has the correct weight.

**Chapter 6 — The Fire and the Deal** (2,506 words)
As a child, Abraham wakes into a burning room. The mosquito coil has fallen; the mattress is alight. He wakes his sisters, smothers the fire with a blanket, and saves the room. Before waking, he had a dream — a deal made in a place he could not name. The chapter is the book's first full encounter with fear, prayer, and survival as intertwined experiences.

**Chapter 7 — The Name That Had to Be Powerful** (1,435 words)
Abraham's father, David Senior, names his son Abraham. Not casually — but because he recognises something in the child that connects him to the grandfather who built and died before his work was finished. The chapter explores the weight of inheritance as a conscious act of naming.

**Chapter 8 — The Spiritual Covering** (1,107 words)
Iyalode, Abraham's great-grandmother. She carried both Yoruba spiritual knowledge and Christian faith without perceiving them as contradictions. Her presence in the family's spiritual history is the book's deepest theological strand — the one that precedes doctrine and outlives it.

**Chapter 9 — The Boy Who Became a Father at Fifteen** (1,111 words)
Abraham's father, David Senior, becomes a parent at fifteen when his own father dies. He educates his siblings, keeps the family together, builds a life from promises made in grief. The chapter is a direct mirror of the narrator's own position — becoming a father before the circumstances permitted it.

**Chapter 10 — Leaving Lagos** (1,660 words)
Abraham leaves Lagos for London under his father's prayer from Psalm 27. David Senior lays hands on his head and speaks provision over him. Abraham does not yet know how literal the prayer will become. The chapter is both an ending and an opening — the book's movement from one world to another.

**Chapter 11 — A Post on Facebook** (1,450 words)
Back in London after returning to bury his father in Lagos, Abraham encounters a Facebook post from someone who has also recently lost a father. A reply becomes a conversation. A conversation becomes a relationship. The chapter is the book's gentlest account of how loss creates proximity — and how proximity creates momentum.

**Chapter 12 — Married by December** (3,795 words)
The marriage. Abraham returns to Nigeria in December 2017 and marries quickly — not without thought, but with the momentum of a man who has decided to act on faith. The book does not hide the warnings Abraham saw and made smaller. The chapter is long because the weight it carries is long.

**Chapter 13 — Serena** (2,259 words)
Abraham's brother Serena — present at the National Service posting in Awka, the fabric trips to Aba, the ordinary texture of a shared adulthood. The chapter restores a generation of family life that precedes the crisis.

**Chapter 14 — David Was Missing From the Wedding** (3,753 words)
Abraham's brother David died on 5 May 2003, in Kent. Abraham did not know during the wedding. He searched for David during a trance at the altar, then returned to the ceremony. The next day, he travelled to Lagos and asked a single question at the gate: *Is David dead?* The family was not surprised he already knew. The chapter is the book's grief centre.

**Chapter 15 — Funke** (1,516 words)
Abraham's sister Funke was the family organiser, the first daughter of the first son. She called herself "small mummy" in relation to Abraham's crisis. She ran a dry-cleaning service and a catering business. A doctor found a possible hole in her heart and told her to stop working. She collapsed at work two days later. Two days after her death, Abraham was served with divorce papers. He could not pay her medical bill.

**Chapter 16 — Fatherhood Began Outside** (1,577 words)
Abraham learns he is going to be a father — and is told to leave. Fatherhood does not begin inside a marriage or inside a home. It begins outside a door. The chapter is spare because the moment it holds is spare.

**Chapter 17 — What the System Sees** (3,032 words)
An allegation is made. Abraham is arrested. The case does not proceed. The consequences in family life do. The chapter names the structural pressure without narrating legal specifics, because a living child stands inside the story.

**Chapter 18 — Seven Years** (3,737 words)
Seven years of supervised contact rooms, time limits, files that record that a visit occurred but cannot record what happened inside it. Abraham learns to calibrate every gesture in those rooms — how to respond to a whispered moment without making it visible, how to say goodbye in a way that holds together long enough for the child to leave before it falls apart.

**Chapter 19 — The Version in His Head** (2,295 words)
Between the institutional years and the resolution movement, Abraham rebuilds ordinary adult life. He dances, networks, socialises, builds community. A relationship tests his fatherhood commitment in the most ordinary way: when Elena suggests he could move city or country for work, Abraham ends the conversation. His son is in London. So he stays.

**Chapter 20 — Damisi** (3,376 words)
Damisi rendered entirely as a boy — through football, swimming, vocabulary precision, toy-car races, a favourite animal that changed from snake to tiger, a window he proposed escaping through. A sentence: "I am not your bro. I am your son." The chapter's discipline is that it never uses the child as evidence, proof, or argument. Damisi remains Damisi.

**Chapter 21 — Love Does Not Fear** (3,283 words)
Abraham encounters 1 John 4:18 — "perfect love casts out fear" — not as a sermon, but as a correction. The verse had always been available; he had not been the man who could read it without turning it into justification. The chapter places the book's theological work without becoming devotional literature.

**Chapter 22 — Devotion** (2,893 words)
David Senior: the father who became a parent at fifteen, who watched Abraham with the recognition of someone who had carried the same weight a generation earlier. Devotion rendered not as sentiment but as practice — the accumulated decisions of a man who remained present when remaining was costly.

**Chapter 23 — Final Room** (847 words)
An ordinary room. A table. A laptop. Papers. The physical act of making room. The book closes without false closure.

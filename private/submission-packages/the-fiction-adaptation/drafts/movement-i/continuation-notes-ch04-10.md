# Movement I — Continuation Notes (Chapters 4–10)

**These are drafting briefs, NOT drafts.** They carry the function, force, and key beats for the remaining Movement I chapters so the next pass can draft them at the same standard as Chs 1–3. Word targets: 2,200–3,000 each. Past tense = real; present tense = trance. Obianuju's duality active in every chapter she appears.

---

### Ch.4 — The Speed of It
- **Function:** How fast the marriage formed, rendered from inside, where speed felt like a current to ride rather than a thing to question. Seed the protagonist's reading of momentum as destiny — his fearlessness applied to time itself.
- **Force:** Spirit Husband at the edge (the speed was never only theirs). No full trance.
- **Beat:** A genuinely good day — the relationship at its most real and warm — placed deliberately so the reader cannot later hate Obianuju cleanly. The gift that anchors the movement's darkening.
- **Turn:** He recognises, without alarm, that he did not choose the speed so much as fail to resist it — and is glad. The gladness is the danger.
- **Risk to avoid:** memoir's marriage chronology; any 1:1 detail. Keep it the novel's own courtship, liminal and unmappable.

### Ch.5 — The Confidant
- **Function:** Introduce the brother/friend who can see the cold rooms from outside and cannot name them — the reader's proxy for the forbidden question.
- **Force:** Spirit Husband seen from the outside for the first time (the confidant feels the house go wrong without knowing why).
- **Beat:** The confidant asks, obliquely, *are you alright in that house* — and the protagonist, fearless, says yes, and means it, which frightens the confidant more than a no would have.
- **Turn:** The first external confirmation that the cold is real and not the protagonist's private weather.
- **Risk to avoid:** the confidant becoming an exposition device; let him carry warmth and limitation, not explanation.

### Ch.6 — The Elder
- **Function:** Introduce the Iyalode-function figure — an old woman of spiritual authority who holds two cosmologies without conflict, who *sees* what Obianuju carries and does not pretend not to.
- **Force:** All three forces named obliquely for the first time, through her — but she explains nothing; she gives one sentence the protagonist will not understand until the end.
- **Beat:** She neither condemns Obianuju nor offers to rescue him. She treats the claim as weather, not as evil. Cultural authority rendered from the inside, no exotica, no cosmology dump.
- **Turn:** The protagonist is handed the sentence (to be discovered in drafting) that the novel's ending will pay off.
- **Risk to avoid:** the wise-elder cliché; prophecy clichés; making her a oracle who solves the plot.

### Ch.7 — Territory
- **Function:** A domestic chapter wholly governed by the Spirit Husband, in which nothing "happens" and the horror is entirely in the weather.
- **Force:** Spirit Husband, full and undiluted — the claim as the climate of the shared life.
- **Beat:** The cold hour seen now from inside the marriage's routine; a room that goes cold on a schedule; her hand going still over a task; the first time the protagonist's fearlessness reads, to the reader, as a man failing to flee a burning building because he cannot smell smoke.
- **Turn:** He stops noting the hour as strange. Habituation. The claim has made a home of his calm.
- **Risk to avoid:** event-plotting; let dread accumulate without incident.

### Ch.8 — What He Brought Into It
- **Function:** His own inheritance surfaces — the fearlessness as family weather, the account opened in the brown water — and the novel suggests his certainty of being *kept* is itself a kind of claim on him.
- **Force:** The rhyme between his claim (kept) and Obianuju's claim (the one who arrived first) becomes visible to the reader, not yet to him.
- **Beat:** A memory of the drowning re-entered, deepened; the price he felt at nine named a little more.
- **Turn:** The first faint approach to the book's truth — that being kept is not being spared — felt, not stated.
- **Risk to avoid:** thesis-stating; transcribing the memoir's covenant language.

### Ch.9 — The Second Trance
- **Function:** A full, sustained trance (present tense) that *shows* him something true he cannot yet place — an absence, a figure at a threshold — whose accuracy will land in Movement II.
- **Force:** Trance, full deployment — messenger, never threat; horror entirely in accuracy.
- **Beat:** The threshold-figure from Ch.1/Ch.3 resolves a degree further; he sees an absence that has not yet happened in the waking world.
- **Turn:** He carries the trance back into the day and, for the first time, almost does the arithmetic — and stops.
- **Risk to avoid:** dream-vagueness; "was that real?"; making the trance ominous rather than accurate.

### Ch.10 — Continuation (threshold) — *Movement I close*
- **Function:** The turn that ends Movement I: continuation enters the world — a future begins — and the Spirit Husband's claim visibly reaches toward it. Obianuju begins to change; the reversal starts here.
- **Force:** Spirit Husband reaching to extend the claim into what comes next. **No child on the page — threshold and expectation only** (architecture §E; child-protection firewall).
- **Beat:** The first cold the protagonist's fearlessness cannot find interesting. Obianuju's duality at its most painful — the change is also her grief, also done *to* her.
- **Turn:** The narrative question that carries the reader into Movement II: *what does a man who cannot fear death do when the thing he cannot fear begins reaching for the future, and his one strength is the wrong tool against all of it?*
- **Risk to avoid:** depicting a child; resolving anything; over-delivering — Movement I opens doors, it does not explain the house.

## How Ch.4-5 widen the novel beyond Ch.1-3

Ch.1-3 establish the threshold: the hour, the journal, the town, the first trance, and fearlessness as danger. Ch.4-5 widen the book by moving into social and relational consequence:

- **Ch.4** opens the courtship/commitment period through ordinary town scrutiny, market movement, rain, salt, repairable domestic fact, and a wholly good day. The speed is rendered from inside as mercy/current, not as plot summary.
- **Ch.5** introduces Kene as a human witness, not an explanatory device. The cold crosses from private experience into another body, and his friendship gives the protagonist an available road he refuses to use.

The new width comes from lived event: market bargaining, repairs, food, weather, a road between houses, and ordinary loyalty under pressure.

---

## Ordinary life before metaphysical pressure

**Ch.4:** ordinary life comes first through the courtyard line, market day, rain under the awning, groundnuts, Obianuju's bad scarf, salt on her palm, and the commitment walk at dusk. The first cold edge arrives late and briefly, after the reader has seen the relationship as real and good.

**Ch.5:** ordinary life comes first through Kene's repair visit: the scraped door, hinges, planing, plantain, jokes, the chipped glass, the kitchen court, and the town's street life. The cold arrives into a repair scene, not a supernatural set-piece.

---

## Obianuju interiority / unfinished business

Obianuju is protected from becoming only "the claimed woman" by scene-based interiority:

- Ch.4 gives her competence, wit, market authority, her own prior longing to leave the town, and the sentence: "Some days I think I already went and came back wrong."
- Ch.4 also renders her consent as costed and intelligent, not romantic carelessness: "No. But it will make me know what kind of yes I am saying."
- Ch.5 gives her ordinary authority in the house, her comic edge with Kene, and a private gesture after the room warms around her entrance.

She remains warm, exact, resistant, and unresolved.

---

## Fearlessness as danger

**Ch.4:** the protagonist reads speed as current and mercy. The acceptance of speed is the danger: he is glad where fear should have asked useful questions.

**Ch.5:** his calm "yes" frightens Kene more than a "no" would have. The chapter names the limitation through Kene, without exposition: "You think because you are not afraid, nothing has authority over you."

The protagonist remains compelling, but the chapters refuse to admire his fearlessness uncritically.

---

## Motifs used organically

- **Current / speed:** Ch.4 uses the current as his inner experience of momentum; it changes from water-origin to relational momentum.
- **Ribbon / line:** Ch.4 begins with Obianuju's slipped line in the courtyard, echoing the unbroken ribbon without redeploying it mechanically.
- **Salt:** Ch.4 uses salt on Obianuju's palm; Ch.5 uses salt in the air and on the ordinary tongue of the town.
- **Cold:** Ch.4 introduces a brief cold edge; Ch.5 lets another person feel it.
- **Door / repair:** Ch.5 makes the door practical before it becomes metaphysical. The scraped door is real wood first, threshold second.
- **Road:** Ch.5 turns the road between houses into a real offer of refuge, not a symbol.

No attempt was made to force all ten motifs into the chapters.

## Acceptance-gate sentences

**Ch.4:** `He had seen the speed and called it current. He had seen the cold and called it weather. He had seen the look on her face when she said she had already gone and come back wrong and called it sorrow, because it was sorrow, and because naming a thing accurately in part is one of the ways a man avoids naming the whole.`

This is Ch.4's gate because it holds the chapter's whole error: he is not stupid, not blind, not careless. He is partially accurate, and the partial accuracy protects him from the whole.

**Ch.5:** `You learn that you can cross the road.`

This is Ch.5's gate because it is the Confidant's whole gift: not rescue, not cosmology, not explanation, only the practical knowledge that exit remains available. The protagonist's refusal of that simple road is the chapter's turn.

# Ch.6 Elder Sentence Discovery Pass

**Date:** 2026-06-28
**Scope:** sentence-finding only. No Ch.6 drafting.
**Status:** Ch.6 remains locked until owner selects, revises, or rejects the candidate sentence.

---

## Governing requirement

The Elder's sentence must do three things at once:

1. name the pressure without explaining the cosmology;
2. sound like ancestral knowledge, not genre prophecy;
3. alter the protagonist's understanding without giving him an answer.

It must not sound like:

- "You are chosen."
- "A spirit follows her."
- "You must fight for her."
- "The dead have marked you."
- "This is your destiny."

Those fail because they are too direct, too genre-coded, and too easy.

## Evaluation standard

Each candidate was tested against:

| Test | Requirement |
| :--- | :---------- |
| Plainness | Could an old woman say it without theatricality? |
| Domestic authority | Does it use household/ordinary language rather than prophecy language? |
| Pressure without exposition | Does it imply prior claim without naming spirit-spouse mechanics? |
| Duality | Does it protect Obianuju from villainy? |
| Late payoff | Can it return in Ch.25 with deeper meaning? |
| Non-solution | Does it refuse to tell him what to do? |
| Voice fit | Does it sit near the book's corrected-sentence gravity? |

## Candidate lines

### 1. `Do not call a room yours because you are allowed to sit in it.`

**Result:** **SHORTLIST.**

Why it works: plain, domestic, irreversible. It names the pressure as prior claim without naming the claim. It alters his understanding of marriage, house, and permission. It does not accuse Obianuju or instruct him to leave.

Risk: slightly severe; may need softening in scene through the Elder's care.

### 2. `What arrived before you will not leave because you are kind to it.`

**Result:** **SHORTLIST.**

Why it works: directly uses the title architecture without naming the Spirit Husband. It corrects his most dangerous assumption: that tenderness can negotiate with prior claim.

Risk: more conceptual than domestic. Strong, but may show the blueprint if placed too cleanly.

### 3. `A thing can open its door to you and still keep its house.`

**Result:** **SHORTLIST.**

Why it works: domestic, elliptical, and aligned with door/threshold motifs. It protects Obianuju's duality: she can love him and still remain held by something he cannot enter.

Risk: "thing" may feel too abstract unless the scene supplies a concrete object or doorway.

### 4. `The hand that feeds you may still be carrying what you cannot carry.`

**Result:** **POSSIBLE.**

Why it works: protects Obianuju; ties to hands/food/domestic giving. It warns him not to reduce her gift to safety.

Risk: leans toward aphorism and does not clearly touch prior claim.

### 5. `Some doors open only to prove they were never empty.`

**Result:** **POSSIBLE.**

Why it works: threshold motif; gives the prior-presence pressure without saying what is there.

Risk: more uncanny than ancestral-domestic. Could drift toward genre if overemphasized.

### 6. `If a woman comes warm to your table, do not think the cold has released her.`

**Result:** **STRONG BUT DANGEROUS.**

Why it works: exact to Ch.1-5, preserves warmth/cold duality, refuses to make Obianuju false.

Risk: too close to explaining the current plot mechanics. Better as narrator insight than Elder sentence.

### 7. `You are asking whether she is yours. Ask first what has been keeping her.`

**Result:** **REJECT FOR CH.6.**

Why it works: clear.

Why rejected: too explanatory and too close to the cosmology. It tells him the question, which the chapter should not do.

### 8. `A kept thing is not always a spared thing.`

**Result:** **REJECT FOR CH.6 / RESERVE FOR LATE BOOK.**

Why it works: hits the central thesis.

Why rejected: too early and too naked. This belongs near Ch.25 recognition, not Ch.6 seed.

### 9. `Do not fight a house because one room is cold.`

**Result:** **REJECT.**

Why it works: domestic and plain.

Why rejected: it risks advising passivity and could sound like "stay," which the Elder must not do. It also makes the cold too simple.

### 10. `The thing you call silence may only be someone else being answered.`

**Result:** **POSSIBLE.**

Why it works: reframes absence without naming the force. It could alter his understanding of the unreachable hour.

Risk: a little too polished and abstract; less domestic than the best candidates.

### 11. `No woman is only the room where you meet her.`

**Result:** **POSSIBLE.**

Why it works: strongly protects Obianuju from being reduced to his experience. It is morally right for the Elder.

Risk: more relational than metaphysical; may not carry enough ancestral pressure alone.

### 12. `The first owner of a thing is not always the one holding it.`

**Result:** **REJECT.**

Why it works: title/claim pressure.

Why rejected: "owner" makes Obianuju sound like property and risks violating the non-villain/non-possession discipline.

### 13. `There are things love may enter and still not inherit.`

**Result:** **SHORTLIST.**

Why it works: grave, restrained, and irreversible. It refuses both rescue and possession. It also points toward the later truth that love is real but not sovereign over every prior claim.

Risk: less domestic than candidate 1; it may need an object in scene to ground it.

### 14. `When a house is already full, even love must learn where to stand.`

**Result:** **SHORTLIST.**

Why it works: domestic, plain, and sceneable. It alters his idea of love as enough without telling him to leave or fight. It protects Obianuju because the house is "full," not evil.

Risk: "full" may need careful context so it does not sound like another person in the ordinary romantic sense.

### 15. `You can be welcomed and still be late.`

**Result:** **SHORTLIST.**

Why it works: devastatingly compact. It ties directly to lateness, the Claim's grammar, the journal, and the title pressure. It does not explain anything. It can return in Ch.25 with full force.

Risk: it is almost too perfect structurally. Needs scene pressure so it does not feel like the author showing the key.

## Ranked shortlist

### Recommendation A — strongest overall

`You can be welcomed and still be late.`

Why: it is short, plain, irreversible, and native to the existing architecture. It names the pressure through lateness, not cosmology. It fits the Elder's authority because it sounds like a sentence she has known longer than he has known the problem. It also returns cleanly in Ch.25 when he finally understands that being welcomed by Obianuju did not make him first.

Concern: because it fits the architecture so exactly, Ch.6 must bury it in ordinary scene, not frame it as revelation.

### Recommendation B — strongest domestic image

`Do not call a room yours because you are allowed to sit in it.`

Why: household language, clean moral pressure, no prophecy. It prevents him from mistaking access for standing. It can return late as a recognition about love, rooms, and claims.

Concern: slightly more admonishing than Elderly if not softened by scene.

### Recommendation C — strongest love/claim balance

`There are things love may enter and still not inherit.`

Why: serious, memorable, and not explanatory. It keeps love real while refusing to make love sovereign.

Concern: more abstract; needs concrete scene grounding.

### Recommendation D — strongest house metaphor

`When a house is already full, even love must learn where to stand.`

Why: domestic, ancestral, and non-genre. It gives him no instruction except humility before what preceded him.

Concern: could be misread too literally unless the scene shapes it.

## Rejected direction

Avoid lines that:

- name a spirit directly;
- make Obianuju a vessel, trap, or villain;
- command him to fight, leave, save, or endure;
- declare destiny, chosen-ness, or curse;
- explain the three-force architecture;
- sound like fantasy prophecy;
- sound like therapy insight.

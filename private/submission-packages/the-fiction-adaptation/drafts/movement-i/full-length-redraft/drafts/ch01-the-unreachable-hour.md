# Chapter 1: The Unreachable Hour

There was an hour, late in the afternoon, when his wife went somewhere he could not follow, and the strange thing — the thing he never said aloud, because there was no one to say it to who would not try to mend it — was that he had stopped minding.

For all but that one hour of the day she was the most present person he had ever stood near. Mornings, she filled a room the way weather fills a valley, completely and without asking. He had watched her that very morning wring a cloth at the basin and snap it flat against the table before she folded it, two sharp cracks of wet linen that were her whole character in a sound — decisive, a little severe, faintly pleased with itself. She sang under her breath when she thought the singing was private, the same four bars of a hymn her grandmother's people had carried, and she stopped the instant she caught herself, as though singing were a thing a serious woman did not get caught at. She was impatient with waste and impatient with self-pity and not, he had learned, impatient with him, which he had decided was the nearest thing to grace he had been shown by a living person. She kept a knife sharper than the household needed and would not say who had taught her to keep it so. She laughed at her own severity a half-second after committing it, so that her sternness and her amusement arrived together and a man could not separate them. That was the woman: warm, exact, slightly fierce, entirely in the room, her voice always somewhere in the house and the warmth of her filling it. It was that the hour took — the voice, the warmth, the her of her — leaving the chair, leaving even the hands at their work, and going out of the room like a tide, and giving her back at the end as though nothing had borrowed her.

It came on without announcement. One moment Obianuju was a woman in a chair by the window, peeling the skin from an orange in a single unbroken ribbon, and the next she was a woman in a chair by the window who was no longer entirely in the room. Her hands did not stop. That was the part the eye refused at first: the hands kept their work, the ribbon of peel lengthened and curled toward the floor, the small domestic competence of her continued without her. But her face had gone smooth in a way faces do not go smooth in sleep, and her eyes, still open, had stopped reporting what they saw.

He had learned the hour the way a man learns the tide of a coast he has married into — not by being taught, but by being caught out, once, and then never again. The first time he had spoken to her across it. He had asked something ordinary, whether she wanted the lamp lit, and she had not answered, and he had crossed the room and taken her wrist, and her wrist had been cold. Not the cold of a draught. The room had been warm; the afternoon outside was the wet heat of the town in the long season, the air thick enough to lean against. Her wrist had been cold the way the inside of a church is cold on a bright day, a temperature that belongs to the building and not to the weather. He had held it and felt the cold travel up his own arm like water finding a level, and he had thought, with the clarity that came to him in moments other men reserved for fear: *something is here, and it is not for me.*

He had not let go. That was the thing about him, the thing his mother had named before he was old enough to spell it and the thing that had carried him alive through three accidents that should have buried him. The first was water. He had gone under at nine, fetching a child's sandal, down past the grey and the green into a brown that held no light, and when his arms stopped arguing and his lungs gave up the case there had been no terror in it, none — only a flat certainty, arriving from somewhere that was not him, that he was not finished. He had not decided to swim. His body was already on the sand, coughing the brown back up, before the part of a man that chooses had chosen anything. That was the shape of all three accidents, and it had taught him the wrong lesson: he did not let go of the cold things. Other men flinched. He leaned in. A man who has been told, by something that does not use words, that he is not finished walks toward what frightens others the way another man walks toward a lit doorway. It had kept him alive. It had also, he would come to understand, made him useless against the only kind of danger that does not bother to kill you.

So he had held her cold wrist that first time and waited, and at the end of the hour Obianuju had come back into her own face the way the sun comes back into a room when a cloud uncovers it — all at once, and from no direction — and she had looked at his hand on her wrist and smiled, a real smile, the smile that had made him cross a courtyard two years ago to learn her name, and she had said, *you are holding me as though I might float away,* and he had said nothing, because the answer was yes.

Now he did not hold her wrist. He had stopped, not out of fear, but out of a courtesy he could not have defended to anyone. It seemed to him that wherever she went in that hour, she went because she had to, and that a man who loved her would not stand at the door of the place tugging her sleeve. So he sat across the room and kept her company in the only way the hour allowed, which was to be there when it ended.

What that waiting required of him was harder than it looked, and he was good at it the way he was good at all the wrong things. He did not pace; pacing was for men with somewhere to put their fear, and he had none. He sat with his hands open on his knees — he had learned not to fold them, because folded hands were a kind of bracing, and there was nothing in the room to brace against. He kept her in the corner of his attention without staring, the way you keep a pot at the edge of your mind so it does not boil over while you pretend to read. In the early months he had tried to fill the hour — a book, a task, the small accountings of the household — and none of it had held; the hour had a gravity that bent everything in the room toward the still woman in the chair, and a man could either fight that gravity and lose or sit inside it and keep her company. He had chosen, with the same calm he had brought to drowning, to keep her company. He did not know that this was surrender. He thought it was love. It was, he would understand much later, both at once — and the not being able to tell them apart was the whole of his marriage.

The town was the kind of place that did not confirm itself. It lay along a grey-green water that the maps called a sea and the old people called by an older name, and it had a market that smelled of salt and diesel and the particular sweetness of plantain blackening on a grill, and it had a church with a blue door and a mosque with a green one and, between them, a great many houses where the doors were no colour at all because the paint had given up against the wet. Strangers who came for work said it reminded them of three other places and could never agree which three. He had come for love and stayed for the same reason, and he had noticed, the way one notices a thing without yet making anything of it, that he could not have drawn the town on paper.

He had tested this once, going for fish. The fish-sellers worked the far edge of the market where the ground went soft toward the water, and he knew three ways to them, and on the day he tested it he took the way past the tailor's and the standpipe and the wall where the boys played, and the way delivered him instead to the back of the church with the blue door, which had no business being there, which belonged to another quarter entirely. He had stood a moment with the wet heat on his neck and the gulls arguing over something at the waterline and the sea moving against the stones the way a neighbour moves about on the other side of a shared wall — present, incurious, there before him and staying after — and he had felt his body know the town in a way his mind could not follow, his feet sure of a path his eye could not have drawn, and he had thought, *the heat,* and bought his fish from a nearer seller and gone home another way that also was not the way he intended. He had decided this was the heat. The heat made a man's certainties soft at the edges. He had decided a great many things were the heat, in those years, because the alternative was to decide they were something else, and he was not yet ready to keep that company.

The peel reached the floor. The ribbon of it lay on the boards in a loose spiral, and the orange sat naked and forgotten in her still hands, and the room was cooling. He felt it begin at the window and move inward, the warmth retreating toward him as though the cold were a slow tide and he were higher ground. He did not move to the lamp. He had learned that light made no difference to the hour; the hour was not dark. It was only absent.

He watched her hands. He had a private theory, which he had never tested because testing it would have meant asking the forbidden question, that her hands kept working in the hour because the hands belonged to her and the rest of her had been called away — that whatever took the afternoon part of his wife had no use for her hands, and so left them, the way a creditor leaves the furniture and takes the deed. He found, to his own quiet alarm, that he was not frightened by this theory. He was interested in it. He turned it over the way he turned over every cold fact that should have undone a man and had not, with the unhurried curiosity that other men would have called unwell.

*He is standing in a doorway that has no house attached to it. The doorway is old — older than the town, older than the church with its blue door — and on the far side of it is a long room that has been emptied of everyone who ever loved it. There is a cold here that is not weather. There is a woman in the long room with her back to him, and her hands are working at something he cannot see, and he understands, the way one understands a thing in this place, that she has been in this room far longer than she has been in his. He does not call to her. There is a man in the long room too, or a shape with the patience of a man, standing where a husband would stand, and the shape has been there since before the doorway was built, and it does not turn, because it does not need to. It has already counted him and found him late.*

The breath let go. The room was the room again. The window held the wet grey light of the town's afternoon, and the peel was on the floor, and his wife's hands were still.

He sat with his heart going at its ordinary pace, because his heart did not know how to do otherwise, and he thought — not in fear, never in fear, that door had been shut in him long ago — *I have been late to something, and I do not know its name.*

The hour turned. He always knew the moment by the warmth returning before he knew it by her, the cold tide going back out toward the window, and then Obianuju drew a breath that was deeper than the breaths the hour had allowed her, and her eyes reported the room again, and she looked down at the orange in her hands as though someone had placed it there as a riddle.

"It will be bitter now," she said. "Left so long."

"Then we will be bitter together," he said, because it was the kind of thing that made her laugh, and she laughed, and the laugh was real, and the woman in the chair was wholly the woman he had crossed a courtyard for, with nothing of the long room in her, nothing of the cold, only the late warm light catching the side of her face and the orange held out to him across the small distance of their life.

He ate the bitter half and gave her the less bitter, and that, too, was a thing he did without being able to defend it. They sat in the returned warmth while the town outside found its evening, and a man who could not be frightened by death held in his mouth the proof that something he loved went somewhere every afternoon that he was not permitted to go, and did not yet understand that this was the danger — not the cold, not the doorway, not the patient shape that had counted him late, but the calm with which he had decided he could live beside all three.

Outside, the call to evening went up from the green door and, a little after, the bell from the blue, and between them the houses with doors of no colour held their families, and the water that the maps misnamed lay flat and grey and old, keeping whatever the maps could not.

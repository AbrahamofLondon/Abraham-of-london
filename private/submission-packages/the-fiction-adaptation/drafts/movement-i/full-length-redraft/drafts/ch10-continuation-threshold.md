# Chapter 10: Continuation

Kene was mending a net when he arrived, and the net was the first thing that told him this visit would not be like the last.

The last time, Kene had been planing a door, working with the easy rhythm of a man who believed most things could be fixed. This time the net lay across the yard like a wound, tangled in three places he could see from the gate, and Kene was not working so much as picking at it — his fingers moving through the same knot again and again, not solving it, only worrying it the way a man worries a tooth that has begun to ache.

He looked up when the protagonist reached the gate. He did not smile. He did not call out. He looked at him the way a man looks at a friend he has been waiting for and dreading in equal measure.

"You came," Kene said.

"I came."

"Because you wanted to, or because something happened?"

The protagonist stood at the gate with his hands in his pockets. The salt was gone from his palm — he had washed it off at the standpipe — but the cold was still there, a memory in the muscle, waiting.

"Something happened."

Kene set the needle down. He did not ask what. He gestured with his chin toward the other crate, and the protagonist sat.

The yard was smaller than the one at the house, and drier, and full of things that belonged to a man who repaired what broke. A bicycle frame leaned against the wall, its wheels missing. A coil of rope hung from a nail. A tin of oil sat on a windowsill with a rag tucked under its lid. Everything had a place and everything looked as though it had been used recently, which was the opposite of the house he had left, where the cold made even the furniture seem as though it were waiting for permission to exist.

Kene did not pick up the needle again. That was the first sign. The last time, he had kept working while he listened, letting his hands do the thing they knew while his mind attended to the story. This time he sat with his hands still, and the stillness was worse than any question he could have asked.

"Tell me," Kene said.

The protagonist told him about the trance.

He did not tell it well. The words were too small for what they had to carry. He said that he had been sitting at the table and then he was in the yard and the cloth was in his hand and the cloth was wet and cold and there was salt on his palm. He said that he had seen Obianuju in a chair she never used, watching a door he had never seen, waiting for it to open. He said that the cold had touched him, not the room, not the hour, but him, and that it had left a mark.

Kene listened. His hands stayed on his knees. When the protagonist finished, Kene was quiet for a long moment. Then he said, "You are sure you were not dreaming."

"I am sure."

"Because a man can dream a thing and call it vision."

"I know the difference."

Kene looked at him then. "Do you?"

The question was not cruel. It was the question of a man who had seen his friend walk into a house that was colder than the road and call it ordinary, and who had been waiting ever since for the ordinary to become something he could name.

"I know the difference," the protagonist said. "A dream has no laws. This had laws. I could not cross the room. I could not reach her. I could only stand in the doorway and watch her watch a door I have never seen."

Kene nodded slowly. Then he said, "And what did you do, when you came back?"

"I came here."

"No. Before that. When you were standing in the yard with the wet cloth in your hand and the salt on your skin. What did you do?"

The protagonist hesitated. The question was fair, and because it was fair he disliked it.

"I hung the cloth back on the line," he said.

Kene's face did not change. But something behind his eyes shifted — not disappointment, not anger, but a kind of confirmation, as though he had been told a thing he had already suspected.

"You had proof," Kene said. "Real proof. Something you could touch and taste. And you hung it back on the line."

"It was the cloth she had used. It belonged on the line."

"It belonged in your hand. It was evidence. You had evidence that the thing in your house is not only in your head, not only in the hour, not only in her. It touched you. And you hung it back where you found it."

The protagonist said nothing. The yard was very quiet. Somewhere in the next lane a woman was calling a child, and the child was not answering, and the not-answering was so ordinary that it made the silence in Kene's yard feel like a held breath.

"Why did you come to me?" Kene asked.

"Because you asked whether I could leave. I did not answer."

"And you have an answer now?"

"No."

Kene picked up the needle. He did not begin working. He held it the way a man holds a tool when he is deciding whether to use it or set it down.

"Let me ask you something," Kene said. "And I want you to answer it as if I am not your friend."

"You are my friend."

"That is why I am asking."

Kene set the needle down again. He rubbed his thumb against his fingertips, the way he did when thinking through a repair that had no obvious solution.

"You have known for months that something is wrong in that house. You have felt the cold. You have seen her go somewhere you cannot follow. You have been given a sentence by an old woman who sees things other people do not see. And now you have been given proof — physical proof — that the cold can touch you. And you are still there. You are still in the house. You are still sitting through the hour with your hands open. My question is not whether you can leave. My question is whether you want to."

The protagonist looked at him. "I love her."

"I know you do. That is not what I asked."

"Then what are you asking?"

Kene leaned forward. His voice did not rise, but something in it sharpened — not with anger, but with the effort of saying a thing he had been holding back for months.

"I am asking whether you are staying because you love her, or because you have decided that leaving would make you the kind of man who leaves. Because those are different things. And if you cannot tell the difference, you are going to stay in that house until the cold has taken everything it wants, and you will call it faithfulness while it does."

The words hung between them. The protagonist felt them the way he had felt the cold at the boundary in the trance — not painful, but absolute. He could not cross them.

"You think I am staying for the wrong reason," he said.

"I think you are staying for a reason you have not examined. And I think the cold knows that. I think the cold is counting on it."

The protagonist stood up. He did not know why he stood — there was nowhere to go — but the sentence had entered him and he needed to move against it, even if the movement was only standing.

"You are asking me to leave her."

"I am asking you to know why you stay."

The difference was small and everything. The protagonist stood in the yard with the net at his feet and the bicycle frame against the wall and the tin of oil on the windowsill, and he understood that Kene had done what no one else had done: he had refused to let the fearlessness pass for courage.

"Sit down," Kene said. "There is someone you need to see."

---

The woman who sold dried shrimp was near the mosque wall, as Kene had said. She was old — older than Mama Nneoma, or older in the way that age showed more on some faces — and she sat on a low stool behind a tray of shrimp the colour of rust. She did not call out to them as they approached. She watched them the way the town watched everything, without appearing to watch, and when they stopped in front of her tray she did not ask what they wanted.

"You brought him," she said to Kene.

"I brought him."

"He should have come sooner."

"He did not know."

The woman looked at the protagonist. Her eyes were the colour of the sea on a grey day, and they held the same patience, the same indifference to whether he stayed or left.

"You live in the house with the cold front room," she said.

It was not a question.

"Yes."

She was quiet for a moment. Her hands were in her lap, and he saw that they were shaking — not from age, but from something else. She pressed them flat against her thighs to still them, and the gesture was so private that he almost looked away.

"I lived in that house," she said. "Thirty years ago. With my husband and my first child. We left in the middle of the night, and I have not spoken of it since. Not to anyone. Not even to myself, not in words."

She stopped. Her hands were still pressed against her thighs. He saw her knuckles whiten.

"I did not think it would come back," she said. "I thought it had finished. I thought leaving meant it could not follow. But it does not follow. It waits. It waits for the next person who does not know to be afraid."

The protagonist stood very still. The bag of fish and peppers and salt was heavy in his hand, and the weight of it was the only thing keeping him in the ordinary world.

"What was it like?" he asked. "In the house."

She looked at him. For a moment he thought she would not answer. Then she said, "It came first to the front room. A cold that had nothing to do with the weather. My husband said it was the damp. I said it was the damp too, because saying it was the damp was easier than saying what I thought it was. Then it came to the hour. Every afternoon, at the same time, I would sit in the chair by the window and I would go somewhere I could not explain. My hands kept working. That was the worst part. My hands kept working while I was gone, and when I came back I would find that I had done things — folded cloth, shelled beans, turned pages — and I had no memory of doing them."

She paused. Her hands were still pressed against her thighs.

"Then it came to the bed. I would wake in the night and the air above me would be cold, and I would feel something standing there — not a person, not a shape, but a presence, the way you feel a room change when someone enters it. And I would lie still and pretend I was asleep, because I did not know what else to do."

The protagonist felt the cold in his palm return. Not as temperature. As recognition.

"What did you do?" he asked.

"I left," she said. "But not soon enough. I left after it reached for my child. Not to harm her. To claim her. I woke one night and the cold was not above me. It was above her. It was standing where a father would stand, and I understood, in that moment, that it had not been waiting for me at all. It had been waiting for her. It wanted what came next."

The word hung in the air between them. *Next.* He thought of the journal, the spine resewn by hand, the thread that had been used because the household had used what it had. He thought of the ribbon of orange peel curling to the floor. He thought of Obianuju's hands, still working while the rest of her was called away.

"It wanted what came next," she said again. "Not me. Not my husband. The next life in the house. It did not miss us when we left. That was the worst of it. It only waited for the next warmth."

She looked at him then, and her eyes were dry, but the effort of keeping them dry was visible in the set of her jaw.

"It is still waiting," she said. "It has been waiting in that house for thirty years. It does not care who lives there. It only cares that someone does. It wants a life to attach itself to. It wants to continue."

The protagonist did not thank her. He could not. The word would have been too small for what she had given him. He stood with the bag in his hand and the fish growing warm against his leg and the salt already beginning to leak through the paper, and he understood that he had been given not an answer but a choice.

She turned back to her tray. The conversation was over.

---

He walked home alone. Kene had offered to come, and he had said no, because he needed to think, and because Kene's presence would have made the thinking into a conversation, and he was not ready for a conversation.

The streets were the same streets. The standpipe had its congregation. The boys were at the wall. The tailor's machine was running. The woman with the mortar was grinding something red. All of it was the same, and none of it was the same, because he was carrying a knowledge he could not set down: the cold had been in the house before Obianuju. It had been waiting. And it wanted what came next.

He reached the house and stood at the gate.

The front door was open. He could see into the front room, where Obianuju was sitting in the chair by the window — not the chair she used during the hour, but the other one, the one she never used, the one that had always seemed to belong to someone who had not yet arrived. She was sitting in it the way she had sat in the trance, her hands in her lap, her eyes on something he could not see.

She was looking at the door.

Not the front door. The door to the back room, the one that led to the kitchen yard, the one that had no colour because the paint had given up against the wet. She was looking at it the way a person looks at a door she has been waiting for someone to open.

He stood at the gate and watched her, and he understood, with a clarity that had no fear in it, that the trance had not shown him a vision of the future. It had shown him the present. She was already waiting. She had always been waiting. And the door she was watching was not a door he could open for her.

He walked through the gate. He walked up the path. He stepped into the front room, and Obianuju turned her head and looked at him, and her face was the face of a woman who had been somewhere he could not follow and had just returned.

"You are back," she said.

"I am back."

"You were gone longer than fish requires."

He set the bag on the table. The paper was wet where the salt had leaked through, and a small white stain was spreading across the cloth beneath it.

"I met someone," he said. "A woman in the market. She used to live in this house."

Obianuju's hands went still. She did not look at him. She looked at the door to the kitchen yard, the no-colour door, the one she had been watching when he arrived.

"She told you," Obianuju said.

"She told me some of it."

"Did she tell you why she left?"

"Yes."

Obianuju was quiet for a long moment. Then she stood up from the chair — the chair she never used — and walked to the window. She stood with her back to him, and when she spoke her voice was the same plain voice she used for everything, but there was something underneath it that he had not heard before. It was not fear. It was not shame. It was the voice of a woman who had been waiting for this conversation and had hoped, until the last possible moment, that it would never come.

"I knew about the house before I moved in," she said.

She said it flatly, the way a person says a thing she has rehearsed too many times.

"Not all of it. Not the details. But I knew that something had happened here, and that the people who had lived here had left quickly, and that the house had been empty for a long time. I thought it did not matter. I thought the cold was mine — that I had brought it with me, that it belonged to me the way the journal belonged to me, the way the waiting belonged to me. I thought that if I moved into the house, the cold would follow me, and that it would be easier to live with in a place that already knew it."

She turned to face him.

"I was wrong. The cold was here before me. It was waiting for someone to come back. And when I moved in, it thought I was the one it had been waiting for."

He looked at her. The room was very still. The market sounds had receded, or he had stopped hearing them.

"When did you know?" he asked.

She did not answer at once. She looked at the no-colour door, and he watched her look at it, and he understood that she was deciding how much of the truth she could say without breaking something between them.

"The first week," she said. "The first time the hour came in this house, I knew it was not the same as the hour in my old room. It was stronger. It had a shape. It felt like something that had been waiting for me, not something I had carried with me."

"And you did not tell me."

"No."

"Why?"

She looked at him then. Her face was unreadable in the way faces become unreadable when the person behind them has decided not to show their hand.

"Because I was afraid that if I told you, you would stay."

The sentence entered him the way the cold entered the room — from the edges, until it had occupied the whole of him.

"You were afraid I would stay," he said.

"Yes."

"Not that I would leave. That I would stay."

She did not answer. She did not need to. He understood, in the silence between them, what she had been carrying. She had not told him because she knew him. She knew that a warning would not make him leave. It would make him plant his feet. It would make him call endurance love. And she had not wanted to be the reason he stayed in a house that was already occupied.

"You should have told me," he said.

"I know."

"It was my choice to make."

"I know."

"You took it from me."

She looked at him. Her voice, when it came, was not defensive. It was tired in the way a voice gets tired when it has been carrying a thing for too long.

"I did not take your choice. I delayed it. The choice is still yours. It has always been yours. You just did not know you were making it."

He crossed the room and stood beside her at the window. The town was doing its grey afternoon, the market sounds arriving muffled, the sea invisible but present. He did not touch her. He stood beside her the way he had stood beside her in the journal morning, with the same distance, the same inability to close the gap between what he wanted to say and what he could say.

"The cold wants to continue," he said.

"Yes."

"Continuation means — "

"I know what it means."

She said it quickly, not sharply, but with the speed of a woman who had been thinking about this for longer than he had. She knew. She had always known. The cold wanted what came next. It wanted to reach past her, into the future, into a life that had not yet begun. And she was the door it would walk through.

He stood beside her and thought of the woman in the market, who had left in the middle of the night with her husband and her first child, who had outlived the cold by leaving everything behind. He thought of Kene, who had asked whether he was staying for love or for the fear of being the man who leaves. He thought of the Elder, who had given him a sentence he could not use, and of his mother, who had said he did not let go of the cold things.

He thought of the trance. The no-colour door. Obianuju watching it. The cold that had touched him and left a mark.

He was still early enough to choose.

He did not know what he would choose.

But he knew, standing beside her at the window, that the choice was coming, and that it would not be a choice between staying and leaving. It would be a choice between two kinds of staying — the staying that sat with open hands and called it love, and the staying that acted, that moved, that refused to let the cold have what it wanted without a fight.

He did not know which kind of staying he was capable of.

He only knew that the cold had touched him, and that he had brought something back, and that the something was not salt.

It was knowledge.

And knowledge, unlike salt, did not wash off.

---

That night, after Obianuju had gone to bed, he sat in the front room with the lamp burning low and the cold gathering at the edges of the room. He did not sit with his hands open. He sat with his hands on his knees, and his hands were not open, and the difference was small and everything.

He thought of the woman in the market. He thought of her husband, who had believed her too late. He thought of the child, who had been the reason they left. He thought of the cold, which had been in the house before any of them, waiting for someone to come back.

He thought of Obianuju, who had moved into a house that was already occupied, who had known and not known, who had been trying to find out ever since whether she was the one the cold had been waiting for.

He thought of the trance, and of the no-colour door, and of her watching it.

He thought of continuation.

He stood up. He walked to the door of the back room — the no-colour door, the one she had been watching — and he put his hand on the handle. The handle was cold. Not the cold of metal left in a draught. The cold of something that had been touched by a room it did not belong to.

He did not open the door.

He stood there with his hand on the cold handle, and he understood that the door was not the door he needed to open. The door he needed to open was the one inside himself — the one that would let him stop sitting with open hands and start moving, even if the movement was wrong, even if the movement cost him, even if the movement led him into a country he had never meant to enter.

He took his hand off the handle.

He did not go back to the front room.

He walked to the front door. He opened it. The night air hit his face, warm and salt-thick, and he stepped out into it.

He did not know where he was going. He only knew that he could not wait for morning. The cold had been waiting for thirty years. It had waited for the woman in the market, and for the woman before her, and for Obianuju. It would wait for him too, if he let it. But he did not have to let it. He could move. He could act. He could walk through the dark toward the one person who had given him a sentence he had not yet learned to use.

He walked through the dark toward Mama Nneoma's compound.

He did not rehearse the question. He did not know what he would say when he arrived. He only knew that he had spent months proving he could sit in the cold, and that tonight, for the first time, he had chosen to leave it behind him.

The no-colour door stayed closed behind him.

The cold stayed in the house.

And he walked.

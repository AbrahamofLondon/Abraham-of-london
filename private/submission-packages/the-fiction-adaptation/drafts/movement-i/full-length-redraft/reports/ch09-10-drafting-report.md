## How Ch.9 Functions as a Trance Scene, Not Trance Atmosphere

The trance in Ch.9 is a scene with:
- **A clear entry point:** the air moving near the window, the italic present tense shift.
- **A governing action:** walking. The protagonist walks through the yard, toward the door, into the room. He is not a passive receiver of vision.
- **A spatial logic:** the yard lengthens, the walls recede, the basin stays in place — impossible geography with internal consistency.
- **A physical object:** the cloth, which he touches, which is stiff with cold, which becomes wet with salt.
- **A return with evidence:** he comes back holding the wet cloth, with salt on his hand. The trance leaves a physical mark.
- **A consequence:** he cannot pretend the cold is only weather. It has touched him.

The trance is not atmosphere, mood, or dream. It is a place with jurisdiction, entered and exited through formal signals (italic present tense in; past tense out).

---

## What New Thing Ch.9 Reveals Without Explaining

**The cold has a boundary.** The protagonist cannot cross the room. The cold at the threshold holds him back. This is the first time the cold has been shown to have limits — and therefore, structure.

**Obianuju is not merely absent during the hour.** She is present somewhere else, watching a door. She has agency in the trance: she is sitting in the chair she never uses, looking at a door he has never seen, waiting for it to open. She is not a passive vessel.

**The cold can leave physical evidence.** The cloth is wet, cold, and covered in salt. The protagonist brings it back into the waking world. This changes the rules: the trance is not only perceptual. It is material.

**The cold has recognised him.** The salt running into his hands, the cold that is "the cold of something that has recognised him" — this is the first suggestion that the cold is aware of him specifically, not merely occupying the same space.

None of these are explained. They are shown through image and action.

## What Action the Protagonist Takes After Ch.9

He does not sit through the next hour with open hands.

He goes to Kene.

This is the pivot from endurance to action. The decision is small — he walks to Kene's yard — but it is the first time in the novel that he has moved toward someone else with the intention of speaking about what he has experienced. He does not go for advice or rescue. He goes because he needs to say one sentence aloud to another human being.

The chapter ends with him in motion: "He was moving. He did not know whether the movement would save him or cost him."

## How Ch.10 Returns Kene as a Full Character

Kene returns with:
- **Appetite and humour:** he buys fish, argues about price, makes a dry joke about walking to the market for fish not being the worst use of a morning.
- **Practical intelligence:** he has already been investigating. He found the shrimp seller. He asked the right questions.
- **Loyalty and limitation:** he brings the protagonist to the witness but does not interpret the evidence. He knows his limits.
- **Challenge without reverence:** "You are sure you were not dreaming" / "Because a man can dream a thing and call it vision." He does not accept the protagonist's account uncritically.
- **His own reason for arriving:** he sought out the shrimp seller because he had noticed the cold and wanted to understand it. He is not merely receiving the protagonist's confession.

Kene is not exposition furniture. He is a character with his own initiative, his own doubts, and his own way of loving his friend — which is to bring him evidence and let him decide what to do with it.

## How Ch.10 Widens the Social World

Three new social elements enter:

1. **The shrimp seller (named in the report as a market woman):** an old woman who lived in the house thirty years ago, who left in the middle of the night, who tells the protagonist that the cold had been there before Obianuju. She is the first external witness to the house's history. She gives the chapter its structural revelation: the cold wants to continue.

2. **The house's history:** the cold was in the house before Obianuju. It was empty for a long time. The previous occupants left suddenly. The town does not talk about it. This widens the stakes from a private marriage problem to a recurring condition of the house itself.

3. **Kene's network:** he found the shrimp seller because he asked questions. The town has people who know things. The novel's social field is no longer limited to the protagonist's immediate circle.

---

## What External Stake Becomes Visible by the End of Ch.10

**Continuation.**

The cold wants to reach into the future — to claim what comes next. The shrimp seller left because the cold began reaching for her child. Obianuju knows what continuation means. The protagonist understands it by the chapter's close.

This is the first concrete stake that can be lost: not the marriage, not the house, but the future itself. The cold is not content to hold Obianuju. It wants to extend itself.

The stake is visible without being melodramatic. It is stated plainly by the shrimp seller ("It wants to continue") and confirmed by Obianuju ("I know what it means"). No child appears on the page. The threat is in the threshold, not in the depiction.

## How Emotional Register Varies

**Ch.9:**
- **Opening warmth:** Obianuju's laugh in the kitchen yard. The ordinary gold light. The cloth on the line. A moment of genuine, unguarded domestic tenderness.
- **Trance register:** precise, visual, strange. The cold as boundary. The no-colour door. The salt.
- **Return register:** physical consequence. The wet cloth. The salt on his hand. The cold cup of tea.
- **Closing register:** decision. Movement toward Kene. Not hope, but the end of passivity.

**Ch.10:**
- **Kene's yard:** dry, practical, full of tools and repairs. The opposite of the cold house.
- **Market register:** loud, ordinary, comic (the woman with one gold tooth charging Kene double).
- **Shrimp seller register:** grave, plain, ancestral. The story told without drama.
- **Walk home register:** solitary, reflective.
- **Doorstep with Obianuju:** tender, painful, honest. She confesses what she knew and did not know.
- **Night register:** cold gathering at the edges, the protagonist with his hand on the handle, the decision to go to Mama Nneoma.

The registers modulate across both chapters. No single register is held for too long.

## How Obianuju Remains Active and Unfinished

**Ch.9:**
- In the trance, she is not a captive. She is sitting in the chair she never uses, watching a door, waiting. She has her own purpose.
- In the waking world, she knows he has been somewhere. She sees his wet hands. She does not press. She lets him come to her.

**Ch.10:**
- She confesses what she knew about the house before she moved in. This is her own story, told in her own voice.
- She says "I have been trying to find out ever since" — she is actively investigating her own condition.
- She knows what continuation means. She says it before he can finish the sentence.
- She is not a victim waiting to be saved. She is a woman who moved into a house that was already occupied, knowing some of the risk, and has been trying to understand the terms ever since.

She is more alive, more knowing, and more complex after Ch.10 than before.

## Acceptance-Gate Sentence for Ch.9 (per editorial ruling)

> *He had never asked her where she went. He had called it courtesy, respect, love. But the trance had shown him something he could not unsee: she was not merely absent during the hour. She was present somewhere else. She was watching a door. And the door was not for him.*

This is the chapter's deepest sentence. It does four things at once:
1. Corrects the protagonist's earlier misreading (courtesy, respect, love → avoidance)
2. Gives Obianuju interior life inside the hour (she is present somewhere else)
3. Turns the door into a future engine (she is watching a door)
4. Shifts the novel from "where does she go?" to "what is she waiting for?"

The evidence/escalation sentence remains structurally important but is not the acceptance-gate sentence:

> *He had brought something back. He did not know what it meant. He did not know whether it was a gift or a warning or a receipt for a debt he had not known he owed. But he knew that the trance had never left him with a physical mark before.*

This proves the Trance has become material. The acceptance-gate sentence carries the chapter's deepest revelation.

---

## Acceptance-Gate Sentence for Ch.10 (per editorial ruling)

> *"The cold does not care who lives there. It only cares that someone does. It wants a life to attach itself to. It wants to continue."*

Accepted under one condition: it must remain testimony, not doctrine. It works because it belongs to the shrimp seller's lived knowledge, trauma, memory, and partial understanding. It must not become the narrator's explanation of the system. Future chapters must not repeat "it wants to continue" as a slogan. The sentence should haunt the book, not define it.

## Recommendation for Next Step

**Ch.10 rewritten and re-audited. Movement I locked.** Proceed to **Movement II — The Cost** (Ch.11–21).

### Movement II Governing Standard

Every chapter in Movement II must exact a cost. Not atmosphere. Cost. Each chapter must make something harder after it happens. Possible costs: a relationship becomes less innocent; a room becomes less safe; Obianuju remembers too much; the protagonist acts and damages something; Kene pays for knowing; Mama Nneoma withholds or reveals at a price; the town recognises the house more publicly; the cold crosses a boundary; the protagonist's own keeping becomes dangerous; love loses one of its illusions.

If a chapter does not cost anyone anything, it is not a Movement II chapter.

### Movement II Immediate Priorities

**Ch.11 — The Reversal (~3,300 words):**
- Must not make Obianuju unsympathetic
- The reversal must be rendered as grief, pressure, and something done *to* her — not sudden cruelty
- The reader must feel wounded by her turn, not invited to judge her
- Begin with ordinary life; show Obianuju before the reversal
- Make the reversal emotionally legible; do not over-explain the cold's role
- Do not make the protagonist the sole victim
- Make his action in Ch.10 have cost
- Preserve reader devotion to Obianuju
- The question is not "Why is she turning against him?" but "What has the pressure made love unable to carry?"

**Ch.12 — Social Consequence (~3,000 words):**
- Widen beyond the house again
- The social world must feel the pressure
- Introduce or deepen a significant secondary figure
- The town should no longer be merely atmospheric; it should begin to respond

**Ch.13–15 — Midpoint Build:**
- By Ch.15, the novel needs a major turn — not a lore reveal, but a turn that changes the reader's understanding of Obianuju, the cold, the protagonist's survival, the house, or the meaning of being kept
- The turn must increase agency and cost; it must not solve the mystery

### Future Chapter Laws (from editorial ruling)

1. Beauty must now carry consequence. No ornamental beauty.
2. The protagonist must keep acting — from flaw, not wisdom.
3. Obianuju must become more alive, not more symbolic.
4. Kene must remain a man, not a device.
5. The social world must widen chapter by chapter.
6. The cold must remain implicit. Never write a cosmology lecture.
7. The Trance must remain messenger — precise location, present tense, one governing action, one new consequence.
8. The Voice must be withheld until it can alter the book.
9. Emotional register must vary. The reader needs humour, tenderness, appetite, ordinary irritation.
10. Every chapter needs an acceptance-gate sentence that could only belong to this author, this protagonist, this Obianuju, this town, this pressure, this book.

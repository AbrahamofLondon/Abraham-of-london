## Lock 1 — The protagonist's name

**Status: RESOLVED as a deliberate formal device (Option A).** Architecture v2 §B2 establishes he carries no proper name to the reader; he is named only at the very end, by the Trance. This is not an undecided name — it is a chosen namelessness.

**The formal rule (how it works across 30 chapters):**
- Narration is **close third anchored to him**; "he" carries the intimacy, and the reader lives inside his interior continuously, so a proper name is never needed for closeness. (Chs 1–3 confirm this works — he is the most intimately known person in the book without a name.)
- He is addressed and referred to by **relational and functional epithets**, never a placeholder: "his wife's husband" by implication, "the man who leans toward cold things," and — load-bearing — by what the forces and the town call him (the Spirit Husband **refuses to name him**, which is characterisation, not a gap).
- **Naming pressure enters deliberately**, not accidentally: the Spirit Husband's refusal to name him (it grants no standing) is felt as a pressure; Obianuju's address to him (see Lock 2) is the one warm naming he receives; the Trance's final naming (Ch.28) is the payoff.
- **The true name is withheld until the Trance** (Ch.28) and is a function-word revealing which quality the world needed from him — mirroring the memoir's "preserve, not sustain" (the exact word as the site of truth).

**Action needed from owner:** confirm Option A stands (recommended — it is working and it is structural). No name is to be casually assigned.

## Lock 2 — Obianuju's diminutive (the intimate name)

In Chs 1–3 she addresses him directly without a fixed pet name ("you are holding me as though I might float away"), which works. **Owner to supply her endearment for him, or confirm direct address continues.**

Until the owner confirms, drafting continues with the full name "Obianuju" and direct address. The diminutive(s) should ideally enter no later than Ch.4 (The Speed of It), where the early intimacy is dramatised.

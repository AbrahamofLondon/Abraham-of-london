## 3. Length calibration note (flagged for decision)

The three drafted chapters run **below** the provisional 2,200–3,000/chapter target (avg ~1,532). This is deliberate first-pass density — the opening is compressed and controlled, which serves the "submission-grade opening" bar but, if held across all ten chapters, would land Movement I nearer ~16–18k than the 22–28k target, and the novel nearer novella length than the full literary length the market plan wants (`03` §5).

**Recommendation:** in the revision pass, expand Chs 1–3 toward 2,200–2,600 by deepening *scene* (more embodied incident in the town, more of Obianuju's ordinary life) rather than by adding cosmology or commentary. Do not pad. The density is an asset to keep; the length is a target to grow into through dramatised life, not exposition.

## 4. Protagonist — remains unnamed

Confirmed. The protagonist carries **no proper name** in narration across all three chapters (architecture §B2). He is rendered only through act, weather, and the relational frame (husband, the man who leans toward cold things). His defining condition — *a man who cannot fear death is defenceless against forces that do not need to kill him* — is **dramatised, never stated as thesis**: the near-drowning (Ch.3) shows the origin; the cold hour (Ch.1) and the journal (Ch.2) show the fearlessness becoming the reason he stays too long. The fearlessness is shown as **danger**, not admiration — explicitly named in-text as "not courage… only available."

His near-death is the novel's own invention (a **drowning**, fitting the coastal world), sharing only the *theme* of being "kept" with the memoir — **no transcription of the memoir's fire scene** (firewall).

## 5. How the three-force structure is introduced

Per the architecture's structural homes (§C), Movement I introduces *pressure*, not the full cosmology:

- **The Spirit Husband (CLAIM)** — introduced as the dominant force of Movement I, felt as **absence**: the unreachable hour (Ch.1), the room cooling on a schedule, and the patient "shape" in the trance that "counts him late." Never explained; never named outright; present as weather and as the creditor's arithmetic ("you are late").
- **The Trance (WARNING/messenger)** — introduced as a one-paragraph present-tense flicker (Ch.1) and a first sustained present-tense sequence (Ch.3). Morally neutral; it **shows** (the doorway, the long room, the woman's back), never threatens. The horror is accuracy, deferred to Movement II.
- **The Voice (INTIMIDATION)** — correctly **withheld**. It governs only the single mid-book hinge (Ch.16) and does not appear in Movement I. Its rarity is being protected.

No single exposition block explains all three. "Was that real?" is never asked. The supernatural is rendered as part of reality, not as special effect.

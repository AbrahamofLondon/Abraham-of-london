# Chapter 12: Social Consequence

The pepper woman had sold to that house for two years, and she had always sold to Obianuju, and so the first thing she did when the husband came instead — alone, with a basket he carried like a man holding someone else's child — was laugh.

"They have sent the wrong one," she said, to the market in general, so that the yam seller and the woman with the snails and a man buying nothing all turned to enjoy it. "Two years I deal with the wife, who knows the price of everything and the value of me, and today they send me the one who thinks tomatoes grow already in a tin."

"I know what a tomato is," he said.

"You know what it is. You do not know what it costs. That is a different education." She held one up, turned it in the light the way a jeweller turns a stone, and set it back on the heap with the small violence of a woman making a point. "Pick."

He picked. He picked badly, by her face, which she did not trouble to hide, because half of what the pepper woman sold was the performance of being robbed. He chose the soft ones, then the bruised ones, then — recovering — three that were merely ordinary, and she watched his hand move over her heap with the patience of a teacher who has decided to let the child fail in full view so the lesson will keep.

"Your wife," she said, "would have insulted my mother by now."

"My wife is more patient than I am."

"Your wife is not patient. Your wife is fast. There is a difference. A patient woman lets me cheat her slowly. A fast woman lets me know, in one look, that she has counted the tomatoes, counted my children, and decided how much of my foolishness she will pay for today." She bagged his tomatoes, added a fourth he had not chosen — the best on the heap, dropped in without comment, the tax the town levied on anyone it had decided to keep — and named a price that was robbery and a mercy at once. "Tell her the market misses her."

He paid. He paid too much and they both knew it and neither of them said it, because the overpayment was a kind of rent on a thing he could not have named: the right to stand here, in the noise and the salt and the smell of pepper sharp enough to make the eyes of strangers water, and be, for the length of a transaction, a man with an ordinary errand and an ordinary wife who was simply at home.

That was the warmth. He should have left inside it.

---

The market did not let a man leave inside anything. It held him the way water holds a swimmer, on every side at once, indifferent and total. A boy went past with a tray of bread on his head and a second boy went past hunting the first. Somewhere a radio found the brass it always found. The snail woman was arguing theology with the yam seller — whether a man who fed his family by cheating slightly was worse than a man who fed no one honestly — and enjoying it more than either would have enjoyed agreement. The town was doing what the town did, which was to live so loudly and so completely that the living itself became a kind of argument against everything quiet.

It was the pepper woman who turned it.

She did it kindly, which was worse. She leaned across her heap while he was settling the basket on his arm, and she lowered her voice to the register the town used for the things it most wanted to say loudly, and she said, "They tell me you have been walking."

"I walk. Every man walks."

"Not like that. They say you have been to the shrimp woman by the mosque wall. They say you have been to Mama Nneoma in the dark, at an hour when a man does not visit an old woman unless his house has frightened him." She was not unkind. Her eyes had the wet brightness of a woman handing over a thing she believes is a gift. "I am only telling you what reaches me. A thing that reaches me has already reached forty others. You understand. I sell pepper. Pepper and news travel the same road."

He felt the cold then, though there was no cold — only the recognition of it, the way you recognise a face in a crowd before you have placed the name. His visits, which had felt like a man moving at last, taking the town's knowledge into his hands one careful piece at a time, had been the whole time a man walking through a house of open windows, and the town had been at every window, and he had not once looked up.

"It is nothing," he said.

The pepper woman looked at him with something close to pity, which from her was almost tenderness, and said, "When a man tells the market it is nothing, the market knows the size of it exactly."

---

Kene found him at the edge of the fish tables, and Kene was not looking for him. That was the first mercy and the last. Kene had come to sell a mended creel and buy the particular twine he trusted, and he had a length of it already over his shoulder and a piece of smoked fish in his free hand that he was eating with the focused dishonesty of a man pretending he had not bought it to comfort himself.

"You," Kene said, around the fish. "You have a face."

"I have one face. The same as yesterday."

"No. Yesterday your face was a man's. Today it is a man who has heard something." He tore off another piece of the fish and did not offer it, which between them was a form of intimacy; Kene offered food only to people he was not worried about. "Let me guess. The pepper woman has been generous with you."

"She told me the town is talking."

"The town is always talking. The question is whether it has started talking about you, and from your face it has." He chewed. He looked, for a moment, less like a man eating and more like a man swallowing on purpose, putting something down inside himself where it would stop showing. "I warned you. I took you to the shrimp woman because you asked, and because I am a fool who cannot watch a friend stand in cold water and call it weather. But I took you in daylight, past forty windows, and now the town has done with it what the town does. It has decided your house is a story. And a story, in this place, is not a thing that sits quietly on a shelf. It is a thing the town eats."

"You are angry."

"I am eating fish so that I do not have to be angry in the market, where it would become its own story by sunset." He said it lightly, and the lightness was the work of a man holding a heavier thing flat. "I am not your witness, you understand. I felt your house go cold, once, with a plane in my hand, and I will tell that to you in a lane because you are my friend. I will not tell it to the pepper woman, or to Obiefuna, or to the standpipe. The moment I become the man who confirms the cold, I become part of the cold's address. I have my own house to keep warm."

It was the most Kene had ever protected himself in front of him, and it was fair, and because it was fair he felt the loss of it the way he felt every fair thing — as a door closing softly, with apology, but closing.

---

He noticed the fish table then, because Kene's eye had gone to it twice and a man learns to follow Kene's eye.

It was the last table in the row, set a little apart — not much, a hand's width, the kind of distance that no one decides and everyone keeps. The seller was a younger man, new enough that the others had not yet taught him where the good ground was, and his catch lay on the board in the full heat of the morning and it had not softened. Fish in that heat went slack within the hour; it was why the women shopped early and insulted anyone who came late. But this man's fish lay stiff and bright and faintly silvered, as though the morning had not reached it, as though it were keeping a colder hour of its own under the same sun that was making sweat run freely down the back of every other neck in the market. The seller kept touching it, turning it, frowning, blaming his ice that had run out two hours ago and could not have lasted in any case. The fish did not care about his ice. It stayed cold because something in it had decided to.

The other sellers had drifted. Not fled — drifted, over weeks, a table here and a table there, the way furniture moves in a house where no one will admit a draught, until the young man with the good cold fish sat at the end of the row with a clear arm's reach of empty board on the side of him that faced, if a man drew the line, the lane that led to the protagonist's house.

He understood, standing there, that the cold did not need to be in his house to be the town's. It had a public weather now. It had a table at the market. And the town, which could not name it and would never name it, had begun the slow, wordless work of keeping its furniture a hand's width clear.

---

Obiefuna came to him at the fish tables, which meant Obiefuna had been told he was there, which meant the news had reached the one man in the town who turned news into arithmetic.

Obiefuna owned the house. He owned three others, and a share in a lorry, and the kind of authority that comes to a man who holds the deeds to the places other people sleep. His name meant *may the homestead not be lost*, and his mother had given it to him in a season of dying, and he had spent his life making certain that no homestead of his was ever lost, ever empty, ever earning nothing. He was not a cruel man. He was a careful one, which in a hard season can wear the same coat. He came across the market in a clean shirt that he kept clean by never doing anything that would dirty it, and he greeted the fish seller by a name, and the pepper woman by a name, and the protagonist by his relation to a house — "the husband from the lane" — because Obiefuna filed men under their addresses.

"You are well?" Obiefuna said, and did not wait for the answer, because the answer was not the purpose. "Your roof held in the last rain? The back door — I am told the back door was repaired." He smiled. He missed nothing; he had heard about Kene's plane the same week it had planed. "Good. A house should be looked after. A house that is looked after keeps its people."

"It keeps them," the protagonist agreed, and felt the second meaning arrive under the first like the cold under the door.

"I will say a thing to you," Obiefuna said, "as a man who wishes you no harm, because a tenant who comes to harm is a tenant who leaves, and a house that empties is a house that talks." He took the protagonist lightly by the arm and turned him a few degrees, away from the fish seller, toward the open board, in the unconscious choreography of a man who arranges everything, including conversations, so that the valuable thing is on the inside. "There are houses in this town that have a name. You know this. Every town has them. A house gets a name the way a man gets one — from something that happened, or something people decided happened, which after enough years is the same. A house with a name is hard to fill. I have filled yours. Twice before you, I filled it, with people who did not ask the town for its memory, who paid and left quietly when it was time. I am asking you to be the third such tenancy. I am asking you to stop knocking on the town's memory, because the town has a long one, and it does not like to be reminded that it remembers."

He should have said yes. A wiser man — Obianuju, who had lived two years saying a kind of yes to exactly this, keeping the door closed alone — would have said yes, and gone home, and let the fish be cold at the end of the row, and let the name of the house stay where names of houses stayed, in the low register, in the drift of furniture, in the things the town agreed not to say.

But he had learned, lately, to move. And a man who has only just learned to move mistakes every motion for progress. He heard *I filled it twice before you* and what he heard was not a warning. It was a door. It was the shrimp woman confirmed, multiplied — *others, there were others, the town knows there were others* — and he wanted, with the whole of the appetite a quiet man develops for a fact, to open it.

"Who were they?" he said. He did not lower his voice. That was the error, and it was a small one, the size of a hand's width, the size of the empty board beside the cold fish. "The two before me. You filled it twice. Who left it, and what did they leave?"

The market did not stop. Markets do not stop. But a pocket of it cooled — not the air, the attention — the way a pond cools where a cloud crosses, and the pepper woman three tables down found something to do with her hands, and the fish seller looked up, and Obiefuna's clean face did a thing that was not anger and was worse than anger. It became closed. It became a ledger shut in front of a man who had asked to see the figures.

"You see," Obiefuna said quietly, "this is the knocking."

---

The cost did not arrive all at once. The town was too well-mannered for that. It arrived the way the cold arrived: from the edges, in the small withdrawals, until it had occupied the whole of the morning.

The pepper woman, when he passed her stall again on his way out, did not call him the wrong one. She did not insult his mother or tax him a tomato. She said, "Greet the house," in a voice with the warmth taken out of it and only the courtesy left, and the courtesy was the cruelest thing she had given him all day, because it was what she gave to strangers.

And there was a woman by the snail baskets, older, with a mouth that had been disappointed by the world and had decided to disappoint it back, who said — not to him, never to him, but at the volume the town used when it wanted a man to carry a thing home in his ears — "It is not the house. A house is only a house. It is who he brought into it. They are quiet houses until somebody brings the cold a wife." And she said a name. Not Obianuju's name; the town did not yet have that, and he thanked God it did not. She said *that one*, *the fast one*, *the one who keeps her face*, and turned his wife — who had counted the tomatoes and the snail woman's children in a single look, who had kept a door closed alone for two years so that exactly this would never be said — into a thing with a shape and a fault and a colour, a woman the cold had chosen because something in her had invited it.

He should not have answered. Honouring her boundary, her *do not make me public*, would have meant walking on, letting the lie sit in the air where lies sat, refusing to feed it with his face. But she had taken Obianuju and made her small to make the warning easy, the one thing Kene had refused to do in a lane a lifetime ago, and the refusal Kene had taught him by example deserted him exactly when he needed it, and he stopped, and he turned, and he said, too loud, in front of the baskets and the drifting tables and the cold bright fish, "You do not know her."

Which told the market that there was a *her*, and that she was his, and that he would defend her, which is to say that there was something to defend.

The snail woman smiled, because he had given her the only thing she had been fishing for, which was confirmation. "No," she agreed, pleasantly. "I do not know her. That is the safest way to know a woman like that."

---

Kene caught him at the market's edge, where the noise thinned and the lane began, and Kene's hand on his arm was not gentle.

"You answered her," Kene said. It was not a question. "I watched you do it. You took the thing she was guessing at and you handed it to her warm."

"She called Obianuju — "

"I know what she called her. The whole market knows what she called her, and now the whole market knows you flinched, which means there is a wound, which means there is a place to push." Kene let go of his arm. He looked tired in the way he had looked tired in the lane after the door, tired *for* him, which was the worst of Kene's angers because it had no exit. "You have spent months learning to move. I was glad of it. I told you to cross the road and you would not, and I thought, the day he finally crosses a road, it will be the saving of him. But you have learned to move the way a man learns to swing a machete in a crowded room. Movement is not the same as aim."

"I could not let her stand there and — "

"You could. You should. The standing-there was Obianuju's to bear, and she had built a whole life out of bearing it quietly, and you have just told the town there is a thing she cannot bear, which is the one fact she spent two years keeping from them." Kene shook his head. "She told you to stop trying to save her. This is what she meant. Not the cold. This. The market. The saving that makes a private grief into a public name."

He had no answer. The fish at the end of the row would be cold until the seller gave up and carried it home to a family that would eat it and not understand why it had kept so well. Obiefuna would fill the house a fourth time, someday, with someone who did not ask. The pepper woman would sell to him for the rest of his life with perfect courtesy and never again call him the wrong one. And somewhere behind him, in a lane that had been ordinary that morning, was a house that had stopped being private between the time he left it with an empty basket and the time he would return to it with a full one.

"Go home," Kene said, and the gentleness came back into him at the last, because Kene could not stay hard at a friend any longer than he could stay angry at wood. "Go home and do not tell her what they said. She will know. She always knows. But do not be the one who carries it to her. You have carried enough into that house this morning."

---

He walked home the long way, which in that town was the same length as the short way, and the lane received him differently than it had sent him out.

It was nothing he could have sworn to. A greeting that came a half-beat late from a neighbour mending a stool, and did not, as it always had, become a small conversation about the weather and the price of kerosene. A woman at her own gate who lifted her chin in the way that is a greeting and not a welcome. The standpipe congregation did not stop its argument as he passed, but it bent — he felt it bend, the way a field of grass bends from a thing moving through it — and a word he could not catch went under the ordinary noise the way the cold went under the door.

He reached his own gate. The basket was heavy on his arm with tomatoes he had bought badly and a fourth he had not earned, and the salt of the market was drying on his hands, and the door of the house stood as he had left it, a little open, the no-colour wood holding the heat. He had wanted a witness. He had wanted, for months, to stop being a man alone with a cold he could not prove, and that morning the town had given him a hundred witnesses, and not one of them was on his side; the town had been glad to feed him and would be just as glad, now, to feed on him and on the woman whose name it did not yet have but had already begun to sharpen toward.

Through the front window he saw her. Obianuju was standing in the room, not in the chair she used during the hour and not in the chair she never used, only standing, in the middle of the floor, with her back half to the window, very still — and for one cold instant he could not tell whether the hour had her or whether she had simply heard him at the gate and stopped, the way a person stops when the thing they have been dreading arrives at last and turns out to be only their husband, home from the market, carrying the proof of where he had been and what it had cost on his face for anyone, even a wife who had asked not to be made public, to read.

She turned and looked at him through the glass.

She had not gone to the market in two days. She did not need to have gone. He watched her read the basket, and the late hour, and the set of his shoulders, and his face, which had never in his life been able to keep a thing, and he watched her understand — before he had crossed the threshold, before he had said a word, before he could decide which mercy to attempt — that he had done the one thing she had asked him not to do.

The door was open between them.

He had opened it himself, in a market, in front of forty windows, and there was no plane in the world, and no friend with hinges under his arm, that could hang it shut again.

# Chapter 16: Leave the Door Alone

The rent book was still on the table in the morning, where Obianuju had not put it and he had not moved it, and beside it was the heel of yesterday's bread, and the two things sat together in the ordinary light — a deadline and a breakfast — as though the house could not decide which kind of day it was going to be.

He had done the work Mama Agnes gave him, which was no work. He had been in the house, quietly, the way a wall is in a house, holding something up without asking the room to admire it, for one whole evening and the night after it. He had not offered Obianuju anything. He had not explained the not-offering. He had folded his own shirt and put it where it belonged and slept on the near side of the door without arranging to be found there. It had cost him more than any action of his life, the being-no-use, and the cost had bought a strange thin quiet between them, not warmth, not the old turning-toward, but a truce — the kind two people keep when both are too tired to fight and neither has surrendered.

He thought, that morning, that he had learned the lesson. That was the danger. A man who thinks he has learned the lesson has stopped watching for the next one.

---

Kene came before the rain, with a bottle of palm oil he had not been asked for and the particular face of a man delivering a thing in order to be allowed to look at another thing.

"From the woman at the mosque end," he said, setting the bottle on the table beside the rent book, glancing at the rent book, saying nothing about the rent book. "She owed me. I do not need oil. You do. A house should not run out of oil in the same week it runs out of everything else." He looked at the bread heel. "You are eating yesterday."

"I am eating what there is."

"That is the same sentence a poor man and a holy man both say, and only one of them means it." He did not sit. That, the protagonist noticed, was new; Kene had always sat. "I will not stay. The rain is coming and I do not — " he stopped, and rearranged it into something he could say " — I have a roof of my own to be under when it breaks." But they both heard the first version, the one he had not finished, the one that ended *I do not like to be in this house when the weather changes,* and Kene's not-finishing it was a kindness and a confession at once.

He left the oil. He took nothing. At the gate he turned, the way he always turned, and the protagonist waited for *if it gets worse, come* — the old refrain, the open road between two houses — and Kene opened his mouth to say it and did not. He looked at the sky instead, and said the rain would be heavy, and went, fast, ahead of it.

The not-saying of *come* was the loudest thing anyone had said to him all week.

---

For an hour after Kene went, the house tried to be a house.

Obianuju did the things the coming rain asked of a house. She checked the seam of the roof where it had wept last season. She moved the good clothes off the floor and onto the high shelf. She set a pot under the place over the back room where the iron had never sat true. He watched her do it and did not help, and the not-helping was the work Mama Agnes had given him, and it was the hardest hour of labour he had ever not performed. Twice his body started toward a task — the shelf was high, the pot was heavy — and twice he made himself sit, hands open on his knees, and let her reach and lift and carry. Each time she felt him not-help and said nothing, and the saying-nothing was, he thought, the first wage his stillness had ever earned: she let him be useless without making him pay for it, which was the nearest thing to forgiveness the afternoon could hold.

The rent book sat on the table through all of it. Neither of them moved it. It was Mr Stephen's clock, and it kept its time by not keeping it, and the bread went stale beside it, and the ordinary objects of an ordinary house arranged themselves around the one object that was counting. The house held its breath. A man and a woman moved through it carefully, not touching, keeping a truce that neither had signed, waiting for a weather that had not yet come.

The rain did not break. It gathered. The light went the colour of a held breath, and the air thickened the way it thickened before the long rains began their season in earnest, and the town outside drew in its washing and its children and its small loud business, and the lane went quiet under the weight of the weather that had not yet arrived. Mr Stephen's deadline was made of exactly this. *Until the rains break.* The sky had begun to keep the appointment.

Obianuju moved to close the back shutter before the wind could find it. She did it the way she did everything, without announcement, lifting the bar, and she went toward the back of the house, toward the kitchen yard and the no-colour door, the door she watched in the hour, the door she had closed against him and told him not to follow her to.

And he stood up to help her.

That was all. It was the smallest thing. A husband stands to help his wife with a shutter before the rain. In any other house in the town it would have been nothing, would have been love in its most ordinary coin. He stood, and he took a step toward the back of the house, toward the door, because there was a task and his whole nature was a hand reaching for a task, because being no use had become unbearable in the exact moment the weather gave him something to *do* — and because, under the helping, in the place he did not look at directly, there was the other thing, the thing that had been growing in him since she shut the door: the need to see what was behind it. To go through. To stop standing on the warm side of his own life. To act.

He took the step.

And the room changed temperature.

Not the way it changed in the hour. This was faster, and it was aimed. The cold came in not low along the floor but all at once, from the centre, the way a held thing is let go, and it stood between him and the back of the house like a body, and he understood — the way he understood things in this house, without being told — that it was not the weather, and that it had been waiting, all the quiet evening and the quieter night, not for the rain.

He stopped. His heart kept its plain time, because it did not know how to do otherwise, and he was not afraid, because the door through which fear came to other men had been walled up in him in the brown water thirty years ago, and that — he would understand only later — was the whole of the trap. A frightened man would have stopped and stayed stopped. He stopped, and felt the cold, and was *interested* in it, and his interest leaned him forward again toward the door.

Then the room spoke.

"Leave the door alone."

It was plain. That was the first and worst thing about it. It did not boom and it did not hiss and it did not arrive on a wind. It was said at the volume a person uses across a kitchen, in the flat tone of a man telling another man a fact about a tool — *leave the door alone, it sticks, you will break the latch* — and it came from no direction, from the room itself, from the air a half-step behind him and the cold a half-step in front of him at once, and there was no one in the house to have said it but Obianuju, and Obianuju was at the back, by the shutter, and had not spoken, and he knew her voice the way he knew his own breath, and this was not her voice.

It was not a voice at all, the way he had always meant the word. It used no throat. But it had said four words in plain order and he had understood every one.

He should have gone cold himself. Instead something in him — the thirty-year-old certainty, the accounting, the conviction that he had been kept *for* a reason and that here, at last, was the reason arriving to address him — something in him rose to meet it, almost glad.

"You spoke to me," he said.

He said it out loud. To the room. And he heard, in his own voice, the terrible thing: not fear, not even defiance, but a kind of relief, the relief of a man who has been told for weeks that he is no use and has just been informed, by the thing itself, that he is a factor after all. He had been spoken to, and his whole starved usefulness had stood up inside him and called it honour.

"I am not afraid of you," he said — and took the step.

---

"Don't."

This time it was her voice. She had come back into the front room without his hearing her — she always came back without his hearing her — and her hand was on his arm, not gripping, the way she rested a hand on the door to remind it whose hand decided, and her face was not afraid either, but hers was the not-afraid of a person who has known a thing for a long time and made her peace with the size of it, which is a different country entirely from his.

"Do not answer it," she said, low and fast. "Do not tell it what you are or are not. It did not ask. It is not interested in your courage. You think it spoke to you because you matter. It spoke to you because you were about to cost me something, and it would rather warn you than collect." Her hand tightened, once. "Sit down. Take your hand off the idea of that door."

"It spoke, Obianuju. It is real, it is here, I can — "

"You can nothing." Not unkind. Exhausted, and exact. "There is no *can* in this. That is the thing you have never been able to hold. You are standing in a doorway shouting at the rain, and the rain has just done you the courtesy of telling you, in words, that it is rain. And you have heard the words and decided they make you a man who can stop the weather." She did not let go of his arm. "I have lived with it speaking for longer than you have lived in this house. It does not speak often. When it speaks, it is not to frighten me. I am past being frightened. It is to tell me the price of the next thing. And it has just told me the price of you going through that door, and the price is not yours."

He looked at her. The cold stood in the room around them, patient now, no longer aimed, having said its plain thing and gone back to being the climate it had always been — and there was the distinction his whole body finally understood: the cold was the house, the cold was the lease, the cold had been here before all of them and would be here after; but the cold had never, in two years, *said* anything. The cold claimed. The thing that had spoken was not the cold. It was the cold's plainer, colder cousin, the one that did not need to occupy a room because it could simply name what the room would cost.

"Then let it take the price from me," he said. "Whatever it is. I am not afraid to pay. I have never been afraid of anything. Let me be the one who—"

And the room spoke its second thing, and its last, and it was so quiet and so reasonable that he carried it for the rest of his life exactly as it was said, without the mercy of being able to remember it wrong.

"Your courage is not the price," it said. "She is."

---

Then the rain broke.

It came down all at once on the iron roof, the long-rains' first real fall, loud and ordinary and enormous, the sound the whole town had been waiting for, the sound that meant the season had turned and the deadline had begun, and under it the cold withdrew to the edges of the room the way it always did, unhurried, territorial, sure of its lease, and the air came back to the temperature of an ordinary wet afternoon, and the bottle of palm oil sat on the table beside the rent book and the heel of yesterday's bread, and nothing in the room looked as though anything had happened in it.

That was the worst of it. The room let go of the moment as completely as it had held it, and went back to being a room, and a man could have walked in from the lane and seen only a husband and a wife standing close together by a table on a rainy day, his hand at his side now, her hand no longer on his arm, the two of them not touching, listening to the rain.

He understood, standing there, that he had been wrong about what had just happened to him in the deepest possible way. He had thought, when the room first spoke, that he had been singled out — addressed, reckoned with, finally made to matter. He saw now that being addressed was not honour. It was exposure. The thing had not spoken to him because he was strong enough to be worth speaking to. It had spoken to him because he was the soft place, the loud man, the fool with the unwalled hands, the one most likely to do the very thing that would make her pay — and it had told him so plainly, the way you warn a child back from the lip of a well, not because the child matters to the water, but because the drowning would be loud and inconvenient and is easily avoided if the child can only be made to understand one simple rule.

Leave the door alone.

He had spent his whole life unable to be frightened, and had carried the inability like a gift, like a proof of his keeping, and in a single rainy afternoon the thing in his house had shown him the precise shape of the curse inside the gift: that a man who cannot be frightened cannot be deterred by the one threat that would stop a wiser man — the threat to himself. So the thing had not bothered to threaten him. It had reached past him, the way everyone in the book had been reaching past him for weeks — the town, Mr Stephen, even Mama Agnes — and put its hand on the one thing he *could* be frightened by, the only thing his walled-up fear had ever let back through the wall, and it had named her, and it had been right to.

"You see now," Obianuju said. She was not looking at him. She was looking at the rain through the window, the long grey ropes of it, the season arriving on Mr Stephen's schedule. "Why I shut the door. Why I told you not to follow. Why I do not let you help. It is not that I do not want your help. It is that your help has a price and the price is not charged to you. You have been trying for weeks to find a way to pay. There is no way for you to pay. You are not the kind of money it takes."

"Then what do I do." It was not the old question, the one that wanted a task. There was no wanting left in it. It was the flat question of a man who has finally run out of the thing he ran on.

She turned from the window. And she did the thing the Voice could not do and the cold could not do and he had never learned to do: she gave him, across the whole ruined evening, the truth, plainly, as a gift and not a weapon.

"You hold still," she said, "and you let me be the one who knows the price. That is not nothing. It is the hardest thing I will ever ask you, and you will fail at it, and you will try again, because you are not made to hold still while someone you love stands nearer the water than you do. But it is the only door left that you are allowed to open." She picked up the bottle of palm oil and the bread, ordinary things, and carried them to the shelf where they belonged, restoring the room one small object at a time. "Now help me bring in the rest of the washing. That much you are allowed."

He went to help her with the washing.

And the rain came down on the roof, ordinary and enormous, and somewhere under it the cold kept its lease, and the thing that had spoken did not speak again, and the not-speaking was not relief. It was the silence of a debt that has been named and not yet called, sitting in the house between them like a third resident, quiet, patient, and entirely sure that the man who could not be frightened would, in time, reach for the door again — because that was what he was, and it had all the rains in the world to wait.

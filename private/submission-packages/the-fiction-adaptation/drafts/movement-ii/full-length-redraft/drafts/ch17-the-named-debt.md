# Chapter 17: The Named Debt

The rain stopped in the night and left the morning rinsed and ordinary, and the first thing he saw when he woke was the rent book on the table, exactly where it had been, dry, square, patient, with a bead of damp on the wood beside it where the roof had let the night in.

The Voice did not speak in the morning. It had not spoken since. That was the new fact of the house, and it was a stranger fact than the speaking had been: a man can brace against a thing that speaks; there is nothing to set the shoulder against in a thing that has said its piece and gone quiet. The sentence sat in him where the cold sat in the room — not at the edges now, not on a schedule, but in the centre of everything, in the grain of the table and the handle of the cup and the square dry corner of Mr Stephen's book. *Your courage is not the price. She is.* He did not repeat it to himself. He did not need to. It had stopped being a thing he remembered and become a thing he lived inside, the way a man lives inside a climate he has stopped describing.

Obianuju was already up. He heard her before he saw her, the small competent noises, and when he came into the front room she was on the low stool under the place where the back-room roof met the wall, looking up at the seam that had wept in the night, with the catch-pot in her hands and an expression he knew: the expression of a woman pricing a repair.

"It has opened," she said, not turning. "The seam. It wept last year; this year it has decided to bleed. It will not hold the long rains." She set the pot under the worst of the drip, where a dark coin had spread on the floorboards in the night. "We will need the roofer before the week is out, or we will need new floorboards before the month is, and one of those costs money and the other costs more than money."

"I can look at it."

He heard the words leave him and heard, underneath them, the whole of his trouble, intact, unlearned: *I can look at it. I can climb. I can do the thing. Give me the thing to do.* The seam was over the back room. The back room held the no-colour door. To get to the seam from inside a man would stand on a chair in the very doorway he had been told to leave alone, his hands at the height of the lintel, his head bent under the place where the hour lived.

Obianuju did turn then. She did not say the obvious thing, which was *no*. She did better and worse than that: she looked at him until he heard his own offer land in the room and curdle, until he understood, without her spending a word, that he had just volunteered, brightly, helpfully, to climb a chair in the one doorway in the world he was forbidden, and to call it fixing the roof.

"You can look at it," she said. "With your eyes. From here."

For a while they let the morning be a morning. The lane outside was loud with recovery — the particular cheer a town allows itself the morning after the first big rain, when the dust has been beaten down and the gutters run and everyone has survived a thing that happens every year and is greeted every year as a deliverance. A neighbour called that her hens had drowned, and was answered by another that hens were fools and had always been fools and that God had made them so to teach women patience. Somewhere a child was being scrubbed and objecting to it at the top of its considerable lungs. The world, having heard nothing in the night that he had heard, went cheerfully on, and the cheer came in through the window and lay in the room without warming it.

Obianuju made tea and set his where she always set it — and then, halfway through the setting, her hand slowed, and he watched her remember, and watched her decide, and watched her finish the setting anyway, putting the cup down in front of him, to him, with the small push of two fingers she had withheld for a week. She did not look at him while she did it. It was not forgiveness. It was older and plainer than forgiveness: it was habit, two years deep, surviving under the new terms the way grass survives under a stone, finding the one edge of light. He drank the tea, and he said nothing about the push, because to name it would have been to spend it. He let it be what it was — the smallest possible amount of their old life, kept alive by a tired woman who had decided, this one morning, not to let the thing in the house take her courtesy along with everything else it was taking.

---

Mr Stephen's word came mid-morning, before the heat, the way his pressure always came — not in person, this time, but folded under a stone on the gatepost, a half-sheet in a careful hand, the careful hand of a man who put even his threats in writing because writing was tidy and could be produced later if a thing were ever *discussed*.

*The rains have started,* it said. *Your roof is the oldest on the lane and I have not forgotten it. I will send Brother Paul, who roofs for me, before market day. He will want the inside as well as the out; a seam is mended from the wet side or it is not mended. See that the house is in order for him.* And then, at the foot, in the same even hand, the thing that was the whole of the letter: *A quiet house is easy to keep in repair. I am glad yours has gone quiet again. Let us keep it so.*

He read it twice. He read the kindness in it, which was real, and the leash in it, which was also real, and he understood that Mr Stephen had found, in an old man's roof, the one lever that married all his levers — the repair that the rains made urgent, the access that the repair made reasonable, the quiet that the access endangered, and the deadline that the weather had already started to count. Brother Paul would come, a decent man with a ladder and a bucket of pitch, and he would want the inside of the back room, because that was where the seam wept, and the inside of the back room was the one place in the house that could not be let to a stranger, and Mr Stephen knew none of why and all of that it was so, because he had filled the house before and a man who fills a house twice learns where its tenants will not let him stand.

"He wants the back room," the protagonist said.

"He wants the house quiet and full and his," Obianuju said, taking the half-sheet from him, reading it once, folding it along its own crease and setting it on the table beside the rent book, where the two papers lay together like a small archive of the ways a house can be owned. "The back room is only where the leak is. Mr Stephen does not want the back room. He wants me to have to choose between the floorboards and the door, and he wants the choosing to be loud, because a woman choosing loudly is a woman who can be discussed." She said it without heat, the way she said the things she had known longest. "He is not a wicked man. He is a man who has noticed that my husband makes my life into a thing the lane can hear, and he has decided to use the weather to do the same."

---

He held still all morning, and he held still loudly.

He could not have said when the holding-still turned into a performance of holding-still, because the turn was the most natural thing in the world and that was its danger. He sat in the chair by the window where she could see him not-acting. He folded his hands in his lap and then, remembering that folded hands were a bracing, unfolded them and laid them open on his knees where the openness could be observed. When the drip in the back room quickened and his whole body started up toward it, he made himself sit back down with a slowness that was meant — he caught himself meaning it — to be witnessed, so that she would see the size of what he was mastering, so that the not-doing would be entered somewhere as a doing, so that his restraint would be paid the wage his action had stopped earning.

He was doing nothing, and he was doing it at her.

Kene found him at it, because Kene came by before market day with the news that Brother Paul had been heard at the cooperage saying the lane house was on his list, and Kene took one look at the protagonist arranged in the chair like a man sitting for a portrait of his own patience and laughed, short and unkind, the laugh of a friend who loves you too much to let you get away with it.

"What is this," Kene said. "What are you doing."

"Nothing. I am doing nothing. It is the work I have been given."

"You are not doing nothing. I have seen men do nothing. My uncle did nothing for forty years and he did it lying down, with his mouth open, and not once did he arrange his hands for an audience." He came in, uninvited, and sat, and took from his pocket a twist of groundnuts and began to eat them with the focused calm of a man who intended to stay long enough to be a nuisance. "You have found a way to make sitting still into the loudest thing in the room. You could not be useful, so now you are being *restrained*, and you want it counted. It is the same hunger, brother. You have only changed its coat."

"Mama Agnes told me to hold still."

"Mama Agnes told you to be a wall." Kene cracked a shell. "A wall does not look around to see if anyone has noticed it holding up the roof. A wall holds up the roof and lets the roof take the credit. You are not being a wall. You are being a man pretending to be a wall and hoping someone writes it down." He held out the groundnuts; the protagonist did not take them; Kene shrugged and ate the offered ones himself. "I will tell you the thing you do not want to hear, because that is what I am for. Doing nothing is not the same as wisdom. Sometimes a man does nothing because it is wise. Sometimes he does nothing because he is afraid, and calls it wise, and the two look exactly the same from outside, and only one of them is honest about which it is. You have to know which yours is. And you cannot know by sitting prettily. You can only know by what you do when no one is watching — including her."

He ate the last of the groundnuts and folded the paper twist into a smaller and smaller square, the way he did with his hands when his mouth had said a true thing and wanted somewhere to put the discomfort.

"I would have done your roof," he said. "But I hear your landlord is sending his own man. Of course he is. A landlord who sends his own roofer instead of letting a tenant call his own friend is not thinking about the roof. Do not let his man past the step." He stood, and brushed the salt from his trousers. "And do not hear me wrong and go and do something brave about it and make her pay; if you do that and tell anyone Kene sent you, I will deny you in the market in front of children. There is a third thing, between the brave fool and the still fool. It is the hardest thing, it has no audience and no name, and you will never once be sure you are doing it right, because the only person who could tell you is the one you are not allowed to ask." He went to the door, and turned, the old turn, and this time — because it was day, because the rain had passed, because a man can only stay frightened of his friend's house for so long before loyalty wins the argument — he finished the sentence he had not finished the day before.

"If it gets worse," he said, "come. I will be under my own roof being a coward about my own troubles, and there will be room."

---

He went to Mama Agnes, because the thing Kene had said needed a harder voice than Kene's to finish it, and because a man who has been told he is performing his own stillness will go looking for the one person who has never once been an audience for anything.

She was sorting seed, as she was always sorting seed, and she did not look up, and she nodded at the low stool, and he sat, and he told her — not all of it; he did not tell her the Voice; some things he had learned not to carry out of the house — that he had been holding still as she said and that Kene had told him he was holding still *at* his wife, and that he could no longer tell, himself, whether he had stopped acting out of obedience or out of fear or out of the wish to be seen obeying.

Mama Agnes let a bad bean fall into the lamp-light and considered it.

"I told you to keep your hands empty," she said. "I did not tell you they would come clean by being empty. Those are different things. A thief who is caught and made to drop what he took has empty hands. They are not clean hands. They are only empty ones, and he stands there showing you the emptiness as if the emptiness were innocence." She set the bad bean aside. "You have emptied your hands. Good. It is more than you could do a month ago. But you have begun to hold the emptiness up for your wife the way the thief holds it up for the constable — *see, I am not taking anything, see how I am not taking it* — and that is not the obedience I gave you. That is the old hunger, wearing the only shirt it has left." She looked at him then, the calm look, the one that had stopped needing the categories of soft and hard. "Empty hands are not clean hands. Clean hands are hands that have stopped counting what they hold and what they do not. You are still counting. You are counting your own stillness like coins. Stop counting, and the hands will come clean on their own, and you will not notice the day it happens, because a man who notices his hands are clean has started counting again."

"How do I stop counting?"

"You do not. You do a thing so much harder than your own stillness that the stillness stops being interesting to you." She tipped the good seed into the cooking bowl. "Your roof is leaking over the one room you cannot enter. Your landlord is sending a man you cannot let in. Your wife is going to have to choose between her floor and her door, and she is going to choose without you, and you are going to have to stand in the house and let her choose and not turn the letting into a gift you give her. *That* is the work. Not the sitting. The standing-by while she does the dangerous thing, with your hands open and not on display. Do that, and you will not have time left over to arrange your patience for the lamp." She sent him away, again, with nothing in his hands, and this time he understood that the empty hands were the lesson and not the insult, and that understanding it was not the same as being able to do it.

---

Brother Paul came the next morning with a ladder and a bucket of pitch and the good cheer of a man who liked roofs better than people, and he set the ladder against the front wall and went up and was happy up there, tapping the iron, finding the lifted sheet, talking down to them about the wickedness of last year's nails. From the outside he could do most of it. But the seam that wept was where the back-room roof met the wall, and a seam, as Mr Stephen had written and as Brother Paul now said, cheerfully, through a mouthful of nails, is mended from the wet side or it is not mended, and the wet side was inside, under the lintel of the no-colour door.

"I will need to come in through the back," Brother Paul called down. "Five minutes. I will not disturb the lady."

And the protagonist felt the whole machinery of himself engage — the readiness, the wish to manage it, to go in first, to stand in the doorway and receive the roofer and so be the man who handled the dangerous threshold, to spend himself, finally, usefully, on the one thing that had a shape — and he felt his foot take the weight to rise, and over the top of all of it he heard, not spoken, not repeated, only present, the way the dry corner of the rent book was present: *leave the door alone.*

He did not rise.

He sat with his hands open and not on display — he checked them, once, and then made himself stop checking them, which was the whole of the war — and he let his wife answer the roofer, because it was hers to answer.

"You will not come in through the back," Obianuju said, up to the man on the roof, pleasant and final. "You will pitch it from above as well as it will take, and where it still weeps, it will weep into a pot, and I will empty the pot. The floor is mine to lose. I would rather lose a floor than open that room to the lane." She said *the lane* and not *you*, which left Brother Paul his dignity, and Brother Paul, who roofed for Mr Stephen and had learned from Mr Stephen the art of not asking, said that the lady knew her own house, and pitched it from above as well as it would take, and came down, and was paid, and went, and the seam, where his pitch could not reach the wet side of it, went on weeping, slower now, into the pot Obianuju had set.

---

Mr Stephen would hear that the back room had not been opened. He would hear it the way he heard everything, and he would draw from it the thing he had been fishing for — not access; he had never wanted the back room — but the fact, now confirmed and confirmable, that there was a room in the lane house that its tenants would lose a floor before they would open. A house with a room like that is a house with a name. He had his lever now, cleaner than before, and he had got it without a hard word, and the rains would do the rest of his arguing for him, and somewhere across the town he was, the protagonist did not doubt, already turning the fact under his two fingers, squaring it, deciding when to set it on the gatepost.

That was the cost of the floor she had chosen to lose. She had known it when she chose. She had chosen it anyway, with her eyes open, in five seconds, up to a man on a roof, while her husband sat on his hands and learned what it was to let her pay a price he had been told he could not pay for her.

He did not thank her. He had learned that much. To thank her would have been to make her choosing into a gift to him, a thing he had received, a thing that therefore passed through his hands and so belonged, a little, to him. It did not belong to him. None of it had ever belonged to him; that was the whole of what he had spent the season failing to learn and was now, in a chair, with his hands open, beginning to. He only watched her cross the room, when the roofer had gone, and put her hand on the no-colour door — her door, the one he was forbidden and she was not — and open it, calmly, the way a woman opens a door in her own house, and go through into the back room where the seam wept into the pot, to deal with the ordinary water in the ordinary way that was hers and not his.

The door stood open behind her. He could see, from the chair, the grey wet light of the back room, the pot, the dark coin on the floor. He could have crossed to her in four steps. Every instinct he had been born with and every instinct the brown water had put in him stood up at once and told him to cross, to help, to be in the room with her, to share the danger she was standing in alone.

He stayed in the chair.

He stayed in the chair with his hands open and unwatched, wanting nothing to be noticed, wanting only — for the first time in his life, cleanly, without an audience, without a wage — for her to be safe in a room he was not allowed to enter, and he understood, sitting there, that this was the shape of the rest of it: not the grand fidelity he had always imagined, not the man who walks into the water for the sandal, but a man in a chair, holding still while the woman he loved opened the door he had been forbidden and dealt with the weather, and the keeping-still cost him everything the walking-in never had, and bought nothing, and was witnessed by no one, and was, at last, the truest thing he had ever done.

The pot took the water, drip by drip.

The rent book sat dry and square on the table, counting.

And the debt the room had named and not yet called sat with him in the chair, quiet, unhurried, certain of its lease — sure, the way the cold was sure, that a man who could not be frightened would not be able to do this every day for the rest of his life, and that one day, out of love, out of his own unkillable need to be of use, he would rise from the chair and cross the four steps and through the open door, and that on that day it would not need to speak again, because she would already be paying.

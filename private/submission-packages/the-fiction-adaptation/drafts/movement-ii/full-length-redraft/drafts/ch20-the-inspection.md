# Chapter 20: The Inspection

Obianuju moved the cup first.

That was how the inspection began, before Mr Stephen arrived, before the gate clicked, before any man outside the marriage put his eyes on the floor. She took the cup from the table and set it on the shelf, not because the cup mattered, but because the note beneath it did. Mr Stephen's folded paper had sat there all night, dry, exact, waiting under an ordinary object. In the morning she lifted the cup, smoothed the note once with the side of her thumb, folded it smaller, and placed it inside the rent book.

Then she brought the rent book down from the high shelf.

The protagonist watched her do it and said nothing, which was not the same as not wanting to speak. The house was being arranged around a visitor. That was the pressure. Not hidden, not exposed. Arranged. The chairs were set where chairs always were, but more exactly. The mat by the threshold was shaken and returned with the faded edge turned inward. The packet of wedges Brother Paul had sent was moved from the no-colour door to the lower shelf, close enough to be found if needed, far enough not to announce why it existed. The stain near the board could not be moved. Obianuju wiped it once with a damp cloth and then stopped, because wiping a thing too much teaches the room where to look.

He understood that, and still nearly told her the cloth had left a darker ring.

"You see it," she said.

"Yes."

"Then do not point at it with your mouth."

He closed his mouth.

The board had clicked once at dawn under no one's foot. A small sound. Enough to make them both turn from different rooms. Since then it had been quiet, the way a person can be quiet after causing trouble, not from innocence but from satisfaction. The roof held. The pot stayed dry. The house looked, to a person who had not lived through yesterday, like a house after repair.

That was the problem.

It looked repairable.

"What do you need me to do?" he asked.

Obianuju was kneeling by the front threshold, brushing the mud line from the step with a short broom. Her right wrist moved better than it had the day before, but not freely. She had tied a strip of cloth around it, not as bandage, not as decoration, only to remind the hand of its limit. When he looked at it, she did not turn away this time. She let him see the cloth. Then she went back to the step.

"When he comes, you greet him," she said. "You do not explain the house to him. You do not apologise for the house. You do not offer him rooms he has not asked for. If he asks you a question, answer the question he asked. Not the one you are afraid he is thinking."

"And if he asks to see the wet side?"

"He will not ask first."

"How do you know?"

"Because he is correct."

She said it without admiration. Correctness, in Mr Stephen, had become a kind of knife. It cut without lifting its voice.

The protagonist took the second broom and swept the yard again, though the yard did not need sweeping. It needed something else, something no broom could perform: it needed to forget that a ladder had stood there, that neighbours had watched, that a room had refused repair and thereby become a fact other people could carry away in their mouths. He swept anyway. The work was small and permitted. It was also visible. He had begun to distrust visible work.

At the gate, Kene appeared just long enough to look at the yard, the chairs, the doorway, Obianuju's tied wrist, and the protagonist with a broom in his hand.

"Ah," Kene said. "The house has combed its hair."

"Do not start," the protagonist said.

"I have not started. I am only admiring the parting." Kene looked toward the lane, where nothing yet moved except a woman balancing greens on her head and the slow drift of damp heat. His voice lowered. "A polite man is harder to survive than a wicked one. A wicked man helps you know where to stand."

Obianuju came to the doorway with the short broom still in her hand.

"If you stay, you will talk," she said.

Kene looked offended by the accuracy. "I can be quiet."

"Not at the right size."

He accepted that with a small bow that made mockery serve obedience. "Then I will be in the lane pretending not to be nearby."

"Pretend far enough," she said.

He went. He was still visible two houses down, buying something he did not need from a woman with a tray, but he was no longer in the yard. That was Kene's obedience: imperfect, hungry, and probably the best available version.

The protagonist swept one final line of water toward the drain.

"Enough," Obianuju said.

He stopped while still wanting to improve it. That was the first work of the day.

---

Mr Stephen arrived exactly when he said he would.

He came without umbrella, because the rain had lifted an hour earlier and only the edges of his trousers showed the road. He wore a pale shirt with the sleeves rolled once, neither too casual nor too formal, and carried a narrow notebook in one hand. Not a ledger. Not the rent book. A smaller thing, private to him, where houses became facts a man could arrange. He greeted them from the gate before opening it, as though courtesy itself were a key.

"Good morning," he said. "I hope I have not come at a bad time."

There was no answer to that which did not give him something.

"Good morning," Obianuju said. "You came at the time you gave."

Mr Stephen smiled slightly. He had always liked exactness when it belonged to him. He seemed to appreciate it less when it answered him back.

The protagonist opened the gate.

That was his first error.

It was small, and ordinary, and a husband may open a gate for a visitor to his house. But this was not that house, and this was not that visitor, and Obianuju had not asked him to open it. Mr Stephen stepped through with thanks, and the yard, which Obianuju had arranged carefully as a boundary, became a little more welcoming than she had intended.

He saw it the moment after it happened.

Obianuju saw it too. She did not look at him. That was worse than a look.

"The roof has held?" Mr Stephen asked.

"It held through the night," Obianuju said.

"Good. Brother Paul is not a careless man."

"No."

"He said the seam was difficult."

"It was."

Mr Stephen looked up at the patched place. He did not go near the door. He did not look at the no-colour door first. That would have been vulgar, and Mr Stephen was never vulgar unless forced. He looked at the wall, the pitch, the place where water had run and stopped. Then he looked at the yard, clean in the way a yard is clean after too much sweeping.

"You have tidied well."

"It is a house," Obianuju said. "It receives people better when it is tidy."

"Some houses do." He wrote nothing. That was one of his powers. He could make a person feel recorded without opening the book. "May I see where the water came through?"

There it was. Not the room. The water. Not access. Responsibility. The question wearing the clothes of maintenance.

The protagonist almost answered. The sentence rose in him: *It came through by the back seam, but the floor is soft; we can show you from here.* It was a useful sentence. Reasonable. Protective. It would have made the route easier.

It would also have drawn a map.

Obianuju answered before he could turn usefulness into geography.

"You may stand at the front room threshold," she said. "From there you will see the stain. The room itself is not for entering today."

Mr Stephen did not react quickly. That was the courtesy of a man who knows quick reaction spends advantage.

"Not for entering because of the floor?"

"Because I have said so."

The answer was too plain to be rude and too final to be safe.

Mr Stephen looked at her tied wrist. He did not ask. He looked at the protagonist's hands. The rope marks were faint now but not gone. He looked at the lower shelf where the wedges sat wrapped in brown paper, their string retied badly by a man's fingers. Then, only then, he looked toward the no-colour door.

The room seemed to become more present because no one named it.

"Of course," he said. "A tenant knows her own arrangements."

Her.

The word landed softly and opened a floor beneath it. Not *your* arrangements, not the household's, not the plural form that would have let the protagonist stand beside her inside the sentence. Her arrangements. Her refusal. Her room. Her oddity, if oddity became useful later.

He felt himself move toward speech.

Obianuju turned the short broom in her hand once, bristles down, the way a person turns a key without using it.

He stopped.

Mr Stephen saw the stopping.

That was the trouble. A man can obey and still make the obedience visible.

---

They entered the front room in the sequence Obianuju allowed.

She went first. Mr Stephen followed. The protagonist came last and hated the correctness of it, because the sequence made him feel both excluded and protected, and he had not yet learned to accept protection without mistaking it for judgment. The front room was neither warm nor cold. It had chosen the more dangerous temperature: ordinary.

The stain near the no-colour door had dried into a pale-edged shadow. The floorboard did not click while they crossed. Of course it did not. Houses, like people, sometimes know when to behave in front of authority.

"There," Obianuju said, pointing with the broom handle, not her hand. "That is where the pot stood."

Mr Stephen stood where she had said he could stand, one pace inside the front room and no more. He bent slightly, not enough to be accused of peering, enough to see. His notebook remained closed. He had an inspector's discipline and a neighbour's manners. Together they were almost unbearable.

"The water came further than I expected," he said.

"It came as far as it came."

"And the board?"

"It speaks when it wants attention."

Mr Stephen gave the board a thin smile. "Many things do."

The protagonist disliked him for that, then disliked himself for recognising the sentence was good.

"Brother Paul left wedges," Mr Stephen said.

"Yes."

"He told me."

Of course he had. Decent people again, damaging with accuracy.

"He should not have troubled you with small things," Obianuju said.

"No trouble. Small things keep houses standing. Large things are often only small things neglected with confidence." Mr Stephen looked at the lower shelf. "May I?"

He meant the wedges.

Not the door.

Not yet.

Obianuju considered the request. It was reasonable. It was almost too reasonable to refuse, and therefore had to be weighed more carefully than a rude request.

"You may look at them," she said. "Leave them where they are."

Mr Stephen crossed two steps to the shelf. The protagonist's body tightened before his mind permitted it. Two steps brought Mr Stephen closer to the no-colour door than any outsider had stood in months. Not at it. Near it. Near enough that the air around the door seemed to hold itself flat.

The cold did not arrive.

That was worse.

It remained withheld, as though the room understood that a visible weather would help them. Ordinary air made them look unreasonable.

Mr Stephen lifted the packet with two fingers, looked at the wedges without unwrapping them fully, and set them down exactly where he found them. He did not touch the door. He did not need to. A correct man can make touching unnecessary.

"These will do for a few days," he said.

"A few days," Obianuju repeated.

"If the board is not lifted."

"It will not be lifted."

"Then the damp will stay under it."

"Some damp is better left under a board until the rain season passes."

Mr Stephen looked at her then with something like respect and something like warning. "That is true in houses. Not always in houses with tenants."

The protagonist spoke.

Not loudly. Not foolishly, if judged by the sentence alone. That was how the new failure came: dressed as adequacy.

"We will keep it quiet," he said.

Obianuju's face did not change.

Mr Stephen's did.

Only a little. Enough.

The word *we* had made a room of its own and put him in it.

"I am glad to hear it," Mr Stephen said.

There are sentences that reward the wrong part of a man.

The protagonist heard the reward and felt, with shame, a small warmth where the reward landed. He had been correct. He had represented the house. He had spoken in the language a practical man understood. Quietness. Maintenance. Responsibility. He had made the inspection easier.

He had also made the quiet a promise.

Obianuju set the broom against the wall.

The sound was small. It ended the room.

---

Mr Stephen asked to see the outside wall again.

Obianuju allowed it. Outside was safer, until it was not. They returned to the yard, and the rain began again while they were there, first a dusting of drops, then a thin sheet that made the repaired pitch shine. Mr Stephen stepped under the eave without hurry. The protagonist remained in the rain because moving under shelter beside him felt like accepting a fellowship he had not earned. Within half a minute his shirt was damp at the shoulders. Mr Stephen noticed and said nothing. Obianuju noticed and said, "Stand under the eave."

He obeyed.

Kene, still two houses down and failing at absence, laughed once from under a trader's awning.

Obianuju did not look toward him. "If he is close enough to laugh, he is close enough to carry water later."

The laugh stopped.

Even Mr Stephen smiled at that. It was the day's human mercy, and like most mercy in that house, it did not last long.

"The outside will hold," Mr Stephen said. "Brother Paul has done what he can."

"Then we thank him," Obianuju said.

"But if the board has taken water underneath, the room will need opening when the rains ease."

There it was, said at last, not as demand, not as threat, as maintenance.

The protagonist felt the yard narrow.

Obianuju did not answer immediately. She looked at the wall, at the shine of pitch, at the run of water from roof to ground. She looked like a woman judging weather, not a woman receiving a sentence. That was her authority: she did not hurry to become the shape another person's question made for her.

"When the rains ease," she said, "we will see what the floor requires."

"I am responsible for the structure."

"I know."

"If a board rots because I am not allowed to see it, that becomes difficult."

"For whom?"

The question was not sharp. That made it sharper.

Mr Stephen folded his hands behind his back, notebook still closed. "For all of us, Mrs..."

He did not finish with a name.

He had no name to finish with that belonged to the life inside the house. That, too, was part of the inspection: the way public forms reach for a private life and find no correct handle.

"For the house," he said instead.

"The house has survived worse than a wet board."

"Houses do survive. That is true. But not always with the same tenants."

He said it gently.

That was the blade.

The protagonist moved before he knew he had moved. One step, no more, toward the space between Mr Stephen and Obianuju, as though space itself had become something he could repair. Mr Stephen looked at the step. Obianuju looked at the step. Kene, from the awning, stopped being amused.

The step had answered before the man had.

"I beg your pardon," the protagonist said.

It was a bad sentence because it was not clear what he was apologising for, and in uncertainty a polite man may choose the version most useful to him.

"No offence taken," Mr Stephen said.

Of course.

Offence would have made him crude. He preferred record.

Obianuju said, "He is moving because the rain is coming in under the eave."

It was not true. It was kind. It was also an instruction. She had corrected his meaning in public and given him the smaller lie that would let the day proceed.

He stepped back under the eave.

The rain did not touch his face there. He wished, stupidly, that it would.

---

Mr Stephen opened the notebook then.

The smallness of it made the act worse. A large ledger would have been theatrical; a form would have been too official; a letter would have made the moment too easy to hate. The narrow notebook belonged to a man keeping his own arrangement. He wrote the date. He wrote something else the protagonist could not see. He wrote with a pencil sharpened carefully and kept too short for a careless hand.

"I will send Brother Paul again after three dry days," he said.

"For the outside?" Obianuju asked.

"For the board."

"No."

"Not to open the room today."

"No."

Mr Stephen looked up. "Mrs, I am trying very hard to say a reasonable thing."

"I hear you."

"Then hear the reasonable thing. I do not need to know your rooms. I do not need to make your house public. I do not want talk on the lane. Talk weakens a house faster than rain. But if a repair is needed and I do not see it done, I become the sort of landlord who lets rot begin under a tenant's floor. That is not my way."

It was beautifully done. He had made his pressure sound like care for his own character. He had put their closed room between him and the accusation of negligence, and now the room itself was injuring his correctness.

The protagonist understood then why cruelty would have been easier. Cruelty gives a person somewhere clean to stand.

Obianuju still did not yield.

"After three dry days," she said, "you may send Brother Paul to the yard. I will show him what the board has done from the threshold."

"The threshold is not the floor."

"It is the place I am offering."

"And if it is not enough?"

"Then it is not enough."

Mr Stephen's pencil rested on the page. Rain ticked on the patched roof. Somewhere beyond the gate, Kene said something to the trader that made the woman laugh and then hush herself. The house waited in the shape of a house, which is to say it waited by making objects hold still.

"Three dry days," Mr Stephen said. "Brother Paul will come. If he cannot assess it from the threshold, I will need the room opened before the next full rain."

The protagonist felt the sentence enter the house and look for somewhere to sit.

"A date," Obianuju said.

"A condition."

"Conditions become dates when men write them down."

Mr Stephen did not deny it. That was one of the few honest things about him.

He wrote again.

Obianuju watched the pencil move.

"Then write this also," she said. "The room was not opened today because I refused it. Not because my husband prevented access. Not because the house was disorderly. Not because your roofer failed. I refused. If there is discussion, let it begin accurately."

The protagonist looked at her. He could not help it.

She had taken the thing he wanted to shield her from and put her name under it.

Mr Stephen looked at her for a long moment. Then he wrote.

"Accurately," he said.

The word could have been respect. It could have been warning. In Mr Stephen's mouth those had become close relatives.

---

He left after ten more minutes of looking at things that were not the room.

The drain. The outside wall. The roof edge. The place where the ladder had stood. The yard the protagonist had swept too well. He praised the swept yard once, which was almost crueler than criticism, because praise made the labour visible and useless in the same breath.

"You keep a clean place," he said.

Obianuju answered, "A clean place is easier to accuse of dirt."

Mr Stephen smiled. "You do not make conversation simple."

"It has not made itself simple to me."

At the gate he paused, one hand on the latch. Kene, suddenly fascinated by vegetables two houses away, turned his back badly.

"Three dry days," Mr Stephen said.

"If the sky gives them," Obianuju said.

"The sky usually gives what it intends."

"So do houses."

He accepted that with the smallest bow. Then he left, stepping around the puddle at the gate rather than through it. Correct to the end.

For a while after he went, none of them moved.

The yard seemed larger with him gone, which was not the same as safer. The inspection had not opened the no-colour door. It had done something more practical. It had made the door a maintenance problem with a condition attached. Three dry days. Brother Paul. Threshold first. Room after, if threshold failed. Mr Stephen had not threatened them. He had made a schedule.

Kene came in only when Obianuju looked toward the lane and said, "If you are going to pretend badly, come and do it inside the gate."

He entered with both hands raised, carrying a small paper twist of groundnuts like a peace offering that had lost confidence.

"I brought food," he said.

"You brought noise wrapped in paper," she said.

"Also salt."

"Salt is honest."

He gave her the paper twist. She took it. That, for Kene, was absolution enough to make him almost foolish with relief, but he had the sense not to show too much of it.

"He is a dangerous man," Kene said.

"He is a correct man," Obianuju said.

"Those are worse. Dangerous men get tired. Correct men rest inside their correctness."

The protagonist leaned the broom against the wall. "I opened the gate."

Kene looked at him. "You did."

"I should have waited."

"Yes."

The answer was merciless and clean.

"I spoke for the quiet."

"You did."

"I made it a promise."

Kene shook the last groundnut into his palm. "You are becoming teachable. It is unpleasant to witness, but there may be fruit in it."

Obianuju did not laugh. She had gone to the no-colour door and stood near it, not touching it. Her tied wrist hung at her side. The board under the threshold stayed silent.

"Three dry days," she said.

No one answered.

Because what could be said? The house had been inspected and had passed exactly enough to fail later. The roof held. The room stayed closed. The correct man had left them with weather as a witness and dryness as a clock.

The protagonist looked at the packet of wedges on the shelf.

"Should I put one under the board?"

Obianuju shook her head.

"Not yet."

"When?"

She looked at the door then, and there was fatigue in her face, and calculation, and something older than either.

"When the house asks without witnesses."

Kene stopped eating.

That was the sentence that settled the afternoon.

---

Evening came without rain.

That was the first dry hour.

The town noticed. People came out of their houses with the suspicious cheer of those who have been given relief by a sky they do not trust. A woman shook water from a mat and held it up like a defeated argument. Somewhere a radio tried to play music through damp speakers and managed only rhythm and complaint. Kene left before dark, saying he had his own roof to mistrust, and Obianuju told him to mistrust it from under it, which pleased him more than it should have.

Inside, the house settled into the sound of things drying.

Cloth tightening on the chair. Water withdrawing from wood. The faint tick of the roof cooling. The board near the no-colour door did not click again. It did not need to. It had been given a deadline and could afford silence.

The protagonist carried the cup to the shelf, then brought it back to the table because Obianuju had not asked him to move it. He caught himself halfway through the second movement, cup in hand, ridiculous with care.

Obianuju saw him.

"Leave it," she said.

"Where?"

"Where your hand can stop learning by moving things."

He set it down on the table.

Mr Stephen's note remained inside the rent book now. Three dry days had entered the book. Not as threat. As condition. That was worse. Threats can be resisted. Conditions have to be lived around.

Obianuju went to the no-colour door at the close of the evening, as she always did now, not ceremonially, not with drama, only with the tired authority of someone checking the part of the house no one else was allowed to misunderstand. She did not open it. She placed her tied wrist flat against the wood and left it there longer than checking required.

He did not go to her.

That was not innocence.

It was only the correct distance.

When she lowered her hand, the board clicked once.

Softly.

Under no one's foot.

On the table, the rent book stayed closed around Mr Stephen's note, and outside the first dry hour became the first dry night, and the house began to count.

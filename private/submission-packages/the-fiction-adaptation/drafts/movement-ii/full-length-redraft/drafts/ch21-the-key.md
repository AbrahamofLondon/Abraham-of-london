# Chapter 21: The Key

The key was already on the table when he came into the front room.

Not the key to the no-colour door.

That was the first thing he knew, before Obianuju spoke, before he saw the rent book open beside it, before he understood that Mr Stephen had sent another message. The key on the table was smaller, rubbed silver at the teeth and dark at the bow where fingers had worried it for years. It belonged to the back lean-to, the room where the broken broom, the spare basin, the cracked stool and the old oil tin had been kept since before he arrived.

A harmless key.

That was why it frightened him.

Obianuju sat with both hands flat on either side of the rent book. The tied cloth was gone from her wrist, but the skin beneath it had kept the memory of pressure in a pale ring. She had not hidden it. She had not presented it. Her hand rested there with the same practical honesty as the key.

The morning had given them the second dry hour.

Outside, the lane was beginning to steam. Roofs released the rain back into the air. Damp clothes hung from lines with the defeated patience of flags after a bad ceremony. Someone had put beans on too early and the smell moved through the compound as if nothing in the world could be wrong before food. A goat stood in the middle of the lane and considered a puddle with priestly seriousness.

The house had become ordinary enough to be dangerous again.

"Mr Stephen sent word," Obianuju said.

The protagonist looked at the rent book.

"He sent it himself?"

"Through the boy from the provision shop."

"What did he say?"

She moved the book with two fingers, turning it so he could read the line she had marked with a dry stem from the broom.

Three dry days still stood there, in Mr Stephen's hand, because Mr Stephen was the kind of man whose handwriting made waiting look deliberate. Beneath it, in Obianuju's hand, was another line:

Brother Paul to return tomorrow before noon. Access needed for rear board assessment.

That was all.

No threat. No accusation. No cruelty. Only need.

"Rear board," he said.

"That is what he calls it."

"Not the room."

"Men who want a room begin by naming wood."

He read the line again. Brother Paul to return tomorrow before noon. Access needed. The words were so reasonable that he felt his own refusal become unreasonable inside him before he had even made it.

That was Mr Stephen's gift.

He could make a man ashamed of a boundary by giving it a practical name.

The protagonist reached for the key, then stopped before touching it.

Obianuju saw the stopping.

"It will not bite you," she said.

"I know."

"That is not what your hand believes."

He put his hand beside the key instead. The wood of the table was warm where the sun had reached it. The key was not. It lay in the sun and refused the room's temperature.

"This is not for him," he said.

"No."

"Then why is it here?"

"Because I am deciding what can be given."

She said it without weight. That made the sentence heavier. It was not argument. It was inventory. A woman in her own house, counting what may leave her hand and what may not.

He sat down opposite her. The chair scraped more loudly than it needed to. He hated that. Everything he did now seemed to inform the house of his size.

"You can refuse," he said.

"I know."

"You can tell him the room is not available."

"I know."

"I can speak to him."

She lifted her eyes.

There it was. The old mistake, dressed as service.

He had not meant to make it. That was no defence. The wrong offer had come to his mouth with the ease of water finding the old channel. I can speak. I can stand. I can explain. I can become the thing between you and the demand. He had almost offered himself again because himself was the only currency he understood.

Obianuju did not scold him.

That was worse.

"You can speak," she said. "And then he will know there is a thing I cannot say for myself."

The sentence did not strike him loudly. It entered under the rib and sat down.

"I did not mean it like that."

"You rarely mean it like that."

He closed his mouth.

She returned her gaze to the rent book. The page had absorbed the damp in the air and lifted slightly at one corner. Obianuju pressed it flat. Her hand did not tremble, but the pale ring at the wrist deepened as blood moved under it.

"He asked for a key?" the protagonist said.

"He asked for access."

"That is different."

"Not to a man with a tool."

The room held still around them. Not cold yet. Not warm either. The same dangerous ordinary from yesterday, made sharper by sun.

Obianuju picked up the small key and turned it over. "The lean-to shares the outside wall. Brother Paul can see part of the board from there if he removes the oil tin and the stool."

"Will that be enough?"

"For repair? Maybe."

"For Mr Stephen?"

"Nothing is enough for a man who has learned there is a door."

She set the key down again.

This was how she decided. Not with drama. Not with pleading. Not by asking the room to pity her. She moved facts the way another woman might move bowls before serving food: this here, that there, this not to be touched, that to be given because the larger thing must remain whole.

The protagonist looked toward the passage.

The no-colour door was not visible from where he sat, but he knew the angle of it now the way the body knows an old injury. He knew how the air thinned before it. He knew which board kept memory and which one performed innocence. He knew the distance from table to threshold in steps. He knew too much and not enough.

"If he says the lean-to is not enough?" he asked.

"Then he will say it tomorrow."

"And then?"

"Then tomorrow will have its own mouth."

He almost smiled. Not because it was funny. Because it sounded like Obianuju before all this had become named by rooms and repair notes. The woman who could put a sentence down beside a pot and make the pot look foolish for thinking it was the centre of the kitchen.

The almost-smile died before it reached his face.

There was a knock at the gate.

Not Mr Stephen's knock.

This one had appetite in it.

"If that is Kene," Obianuju said, "tell him the house is not receiving foolishness."

"And if he brought food?"

"Then foolishness may wait at the gate while the food enters."

The protagonist went out.

Kene stood in the lane holding a small calabash covered with cloth and a ring of keys hanging from one finger like an insult.

"Before you accuse me," Kene said, "these are mine."

"Why are you carrying keys?"

"Because the world is rude and has many doors."

"Obianuju said the house is not receiving foolishness."

"Did she mention soup?"

"She made a distinction."

"A wise woman."

He pushed the calabash into the protagonist's hands and stepped through the gate without being invited, then stopped, remembered himself, and stepped back out again.

The protagonist looked at him.

Kene looked at his own feet outside the gate.

"I am learning," Kene said. "Do not make a feast of it."

Obianuju had come to the doorway. For the first time that morning, something like amusement passed through her face and was gone before it could be claimed.

"Enter with the soup," she said.

"And the foolishness?"

"Leave some at the gate. You may bring the smaller portion."

Kene came in carefully, which made him look more suspicious than his carelessness ever had. He set his keys on the table before the protagonist could tell him not to. They struck the wood with a bright metal sound.

Everyone looked at them.

Kene followed their eyes and understood too late.

"Ah," he said. "Wrong music."

"Pick them up," Obianuju said.

He did. Quickly.

"I did not know."

"You know now."

He put his keys in his pocket with exaggerated gentleness, then saw that exaggerated gentleness was another kind of noise and stopped moving altogether.

The protagonist put the calabash on the floor beside the chair. The smell of pepper and dried fish rose into the room, absurdly alive. Hunger entered before dignity could refuse it.

Kene saw the small key on the table.

"That one is not yours," he said to the protagonist.

"I know."

"Good. Knowing is cheaper than being taught."

Obianuju opened the rent book toward him. "Mr Stephen wants Brother Paul to return tomorrow before noon."

"Of course he does."

"He wants access."

"Of course he does."

"You say that as if ordinary men do not ask ordinary things."

"Ordinary men ask ordinary things until they find the thing that is not ordinary. Then they become very ordinary about it. That is when you must be careful."

He sat only after Obianuju pointed to a chair. His eyes kept going back to the key, then away from it, then back. He was trying not to be dramatic. It made him look ill.

"What does that open?" he asked.

"The lean-to," Obianuju said.

Kene let out the breath he had been holding and then frowned at himself for being relieved.

"That is not nothing."

"No," she said.

"But it is not the door."

"No."

"Will Brother Paul know the difference?"

"Brother Paul knows tools, not doors."

"Sometimes tools teach men doors."

The protagonist heard the warning and disliked how much he wanted to use it as permission to intervene.

"I can be there," he said.

Both Obianuju and Kene looked at him.

He heard himself.

Again.

"Not to speak," he said, already making it worse. "Only to stand."

Kene rubbed both hands over his face. "You see? This is why I brought soup. A man should eat before embarrassing himself. Then at least shame has something to sit on."

"I am trying to help."

"Yes. That is the difficulty. If you were trying to harm, we could beat sense into you and go home."

Obianuju closed the rent book.

The sound was small. Final enough.

"You may be present," she said.

The protagonist turned to her.

"At the back," she continued. "By the water jars. Brother Paul may ask for cloth, wedge, basin, oil, stool. You will bring what I ask you to bring. Not what he asks. Not what Mr Stephen asks. What I ask."

"And if he asks me directly?"

"You will say, 'Ask Obianuju.'"

Kene nodded once. "Good."

The protagonist hated the relief that moved through him. A task had been given, and some part of him wanted to bow to it as if it were trust. But it was not trust in the way he wanted. It was boundary. It was a small place to stand where his usefulness could not grow teeth.

"Say it," Obianuju said.

"Ask Obianuju."

"Again."

"Ask Obianuju."

"Not like a prayer. Like a man who has been told where the answer lives."

He took the correction.

"Ask Obianuju," he said.

Kene leaned back. "Better. Still ugly, but better."

Obianuju stood and went to the shelf. She took down a cloth pouch, the one she used for market coins when she did not want to carry a full purse. From it she removed two coins, then a third, then stopped. She did not count aloud. She did not explain. She placed the coins beside the key.

"What is that for?" the protagonist asked.

"Brother Paul's time."

"Mr Stephen should pay."

"Mr Stephen pays for what belongs to him."

"The repair belongs to the house."

"The refusal belongs to me."

No one spoke.

The soup smell went on living in the room, indecent and necessary.

The protagonist wanted to say that the refusal did not belong only to her. He wanted to say that the room had made claims on both of them, that the cold had touched him too, that the board had clicked under no one's foot and still entered his body, that he had stood outside and obeyed the size of her need. He wanted, in other words, to make his injury a receipt.

He did not.

Not because he was clean.

Because he saw Kene watching him, and Obianuju not watching him, and understood that being unwatched was part of the test.

"I can pay Brother Paul," he said, and heard the wrongness of it before the sentence finished.

Obianuju looked at the coins.

"With what?"

He had money. Not much. Enough for a man to make a gesture and then require gratitude from the room for having made it. He saw that too.

"With money," he said, because cowardice sometimes tells the truth badly.

"Money is not the only thing people spend."

"I know."

"No," she said, not cruelly. "You are learning."

Kene got up then, opened the calabash, and began serving soup into the two bowls on the shelf as if appointed by a higher power to save them all from starvation and honesty.

"Eat," he said. "Nobody has ever become wise on an empty stomach. People say they have. They are lying or dead."

"There are two bowls," Obianuju said.

"Then the third person will eat from dignity."

"Dignity has no pepper."

"That is why it is unpopular."

The protagonist found himself almost laughing, then did not, then did. Once. A small sound, broken at the edge. Obianuju did not laugh, but she allowed the sound to remain in the room.

Kene handed her the first bowl. He handed the protagonist the second and kept the spoon, which he said was his ancestral portion. Obianuju told him ancestors had suffered enough without being blamed for theft.

For a few minutes, the house accepted food.

The key stayed on the table beside the coins.

It was possible, while eating, to see how a life might continue around impossible objects. A man could lift soup to his mouth. A woman could wipe pepper from her thumb. A friend could complain that the fish had been cut by a person with no respect for hunger. Outside, someone called for a missing chicken with the full dignity of public grief. The lane answered with no chicken.

Then Brother Paul came to the gate.

He did not knock like Mr Stephen. He tapped the metal twice with the back of something hard, then called, "I am not entering trouble. I am only asking whether trouble wants me tomorrow."

Kene closed his eyes.

"This is why repairmen are dangerous," he said. "They have no respect for atmosphere."

Obianuju stood before either man could move. She wiped her hands. At the doorway she became neither host nor supplicant. She became the person who knew where things were kept.

"Tomorrow before noon," she said from the threshold.

Brother Paul stood outside the gate with a coil of measuring line over one shoulder and a short-handled tool wrapped in sacking. His sandals were still wet from the lane. A streak of dried mud crossed one shin like a careless signature.

"Before noon is good," he said. "After noon the wood starts lying. Too much heat. It swells and pretends to be stronger than it is."

"You will look from the lean-to."

"That is what Mr Stephen said."

"That is what I say."

Brother Paul shifted the coil on his shoulder. He had the expression of a man trying to agree without promising that agreement would make the work sensible. "From there I can see some of it. Not all."

"Some is what is available."

"Some repair gives some peace."

"Then bring some tools."

Kene made a small approving sound despite himself.

Brother Paul glanced past Obianuju only once. His eyes found the table, the rent book, the key, the coins. He was not a curious man by temperament. Work had made him observant. That was worse. Curiosity can be shamed. Observation has already happened.

"There is a key," he said.

"There is a lean-to," Obianuju said.

He accepted the correction the way he accepted weather: not happily, not angrily, because work continued either way.

"If the back board is worse than it sounded yesterday, I may need to brace from inside."

"You may say so tomorrow."

"And tomorrow you may say no."

"Yes."

Brother Paul nodded. "Then I will bring the smaller brace first."

There was kindness in it, or something near kindness. Not understanding. Not agreement. A practical mercy: begin with the tool that asks for least.

The protagonist felt gratitude rise in him toward the man and distrusted it immediately. He wanted to reward anyone who made the demand smaller. He wanted to mistake a smaller demand for safety.

Brother Paul lifted the wrapped tool slightly. "I came for my line. I thought I left it here yesterday."

"You did not," Obianuju said.

"Good. Then I have not become careless."

"Not here."

He laughed once, startled by her accuracy, and backed from the gate. "Tomorrow then."

"Tomorrow," she said.

When she returned to the table, the key looked less like an object and more like a word several people had now pronounced without agreeing on its meaning.

Then the key cooled the table again.

The protagonist felt it before he looked. A small withdrawal, as if the sun had shifted though the sunlight on the wood had not moved.

Kene stopped talking.

Obianuju set her bowl down.

No one said cold.

That was one of the disciplines now.

The key to the lean-to lay there, harmless and unharmless, carrying a room it did not open. The protagonist understood suddenly that the wrong key could still change the house. It could invite a man to the back wall. It could let a tool enter from another side. It could make access look partial until partial access became proof that full access was only reasonable.

"No," he said.

Obianuju looked at him.

He had not meant to speak.

Kene put the spoon down.

"No what?" Obianuju asked.

He took time. That was new. Not wisdom. A delay in the old machinery.

"No," he said again, quieter. "Not because I decide. Because I was about to make this key small in my mind so I could bear giving it."

Obianuju's face changed. Not softened. Attended.

"And now?"

"Now I think it is smaller than the door and still not small."

Kene made a low sound. Approval or discomfort. With him they were often related.

Obianuju picked up the key.

The cold left the table when she lifted it. Or seemed to. The wood beneath it held a pale mark where the metal had lain, though there had been no moisture.

She closed her hand around it.

"Tomorrow," she said, "Brother Paul may enter the lean-to. He may remove the oil tin and the cracked stool. He may look at the rear board from outside the room. He may touch nothing inside the no-colour door. He may ask questions. I may refuse them."

She turned to the protagonist.

"You will stand by the water jars."

"Yes."

"You will bring only what I name."

"Yes."

"If Mr Stephen comes with him, you will not become larger."

That was the hardest instruction.

Kene looked away from him then, which was kindness.

"Yes," the protagonist said.

"If he asks you whether there is a key to the room, you will say nothing."

"Should I not say ask Obianuju?"

"Not for that."

"Why?"

"Because some questions become doors when answered."

She put the key in the cloth pouch with the coins. Then she tied the pouch once, loosened it, tied it again differently, and slid it into the rent book between Mr Stephen's note and the page where she had written Brother Paul's return.

"You are giving the key?" he asked.

"I am carrying it."

"Tomorrow?"

"Tomorrow I will decide again."

That was the first mercy in the chapter of the day: the decision had not pretended to be permanent.

It was also the cruelty. Tomorrow had been given work.

Kene stood to leave before darkness could make him useful in ways nobody had asked for. At the door, he paused and looked at the protagonist.

"A key is not trust," he said.

"I know."

"No. You like keys. Men like keys. A key lets a man feel chosen by a lock."

Obianuju made a sound that might have become laughter in another house.

Kene pointed at the protagonist with two fingers. "Do not fall in love with being allowed to hold what can open nothing."

"I am not holding it."

"Exactly. Improve from there."

He left with his empty calabash and his own keys kept silent in his pocket.

After he was gone, the house grew aware of evening.

The second dry hour became the second dry day not by declaration but by accumulation. Water disappeared from corners. The stain near the board turned lighter at the edge. The air around the no-colour door did not warm. The repaired roof held. The lane outside became passable. Barefoot feet from other houses ran through puddles and were called back by women who had surrendered to damp but not to foolishness.

Obianuju took the rent book from the table and placed it on the high shelf.

Then she took it down again.

The protagonist did not ask.

She opened it, removed the pouch, and took out the lean-to key. The coins stayed inside. She set the key on the table once more.

"Bring the red thread," she said.

He did not know which red thread.

He nearly asked.

Then he remembered the sewing tin on the lower shelf, the one with three needles tucked into cloth and thread wound around a card from an old packet of soap. He brought the tin and set it beside her.

She chose the thread herself. Good. He had learned enough not to be proud of bringing the right container.

Obianuju tied the red thread through the key's bow. One knot. Then another. Not decoration. Identification. Warning. A way to know if it had been touched by someone who did not know how she tied knots.

"For Brother Paul?" he asked.

"For me."

She placed the key in the centre of the table, not hidden now, not offered.

The thread made it look less harmless.

"Leave it there," she said.

"All night?"

"All night."

He looked toward the no-colour door.

"Will that be safe?"

"No."

There was no drama in the answer.

"Then why?"

She looked at him for a long time. The evening made her face both tired and exact.

"Because if I hide what may be given, I may begin to mistake it for what must not be given."

He did not answer.

She went to the door then, not the no-colour door but the front one, and opened it to the lane. Air entered, carrying damp, pepper, old rain, a call to evening, the smell of someone frying plantain too late. The house received it badly and received it anyway.

The key remained on the table.

Red-threaded.

Not taken.

Not refused.

Beside the closed rent book, under the second dry evening, waiting for the morning to ask what kind of access had been purchased by reason.

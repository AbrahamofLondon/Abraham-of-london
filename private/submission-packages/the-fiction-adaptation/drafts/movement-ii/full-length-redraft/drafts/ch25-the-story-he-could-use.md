# Chapter 25: The Story He Could Use

The story arrived before Mr Stephen did.

The protagonist heard it first from the woman who sold peppers at the edge of the market. She was weighing red peppers into a paper cone, and she did not look up when she spoke. That was how he knew she had been waiting to say it.

"I hear the old tenant has been found."

She said it the way she might have said the rain would come late this year — a fact about the town, requiring neither alarm nor proof. But her hands paused on the scale, and he understood that she was waiting to see whether the information fit.

He did not ask who had told her. He did not correct her. He took the paper cone and paid the price she named, and she looked at him then, the way the town looked at everyone — without appearing to look, without missing anything.

"They say she left something behind."

He met her eyes. "People leave many things behind."

She considered this. Then she nodded, once, and turned to the next customer.

He walked home with the sentence burning in him, and by the time he reached the gate he understood that the photograph had done what Obianuju had feared: the story had detached from the object and was travelling on its own.

He found Obianuju in the kitchen yard, wringing out a cloth at the basin. She did not turn when he entered, but she spoke.

"You have heard."

It was not a question.

"Yes."

"The woman who sells peppers?"

"Yes."

She wrung the cloth again, though it was already dry enough. "She heard it from the woman who sells dried shrimp. The woman who sells dried shrimp heard it from Brother Paul's wife. Brother Paul's wife heard it from Brother Paul, who heard it from Mr Stephen."

The protagonist stood in the doorway of the yard. The morning was ordinary. The basin was where it always was. The cloth moved through her hands with the same economy it always had. But the story was no longer in the room. It was in the market, in the lane, in the mouths of people who had never seen the photograph and did not need to.

"How much did Mr Stephen tell him?"

"Enough. Not everything. He said there was a photograph. He said it showed a woman who had lived in the house before. He said she had left suddenly, and that the house had not been the same since."

"That is true."

"It is true enough. That is what makes it dangerous."

She hung the cloth on the line. She did it without ceremony, the way she did everything, and for a moment the house was only a house and the story was only a story. Then she turned. Her jaw had closed the way it closed when she was about to name something she had been keeping separate.

"Mr Stephen will come today. Not for the room. For the story."

"How do you know?"

"Because the story is worth more to him now than the room. The room is a problem he has to fix. The story is a thing he can use."

---

Mr Stephen arrived in the early afternoon, not at the gate but at the side of the house, where the lean-to door faced the lane. He was standing where Brother Paul had stood, looking at the chalk line, and he did not turn when the protagonist approached.

"The line has moved again," Mr Stephen said.

It was not a question.

"The wood moved around the brace," the protagonist said.

"Brother Paul said it would."

"Yes."

Mr Stephen looked at the line for a moment longer. Then he turned. His shoulders had settled into the shape they took when he was carrying something he had decided not to put down.

"I have been thinking about the photograph," he said.

The protagonist said nothing.

"I have been thinking about the woman in it. I have been thinking about what it means that she lived here, and that she left, and that no one in the town seems to know where she went."

"She did not tell anyone where she was going."

"No. That is what I find interesting."

He paused. He looked at the lean-to door, then at the house beyond it, then back at the protagonist.

"I have a tenant in another property. A woman. Older. She has been there for years. She does not cause trouble. She pays on time. She keeps to herself. But she has a photograph. A folded one. She keeps it in a cloth pouch, and she has never shown it to anyone."

The protagonist felt the cold in his palm return.

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because I asked her about it. I asked her whether she knew the woman in your photograph. And she said no. But she said it too quickly."

The protagonist stood very still.

"You showed the photograph to someone else."

"I showed it to no one. I described it. The face. The house behind her. The way she was standing. I described it to a woman who has lived in this town longer than I have, and she said she did not recognise it, and then she asked me not to describe it again."

The protagonist said nothing.

Mr Stephen looked at him. His face was unreadable.

"I am not here to threaten you. I am here to tell you that the photograph has become more than a photograph. It has become a story that people in this town are beginning to carry. And I cannot control where a story goes once it has left the house."

He waited. The protagonist did not answer.

"I will not need to inspect the room today," Mr Stephen said. "But I will need to come back. And when I come back, I will need to know whether the story has settled or grown."

He paused. He looked at the house, then back at the protagonist.

"The woman I described the photograph to — the one who asked me not to describe it again. I have been thinking about her. I have been thinking about why she would ask that. A person does not ask a question like that unless she recognises what she is being asked to recognise."

"She said she did not recognise it."

"She said it too quickly. That is the same as saying it too slowly. Both are confessions."

The protagonist said nothing.

Mr Stephen looked at him for a long moment. Then he said, "I will return in one week. Not for the room. For the story. I will want to know whether the woman who asked me not to describe the photograph has come to see you. And I will want to know what she said."

He did not wait for an answer. He walked away, and the protagonist watched him go, and he understood that Mr Stephen had not come for the chalk line or the brace or the damp. He had come to confirm that the story was real. And he had confirmed it by watching the protagonist not answer.

The chalk line had moved again.

But the story had moved further.

The protagonist stood at the side of the house, looking at the chalk line that had moved again. Mr Stephen had done exactly what Kene had warned: he had used the face. Not by showing the photograph. By describing it. By letting the story travel without the object. By making the photograph into something that could be carried in the mouth, not the hand.

---

He went inside.

Obianuju was in the front room. She was sitting in the chair by the window, not the one she used during the hour, the other one, and she was holding the cloth pouch in her hands. She was not looking at it. She was looking at the no-colour door.

"He described it," the protagonist said.

"I know."

"He described it to a woman who asked him not to describe it again."

"I know."

"How do you know?"

She looked at him. In her silence was the shape of a question she had been carrying since before he arrived at the gate.

"Because the woman he described it to came to see me this morning."

He stared at her.

"She came here?"

"She came to the gate. She did not come inside. She stood where the lane meets the road, and she told me that a man had described a photograph to her, and that she had told him she did not recognise it, and that she had asked him not to describe it again. She said she was sorry. She said she had not meant to become part of the story."

"What did she look like?"

Obianuju looked at him. The question surprised her. He saw it in the pause before she answered.

"She was old. Older than I expected. Her hands were rough — the kind of rough that comes from years of work, not from age. She was wearing a blue wrapper that had been washed so many times the colour had gone soft at the edges. She kept her arms folded while she spoke, as though she was cold, though the morning was warm."

He waited.

"She did not look at me when she spoke. She looked at the ground, or at the gate, or at the house behind me. She only looked at me once, at the end, when she asked me to destroy the photograph."

"What did her voice sound like?"

"Tired. Not the tiredness of a long day. The tiredness of a long life. She spoke the way a person speaks when she has been carrying a thing for so long that the words have worn smooth."

He looked at the cloth pouch.

"She is the one who hid it in the shelf."

"Yes."

"Not the woman who left."

"No."

He understood, then, that the photograph had been a gift and a warning and a receipt for a debt that had never been paid. The woman who took it had hidden it in the house because she could not destroy it and could not carry it. And Obianuju had found it, and had kept it, and had used it to protect the room, and now the story had travelled back to the woman who had taken it, and the woman had come to the gate to say that she was sorry.

"What did you tell her?" he asked.

"I told her that the photograph had done what she had hidden it to prevent. It had made the cold visible. And that was not her fault. It was the house's fault, for keeping the cold after the woman left."

"Did she believe you?"

"I do not know. But she thanked me. And she asked me to destroy the photograph."

He looked at her.

"Will you?"

She looked at the cloth pouch. Her hand moved toward it, then stopped. He saw that her hand was shaking — not much, but enough. It was the first time he had seen her hand shake since the day she had stood at the no-colour door and told him he had taken something from her.

"I do not know yet," she said.

He looked at the cloth pouch. The photograph was inside it. The face was still there, unsmiling, looking at a camera held by a woman who was still alive, still in the town, still carrying the memory of the day the picture was taken.

"Why did she take it?" he asked.

Obianuju was quiet for a long moment. Then she said, "Because the woman in the photograph asked her to. Before she left. Before she disappeared. She asked her to take one picture of her standing in front of the house, so that someone would remember that she had been there."

"And she kept it."

"She kept it for years. Then she hid it in the house, because she was afraid that keeping it would make the cold notice her. And then she left it there, because she was afraid that taking it away would make the cold follow."

The protagonist looked at the pouch.

"She is the one who hid it in the shelf."

"Yes."

"Not the woman who left."

"No."

He understood, then, that the photograph had been a gift and a warning and a receipt for a debt that had never been paid. The woman who took it had hidden it in the house because she could not destroy it and could not carry it. And Obianuju had found it, and had kept it, and had used it to protect the room, and now the story had travelled back to the woman who had taken it, and the woman had come to the gate to say that she was sorry.

"What did you tell her?" he asked.

"I told her that the photograph had done what she had hidden it to prevent. It had made the cold visible. And that was not her fault. It was the house's fault, for keeping the cold after the woman left."

"Did she believe you?"

"I do not know. But she thanked me. And she asked me to destroy the photograph."

He looked at her.

"Will you?"

She looked at the cloth pouch. Her hand moved toward it, then stopped.

"I do not know yet."

---

Kene arrived in the late evening, after the story had settled into the house the way water settles into wood — slowly, invisibly, changing the weight of everything it touched. He came without food this time, and the absence of food was the first sign that he had not come for an ordinary visit.

"The woman who took the photograph," he said.

"You know about her."

"I know that she came to the gate. I know that she spoke to Obianuju. I know that she asked her to destroy the photograph."

The protagonist looked at him.

"How do you know all this?"

Kene sat down. He did not answer at once. He looked at his hands, then at the table, then at the cloth pouch on the shelf.

"Because she is my mother's neighbour."

The protagonist stared at him.

"Your mother's neighbour."

"Yes."

"And you did not tell me."

"I did not know until today. I knew there was a woman who had lived in this house before. I did not know she was the one who took the photograph. I did not know she was still in the town. I did not know she had been carrying the memory of that day for longer than I have been alive."

He looked at the protagonist.

"She is old now. She does not go to the market. She does not speak to strangers. But when Mr Stephen described the photograph to her, she recognised it. And when she recognised it, she came to the gate. And when she came to the gate, she saw Obianuju, and she understood that the photograph had been found by the one person who could decide what to do with it."

"What did she say to you?"

Kene was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "She said that the woman in the photograph was her friend. That they had grown up together. That the woman had married a man who brought her to this house, and that the house had changed her, and that she had left one night without saying goodbye. She said she had taken the photograph because she wanted to remember her friend the way she was before the house changed her. And she had hidden it because she could not bear to look at it and could not bear to throw it away."

He looked at the cloth pouch.

"She said that if Obianuju destroys it, she will be free of it. And if she keeps it, she will be carrying the same weight that she has been carrying for thirty years."

The protagonist said nothing.

Kene looked at him. "You are very quiet."

"I do not know what to say."

"That is honest. That is more than most men manage when they are trying to seem wise."

"I am not trying to seem wise."

"I know. That is why I am still here."

He leaned back in his chair. He looked at the ceiling, then at the table, then at the cloth pouch again.

"She asked me to tell Obianuju something," he said. "She asked me to tell her that the woman in the photograph had a name. That she was not just a face in a picture. That she had been someone's friend, someone's wife, someone who laughed and cooked and argued about the price of fish like everyone else. She said that if Obianuju destroys the photograph, she should know whose face she is destroying."

"Did she tell you the name?"

Kene looked at him. "She told me. But I am not going to tell you. That is for Obianuju to hear, if she wants to hear it."

The protagonist nodded. He understood. The name was not his to carry.

Kene stood up. He walked to the door. He stopped with his hand on the frame, the way he always stopped, and the protagonist waited for the usual joke, the usual bread, the usual something that would make the weight bearable.

It did not come.

"I will come tomorrow," Kene said. "If she decides to destroy it, I will be there. If she decides to keep it, I will be there. Either way, I will bring food."

He left.

The protagonist sat alone in the front room.

The cloth pouch was on the shelf.

The cold was at the edges.

And he understood that Kene had not made a joke because there was no joke to make. The story had travelled too far for jokes. It had reached a woman who had been carrying it for thirty years, and it had reached Kene's mother's neighbour, and it would reach more people before it was done. The only question was whether the photograph would survive the journey.

The protagonist sat alone in the front room.

The cloth pouch was on the shelf.

The cold was at the edges.

The story had become more than a story. It had become a choice. Not his choice. Obianuju's choice. Whether to destroy the photograph and let the story become just a story, or to keep it and let the story become the thing that the house was built around.

He did not know what she would choose.

He only knew that he would help her, whatever she decided, and that the helping would not give him the right to understand.

---

That night, after Obianuju had gone to bed, he sat in the front room with the lamp burning low. The cloth pouch was on the shelf. The cold was at the edges of the room, waiting, patient, unhurried.

He thought of the woman who had taken the photograph. He thought of her standing at the gate, her rough hands, her faded wrapper, her voice worn smooth by years of carrying. He thought of her friend, the woman in the photograph, who had left the house one night without saying goodbye, and who had never been seen again. He thought of the name that Kene would not tell him, the name that belonged to Obianuju now, the name that would either be spoken or silenced in the morning.

He thought of Obianuju, who had found the photograph and kept it, and who had used it to protect the room, and who now had to decide whether to destroy it or keep it.

He tried to imagine the photograph being destroyed. He tried to imagine the flame taking the edge of the paper, the face curling inward, the woman in the picture becoming ash. He thought it would feel like a relief — the story finally ended, the face finally released, the cold finally losing its evidence. But he also thought it would feel like a loss — the last proof that the woman had existed, the last connection to the life she had lived before the house changed her.

He did not know which feeling was stronger. He did not know which feeling Obianuju would choose.

He thought of the story, which had left the house and was now in the market, in the lane, in the mouths of people who had never seen the photograph and did not need to. He thought of Mr Stephen, who would return in one week to ask whether the woman had come to the gate. He thought of the woman with the rough hands, who had asked Obianuju to destroy the photograph, and who would wake tomorrow not knowing whether her friend's face still existed.

He stood up.

He walked to the no-colour door and put his hand on the handle. The handle was cold. Not the cold of metal left in a draught. The cold of something that had been touched by a room it did not belong to.

He did not open the door.

He stood there with his hand on the cold handle. The door was not the door he needed to open. The one he needed to open was the one that would let him accept that the story was no longer theirs to control, and that the only thing they could still control was what they did with the photograph.

He took his hand off the handle.

He went back to the front room.

He sat down.

The cloth pouch was on the shelf.

The cold was at the edges.

And in the morning, Obianuju would decide whether to destroy the photograph or keep it. Either way, the story would continue. Either way, the house would remain what it was. Either way, the cold would still be at the edges, waiting, patient, unhurried.

But the choice would be hers.

And he would help her, whatever she decided.

That was all he could do.

That was all he had ever been able to do.

That was enough.

## How the Chapter Begins with Ordinary Life

The chapter opens with the protagonist returning from Mama Agnes's at dawn. The first scene is domestic — the house, the grey light, the ordinary morning. Obianuju is in the kitchen doing the ordinary work: the kettle, the basin, the cloth. He watches her hands. The chapter establishes the person before the wound — her hands measuring, wiping, folding — before the conversation turns.

The opening also includes a flashback to the first time he saw her hands (the nail, the line, the cooking knife), grounding the chapter in the marriage's origin before the rupture.

---

## What Ch.10 Action Costs

The protagonist's night movement toward Mama Agnes has three immediate costs:

1. **Obianuju knows he left.** She woke and he was not there. The absence is the first wound.
2. **Mama Agnes's involvement changes the terms.** "You have made the house notice you. That is not the same as understanding it." The cold has been acknowledged by a third party with authority.
3. **The cold has been made public.** Obianuju names this directly: "You have been walking through the town with the cold on your hand, showing it to people, and every person who has seen it has become part of it."

The cost is not abstract. It appears in scene — in her withdrawal, in her moved hand, in her plainest sentence: "You have taken that from me."

---

## How Obianuju Turns Without Becoming Cruel

The turn is rendered as grief, pressure, and something done *to* her:

- **She moves her hand away** — not sharply, not dramatically. "She moved it the way a person moves a hand from a surface that has become too hot to touch."
- **She names the cost without accusation.** "You have taken that from me" — plain, not loud, not angry.
- **She admits her own fear.** "Because I was afraid that if I told you the cold could see you, you would try to make it see you more."
- **She does not punish him.** She tells him what she needs: "I want you to stop trying to save me."
- **She leaves the door open — literally and figuratively.** She says "right now" — not "forever."

The reader feels: *She has turned. But I understand why. She is protecting the one thing she could control.*

---

## How Obianuju Remains Sympathetic and Active

- She is the one who names what has happened. She is not passive — she confronts him directly.
- She admits her own concealment and her own fear. She does not pretend to be innocent.
- She gives him a task: "Then you need to find out. Before you act again."
- She sets the terms: "I need you to not touch me. I need you to not follow me. I need you to not try to fix what you do not understand."
- She says "right now" — preserving the possibility of return without promising it.

She is not a victim. She is a woman who has been managing a pressure he intensified, and she is now managing him too.

---

## How the Protagonist Misreads the Reversal

He misreads in several ways:

1. **He thinks his action was love.** He thought the investigation, the movement, the walk to Mama Agnes was the kind of staying that refused to let the cold win. She shows him it was trespass.
2. **He thinks being kept qualifies him.** He has always believed the brown water preserved him for something. He assumed that something was this — the ability to act, to fight, to save. He never considered it might have been for the opposite: to stay still while someone else did the work he could not see.
3. **He confuses staying with presence.** He thought sitting through the hour with open hands was love. He learns that staying is a position, not an act.
4. **He thinks "I do not know" is failure.** She receives it as honesty.

The reader understands more than he does: she is responding to a pressure he has intensified, not punishing him for caring.

## Mama Agnes Handling

Mama Agnes appears in the opening. She is handled correctly:

- She does not explain the cold.
- She does not tell him what to do.
- She gives him tea in a chipped cup and lets him sit in the silence.
- She asks what he has done with the sentence.
- She gives him one sentence: "You have made the house notice you. That is not the same as understanding it."
- She remains practical, ancestral, and limited.

The full Mama Agnes encounter is not conducted here — it is preserved for Ch.14 (per the blueprint). This is a consequence scene, not a solution scene.

## Kene Handling

Kene does not appear in Ch.11, but his involvement is felt:

- Obianuju names that he went to Kene.
- The protagonist repeats something Kene said ("He said I was calling endurance love") and regrets it immediately.
- Obianuju's response — "Kene is not married to me" — is the cost of Kene's widening of the world.

Kene's challenge from Ch.10 echoes through the chapter. The private house is no longer private.

## How the Cold Remains Implicit

The cold is rendered through:

- The house's atmosphere at dawn — "the same cold waiting at the edges of the rooms"
- Mama Agnes's sentence: "You have made the house notice you"
- Obianuju's description: "It does not want my fear. It wants my presence. It wants to be acknowledged."
- The cold cup on the table
- The no-colour door, still closed
- The closing image: "The cold at the edges of the house waited too, patient and unhurried"

No name is given. No explanation is offered. The cold remains claim, not monster.

## What Structurally Changes by the End

The marriage has a new term:

1. **He cannot touch her as though nothing has changed.** The hand withdrawal is the chapter's central image.
2. **He cannot act without understanding.** She tells him to find out what he is doing before he acts again.
3. **He must learn a new kind of waiting.** Not endurance — attention.
4. **She has set the terms.** She is now the one controlling access to the marriage.
5. **The door remains closed.** But she said "right now" — not "never."

The chapter cannot end with the same arrangement as before. It does not.

---

## Motifs Used and Why They Were Organic

- **Hands** (dominant): The chapter opens and closes with her hands. The first time he saw them (the nail, the line). The way they move with precision to hide her interior. The withdrawal when he reaches for her. Her hands in the basin at the close. The motif carries the entire emotional arc.
- **Door / Threshold:** The no-colour door, still closed. The threshold of the yard where he stands and waits. The door inside himself that he has opened.
- **Cold / Temperature:** The cold cup. The cold at the edges. The cold between them that is not the cold of the house.
- **Water:** The kettle, the basin, the water running over her hands. The ordinary work that continues despite the rupture.
- **Salt:** Not present in this chapter — reserved for later.

---

## Acceptance-Gate Sentence

> *She moved her hand away. Not sharply. Not dramatically. She moved it the way a person moves a hand from a surface that has become too hot to touch — a small, precise withdrawal, without explanation.*

This sentence carries the reversal, the cost of love, Obianuju's humanity, and the new term of the marriage. It is not a summary. It is not explanatory. It is an image that cuts.

The secondary sentence — *"I do not know how to be married to you right now"* — is the chapter's verbal heart, but the hand withdrawal is the image that will stay with the reader.

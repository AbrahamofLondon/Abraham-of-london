## Executive Summary

- **Verdict: PASS.**
- **The chapter's special thing — the town becoming a character against him without becoming a mob — was achieved.** The town never attacks. It adjusts: a greeting a half-beat late, the pepper woman charging the *correct* price (the price she keeps for strangers), a shutter drawn half across, a child's rudeness withdrawn by an older boy's flat hand, a landlord who will not cross a gate. ## Structure

**How the chapter inherits the isolation beat.** It does not announce isolation; it accumulates it. The thesis is stated only once, late, as earned consequence: "being seen was not the same as being helped… he was alone in front of all of them, which the town had known all along was the colder of the two solitudes." The protagonist got his Ch.9–12 wish (to stop being alone with an unconfirmed cold) and discovers it was the worse of the two solitudes.

**How town life changes after Ch.12.** The market is *identical* — same fish, same mortar — and that sameness is the cruelty; only the temperature of attention has changed. Beats: the pepper woman selling at the correct price in the stranger's voice ("Greet the house"); the late, conversation-less greeting; a woman stepping into her own house; a child drawn in by the shoulder "the way you bring in washing before a rain"; the boys' withdrawn rudeness; Mama Chinwe's half-drawn shutter ("the whole town in a single slat of wood").

**How Obiefuna functions.** Civility as threat, deepened from Ch.12. He calls correctly, at the decent hour, with a **rent book** he sets on the gatepost but **will not carry through the gate** (the established won't-cross-the-threshold, now weaponised). He is not there for money; he is there for **quiet** — "a house that carries noise is a house I cannot fill when it empties" (*when*, not *if*). He mentions **an enquiry** from a respectable family (implied eviction as leverage). He lets his own **name pressure the scene** — reads it off the rent book, *"may the homestead not be lost,"* a prayer he has never failed to answer: "the ones that will not stay quiet… those I empty, and fill again, and they forget. Houses are very good at forgetting." Practical, not villainous; the menace is entirely in the courtesy and the ownership.

**How Obianuju remains protected from public reduction.** She is off-page until the final image, never diminished by the narration. The town **still does not own her name** — it has only "the one in that house," "she," "the one who keeps her face." The narration turns even the rumour's phrase back into her dignity: she *does* keep her face, has kept it for two years across the hour, and the town has taken "the very thing he loved her for" and made it the mark of the chosen woman — shown as the cruelty it is. **She acts:** her single decisive act is to **close the door** — the door that had stood ajar the whole book — the precise, undramatic withdrawal of a woman whose privacy her husband keeps giving away. She is not only a recipient of consequence; she answers it.

**How Kene appears or is felt.** Present, under strain, fully human. He performs not-seeing at the cooperage ("Do not stand in front of me. Stand beside me. Look at the wood."); helps while refusing to be seen helping (the orange pressed at waist height); refuses to be the witness again ("I will not be the man who tells the town the cold is real… I have a wife now to consider. She is imaginary, but I am building toward her"); and delivers the chapter's governing warning — "The town is faster than the truth" and "a defence is a signpost." Loyal, irritated, hungry, afraid enough to be sharp.

**How the protagonist acts and what it costs.** Twice he acts and is wrong. (1) At his own gate he asks Obiefuna — within Mama Chinwe's hearing — what became of the previous tenants; Obiefuna names the error ("You think you are gathering. You are scattering."). (2) At the standpipe he defends Obianuju ("She counts… because she is the only person in this town who pays attention to anything but talk") — true, well said, and the worst thing he could do: he confirms he heard the rumour, that the woman is his, and restates her in the exact terms the rumour is hungry for. The kind woman even nods, "as if he had helped." Public action has a different cost from private endurance: questions asked in public become public property; a defence is a signpost.

**Where warmth / humour / appetite appears.** Kene's fear-sharpened comedy (the imaginary wife who "will not want to marry into a thermometer"; "a man who shares fruit with a marked man is sharing more than fruit"); the orange itself (appetite + the only warmth offered all morning, given low where it can't be seen); Obiefuna's absurd, fastidious civility (squaring the rent book on the post). The pleasure is real and each beat also carries danger — none is relief pasted on.

**How the cold remains implicit.** Rendered almost entirely as **social temperature** this chapter (deliberate — a social-pressure chapter, not a supernatural-event one): the withdrawn warmth, the half-drawn shutter "the whole town in a single slat," the gate Obiefuna won't cross, the ajar door now shut. No literal cold object was used (the brief listed those as options, not requirements); the cold is never named, never explained, never doctrinated.

**Trance:** does not appear. **Voice:** does not appear. (Neither required by the blueprint here.)

**Motifs used (organic, deepened not repeated):**
- **Door / threshold** — the book's spine image pays off hard: the door that "had stood a little open every day he had known it" is, for the first time, shut; the chapter ends with him knocking at his own house. Obiefuna's gate he will not cross rhymes with it.
- **Naming** — the town reaches for Obianuju's name and does not yet own it; Obiefuna reads his *own* name off the rent book as pressure. The book's naming motif made social and economic.
- **The unmappable / town** — the familiar town becomes an unknowable "city"; the motif of a place that cannot be mapped turns inward (he never knew the town, he was only let alone inside it).
- **Hands / salt** — the orange at waist height; salt drying white on his knuckles as he carries the lonely water.

## Acceptance

**Acceptance-gate sentence:**
> "The town had not decided against him. It had decided to wait."

It could belong only to this chapter: it names the special thing (the town as a patient character, not a mob), carries the public greying and the social consequence, holds the menace of Obiefuna and the standpipe under one verb, and sets the isolation that lands later. It cuts, and it is not summary or lore. (Its isolation payoff — "the colder of the two solitudes" — is the same gate's later half.)

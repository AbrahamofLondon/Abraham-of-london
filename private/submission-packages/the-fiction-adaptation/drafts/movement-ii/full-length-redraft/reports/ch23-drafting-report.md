## How the Chapter Opens with Ch.22 Consequence

The chapter opens on the fifth day, with the rain returning. The red thread is still on the table where Obianuju left it. The protagonist checks the chalk line — still intact, but harder to see against the wet. The ordinary return of weather becomes the return of consequence. No explanation. No summary of Ch.22. The rain is the connection.

---

## What Practical Cost the Wrong Key Created

1. **The house accepted the wrong key.** Obianuju diagnoses this: the key did not open the forbidden door, but it showed the house that a key exists, and the house began to prepare for the possibility of being opened.
2. **The chalk line curved.** Brother Paul confirms the wood moved around the brace. The brace will tire before the wood does.
3. **Mr Stephen now uses the weather as an appointment.** He stands at the end of the lane during the second rain, watching to see whether the house reacts.
4. **Brother Paul oiled the lock.** An ordinary repair that becomes evidence that the lock is being used.
5. **The woman who sold the house confirms Mr Stephen knows something is there.** He does not know what it is, but he knows it is worth something, and he is trying to find out how much.

---

## How Obianuju Diagnoses or Chooses Under That Cost

Obianuju is the first to understand what the loosened thread means. She sits with it for four days before speaking. She diagnoses:

- "The house accepted the wrong key."
- "It opened a path. Not a door. A path."
- "The house, after enough trying, begins to prepare for the possibility of being opened."
- "We make the door less worth opening. By giving him something else to find."

She is not passive. She is measuring consequence and preparing a strategy. Her decision to give Mr Stephen something else to find is an active choice under worsened conditions.

---

## How Protagonist Remorse Is Prevented from Becoming Useful Currency

The protagonist tries several times to make his remorse useful:

- He checks the chalk line every morning — relief, then shame.
- He asks "What do you want me to do?" — wanting a task that would prove his usefulness.
- He asks "Do you want me to go and tell [Brother Paul]?" — wanting to be the one who acts.
- He reaches for the thread when she holds it out — wanting the touch to mean something.

Each time, he is met with a correction that prevents guilt from becoming currency:
- She does not give him a task. She gives him understanding.
- She does not let him carry the message. She carries it herself.
- She makes him take the thread, but the thread is not a gift — it is a diagnosis.

Kene also prevents this: "Do not stand there waiting to be sentenced" (implied through his practical pressure). The chapter does not let the protagonist turn remorse into a clean narrative.

---

## How Kene Appears or Is Felt

Kene appears in the late afternoon with fish. He:

- Brings appetite and ordinary life (the fish, the newspaper, the eating)
- Presses the protagonist to see what he actually sees, not what Brother Paul saw
- Refuses to let the protagonist settle for waiting: "I have never liked waiting. It gives the weather too much authority."
- Directs him to the woman who sold the house — practical intelligence, not mystery
- Reveals he knew about the woman and was watching her watch the house

Kene is not a proverb machine. He is a friend who brings food, pressure, and the right question.

---

## How Mr Stephen's Leverage Sharpens

Mr Stephen gains without appearing to gain:

1. **He now uses the weather as a reason to be present.** He stands at the end of the lane during the second rain, watching the house without approaching it.
2. **He knows the chalk line matters.** He waits to see whether the house reacts to his presence. When it does not, he walks away — but the watching itself is leverage.
3. **The woman confirms his strategy.** He did not ask about the cold because he knew enough to wait. He is trying to find out how much the thing in the house is worth.
4. **Brother Paul's oiled lock is now evidence of use.** Mr Stephen did not ask for this, but he will notice it.

His menace remains ordinary reasonableness with a blade inside it. No villain speech. No theatrical reveal.

---

## How Brother Paul Appears or Is Felt

Brother Paul returns on the seventh day. He:

- Checks the chalk line and confirms the wood moved
- Oils the lock as an ordinary repair
- Says, without knowing what he is saying: "Depends on how many more keys are put in that lock"
- Remains entirely ordinary — a repairman doing repairman things whose ordinary work has become evidence

His ordinariness is the pressure. He has no idea what he has said.

---

## Where the Human Counter-Note Appears

1. **Kene's fish.** He arrives with food, eats half before speaking, tears it into pieces with unnecessary ceremony.
2. **Brother Paul's practical focus.** He is concerned with wood, chalk, oil — not with mystery.
3. **The woman shelling groundnuts.** The ordinary act of shelling while delivering testimony.
4. **The rain as inconvenience, not drama.** The town remembers it not as a storm but as an inconvenience.

The reader gets breath without rescue. The breath sharpens consequence.

---

## What Irreversible Consequence Ends the Chapter

The chapter ends with a decision that cannot be undone: Obianuju will give Mr Stephen something to find. Not the room. Not the key. Something small enough to satisfy a reasonable man and large enough to make him stop looking for the thing that cannot be named.

The protagonist will help her give it. The giving will change the house as much as the keeping has. After the giving, there will be no more waiting — only what comes next.

The endgame has narrowed into a practical choice: what to give, and what the giving will cost.

## What the Photograph Shows or Withholds

The photograph shows a woman — older than Obianuju, younger than Mama Nneoma — standing in front of a house that is not the current house. She is wearing a wrapper and a blouse the colour of dried earth. She is not smiling. She is looking at the camera the way a person looks at something that has asked her to prove she exists.

The photograph withholds:
- The woman's name (Obianuju knows it but will not tell Mr Stephen)
- The house in the background (not identified)
- Whether the woman is alive or dead
- What happened to her after she left
- Why Obianuju kept the photograph hidden for years

The photograph wounds more by what it refuses to explain than by what it reveals.

---

## Why Obianuju Chose It

Obianuju chose the photograph because:
1. It belonged to the house, not to the room — giving it away did not open the forbidden door
2. It gave Mr Stephen a story he could carry — a face to attach to the cold
3. It was the smallest loss she could afford — a piece of her hidden history rather than access to the present
4. She had kept it for years without knowing whether it protected her or made her visible — surrendering it was a way of testing whether the cold would notice

---

## What It Costs Her

1. **Privacy:** A piece of her hidden history is now in Mr Stephen's mind
2. **Control:** He now has a story he can verify, repeat, and use
3. **The truth about the cold:** She reveals to the protagonist that the cold was waiting for the woman in the photograph, not for her — and that it thought she was that woman
4. **The photograph itself:** She will not get it back; it now belongs to Mr Stephen's understanding of the house

The cost is visible in her refusal to hand it to him, in the way she leaves it face-down, in the pause before she says "After, it will be his."

---

## How the Protagonist Carries/Helps Without Owning

The protagonist is given specific instructions:
- Stand at the door
- Do not leave the front room
- Do not speak unless asked
- Do not look at the photograph while Mr Stephen is holding it

He performs ignorance — acting as though he has seen the photograph before, as though it means nothing, as though the face is not the first thing he has ever been forbidden to know about her.

He asks the right question: "What do you need me to do?" — instead of the wrong question: "Who is she?"

He sees the photograph only when Mr Stephen turns it over, and only for a few seconds. He does not ask to see it again. He does not ask to keep it. He carries the knowledge without claiming ownership of it.

Kene corrects him when he tries to turn the partial sight into intimacy: "You are trying to turn her protection into intimacy because it is easier than sitting with the fact that she still does not trust you with the full story."

---

## How Mr Stephen Receives More Than He Asked For

Mr Stephen asked for access, key, repair, inspection. He receives:
1. **A photograph** — a physical object, not an explanation
2. **A story** — the woman who lived here before, the one who left the cold
3. **A face** — someone he can now imagine, ask about, recognise
4. **Proof of fear** — Obianuju was willing to surrender a piece of her history to keep him from the room

He gains:
- A reason to delay the full inspection ("I will not need to inspect the room today")
- A condition for return ("after the next rain")
- Leverage he does not yet know how to use (the photograph, the story, the face)
- The knowledge that Obianuju is afraid enough to give something away

His reaction is controlled: he looks at the photograph longer than courtesy requires, his thumb moves across the edge, he asks "Who is she?" and accepts the refusal. He does not press. He does not need to. He has what he came for.

## How Kene Appears or Is Felt

Kene arrives in the late afternoon with bread. He:
- Brings appetite and ordinary life (the bread, the eating)
- Names the danger: "He does not need to know who she is. He only needs to know that she existed."
- Refuses to let the protagonist claim understanding: "That is all you saw, or that is all you are willing to say?"
- Corrects the protagonist's attempt to turn partial sight into intimacy
- Names what Obianuju actually did: "She gave him a face."
- Refuses to look at the photograph himself

Kene remains human, irritated, loyal, and afraid. He does not rescue the chapter — he sharpens it.

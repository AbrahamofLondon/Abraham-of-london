## What Mr Stephen Does With the Photograph Story

Mr Stephen does not show the photograph to anyone. He describes it — the face, the house behind her, the way she was standing. He describes it to a woman who recognises it and asks him not to describe it again. He then returns to the house to confirm that the story is real.

His use of the story is:
- **Reasonable:** he does not threaten, he does not demand
- **Dangerous:** he has set the story in motion without needing to understand it
- **Strategic:** he returns not for the room but to test whether the woman has come to the gate
- **Conditional:** he sets a one-week return date to hear what the woman said

He gains: confirmation that the story is real, a date for return, and the knowledge that Obianuju is afraid enough to have given something away.

---

## What the Photograph Now Costs Obianuju

1. **The story has detached from the object.** It is now in the market, the lane, the mouths of people who have never seen the photograph.
2. **The woman who took the photograph has come to the gate.** She is old, her hands rough, her voice worn smooth by years of carrying. She asked Obianuju to destroy it.
3. **A name exists that Obianuju must now decide to speak or silence.** Kene knows it but will not tell the protagonist — it is for Obianuju to hear.
4. **Mr Stephen has a return date.** One week. He will want to know whether the woman came.

The cost is visible in Obianuju's shaking hand — the first time he has seen it shake since Ch.11.

---

## How the Protagonist Acts Without Owning

The protagonist:
- Does not correct the pepper woman's wrong belief
- Does not ask Mr Stephen to stop describing the photograph
- Does not ask Obianuju to show him the photograph again
- Does not ask Kene for the name
- Does not try to decide what Obianuju should do with the photograph
- Imagines the photograph being destroyed, and feels both relief and loss, but does not act on either feeling

His progress: he accepts that the story is no longer theirs to control, and that the only thing they can still control is what they do with the photograph. He understands that his role is not to decide but to be present for whatever she chooses.

The closing: "That was all he could do. That was all he had ever been able to do. And for the first time, that was enough."

## How Kene Appears or Is Felt

Kene arrives in the late evening without food — the absence of food is the first sign that this is not an ordinary visit. He reveals that the woman who took the photograph is his mother's neighbour. He:
- Brings the human connection between the photograph and the town
- Refuses to tell the protagonist the woman's name — it is for Obianuju to hear
- Presses the protagonist on his silence
- Leaves without his usual joke, proving how serious this is
- Promises to return tomorrow with food, whatever Obianuju decides

Kene remains loyal, irritated, afraid, and useful in an ungentle way.

## Whether the Story Escapes the Photograph

Yes. The story has fully detached from the object. It is now in the market, the lane, and the mouth of a woman who has been carrying it for thirty years. The pepper woman knows about the "old tenant." The woman who took the photograph has come to the gate. The story no longer needs the photograph to exist.

---

## What Irreversible Endgame Turn the Chapter Creates

**The story has escaped the photograph, and Mr Stephen has a return date.**

The photograph can no longer be controlled by being hidden. Even if Obianuju destroys it, the story will continue — in Mr Stephen's mind, in the market, in the lane, in the memory of the woman who took it. The only thing Obianuju can still control is whether the photograph itself survives.

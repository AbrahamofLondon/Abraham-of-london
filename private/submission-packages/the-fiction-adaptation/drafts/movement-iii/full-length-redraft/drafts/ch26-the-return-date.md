# Chapter 26: The Return Date

The story came back on the fourth day, not the seventh, and it came back wrong.

The protagonist heard it from Kene, who arrived before the market had fully woken, with no food and a face that said he had not come to eat.

"The pepper woman is telling people the old tenant died in the house."

The protagonist stared at him.

"That is not true."

"I know it is not true. But it is what she is saying. She is saying the woman in the photograph died in the back room, and that is why the house is cold."

"Who told her that?"

"No one. She invented it. She had a piece of a story and she finished it the way the town finishes things — by making it complete enough to carry. She told the fish-seller, and the fish-seller told the woman who sells oil, and by this morning the story had reached the tailor and the boys at the wall."

The protagonist stood in the doorway. The morning was grey. The market was waking. Somewhere a woman was calling a child, and the child was not answering, and the not-answering was so ordinary that it made the wrong story feel like the only story that would survive.

"I walked past the pepper woman's stall on my way here," Kene said. "She looked at me. She did not call out. But she looked at me the way the town looks at a person when it has decided that his house is worth watching."

The protagonist felt the cold in his palm return.

"Does Obianuju know?"

"Not yet. I came to you first."

"Why?"

Kene looked at him. "Because you are the one who will have to decide whether to tell her."

The protagonist shook his head. "That is not my decision."

"It is your decision whether to bring her a problem she did not ask for."

"She already knows the story is travelling. She knew it would change."

"Knowing it would change is not the same as hearing what it has become. The pepper woman is not saying the old tenant left. She is saying she died. That is a different story. That is a story that brings Mr Stephen back before the week is up."

The protagonist was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "I will tell her. But I will tell her exactly what you told me. I will not add anything. I will not soften it."

Kene looked at him for a long moment. Then he nodded.

"That is the right way."

He did not leave. He stood at the gate, looking at the house.

"There is something else," he said.

"What?"

"The woman who took the photograph. She heard the pepper woman's story this morning. She came to my mother's house. She was crying."

The protagonist felt the cold in his palm return.

"Why was she crying?"

"Because the story she has been carrying for thirty years has become something she does not recognise. She kept the photograph hidden to protect her friend's memory. Now the town is saying her friend died in a back room, alone, and that the cold is her ghost. She asked me to tell Obianuju that she is sorry she ever took the picture."

The protagonist said nothing.

Kene looked at him. "I am not telling you this to make you feel worse. I am telling you because the story is no longer about the house. It is about a woman who is still alive, still in this town, still carrying the weight of a decision she made thirty years ago. And she is watching her friend's face become a ghost in the mouths of people who never knew her."

He paused. He looked at the house, then back at the protagonist.

"I will go to her," Kene said. "After I leave here. I will tell her that Obianuju knows, and that she is not angry. I will tell her that the story will pass. I do not know if it will be true. But she needs to hear it."

The protagonist looked at him. "You do not have to do that."

"I know. That is why I am doing it."

---

He found Obianuju in the front room, sitting in the chair by the window. The cloth pouch was on the table beside her, closed, the drawstring pulled tight. She was not looking at it. She was looking at the no-colour door.

"The pepper woman is telling people the woman in the photograph died in the back room," he said. "She is saying that is why the house is cold."

Obianuju did not turn. She did not answer at once. She looked at the no-colour door the way she had looked at it a hundred times — not with fear, not with anger, but with the attention of a woman who had learned that small things could carry more weight than large ones.

"That is not true," she said.

"I know."

"Does it matter?"

He hesitated. "It matters if people believe it."

She turned then. Her face was the face of a woman who had been sitting with a question for four days and had arrived at an answer she did not want to give.

"It matters if Mr Stephen believes it. And he will not believe it, because he heard the story from me. But he will use it. He will say that the town is talking, and that the house is becoming a problem, and that he needs to be seen to be doing something."

The protagonist looked at her.

"Then what do we do?"

She picked up the cloth pouch. She held it in her hands the way she had held it the night she first took it down from the shelf.

"We let the wrong story travel. We do not correct it. We do not confirm it. We let it become the story that people carry, because the wrong story is easier to disprove than the right one."

"And if Mr Stephen asks?"

She looked at him. "If Mr Stephen asks, I will tell him the truth. And he will believe me, because he wants to believe me. He wants the story to be useful, not true."

"There is something else," he said.

She waited.

"The woman who took the photograph. She heard the pepper woman's story. She was crying. She asked Kene to tell you that she is sorry she ever took the picture."

Obianuju was quiet for a long moment. Her hand moved toward the cloth pouch, then stopped.

"She should not be sorry," Obianuju said. "She took the picture because her friend asked her to. She hid it because she was afraid. She came to the gate because she wanted to do the right thing. None of that is her fault."

"Whose fault is it?"

She looked at him. Her face was unreadable.

"It is the house's fault. For keeping the cold after the woman left. For making the photograph into a thing that could be used. For making a story out of a face."

---

Mr Stephen came on the fifth day, not the seventh.

He arrived in the late afternoon, when the market was thinning and the light was the particular gold it went only in the half-hour before the calls. He stood at the gate with the same notebook, the same reasonable manner, the same umbrella that was not needed because the afternoon was dry.

The protagonist opened the gate.

"I came early," Mr Stephen said.

"I see that."

"There is a story in the market. I do not need to tell you what it says."

"No."

"I came to tell you that I did not start it. I described the photograph to one person, and that person described it to another, and the story grew the way stories grow in a town that has too little to talk about."

The protagonist said nothing.

Mr Stephen looked at him. "I also came to tell you that I will need to see the room."

The sentence entered the air and sat there. The protagonist felt it the way he had felt the cold at the boundary in the trance — not painful, but absolute.

"The room," the protagonist said.

"The back room. The one that has been closed. I need to see it."

"Why?"

"Because the story says someone died in it. I do not believe the story. But if I do not inspect the room, people will say I am hiding something. And if I inspect it, people will say I found nothing. Either way, the story loses its power."

The protagonist looked at him.

"You want to inspect the room to disprove a story you know is false."

"Yes."

"And if we refuse?"

Mr Stephen's face did not change. "Then I will have to tell the town that the tenants would not allow an inspection. And the story will grow."

The protagonist stood very still. He understood that Mr Stephen had done exactly what Obianuju had predicted: he had used the false story as leverage. Not by believing it. By offering to disprove it. The offer was reasonable. The refusal would be unreasonable. And reasonableness, in the hands of a man like Mr Stephen, was the most dangerous tool there was.

"You could have sent word," the protagonist said. "You did not need to come yourself."

Mr Stephen was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "I came myself because I wanted to see whether the story had changed you."

The protagonist looked at him.

"Changed me?"

"Whether you looked different. Whether you looked like a man who was hiding something, or a man who was tired of being watched. I have been a landlord long enough to know the difference."

"And which am I?"

Mr Stephen looked at him for a long moment. His face was unreadable, but something behind his eyes shifted — not cruelty, not kindness, but a kind of genuine curiosity that was more dangerous than either.

"I do not know yet," he said. "That is why I am still here."

The protagonist held his gaze. "I will ask Obianuju."

Mr Stephen nodded. "I will wait at the gate."

---

He found Obianuju in the kitchen yard, standing at the basin with her back to him. The water was running, and her hands were in it, and she was not moving.

"Mr Stephen wants to see the room."

She did not turn.

"I know."

"He says he wants to inspect it to disprove the false story. He says if we refuse, the story will grow."

She was quiet for a long moment. Then she turned off the water. She dried her hands on her wrapper. She turned to face him.

"He is right."

The protagonist looked at her.

"If we refuse, the story grows. If we let him see the room, he finds nothing, and the story dies. Either way, the room is no longer protected by the photograph. The photograph bought us time. The time is up."

"Then what do we do?"

She looked at him. Her face was the face of a woman who had been sitting with a question for five days and had arrived at an answer she did not want to give.

"We let him see the room."

He stared at her.

"The room?"

"The back room. The one that has been closed. We let him see it. He will find nothing. The story will die. And the no-colour door will still be there, closed, waiting."

"But the room — "

"Is not the no-colour door. It is a room. It has walls and a floor and a window. It has nothing in it that Mr Stephen can use. The cold is not in the room. It is in the door."

He looked at her. "You are sure?"

She looked at the cloth pouch, still on the table, still closed.

"I am sure that the photograph cannot protect us anymore. The story has outgrown it. The only thing we can do now is let the story die by proving it false."

"And after?"

She looked at him. Her face was unreadable.

"After, we will see what the cold does when it has no story to hide behind."

---

Mr Stephen entered the back room at dusk.

The protagonist opened the door. Obianuju stood in the front room, by the window, not watching. She had said she did not need to see it. She had said the room would be the same after he left as it was before he came, and that watching would only make it feel like a surrender.

Mr Stephen stepped inside. He looked at the walls first — slowly, the way a man looks at a room he has been told to fear. He ran his hand along the damp seam where the rain had entered. He opened the cupboard and closed it. He looked under the sill. He tapped the floorboards with his foot, once, near the corner, as though expecting a hollow sound.

There was no hollow sound.

The room was ordinary. It was a room. It had walls and a floor and a window and a cupboard. It had a damp seam and a board that had lifted and a smell of old rain and dust. It had nothing else.

The protagonist watched him from the doorway. He watched Mr Stephen's hands move across the walls, the sill, the cupboard. He watched him look for something that was not there. And he understood that Mr Stephen was not looking for evidence of a death. He was looking for evidence of the cold. And he was not finding it, because the cold was not in the room. It was in the door. And the door was still closed.

Mr Stephen straightened. He looked at the room one more time, as though asking it to give him something he could carry. The room did not answer.

He stepped out. He closed the notebook.

"The room is dry," he said.

"Yes."

"There is no evidence that anyone died here."

"No."

He looked at the protagonist. His face was unreadable.

"The story will die now. It may take a few days, but it will die. People will say I inspected the room and found nothing. And the woman who sold peppers will find something else to talk about."

The protagonist said nothing.

Mr Stephen walked toward the gate. At the threshold, he stopped.

"The photograph," he said.

"Yes."

"I will not ask for it again."

The protagonist looked at him.

"Why?"

Mr Stephen was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "Because I have seen the room. I have seen that there is nothing in it. And I have begun to wonder whether the photograph was never about the room at all."

He walked away.

The protagonist stood at the gate and watched him go. The evening light was the particular gold it went only in the half-hour before the calls, and the town was doing its ordinary evening — women calling children, radios playing music, the smell of plantain frying and diesel and salt. All of it was the same. None of it was the same.

Mr Stephen had seen the room.

The room had been empty.

And the no-colour door was still closed.

But Mr Stephen had said he would not ask for the photograph again. And the protagonist understood that this was not a gift. It was a statement. Mr Stephen no longer needed the photograph. He had seen the room. He had found nothing. And he had begun to wonder whether the photograph was never about the room at all.

He had begun to wonder what it was about.

And that wondering was more dangerous than any question he had asked before.



---

He went inside.

Obianuju was still in the front room, sitting in the chair by the window. The cloth pouch was on the table beside her. She had not moved.

"He saw the room," the protagonist said.

"I know."

"He found nothing."

"I know."

"He said he will not ask for the photograph again."

She looked at him then. Her face was the face of a woman who had been sitting with a question for five days and had arrived at an answer she did not want to give.

"That is not a relief," she said. "That is a warning. He has stopped asking about the photograph because he has started wondering what it really means. And a man who wonders is more dangerous than a man who asks."

The protagonist looked at her.

"Then what do we do?"

She picked up the cloth pouch. She held it in her hands the way she had held it the night she first took it down from the shelf.

"We wait. We see what he wonders. And we decide what to do with the photograph when we know whether his wondering will bring him back."

She placed the pouch on the table. She did not open it. She did not put it away.

She left it there, where it could be seen, where it could be found, where it could be given again if needed.

---

That night, after Obianuju had gone to bed, he sat in the front room with the lamp burning low. The cloth pouch was on the table. The cold was at the edges of the room, waiting, patient, unhurried.

He thought about the room. It was open now. Mr Stephen had seen it. He had found nothing. The story would die. The pepper woman would find something else to talk about. The market would move on. But the room was open. It had been entered by a stranger, inspected, measured, dismissed. And it was still a room. It was still ordinary. It still had walls and a floor and a window and a cupboard and a damp seam and a smell of old rain.

But it was no longer closed.

He thought about the no-colour door. It was still closed. It had not been opened. It had not been touched. Mr Stephen had not asked about it. He had not noticed it. He had looked at the room, found nothing, and left satisfied. The door was still there, waiting, patient, unhurried.

He thought about the cold. It had not weakened. It had only become quieter. It was still at the edges of the room, still waiting, still patient, still unhurried. It had not been defeated by the inspection. It had not been weakened by the photograph. It had only become quieter, the way a thing becomes quieter when it is no longer the loudest thing in the room.

He stood up.

He walked to the no-colour door and put his hand on the handle. The handle was cold. Not the cold of metal left in a draught. The cold of something that had been touched by a room it did not belong to.

He did not open the door.

He stood there with his hand on the cold handle, and he understood that the door was not the door he needed to open. The door he needed to open was the one that would let him accept that the cold had not weakened, only changed shape. And that the change was not a victory. It was a new kind of waiting.

He took his hand off the handle.

He went back to the front room.

He sat down.

The cloth pouch was on the table.

The cold was at the edges.

The room was open.

The no-colour door was still closed.

And the story, for now, was quiet.

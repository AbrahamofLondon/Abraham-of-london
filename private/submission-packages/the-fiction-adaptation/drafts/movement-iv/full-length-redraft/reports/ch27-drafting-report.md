## How the Chapter Opens with Quiet, Not Relief

The chapter opens on the third day of quiet. The market has moved on. The pepper woman has found a new story. The false death story has died. But the quiet is not relief — it is the silence before a door that has not yet been named. The protagonist finds himself looking at the no-colour door without meaning to. He sees Obianuju looking at it too. On the third day, she tells him: "The quiet will not last. Something will come."

---

## How Mr Stephen's Wondering Becomes Pressure

Mr Stephen sends a note on the fourth day. He arrives at the hour he named. His shirt is buttoned wrong at the collar — he has not been sleeping. He admits he has been thinking about the door for three days, that he is not the same as he was before he noticed it. He asks about the door without demanding access. He says he came not to demand anything but to see whether the door would look different now that he has noticed it. He leaves without resolution, which is worse than if he had demanded one.

His power: he has made the door into a question that will not leave him alone. A question, unlike a demand, cannot be refused.

---

## Whether and How the No-Colour Door Becomes Central

The door becomes central through:
- The protagonist and Obianuju both looking at it without meaning to
- Mr Stephen's note arriving the day after the quiet settles
- Mr Stephen's direct question about the door
- Obianuju touching the wood for the first time in weeks
- The woman who took the photograph revealing she has been thinking about the door for thirty years
- Kene's warning: "An idea, once noticed, cannot be closed again"

The door is never opened. It becomes more powerful because it stays closed while being noticed.

---

## How Obianuju Controls the Final Boundary

Obianuju touches the door for the first time in weeks — not the handle, the wood. She listens through her skin. She decides they will tell Mr Stephen the truth: that the door exists, that they do not know what is behind it, that they have chosen not to open it. She controls what is said and what is withheld. She is not cornered into passivity — she is choosing the cost of the next boundary.

---

## How the Protagonist Acts Accurately Under Final Pressure

The protagonist:
- Answers Mr Stephen's question about the door without explaining the cold
- Says "It is part of the house" — accurate, not defensive
- Says "You have a right to know that it is there. You do not have a right to know what is behind it" — precise boundary
- Says "Because some doors are not opened. They are lived beside" — the right answer, not the full answer
- Does not offer more than he was asked
- Does not try to explain what he has no right to explain

His growth is visible in precision, not triumph.

---

## How Kene Appears or Is Felt

Kene arrives in the late afternoon with bread. He:
- Presses the protagonist on the decision to tell the truth
- Says the truth is the most dangerous thing they could tell Mr Stephen
- Admits he is afraid — the first time he has said it directly
- Reveals the woman who took the photograph has been thinking about the door for thirty years
- Warns: "An idea, once noticed, cannot be closed again"

Kene is loyal, afraid, socially intelligent, and useful in an ungentle way.

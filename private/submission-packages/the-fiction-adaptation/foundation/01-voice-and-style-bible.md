# Voice & Style Bible — The Author's Prose, Codified

**Purpose:** So the novel reads as **Abraham of London's** work, not as a genre exercise. This bible is derived from close reading of the author's own memoir prose (Ch.7 "The Fire and the Deal," Ch.9 "The Spiritual Covering," Ch.16 "Funke," Ch.22 "Damisi," Ch.23 "Love Does Not Fear," Ch.1 "Hounslow Call"). Every rule below is anchored to how he actually writes. The fiction inherits this voice and *extends* it into the liminal register — it does not adopt a different voice for being fiction.

---

## 1. The core signature (what makes the voice his)

The author's prose is **grave, morally serious, and built on the rhythm of swell-and-stop**: long accumulating sentences that gather weight, broken by very short declaratives that set the weight down. The short sentence is his hammer. He reasons in public, on the page, and lets the reader watch the thought correct itself.

**The five load-bearing habits:**

1. **The one-line paragraph as a beat.** A short declarative stands alone, carrying the pressure of everything around it.
   - *His:* "He had believed that." / "He still believes it." / "She had not stopped." / "She was gone." / "He had been kept."
   - *Rule:* use sparingly and at the point of maximum weight. One per page at most. Overuse turns a hammer into a tic.

2. **Triadic anaphora — repetition with variation.** Three parallel clauses that escalate, then a turn.
   - *His:* "He had mistaken movement for courage. / He had mistaken intensity for faith. / He had mistaken the absence of hesitation for the presence of God."
   - *His:* "Love could wait. Love could test. Love could say no."
   - *Rule:* the third member must deepen, not merely repeat. Follow the triad with a short line that breaks it.

3. **Balanced antithesis — the corrected sentence.** A claim and its precise correction, often as twin short lines.
   - *His:* "The sentence was true. / His use of it was not." / "Sustained was close. / Preserved was closer." / "It was not an answer. It was a refusal to be governed by the absence of one."
   - *Rule:* this is his signature thought-shape. The fiction earns its gravity by *thinking* in these corrections — but the narrator must not over-explain; let the antithesis land and move on.

4. **Abstraction grounded by concrete verbs.** He takes an abstract noun and gives it a body of plain actions.
   - *His:* "Movement has a body. It books tickets. It answers messages. It travels. It appears. It signs."
   - *Rule:* whenever the prose risks floating into the metaphysical, anchor it in a list of small physical verbs. This is how the supernatural stays embodied (world bible §rules).

5. **Naming what it refuses to do.** He defines by negation, marking the boundaries of honesty.
   - *His:* "Not as accusation. Not as a second trial. Not as a post-mortem of another person." / "Not a legacy. Not a lesson. A person — irreducible, unresolved, real."
   - *Rule:* use the refusal-triad to hold the line on restraint (no villainy, no court, no child-as-evidence) *inside the prose itself*, not just as an external guardrail.

---

## 2. Sentence architecture

- **Rhythm:** alternate long and short. A paragraph typically swells across two or three accumulating sentences, then stops on a short one. Never let three long sentences run without a short one to land them.
- **The long sentence** accumulates by parallel clause and by comma-chained plainness, not by subordination-heavy syntax. He rarely nests clauses deeply; he strings clear units. (*"He saw enough to be troubled. He had reasons for continuing. Some of those reasons were tender. Some were inherited."*)
- **The em-dash** is used for the turn or the held qualification — "Not a legacy. Not a lesson. A person — irreducible, unresolved, real." Use it; do not overuse it.
- **The colon** introduces the thing itself after a setup: *"the thing his mother had named before he was old enough to spell it: he did not let go of the cold things."*
- **Avoid:** semicolon-heavy literary throat-clearing; subordinate-clause pile-ups; the periodic sentence that withholds its verb for effect. His gravity comes from *plainness under pressure*, not from syntactic complexity.

---

## 3. Diction

- **Plain Anglo-Saxon core, KJV-inflected cadence.** Biblical rhythm without devotional vocabulary. (*"There is no fear in love."* sits naturally; *"blessing," "anointing," "breakthrough"* do not — they would tip into the devotional register the book refuses.)
- **Abstract nouns are allowed but must be *earned* by surrounding concreteness:** weight, mercy, provision, discernment, restraint, claim, account. He uses these — but always near a body (a wrist, a peel, a coil, a blanket, a laptop).
- **Recurrent lexicon (his):** *kept, carried, weight, room, door, page, late, preserved, spared, account, the price.* These are load-bearing words; the fiction's motif system (`04`) is built from them.
- **Avoid:** genre-fantasy diction (no "darkness gathered," "ancient evil," "the veil," "otherworldly"); motivational diction ("journey," "healing," "embrace"); clinical/legal diction; tourist-ethnographic explanation of culture.

---

## 4. The register palette (versatility)

A masterwork modulates. The novel has **five registers**; the voice stays his across all of them, but the dial moves:

1. **Grave-reflective** (his default; the swell-and-stop, the corrected sentence). For the protagonist's interior reckoning.
2. **Tender-domestic** (warm, concrete, small). For Obianuju's gifts, the ordinary married life. *(Ch.22 "Damisi" is the model — love rendered through specific small fact.)*
3. **Uncanny-plain** (the supernatural stated flatly, no adornment). For the Spirit Husband's weather and the Voice. The horror is in the plainness. *(Ch.7's "He knew it was hell. Not because anyone told him… he knew it the way a body knows temperature" is the model.)*
4. **Lyric-liminal** (present tense, dreamlogic but precise). For the Trance. *(See Ch.1/Ch.3 fiction drafts — present tense, "walking is the only verb.")*
5. **Plain-narrative** (clean forward motion). Connective tissue; the town, the day, the ordinary. Keeps the book from drowning in its own gravity.

**Rule:** no chapter sits in one register the whole way. The grave must be relieved by the tender or the plain; the lyric must be grounded by the concrete. Monotony of register is the single most common failure of "serious" fiction; this book defeats it deliberately.

---

## 5. How the narrator thinks (point of view)

- **Close third, anchored to the protagonist**, who is unnamed. The narrator knows his interior completely and the others only from outside — exactly as the memoir narrator knows Abraham from inside and Iyalode, Funke, Damisi from a loving outside.
- **The narrator reasons and corrects itself in real time** (his signature). It is allowed to circle, to qualify, to say *that was not the only error.* But in fiction this must be **rationed** — the novel dramatises more than the memoir explains. When the prose starts to *explain the cosmology*, stop and render instead.
- **The narrator withholds.** It tells what can be told and marks what it will not tell. ("He did not do the arithmetic.") Withholding is characterisation, not evasion.

---

## 6. Dialogue

- **Spare, weighted, slightly formal.** People say few words and the words carry. *(Ch.22: "I am not your bro. I am your son." / Ch.16: "happy with the bargain.")*
- Each important line of dialogue should be sayable as a standalone — it should be the kind of sentence a reader keeps.
- **No naturalistic chatter.** No filler exchanges. If a line does not carry weight or turn the scene, cut it.
- **The forces' speech:** the Spirit Husband almost never speaks (felt, not heard). The Voice speaks once, plainly, transactionally — its single utterance must be the most quotable line in the book. The Trance shows, never speaks.

---

## 7. Concrete-detail discipline

The author grounds every large thing in one exact physical fact:
- the grey blanket "with two lines at the top — red or orange, he cannot be certain of the colour in the dark of five in the morning";
- the orange peeled "in a single unbroken ribbon";
- Iyalode's "iro and buba in ankara, gele tied high."

**Rule:** every scene must carry at least one such exact, slightly imperfect physical detail — the imperfection (*red or orange*) is what makes it true. Avoid generic sensory wash. One precise object beats a paragraph of atmosphere.

---

## 8. What to avoid (the firewall against losing the voice)

- Genre-fantasy register and vocabulary.
- Dream-sequence vagueness (the Trance is *precise*, not hazy).
- Over-explaining the cosmology (the world bible exists so the prose never has to).
- Motivational / devotional / therapeutic diction.
- Sustained single-register gravity (relieve it).
- Memoir-style direct confession (this is fiction; the "I" reasoning of the memoir becomes the close-third reckoning of the novel).
- Purple lyricism. His lyricism is disciplined — earned by plainness around it.

---

## 9. A calibration passage (target sound)

The drafted Ch.1 opening is on-voice and can serve as the tuning fork:

> "There was an hour, late in the afternoon, when his wife went somewhere he could not follow, and the strange thing — the thing he never said aloud, because there was no one to say it to who would not try to mend it — was that he had stopped minding."

Note: the long accumulating first clause; the em-dash held qualification; the short landing ("he had stopped minding"); the plain diction; the withholding. This is the target. Draft toward it, and toward the memoir's corrected-sentence gravity, and the book will read as his.

---

## 10. Standing operating principles (novel-wide law)

Adopted as standing law for all 30 chapters (from the Ch.1 expansion briefs). These govern structure and effect; §§1–9 govern the sentence.

1. **The ordinary precedes the extraordinary, always.** Before any chapter shows Obianuju in the hour / in trance / in cold / in absence, it must first show her in *presence*. Before the protagonist meets any of the three forces, show him doing something mundane. The reader cannot feel what is taken unless they have first felt what was there.
2. **The supernatural is admitted through the mundane, never announced.** The narration surrounding supernatural material becomes *plainer*, not more theatrical. No "and then the room changed." The contrast carries the power; atmospheric signalling drains it.
3. **Trance is present tense; everything else is past.** Reliable across all 30 chapters. The tense shift (into italic present) is the reader's only register signal — no hybrid passages, no exceptions.
4. **The three forces stay distinct and unexplained.** Spirit Husband = CLAIM (cold, absence); the Voice = INTIMIDATION (speaks once or twice in the whole book); the Trance = MESSENGER (accurate, neutral, never threatens). No conflation; no character names them; no narrator explains them. The reader learns them like the tide — by experience.
5. **Obianuju is never reduced to her function in his story.** Every chapter featuring her contains at least one moment where she exists beyond him — a thought, a gesture, an action wholly her own. (Her own unfinished business is visible to the reader even where he cannot see it.)
6. **The protagonist's fearlessness is the central danger, not a virtue.** Never admired uncritically. He is not brave; he is constitutionally unable to register certain threats, and that inability is what will cost him. Hold it the way Ch.1's close holds it.
7. **Every chapter has an acceptance-gate sentence** — one irreplaceable line that could only belong to this author, this man, this world. Identify it before committing a chapter; if you cannot, the chapter is not finished. *(Ch.1: "He ate the bitter half and gave her the less bitter, and that, too, was a thing he did without being able to defend it.")*

**The over-engineering guard (carried with these):** motifs must feel *discovered, not deployed*; sentence-level truth outranks structural symmetry; the test for any addition is — *would this still belong if the blueprint did not exist?* If no, cut it. Do not let the architecture show.

# World & Cosmology Bible

**Purpose:** The internal logic of the world, stated fully here so the prose never has to explain it. The reader should feel the world is governed by laws older than psychology — and never be lectured on what they are. This document is for the author's eyes; almost none of it is stated on the page.

---

## 1. First principle

The world does not confirm itself, and this is not a flaw in it — it is its nature. The real and the symbolic are not two layers with a seam between them; they are one fabric the protagonist (and, by the second half, the reader) can no longer cut along. The cosmology is therefore not a "system" to be learned but a **weather** to be lived inside.

**The one law above all laws:** *the forces cannot be fought, only outlived — and outliving is not the same as defeating.* Every scene obeys this. A scene in which a force is bargained down, tricked, exorcised, or beaten is a wrong scene.

---

## 2. The liminal register — the physics

- **Geography is unmappable.** The town is real-adjacent and coastal; its streets do not connect in a retainable order; the protagonist cannot draw it. Places are known as islands, not as a network. (Never explained in-text; rendered as his quiet inability to hold the map.) Place-names are coined and singular, never matched to a real map.
- **Time runs on spiritual weight, not the clock.** A scene's length is governed by what it carries. A single afternoon hour can hold more than a year. Seasons can be missing from a record (the journal's gaps) without anyone remarking. The "long rains" are the season of the claim; the cold hour is that season scaled to a daily tide.
- **The supernatural has plain internal logic, stated by behaviour, never argued.** It does what it does, reliably. Cold arrives on a schedule. The Trance shows true things. The Voice keeps its word. Reliability is what makes it real; capriciousness would make it a special effect.
- **"Was that real?" is structurally forbidden.** No character asks it. To ask it would offer the reader an exit the book refuses to provide. Doubt is rendered as *behaviour* (he steps back from the water; he does not do the arithmetic), never as a question.
- **The tense law (the reader's only instrument, then taken away):** "real" = past tense; Trance = present tense. Across the book the present bleeds into the "real" until, by Movement III, tense no longer tells the reader which register they are in. This is the world's central formal argument made operational. (See voice bible §4 register 4; structural blueprint for where the bleed accelerates.)

## 3. The three forces — full mechanics

They are **distinct**, **in tension with each other**, **intelligent or structurally inevitable**, and **undefeatable**. They are not one antagonist in three masks. Each has a want, a method, a limit, and a territory.

### THE SPIRIT HUSBAND — *the Claim*
- **Nature:** a presence, not a person. Older than the marriage; older than the town's name. The marine/ancestral spirit-spouse of the deep tradition — a prior bond that predates and outranks a living man's love.
- **Want:** not to harm the protagonist — to *hold* Obianuju, whom it regards as already its own, and to extend that holding into what comes next (continuation).
- **Method:** **absence.** It is felt, almost never seen or heard. Cold rooms on a schedule; the unreachable hour; her hand going still; a silence with a shape. It is the climate of the shared life.
- **Limit:** it cannot be everywhere; it claims the *season* and the *hour*. It is territorial, not omnipresent. It does not act in haste because it does not believe it needs to — it has the patience of something that arrived first.
- **Jealousy:** Old-Testament, not romantic. It does not compete for affection; it asserts prior right. It does not hate the protagonist; it *counts* him — late.
- **Territory (structural home):** the domestic/interior register; the marriage's weather.

### THE VOICE — *the Intimidation*
- **Nature:** direct address. Speaks once, decisively, with explicit threat; perhaps a faint second echo near the end. Transactional where the Claim is patient.
- **Want:** to stop the protagonist by naming a cost and daring him to test it.
- **Method:** a single utterance — plain, immediate, unanswerable. It names a price.
- **Limit:** its power is its rarity. It cannot speak twice without cheapening. It does not need to act on its threat; it relies on a frightened man stopping himself. (Its tragedy in this book: the protagonist *cannot be frightened*, so he tests it — and the cost is real.)
- **Territory:** the single mid-book hinge (Ch.16). Nowhere else.

### THE TRANCE — *the Warning that comes too late*
- **Nature:** not an antagonist. A messenger-force, morally neutral, even protective. It delivers true information by means the waking world cannot verify.
- **Want:** nothing for itself. It only shows.
- **Method:** it shows an absence before a death is known; it confirms, it does not prevent. Its horror is entirely in its **accuracy**, never in malice.
- **Limit:** it arrives **too late to act, in time to confirm.** It cannot be used, summoned, or bargained with. It does not threaten.
- **Territory:** the novel's relationship to its own ending; the present-tense bleed; the final reactivation (Ch.29) that turns the warning toward something new.

### The tension between them (the engine)
A man caught between a force that **claims** the woman he loves, a force that **threatens** him, and a force that only ever tells him **true things too late to act on.** None can be fought. The novel's resolution is not victory over them — it is what it means to keep walking inside a world governed by forces that cannot be fought, and can only, eventually, stop being bargained with.

## 4. The spirit-spouse tradition (cultural authority, handled from the inside)

The cosmology draws on a real and widely understood tradition (the spirit-spouse / marine-spirit bond) but renders it **from within belief, not from anthropological distance.** Rules of handling:

- Stated plainly, as the characters who live it would state it — never glossed for an outsider.
- The Elder holds **two cosmologies without conflict** (the older knowledge and the church), exactly as Iyalode does in the memoir — "the coexistence was not a contradiction she needed to resolve." This is the book's model of authority: it does not adjudicate between systems; it inhabits both.
- No exotica, no tourist exposition, no italicised "foreign" mystique. The supernatural is as ordinary to this world as the tide.
- The protagonist's own tradition (his certainty of being *kept*, the account opened in the brown water) is the **same kind of bond seen from the inside of the one it holds** — so he, too, is claimed. This rhyme (his claim / her claim) is the book's deepest structural symmetry.

---

## 5. What the world must never do

- Never explain itself in a block of exposition.
- Never let a force be defeated, exorcised, tricked, or out-willed.
- Never let a character ask "was that real?"
- Never resolve the registers back into a safe realism at the end.
- Never render the supernatural as spectacle (no pyrotechnics; the power is in plainness and reliability).
- Never make the cultural cosmology a curiosity for an outside gaze.

---

## 6. The world's emotional truth (so the metaphysics stays legible)

Beneath the cosmology is one human truth the reader feels even without the rules: **to love someone is to discover what arrived before you, and to learn that some of what arrived first will never leave.** The Spirit Husband is the literal form of a thing every reader knows — the past that owns a person before you meet them; the grief, the wound, the prior love, the family claim. The metaphysics is never *only* metaphor (the book refuses to let "was that real?" reduce it), but it is *also* always this, so the strangest pages stay emotionally legible to a reader who has never heard of a spirit-spouse.

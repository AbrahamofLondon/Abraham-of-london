# Character Bibles

**Purpose:** Deep interiors. A novel is rich because the author knows the people far past what the page shows. Most of this never appears directly; all of it pressures every scene. Close third stays anchored to the protagonist — so he is known from inside, and everyone else from a loving, limited outside (the memoir's method: Abraham from within, Iyalode/Funke/Damisi from a loving outside).

---

## THE PROTAGONIST (unnamed)

**The wound that is also the engine.** As a boy of nine he nearly drowned fetching a younger child's sandal, and in the brown water at the end of himself he was given — not as hope but as handed-down fact — the certainty that he was *not finished*, that an account had been opened in his name, that he was kept and there was a price in the keeping. He came out unable to fear death. The door through which fear enters other men was walled up in him. **This is not courage** (courage masters fear; he has none to master). It is *availability* — to the sea, the cold hour, every patient thing that does not need a frightened man to take him.

**The flaw, stated for the author (never for the page):** *a man who cannot fear death is defenceless against forces that do not need to kill him.* His fearlessness lets him stay past the point any frightened man would leave. It is shown as danger, never admiration.

**The secondary trait: lateness.** He arrives after things have already begun — late to his survivals, his grief, his marriage. He carries it as a private shame dressed as temperament. The Claim names him by it: *you are late; you have always been late.* The journal names it too, as love. Lateness is his shame and his identity and the hinge the cosmology grips.

**The third trait: he will not count.** He notices the soft edges — the unmappable streets, the missing seasons in the journal, the cold on a schedule — and decides each is "the heat," "the damp," "ordinary," because to lay them end to end (the *arithmetic*) would force a conclusion he is not ready to keep company with. His refusal to count is the intellectual form of his fearlessness: both are ways of not flinching, and both leave him open.

**What he wants:** to love and be wholly chosen; to build a room with people in it; to be, for once, not late.

**His arc (the truth he reaches):** *being kept is not the same as being spared.* He has always believed he was kept; by the end he learns the keeping never promised the safety of the ones he loved — and that the forces cannot be defeated, only outlived. He is named (by the Trance) only at the very end, with the function-word that says which quality the world actually needed from him.

**Voice cues:** he reasons in the author's corrected-sentence rhythm; he withholds; he leans toward cold things; he is tender in small concrete acts (eating the bitter half of the orange).

## OBIANUJU (the central figure — the merge)

**Rule above all:** *never a villain.* Every harm sits near a gift. The reader must finish unable to hate her cleanly. She is partly true, partly broken, partly weaponised by something she did not fully choose and may not fully understand.

**Who she is on her own terms.** A woman formed in a household of women, neat and capable in the way authority is neat (the Iyalode-formation, fictionalised), who kept a journal across years of solitude and wrote a future toward herself because the present gave her too little to hold. She loved the protagonist before love had a target. The journal is her faith made visible — and it is a **gift** first, a warning only in a later, sadder light.

**What holds her.** The Spirit Husband's claim predates her marriage and predates the protagonist — "I was not the only one waiting." She is not its agent; she is its held. The cold hour is not something she does to him; it is something done to her, daily, that she cannot refuse and largely cannot speak. Her duality is not duplicity — it is the lived condition of a person who is genuinely two things: wholly the warm woman of the kitchen, and wholly claimed.

**The reversal (Movement II).** Once continuation enters the world, she changes — and the change is *also* her grief, also done to her, the claim reaching to extend itself into what comes next. Render the reversal so the reader's sympathy survives it.

**Her own unfinished business (Movement III, the rhyme).** One image, near the end: she is walking toward something unresolved, entirely separate from him, that the novel will never show. The reader is left to wonder about her life permanently — as one does about someone once loved. **No tidy backstory is ever supplied.**

**What she wants:** to be loved as a whole person, not rescued, not solved, not possessed; to set down a thing she has carried since before she could name it. Whether she can is the question the book refuses to answer.

**Voice cues (her dialogue):** few words, weighted, slightly formal, sayable as standalone. ("Before you say anything. I wrote this before I knew your name." / "I was not the only one waiting. I only thought I was.")

## THE ELDER (the Iyalode-function)

An old woman of spiritual authority who holds two cosmologies without conflict — the older knowledge and the church, both hers, the coexistence not a problem she must solve. She *sees* what Obianuju carries and does not pretend not to. She **neither condemns nor rescues**; she treats the claim as weather, not as evil to be fought.

**Function:** she gives the protagonist **one sentence** (discovered in drafting) that he will not understand until the end, and which the ending pays off. She is moral authority precisely because she refuses to solve the plot. She is not an oracle; she is a person who has lived long enough to stop being frightened by what cannot be changed — the wise counterpart to the protagonist's fearlessness (hers earned by age and faith; his by a wound).

**Avoid:** the wise-elder cliché, prophecy clichés, the oracle who dispenses plot solutions. She is irreducible, unresolved, real (the memoir's last word on Iyalode).

## THE CONFIDANT

A brother- or friend-figure who can feel the cold rooms from outside and cannot name them — the reader's proxy for the forbidden question. Warm, limited, loyal. He asks, obliquely, *are you alright in that house*, and is more frightened by the protagonist's calm *yes* than a *no* would have made him.

**Function:** external confirmation that the cold is real and not the protagonist's private weather; the human measure of what is being lost (pushed to the edge in Movement II, returning in III as the gauge of returning life). He carries warmth and limitation, never exposition.

## THE THREE FORCES AS CHARACTERS

(Full mechanics in the world bible §3; here, how to *write* them.)

- **The Spirit Husband:** write it as weather and as a creditor's patience. It never monologues, never gloats, rarely appears. Its characterisation is in temperature, schedule, and the single repeated idea — *prior right, calmly held.* Give it dignity, not menace; menace is for lesser antagonists.
- **The Voice:** write its one utterance as the most quotable line in the book — plain, transactional, unanswerable. Everything before it must earn its single intrusion; everything after lives in its silence.
- **The Trance:** write it in present tense, precise not hazy, as a place where "walking is the only verb." It is the kindest and most terrible of the three — it loves the protagonist enough to show him the truth, and can only ever show it too late.

## Relationships (the web)

- **Protagonist ↔ Obianuju:** love that is real on both sides and claimed on hers; warmth and cold braided in the same marriage.
- **Protagonist ↔ Spirit Husband:** not rivalry — *arithmetic.* The force counts him late; he refuses to count back.
- **Obianuju ↔ Spirit Husband:** the held and the holder; predates everything; never her choice, never fully not-her.
- **Protagonist ↔ Elder:** the man who cannot fear meets the woman no longer frightened — one wound, one wisdom, the same stillness reached by opposite roads.
- **Protagonist ↔ Confidant:** the seen and the seer; the calm and the alarm.
- **His claim ↔ her claim:** the deepest symmetry — both are *kept* by something that arrived first; the book's title belongs to the Spirit Husband and, quietly, to the account opened in the brown water.

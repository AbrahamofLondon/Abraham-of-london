## Before/After Passages (Detailed Examples)

### Example 1: Physical Embodiment Over Face-Reading

**Chapter 23, ~page 13**

**Before:**
> "She looked at him. Her face was the face of a woman who had been sitting with a question for seven days and had arrived at an answer she did not want to give."

**After:**
> "She looked at him. Her jaw had closed the way it closed before she made a decision. She held the red thread as though it was something she had agreed to carry."

**Why:** The reader sees action (jaw position, object handling) rather than receiving interpretation of internal state. More cinematic, more trustworthy. Shows > tells.

---

### Example 2: Declarative Narration Over Meta-Observation

**Chapter 26, ~page 12**

**Before:**
> "The protagonist stood very still. He understood that Mr Stephen had done exactly what Obianuju had predicted: he had used the false story as leverage. Not by believing it. By offering to disprove it. The offer was reasonable. The refusal would be unreasonable. And reasonableness, in the hands of a man like Mr Stephen, was the most dangerous tool there was."

**After:**
> "The protagonist stood very still. Mr Stephen had done exactly what Obianuju had predicted: he had used the false story as leverage. Not by believing it. By offering to disprove it. The offer was reasonable. The refusal would be unreasonable. And reasonableness, in the hands of a man like Mr Stephen, was the most dangerous tool there was."

**Why:** Removes "He understood that" wrapper. The observation is now presented as direct narrative statement, not as protagonist's internal realization. More urgent, more objective.

---

### Example 3: Fresh Image Over Repeated Metaphor

**Chapter 24, ~page 8**

**Before:**
> "The sentence entered him the way the cold entered the room — from the edges, until it had occupied the whole of him."

**After:**
> "The sentence found the seam in him and opened it wide."

**Why:** "Seam" imagery is unexpected, visceral, and specific to this moment. The repeated "the way the cold entered" had become reflexive. New language earns its place.

---

### Example 4: Silence & Physicality Over Interpretation

**Chapter 27, ~page 6**

**Before:**
> "She looked at him. Her face was the face of a woman who had been sitting with a question for six days and had arrived at an answer she did not want to give."

**After:**
> "She looked at him. The silence around her had become the shape of something she had been holding for six days. She was ready to let it out."

**Why:** Shifts from reading her face to sensing her atmosphere. "Shape of something" lets ambiguity breathe. The final sentence implies agency/decision without stating it.

## Sentences Intentionally Preserved & Why

### Ceremonial Verdicts

No "the face of a woman/man who..." constructions in the final chapters function as ceremonial verdicts in the post-edit version. The brief allowed these as exceptions; in practice, the climax is sharper without them.

### Apex Understanding Moments

**Ch.28, Final Statement** — The ultimate "he understood that" moment:

> "He stood there with his hand on the cold handle. The door was not the door he needed to open. The one he needed to open was already open. It had been open since the day he had held a line for a woman with a nail between her teeth. He had not known it then. He knew it now."

**Why Preserved:** This is the arc-completion moment. The understanding IS the point. The edit removed the "he understood that" frame but kept the substance so the realization lands without meta-narration. This is the moment where the protagonist finally grasps that his acceptance (holding the line, staying, witnessing) was itself the opening of a door. To cut this would hollow the climax.

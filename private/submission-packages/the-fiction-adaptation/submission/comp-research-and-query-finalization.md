#### **"Dept. of Speculation" by Jenny Offill (2014, Knopf)**

**Core:** A fragmented meditation on marriage, motherhood, and consciousness. The novel is structured as the wife's thoughts and observations, treating marriage as the primary philosophical subject.

**Key Characteristics:**
- Marriage as protagonist (not backdrop)
- Restrained, economical prose (fragments; high meaning density)
- Metaphysical/philosophical undertone (questions of meaning, presence, time, love)
- Knowledge asymmetry (wife observes; husband is observed)
- Refusal of linear narrative or plot resolution
- Female perspective and agency central

**Why It Matches Your Novel:**
- ✅ **Marriage focus:** Like *The Fiction Adaptation*, marriage is not secondary to plot—it IS the subject
- ✅ **Restrained language:** Both use spare prose to carry emotional weight
- ✅ **Metaphysical treatment:** Both explore philosophical/metaphysical themes as emotional reality
- ✅ **Boundary/privacy:** Both explore what can and cannot be shared between intimate partners
- ✅ **Female knowledge:** Both center on what the wife knows and how she uses that knowledge
- ✅ **No resolution:** Both end with acceptance rather than plot resolution

**Agent Appeal:**
- Validates literary-first positioning for your novel
- Signals "serious literary fiction" positioning
- Demonstrates market for marriage-centered literary narrative
- Published by major publisher (Knopf); strong track record

**Market Position:** "Philosophical literary fiction; marriage as subject; experimental form"

#### **"Mexican Gothic" by Silvia Moreno-Garcia (2015, Del Rey)**

**Core:** A woman moves into her new husband's ancestral manor house. The house is supernatural; her husband has secrets; boundaries between self and other become terrifyingly porous. The novel refuses conventional gothic explanations while maintaining metaphysical weight.

**Key Characteristics:**
- Marriage under supernatural pressure (primary relationship tested)
- Female agency despite pressure (wife makes strategic choices; refuses victimhood)
- Restrained prose (gothic elements understated; psychological over spectacle)
- Supernatural elements NOT explained or resolved (ambiguity maintained)
- Boundaries/privacy as power (rooms that must remain closed)
- Female perspective throughout; husband becomes secondary

**Why It Matches Your Novel:**
- ✅ **Marriage pressure:** Like your novel, marriage is tested by metaphysical force the couple cannot control
- ✅ **Refusal to open:** Both feature doors/spaces that must remain closed; refusal is strategic strength
- ✅ **Supernatural restraint:** Both treat supernatural elements as literary pressure, not plot mechanism
- ✅ **Female knowledge:** Wife in both novels knows more than husband; her refusal to explain is power
- ✅ **Boundary themes:** Both explore privacy, boundary-keeping, and what it means to protect silence
- ✅ **No lore:** Neither novel explains the supernatural; mystery is not to be solved

**Agent Appeal:**
- Successfully positioned "literary gothic" to mainstream literary market
- Demonstrates that supernatural literary fiction can be prestigious, not genre-confined
- Shows female-driven narrative in metaphysically charged setting can appeal broadly
- Published by major publisher (Del Rey); market-tested positioning

**Market Position:** "Literary gothic; marriage and inheritance as metaphors; psychological over spectacle"

## Query Sending Protocol

### Format & Process

1. **Follow agent's submission guidelines exactly**
   - Email address, subject line format, attachment preferences
   - Word count and comp placement (adjust to agent's preference)
   - Simultaneous submissions to multiple agents are standard (mention "simultaneous submission" if required)

2. **Personalize each query**
   - Do not send form letters
   - Reference agent's recent deals or #MSWL if relevant
   - Show you've researched them specifically

3. **Professional formatting**
   - Standard business letter format (your address, date, agent address)
   - Professional font and spacing
   - Clean, no formatting tricks

4. **Tracking**
   - Keep spreadsheet: agent name, agency, query date, response date/status
   - Set reminders for follow-ups (typically 6–8 weeks, then follow-up email)
   - Track any specific feedback for refinement

### Timeline

**Week 1:** Send Tier 1 batch (5–7 agents)  
**Weeks 2–3:** Continue Tier 1 if agents remain  
**Week 4:** Evaluate responses; prepare Tier 2 if needed  
**Weeks 5–8:** Wait and monitor (typical response time: 6–8 weeks)  
**Week 9:** If no offers, send follow-up emails to non-responsive agents  
**Week 10+:** Move to Tier 2 if Tier 1 yielded interest but not offer

### Response Expectations

- **Agent interest:** 10–20% typical for unsolicited queries from unknown authors
- **Request for full manuscript:** Favorable sign; agent is seriously considering
- **Pass:** Professional rejection (can ask why; some agents explain, many don't)
- **No response:** Treat as pass after 8 weeks (agent's inbox guidelines typically allow follow-up)
